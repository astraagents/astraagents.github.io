Prebiotic Synthesis of Threonine
Molecule-specific constraints that shape plausible prebiotic routes
Threonine is a hydroxy-amino acid whose prebiotic formation is complicated by (i) the need to install both an amino group and a hydroxyl-bearing sidechain and (ii) the fact that two diastereomeric products are typically expected: threonine and allothreonine. The co-occurrence of threonine and allothreonine in abiotic product mixtures is explicitly reported in classic spark-discharge experiments, where both “threonine” and “allothreonine” were confirmed among the synthesized amino acids. [1]
A key practical implication for network design is that many plausible routes do not make threonine “directly” from CO₂; instead, they converge on a specific aldehyde-level carbonyl precursor—lactaldehyde (2‑hydroxypropanal)—followed by Strecker-type conversion to an aminonitrile and then hydrolysis to the amino acid. This lactaldehyde-centered view is articulated both in cyanosulfidic photoredox work (where lactaldehyde is produced photochemically from nitrile/cyanohydrin precursors) and in meteoritic Strecker-cyanohydrin discussions (where lactaldehyde is explicitly identified as the carbonyl precursor for threonine/allothreonine). [2]
Hydrothermal persistence is also a constraint: hydroxy amino acids (including serine and threonine) can undergo dehydration and other degradation pathways during diagenesis/hydrothermal exposure, which matters when evaluating vent-based syntheses or post-synthesis survival. [3]
Known prebiotic synthesis routes for threonine
Electric-discharge (atmospheric / volcanic-plume) synthesis.
A strong line of experimental evidence for abiotic threonine production comes from spark-discharge experiments designed to simulate reducing (or locally reducing) atmospheres. In the 1958 H₂S-rich Miller experiment reanalysis, E. T. Parker[4] and coauthors (2011, Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences) report that residues produced from a spark discharge acting on a gas mixture containing H₂S, CH₄, NH₃, and CO₂ contained a broad suite of amino acids and amines, including threonine; chiral amino acids were largely racemic within measurement uncertainty, supporting an abiotic origin (rather than modern biological contamination). [5]
Earlier work also directly confirmed threonine and allothreonine in discharge products from mixtures including methane, nitrogen, water, and traces of ammonia. [1]
UV-driven cyanosulfidic photoredox + Strecker assembly via lactaldehyde.
A second, mechanistically explicit route emerges from cyanosulfidic photoredox chemistry associated with Daniel J. Ritson[6] and John D. Sutherland[7] (2013, Angewandte Chemie International Edition). In this system, UV (254 nm) photoredox chemistry reduces nitriles/cyanohydrins to generate aldehydes (including lactaldehyde, the threonine carbonyl precursor), and subsequent addition of HCN and NH₃ yields the corresponding Strecker aminonitriles (including the (allo-)threonine aminonitrile). [8]
A later synthesis-and-systems perspective by Nicholas J. Green[9], Xu, and Sutherland (2021, Journal of the American Chemical Society) explicitly situates this “lactaldehyde → threonine aminonitrile” step inside a broader UV-photochemical network driven by hydrated-electron photochemistry and cyanosulfidic redox cycling. [10]
HCN-oligomer / aminomalononitrile (AMN) chemistry as an alternative C–N assembly route.
A distinct, often-overlooked proposal is that HCN oligomers provide multifunctional C/N building blocks. In this vein, George Thanassi[11] (1975, Journal of Molecular Evolution) reports that reacting aminomalononitrile (a key HCN oligomer) with electrophiles (including aldehydes) followed by hydrolysis yields a range of amino acids, including threonine and allothreonine. This route is attractive for network design because it bypasses the need to pre-form lactaldehyde in bulk; the tradeoff is that selectivity, geochemical plausibility of AMN accumulation, and competing polymerization pathways remain active questions. [12]
Parent-body (meteoritic) Strecker–cyanohydrin synthesis as evidence of a natural “threonine module.”
Although not an Earth experiment per se, carbonaceous-chondrite organic chemistry provides a natural laboratory for reactions relevant to early Earth delivery and corroborates mechanistic plausibility. A detailed treatment in a Kyushu University-hosted report (associated with threonine/hydroxy-amino-acid analyses in carbonaceous chondrites) describes Strecker-cyanohydrin synthesis where aldehydes/ketones + NH₃ → imines → CN⁻ addition → hydrolysis to α‑amino acids, and it explicitly identifies lactaldehyde as the carbonyl precursor for threonine and/or allo-threonine. [13]
Key intermediates and precursors that define a threonine synthesis network
Across the most experimentally grounded routes, threonine sits at the end of a relatively consistent intermediate chain:
The minimal “Strecker module” requires a carbonyl compound, ammonia (or another nitrogen source that can generate an imine), and cyanide; the carbonyl required for threonine/allothreonine is lactaldehyde. [14]
In cyanosulfidic photoredox networks, the upstream pool can be generated from HCN-derived nitriles and cyanohydrins, with hydrogen sulfide acting as terminal reductant and Cu(I) cyanide functioning as a photoredox-active catalyst/surface. Key intermediates and branch points in the Ritson–Sutherland sequence include: glycolonitrile → glycolaldehyde (and formaldehyde, acetaldehyde as side/rearranged products), acetaldehyde cyanohydrin (lactonitrile-type node) → lactaldehyde, and then lactaldehyde → threonine aminonitrile in the presence of added HCN and NH₃. [8]
In spark-discharge contexts, the primary “precursor-generating” chemistry is atmospheric/plume radical chemistry producing HCN, aldehydes/ketones, and related C–N intermediates that can rain out and further react in surface waters—an explicit motivation for the volcanic-plume framing of Miller-type experiments. [15]
A key branching control is ammonia availability: the meteoritic discussion emphasizes that ammonia-rich environments favor aminonitrile (amino acid) pathways, whereas ammonia-poor settings favor cyanohydrins and hydroxy acids—directly impacting whether the threonine branch is expressed efficiently. [13]
Reaction mechanisms, conditions, yields, and selectivity
UV photoredox → lactaldehyde → (allo-)threonine aminonitrile
The most quantitatively specified prebiotic route to a threonine precursor is the UV photoredox chemistry of Ritson & Sutherland (2013, Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.), which directly reports yields for lactaldehyde formation under defined conditions:
A representative system for first-stage reduction used 10 mM glycolonitrile, 30 mM H₂S, 33 mM sodium phosphate, plus solid CuCN (10 mol % relative to glycolonitrile), irradiated at 254 nm in a quartz cuvette; after 2 h, glycolaldehyde (detected as hydrate) formed in 42% yield in solution, alongside other aldehydic products including acetaldehyde. [8]
Critically, “second-stage” cyanohydrin photoreductions in the same study yield the dedicated threonine carbonyl precursor: irradiation of acetaldehyde cyanohydrin furnished lactaldehyde in 19% yield (after 2 h irradiation). Lactaldehyde is explicitly identified as the Strecker precursor of “(allo-)threonine.” [8]
Downstream assembly to the threonine aminonitrile is demonstrated by adding HCN and ammonia: when HCN and NH₃ were added to photoreduction products, lactaldehyde was “smoothly converted” via its cyanohydrin to the (allo-)threonine Strecker intermediate aminonitrile (aminonitrile 22 in the paper’s numbering). Timescales reported for aminonitrile formation in these systems are days to weeks, indicating that geochemical “holding times” and compartmentalization could matter. [8]
Mechanistically, this route couples: (i) photoredox reduction of nitrile/cyanohydrin nodes to aldehydes (including lactaldehyde) and (ii) conventional Strecker-type conversion (aldehyde → imine → aminonitrile) once NH₃ and CN⁻/HCN are available; hydrolysis of aminonitriles to amino acids is the expected final step in Strecker frameworks, and the meteoritic Strecker discussion explicitly frames hydrolysis of aminonitriles as producing α-amino acids. [16]
A systems-level restatement by Green, Xu, and Sutherland (2021, JACS) emphasizes that photoreduction of the relevant cyanohydrin node leads to lactaldehyde, and lactaldehyde then yields a threonine precursor aminonitrile by addition of HCN and NH₃, embedding this step inside a broader cyanosulfidic network. [10]
Spark discharge / volcanic plume simulation → threonine in complex amino-acid mixtures
In Parker et al. (2011, PNAS), reanalysis of archived Miller-type residues from a 1958 discharge experiment (gas mixture including CH₄, NH₃, H₂S, CO₂) identified 23 amino acids and 4 amines; the paper explicitly notes threonine among amino acids generated by this discharge chemistry and interprets racemic chiral amino acids (D/L ≈ 1 within stated uncertainty) as evidence against modern biological contamination. [17]
Quantitatively, Parker et al. present product abundances as molar ratios relative to glycine (=1). In their Fig. 2, threonine appears at roughly the 10⁻² scale relative to glycine (order-of-magnitude read from the plotted point on a log molar-ratio axis). [18]
In an earlier and more expansive discharge study, Ring et al. (1972, PNAS) report that electric discharges in a methane–nitrogen–water system (with traces of ammonia) produced a wide set of amino acids, and explicitly confirm both threonine and allothreonine (alongside serine and others) by ion-exchange chromatography and GC–MS. [1]
These discharge pathways are usually interpreted as producing amino acids via formation of reactive small C–N intermediates (HCN, aldehydes/ketones) followed by aqueous-phase assembly (often Strecker-type), and Parker et al. explicitly connect volcanic lightning + reduced gases to generation of HCN and other intermediates that can wash out and accumulate in tidal settings. [15]
HCN-oligomer (AMN) electrophile chemistry → threonine/allothreonine after hydrolysis
Thanassi (1975, J. Mol. Evol.) provides experimental evidence that aminomalononitrile-derived chemistry can yield threonine and allothreonine after hydrolysis when AMN is reacted with electrophiles (including aldehydes) under mild conditions. The study explicitly states that amino acids (including threonine/allothreonine) were identified and quantified from hydrolysates. [12]
From a mechanism standpoint, AMN functions as a multifunctional C–N synthon, offering an alternative to “single-carbonyl + HCN + NH₃” assembly; its prebiotic plausibility hinges on whether AMN can accumulate at useful concentrations without being diverted into intractable HCN polymers—an issue treated as part of the broader “HCN chemistry” challenge space in origin-of-life literature. [19]
Hydrothermal Strecker-type synthesis: relevance and limitations for threonine specifically
Hydrothermal Strecker synthesis under mineral-buffered redox conditions is experimentally documented for several amino acids, demonstrating that Strecker chemistry can proceed in plausible vent/flank conditions if aldehydes/ketones and cyanide are available. Andersson & Holm (2000, Origins of Life and Evolution of the Biosphere) summarize hydrothermal experiments (Hennet et al., 1992) using HCN, CH₂O, and NH₄Cl at 150 °C and 10 bar in the presence of a pyrite–pyrrhotite–magnetite buffer; reported products include mM-level glycine (≈9–12 mM) and lower but measurable levels of other amino acids (e.g., alanine ≈0.5–1.3 mM; aspartate and glutamate at ≈0.28–0.36 mM and ≈0.064–0.100 mM, respectively). [20]
However, threonine itself is not among the explicitly tabulated products in that summary table, highlighting a practical bottleneck: even if hydrothermal Strecker chemistry is viable in general, demonstrating threonine specifically requires (i) access to lactaldehyde (or a direct hydrothermal pathway to lactaldehyde/lactaldehyde equivalents) and (ii) managing the competing high-temperature degradation chemistry that can disproportionately affect hydroxy amino acids. [21]
A further caution for purely vent-centric “CO₂ → amino acids” narratives is that some claimed FeS/H₂S-driven syntheses have not held up; Keefe et al. (1995, PNAS) report that their FeS/H₂S system did not produce amino acids from CO₂ (nor via proposed reductive amination/carboxylation), sharpening the distinction between (a) Strecker-type routes requiring carbonyls + CN⁻ and (b) hypothesized direct CO₂ fixation-to-amino-acid routes. [22]
Mineral catalysts and surfaces implicated in threonine-relevant steps
Cu(I) cyanide (CuCN) as a photoredox-active heterogeneous catalyst/surface.
In the Ritson–Sutherland photoredox system, CuCN is added as a solid; mixing generates a fine dark precipitate that is irradiated, consistent with heterogeneous photoredox cycling and surface-mediated electron production. [8]
Green et al. (2021, JACS) discuss Cu(I)/Cu(II) cyanocuprate photoredox cycling as a route to hydrated electrons and connect this catalysis to the production of amino-acid Strecker precursors, including the threonine aminonitrile precursor derived from lactaldehyde. [10]
Sulfide minerals as sources of H₂S (reductant) and CuCN-generating Cu–S chemistry.
Ritson & Sutherland explicitly note a plausible geochemical source: dissolution of copper sulfide minerals in cyanide solution could generate CuCN catalysts, and dissolution of FeS could provide additional H₂S reductant—an explicit mineralogical bridge between rock chemistry and the photoredox network. [8]
Ferrocyanide as a cyanide concentrator and potential “feedstock conditioner.”
Ritson & Sutherland also emphasize that cyanide can be concentrated by complexation with Fe²⁺ to form ferrocyanide, and that thermal processing of mixed ferrocyanide salts can yield mixtures containing cyanide, ammonia, and cyanamide upon rehydration—reagents directly relevant to Strecker chemistry and linked nucleotide-precursor reactions. [8]
Pyrite–pyrrhotite–magnetite and silicate buffers in hydrothermal simulations.
Andersson & Holm’s redox-buffered hydrothermal framework highlights the use of pyrite (FeS₂), pyrrhotite (FeS), and magnetite (Fe₃O₄) to constrain oxygen fugacity (redox), and K‑feldspar–muscovite–quartz assemblages to influence pH via ion activity control—key when extrapolating laboratory hydrothermal yields to natural settings. [20]
Environmental context and the main open challenges for threonine
Most relevant environments by route.
For spark-discharge threonine formation, the empirical basis is strongest for localized, reducing volcanic-plume or eruption-lightning environments, where mixtures of reduced gases (e.g., CH₄, NH₃, H₂S) and oxidized components (e.g., CO₂) can coexist transiently; Parker et al. explicitly argue such localized plume chemistry could generate HCN and other intermediates that rain out and accumulate in tidal zones, supporting subsequent aqueous synthesis steps. [15]
For UV photoredox threonine-precursor formation, the most relevant setting is a sunlit surface environment that can sustain UV photochemistry (at least episodically) and maintain access to cyanide and sulfide redox pairs; Green et al. note that low H₂S solubility and low crustal copper abundance would restrict Cu/H₂S cyanosulfidic networks to specific terrains (e.g., copper-rich, alkaline pools), a critical geochemical constraint on global plausibility. [23]
For Strecker chemistry in aqueous-alteration contexts (meteorites or analogous wet-rock systems), the carbonaceous-chondrite analysis emphasizes that ammonia abundance and redox state influence whether aminonitriles (→ amino acids) are favored over cyanohydrins (→ hydroxy acids), and it frames threonine/allothreonine formation as dependent on lactaldehyde availability. [13]
Key bottlenecks and debates.
A central unresolved bottleneck is sourcing and sustaining lactaldehyde (and/or its direct nitrile/cyanohydrin precursors) at concentrations compatible with downstream Strecker chemistry. The meteoritic analysis proposes that lactaldehyde may be relatively less available than glycolaldehyde/hydroxyacetone because its formation depends on less abundant ethanol-derived radical chemistry in interstellar ices, which would suppress threonine relative to serine/α‑methylserine—an argument that can be repurposed as a constraint on Earth scenarios if analogous precursor biases held in terrestrial inventories. [13]
Another challenge is selectivity and stereochemistry. Multiple experimental contexts support predominantly racemic production for chiral amino acids in abiotic syntheses (e.g., spark discharge products; racemic allo-threonine in some meteoritic extracts where it can be resolved cleanly), but threonine’s second stereocenter makes diastereomeric mixtures (threonine/allothreonine) a near-default outcome unless additional selection mechanisms are invoked. [24]
A third major issue is compatibility of synthesis with survival. Hydroxy amino acids can undergo dehydration and other transformations during heating or long residence times; this is directly relevant to vent models (where high temperatures can accelerate degradation) and also to post-synthesis processing. [3]
Finally, there is an ongoing debate over whether threonine should be expected to be among the earliest-abundant amino acids at all. The experimental record shows that threonine is certainly possible abiotically (spark discharge; cyanosulfidic networks yielding its Strecker precursors), but it is also plausibly scarcity-limited by precursor availability (lactaldehyde) and stability constraints (hydroxy amino-acid degradation), leaving room for models where threonine is incorporated later into biochemistry relative to simpler amino acids. [25]
