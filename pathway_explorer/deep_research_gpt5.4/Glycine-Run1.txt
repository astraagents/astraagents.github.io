Prebiotic Synthesis of Glycine
Known Prebiotic Synthesis Routes
Several abiotic routes to glycine (NHв‚‚CHв‚‚COOH) have been proposed. The classic Strecker synthesis combines hydrogen cyanide (HCN), ammonia (NHв‚ѓ), and formaldehyde (Hв‚‚CO) to form an aminoacetonitrile intermediate (NHв‚‚CHв‚‚CN) that hydrolyzes to glycine. Early experiments (e.g. MillerвЂ“Urey) showed that an electric discharge on CHв‚„вЂ“NHв‚ѓвЂ“Hв‚‚вЂ“Hв‚‚O mixtures produces glycine (among other amino acids)[1]. A related cyanamide route (addition of HCN and NHв‚ѓ to aldehydes) also yields amino-acetonitrile and glycine under mild aqueous conditions, though yields can be low without catalysts.
An alternative is reductive amination of glyoxylate: glyoxylate (HOOCвЂ“CHO) plus ammonia can form an imine that is reduced to glycine. On Fe(II)-rich mineral surfaces (greigite, green rust, magnetite/brucite), glyoxylate has been shown to convert to glycine[3][4]. Similarly, aminomethanol pathways (via NHв‚‚CHв‚‚OH dehydration to formaldimine) have been proposed in computational studies, identifying formaldimine (CHв‚‚=NH) and вЂњiminoglycineвЂќ (NHв‚‚CHв•ђNH) as key intermediates toward glycine[5].
Recently, alkaline hydrothermal vent routes have been demonstrated. Zhang et al. In this route, ethanolamine is oxidized/deaminated (on metal surfaces) to glycine (possibly via an intermediate like aminomethanol or aminoacetaldehyde). Conditions were hydrothermal (80вЂ“160вЂЇВ°C, 0.1вЂ“8вЂЇMPa, pH 9.7) and glycine appeared rapidly and in high yield with metal powders[6].
On silicate grain surfaces in warm interstellar environments, a surface-mediated neutral route has been proposed. Mates-Torres & Rimola (2026) used simulations on forsterite (Mgв‚‚SiOв‚„) surfaces to show that formaldehyde (Hв‚‚CO), carbon monoxide (CO), and NHв‚ѓ can react to give glycine in a single-barrier, exergonic process, bypassing a high-barrier Strecker step[7]. In contrast, a surface Strecker (HCN + NHв‚ѓ + Hв‚‚CO) is predicted to be kinetically blocked.

Key Intermediates and Precursors
Common precursors include HCN, NHв‚ѓ, and simple carbon oxides. Formaldehyde (Hв‚‚CO) and methanimine (Hв‚‚C=NH) are crucial Cв‚Ѓ building blocks; formaldimine (CHв‚‚=NH) is highlighted as especially reactive toward amino acids[5]. Glyoxylate (HOOCвЂ“CHO) and formamide (HCONHв‚‚) can also feed into amino acid pathways. In Strecker chemistry, aminoacetonitrile (NHв‚‚CHв‚‚CN) is the direct precursor to glycine (via hydrolysis). The aminomethanol (NHв‚‚CHв‚‚OH) intermediate appears in some pathways: its dehydration gives formaldimine, which couples with formic acid (HCOOH) to yield glycine[5]. Other important species are formic acid (HCOOH) (often acting as a catalyst/proton shuttle) and iminoglycine (NH=CHCOOH), an imine tautomer of glycine, which was identified as a glycine precursor in simulations[5].
Overall, precursors of glycine are largely Cв‚ЃвЂ“Cв‚‚ molecules: HCN, NHв‚ѓ, CHв‚‚O, NHв‚‚CHв‚‚OH, HOOCвЂ“CHO, CO, HCOOH and the aminoacetonitrile. These serve as branch points to multiple amino acid pathways (e.g. Strecker intermediates also lead to alanine, serine, etc. [8][5]).
Reaction Mechanisms and Conditions
Strecker route: In solution, HCN and NHв‚ѓ first form an imine (HC=NH), which is then attacked by cyanide to give aminonitrile (NHв‚‚CHв‚‚CN). Acidic or heat-driven hydrolysis of the nitrile yields glycine. This typically requires neutral-to-slightly acidic pH and moderate temperatures (0вЂ“100вЂЇВ°C). In Miller-type spark experiments, glycine was produced in yields ~0.1вЂ“5% (carbon yield) from CHв‚„/Nв‚‚/Hв‚‚O mixtures[2]. (Acid work-up was often used to release amino acids.)
Reductive amination (glyoxylate): On mineral surfaces (e.g. Fe(OH)в‚‚ brucite, green rust, magnetite), glyoxylate condenses with NHв‚ѓ to form an imine (glyoxylamide), which is reduced by Fe(II) to glycine[4]. For example, Chimiak et al. (2024) reacted glyoxylate (100вЂЇmM) with NHв‚ѓ or NOв‚ѓвЃ» on ferroan brucite at 20вЂЇВ°C (1вЂЇatm)[9]. Over weeks, the ammonia system yielded ~52вЂ“69% of reactants converted to glycine[10], while the nitrate system (requiring in situ reduction of NOв‚ѓвЃ» to NHв‚ѓ) gave <3% glycine (initially) but up to 97% of that product was mineral-bound[11][10]. Key conditions were alkaline pH (~8вЂ“9) and Fe(II)-rich surfaces; lower Fe/Mg (brucite) increased yield. Glycine formation here is fastest in the presence of free NHв‚ѓ; with NOв‚ѓвЃ» the reaction lags until NOв‚ѓвЃ» в†’ NHв‚ѓ reduction occurs[4].

Hydrothermal vent (ethanolamine) route: Zhang et al. (2017) simulated alkaline basaltic vents (pHв‰€9.7, 80вЂ“160вЂЇВ°C, 0.1вЂ“8вЂЇMPa) by heating an anaerobic solution of ethanolamine with metal powder catalysts (Fe, Co, Ni, Cu). This route does not require free HCN; the Cв‚‚ skeleton comes from ethanolamine, NHв‚‚ from itself, and C=O likely from metal-catalyzed oxidation.
Surface/UV (astro) mechanisms: In cold ices (10вЂ“100вЂЇK), radical pathways dominate. In warmer astronomical settings (>150вЂЇK), Mates-Torres & Rimola find that on bare silicate surfaces (forsterite), a neutral pathway of Hв‚‚CO + CO + NHв‚ѓ yields glycine in one exothermic step with a low barrier, whereas the Strecker (HCN) route is kinetically hindered[7]. In all cases, glycine binds strongly to minerals or ices, often requiring acid to recover it[11][7].
In summary, each pathway has distinct steps and conditions: electric discharge creates HCN and imines (freshly in the gas phase) that hydrolyze to glycine[1][2]; glyoxylate-based routes use NHв‚ѓ + Fe(II) at moderate T/pH[3][4]; vent routes use high T/pH with catalytic metals[6]; and surface reactions exploit ice or mineral catalysis at low temperature[7][12].
Mineral Catalysts and Surfaces
Numerous minerals can catalyze glycine precursors. IronвЂ“sulfur minerals (e.g. greigite Feв‚ѓSв‚„, pyrite FeSв‚‚, magnetite Feв‚ѓOв‚„) promote reductive chemistry. Barge et al. (2019) demonstrated amino acid synthesis (including glycine) in Fe(II)/Fe(III) hydroxide systems under pH/redox gradients[13]. Chimiak et al. (2024) used ferroan brucite (Mgв‚Ђ.67Feв‚Ђ.33(OH)в‚‚) to couple nitrate reduction with glyoxylate amination[14][4]. Green rust (mixed Fe(II)/Fe(III) hydroxide) also catalyzes glycine from glyoxylate[3]. Iron-sulfides (e.g. Mackinawite, marcasite) and mixed-metal minerals likely serve similarly, though specific glycine yields on sulfides are less reported.
Alkaline earth hydroxides (brucite, hydrotalcites) enhance CвЂ“C coupling. The Mg(OH)в‚‚ component in ferroan brucite accelerates glyoxylate aldol condensation[15], producing DHF. Clay minerals (montmorillonite) are known to adsorb organics and catalyze polymerizations, but direct glycine synthesis on clays is not well documented. Metal powders (Fe, Ni, Co, Cu) clearly catalyzed the ethanolamine в†’ glycine conversion[6]. Even pure silicates (forsterite) can act as a surface: simulations show forsterite surfaces mediating the Hв‚‚CO+CO+NHв‚ѓв†’glycine step[7].
In all these cases, the mineral surface provides adsorption sites, electrons (from FeВІвЃє), and sometimes protons (surface OH groups). For example, ferroan brucite both adsorbs organic intermediates and supplies electrons to reduce imines[4]. High-pH minerals (brucite, green rust) maintain NHв‚ѓ in reactive form. Montmorillonite-type clays might concentrate HCN and NHв‚ѓ, though glycine-specific data are lacking.
Experimental Evidence
Key laboratory studies include:
- Spark discharge (MillerвЂ“Urey) experiments: Stanley Miller (1953) and others showed glycine formation from CHв‚„/NHв‚ѓ/Hв‚‚O under electric discharge[1][2]. Later proton-irradiation of CO/Nв‚‚/Hв‚‚O also gave glycine (0.1вЂ“0.3% yields)[2]. These classic experiments prove glycine can arise from very simple gases.
- Hydrothermal simulations: Zhang et al. (2017) report glycine synthesis from ethanolamine at 80вЂ“160вЂЇВ°C, pH ~9.7, with Fe/Ni/Co catalysts[6]. Chimiak et al. (2024) explicitly produced glycine by mixing glyoxylate with NHв‚ѓ or NOв‚ѓвЃ» and ferroan brucite at ~20вЂЇВ°C (lab seawater-like conditions)[14][4]. They detected glycine by NMR and MS after weeks; in the NHв‚ѓ system ~0.5вЂ“0.7 of the carbon was converted to glycine[10]. In the NOв‚ѓвЃ» system yields were initially <3%, but nearly all glycine was mineral-bound (recovered by acid digestion)[11][10].
- Ice-photochemistry: Ioppolo et al. (2020) and later studies showed amino acids (including glycine) form when simple ices (e.g. Hв‚‚O:COв‚‚:NHв‚ѓ or CHв‚ѓNHв‚‚:CO:NHв‚ѓ) are UV-irradiated at 10вЂ“100вЂЇK[12]. These often require warming and acid hydrolysis to liberate amino acids.
- Space-analogue surfaces: Mates-Torres et al. (2026) used quantum simulations to demonstrate a low-barrier glycine formation pathway on Mgв‚‚SiOв‚„ surfaces[7]. While not an experiment, this supports the plausibility of surface catalysis.
- Meteorite and comet studies: Glycine is routinely found in carbonaceous meteorites (e.g. Murchison) and in samples from comets WildвЂЇ2 and 67P (Cassini data)[16]. These detections (though racemic) imply abiotic synthesis outside Earth.
When reported, yields vary widely. Miller-type discharges produced glycine in traceвЂ“percent yields[2]. Zhang et al. did not quantify, but called the vent-route вЂњefficientвЂќ[6]. Chimiak et al. measured up to ~60% yield (of limiting reactant) in brucite systems[10]. However, they note that up to ~97% of glycine stayed bound to the mineral[11], suggesting standard assays may underestimate yields. Overall, experiments confirm glycine formation from HCN or ammonia sources under many simulated prebiotic conditions.
Environmental Context
Different settings favor different routes. Warm hydrothermal systems (alkaline vents, hot springs) provide high temperature, pressure, and pH for metal-catalyzed chemistry. ZhangвЂ™s ethanolamine route mimicked an Archean serpentinizing vent (pHв€ј10, 80вЂ“160вЂЇВ°C)[6], as did Novikov & CopleyвЂ™s pyruvate experiments at ~250вЂЇВ°C (though that yielded other amino acids)[17]. ChimiakвЂ™s brucite experiments used ambient conditions (20вЂЇВ°C, pH 8вЂ“9) relevant to subsurface aquifers or cold vents[9]. These vent systems can also supply Hв‚‚ and reduced minerals (Fe(II), sulfides) for reductive pathways[4][6].
Shallow pools and wetвЂ“dry cycles (freshwater ponds, tidal flats) are conducive to Strecker chemistry: concentrating HCN and NHв‚ѓ, and providing UV/heat for imine formation. Acidic or evaporitic pools allow HCN polymerization to nitriles. Zn or clay minerals in such ponds could catalyze aminonitrile hydrolysis. UV-irradiated ices or atmosphere (high-latitude or stratospheric ices) enable radical glycine formation at low temperatures[12].
Astrochemical contexts also matter: glycine precursors (CO, Hв‚‚CO, NHв‚ѓ, HCN) exist in interstellar clouds and planetary ices. Simulations suggest that as young stellar objects warm to ~150вЂ“200вЂЇK, ices desorb and expose silicate dust where surface catalysis (Hв‚‚CO+CO+NHв‚ѓв†’glycine) can occur[7]. Nitrate-based pathways (Chimiak 2024) imply oxygenated atmospheres (generating NOв‚ѓвЃ») still allow amino acid synthesis in subsurface serpentinizing rock[18].
In summary, alkaline hydrothermal vents (high T, pH, Hв‚‚) favor reductive routes (glyoxylate, ethanolamine)[6][4]. Surface ponds or atmospheric discharges favor Strecker from simple gases[1][2]. Ice-rich space environments can form glycine via radical/neutral pathways on grains or ices[7][12].
Open Questions and Challenges
Despite these pathways, challenges remain. A major issue is nitrogen source: most routes need NHв‚ѓ (or NHв‚‚). Chimiak et al. emphasize that oxidizing early atmospheres (lacking NHв‚ѓ) could still yield glycine via nitrate reduction on minerals[14], but experimental yields were lower. Thus the availability of reactive NHв‚ѓ/NHв‚„вЃє vs NOв‚“ in different settings is debated.
Concentration and stability: Glycine is highly water-soluble and chemically reactive (can cyclize or polymerize). Many experiments find glycine only after harsh hydrolysis or acid extraction[11]. ItвЂ™s unclear whether free glycine would accumulate in prebiotic environments or be sequestered on mineral surfaces. Indeed, Chimiak et al. found ~97% of glycine adsorbed to brucite in their Nв‚‚ system[11], suggesting that standard analyses may miss mineral-bound products. The strength of glycine binding (especially via its zwitterion) raises questions about its mobility and detectability in prebiotic niches.
Competition and yields: Real prebiotic systems contained complex mixtures. For example, HCN tends to polymerize into tar rather than cleanly producing glycine; formaldehyde can undergo formose chemistry instead of coupling to NHв‚ѓ. The Xu et al. (2024) analysis highlights that pathways involving formaldimine have lower barriers than analogous HCN routes[5], implying that some classic assumptions (HCN-centric) may overestimate glycine formation. Rates and yields under realistic conditions are still poorly constrained. For instance, nitrate-derived glycine took weeks to form in ChimiakвЂ™s study[10], which may not be compatible with short-lived hydrothermal flows.
Thermodynamics and kinetics: Some proposed pathways (e.g. HCOOH-catalyzed coupling with formaldimine[5]) have moderate barriers (~40вЂ“50 kcal/mol) and require specific catalysts. The balance between reduction (by FeВІвЃє) and oxidation steps, and the pH dependence of NHв‚„вЃє/NHв‚ѓ, are not fully mapped. High temperatures that drive some reactions (e.g. ZhangвЂ™s ethanolamine route) could also degrade glycine or favor competing chemistry (like Strecker to alanine).
In sum, while many routes to glycine exist in theory and experiment, the dominant prebiotic pathway is unresolved. Key bottlenecks include sourcing free ammonia without a strongly reducing atmosphere, achieving sufficient yields in dynamic environments, and retaining glycine (versus it being sequestered or destroyed). Future work must clarify the interplay of routes (e.g. how Strecker, glyoxylate, and radical mechanisms might coexist) and identify which environmental scenarios best accumulate bioavailable glycine.
