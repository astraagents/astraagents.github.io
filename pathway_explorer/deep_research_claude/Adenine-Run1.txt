# Prebiotic synthesis of adenine: routes, evidence, and open frontiers

**Adenine is among the most thermodynamically favored products of HCN self-reaction chemistry and arguably the most robust prebiotic nucleobase synthesis in origin-of-life research.** Formally a pentamer of hydrogen cyanide (5 HCN → C₅H₅N₅, ΔG₂₉₈ = **−53.7 kcal/mol**), adenine emerges from at least five distinct and experimentally validated pathways — HCN oligomerization, formamide condensation, cyanosulfidic photochemistry, eutectic freezing, and impact-driven radical chemistry — each operating under different environmental conditions but converging on shared intermediates, most critically AICN (4-aminoimidazole-5-carbonitrile). Despite this thermodynamic favorability, the practical challenges of concentration, selectivity, and the pervasive "asphalt problem" of tar formation mean that prebiotic adenine yields rarely exceed 0.5% under plausible conditions, with one notable exception: DAMN heated with ammonium formate at 110°C yields ~18% adenine (Hill & Orgel, 2002). This report synthesizes the complete landscape of prebiotic adenine chemistry — reaction pathways, intermediates, environmental contexts, mineral catalysts, quantitative data, and unresolved questions — for use in guiding synthesis network design and pathway evaluation.

## 1. The classical HCN pentamerization discovered by Oró

Joan Oró's 1960 discovery that adenine forms spontaneously from concentrated ammonium cyanide solutions remains the foundational result in prebiotic purine chemistry. Heating **>1.0 M NH₄CN** at **70°C** for several days yielded approximately **0.5% adenine**, with the remainder being dark, intractable HCN polymer (Oró, *Biochem. Biophys. Res. Commun.* 2, 407–412, 1960; *Nature* 191, 1193–1194, 1961). Subsequent systematic studies with Kimball explored temperature, concentration, and pH dependence (*Arch. Biochem. Biophys.* 94, 217–227, 1961; 96, 293–313, 1962).

The mechanism proceeds through five sequential additions. **Step I (dimerization):** two HCN molecules combine via nucleophilic attack of CN⁻ on HCN to form iminoacetonitrile, with a base-catalyzed barrier of ~21.8 kcal/mol (Sandström & Rahm, *J. Phys. Chem. A*, 2023) or ~11.4 kcal/mol with ammonia catalysis (Kikuchi et al., 2000). **Step II (trimerization):** addition of a third HCN gives aminomalononitrile (AMN). **Step III (tetramerization):** a fourth HCN adds to yield diaminomaleonitrile (DAMN), the first stable, isolable intermediate (~15% yield based on input HCN; Zubay & Mui, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 31, 87–102, 2001). **Step IV:** DAMN undergoes photoisomerization under UV (λ ~298–350 nm) through a three-photon process — cis-DAMN → trans-DAMN (DAFN) → ketenimine → AICN — with an overall quantum yield of **0.0034** for the trans-DAMN → AICN conversion. This photochemical step was discovered by Ferris & Orgel (*J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 88, 1074, 1966) and later confirmed to achieve ~50% AICN yield in approximately **10 seconds** under young-Sun UV flux (Michaelian & Simeonov, *Entropy*, 2022). **Step V:** AICN reacts with a fifth equivalent of HCN (or formamidine) to close the pyrimidine ring and form adenine. This final step is the **kinetic bottleneck**, with an uncatalyzed barrier of **60.4 kcal/mol** that drops to ~27.6 kcal/mol with NH₃ catalysis in bulk water (Roy, Najafian & Schleyer, *PNAS* 104, 17272–17277, 2007).

The highest adenine yield ever reported from HCN chemistry is **~20%**, achieved by Wakamatsu and colleagues (1966) in sealed-tube reactions of HCN with liquid ammonia. The comprehensive kinetics were mapped by Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel (*J. Mol. Biol.* 30, 223–253, 1967), who established that HCN hydrolysis is first-order while polymerization is second-order in total cyanide — making **0.01–0.1 M HCN** the critical concentration threshold where the two processes compete equally at pH 8–9 and 0–60°C. A landmark 2025/2026 computational study by Sandström & Rahm (*J. Am. Chem. Soc.*) used DLPNO-CCSD(T)/aug-cc-pVTZ level quantum chemistry with microkinetic modeling to compare four interwoven base-catalyzed pathways in liquid HCN, confirming DAMN as a necessary intermediate and revealing previously unknown roles for polyimine as an oxidizing agent.

## 2. AICN anchors a branching network to all purines

AICN (4-amino-1H-imidazole-5-carbonitrile) is the **central hub** of prebiotic purine synthesis — a molecule 19.3 kcal/mol more stable than DAMN that branches to every major purine product depending on which one-carbon or one-nitrogen donor reacts with it. The branching pathways, established primarily by Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel (*J. Mol. Biol.* 38, 121–128, 1968), are:

- **AICN + HCN or formamidine → adenine** (the canonical pathway; formamidine provides the C-2 of the pyrimidine ring)
- **AICN + H₂O → AICA** (hydrolysis of the nitrile to carboxamide), then **AICA + formate → hypoxanthine** (Zubay & Mui, 2001; Barks et al., *ChemBioChem* 11, 1240–1243, 2010)
- **AICN + cyanogen (NCCN) → guanine** (Sanchez et al., 1968)
- **AICN + guanidine → 2,6-diaminopurine** (reported at 88.3% yield in synthetic chemistry)
- **AICN + urea → xanthine** (Šponer et al., 2016)

Additional key intermediates in the network include the **HCN dimer** (iminoacetonitrile — never isolated from aqueous HCN but computationally validated), **AMN** (aminomalononitrile, the HCN trimer — highly reactive), **DAMN** (the photochemically pivotal tetramer), **AICA** (4-aminoimidazole-5-carboxamide, which parallels the biological de novo purine biosynthesis intermediate), and **formamidine** (detected in HCN oligomerization mixtures, serving as the one-carbon donor for ring closure). An alternative pathway via **8-cyanoadenine**, proposed by Voet & Schwartz (*Bioorg. Chem.* 12, 8–17, 1983), bypasses the photochemical DAMN → AICN step entirely and has been computationally validated as viable (Sandström & Rahm, 2025).

Importantly, **ammonium formate** can substitute for formamidine in the final ring-closure step: Hill & Orgel (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 32, 99–102, 2002) demonstrated that heating DAMN with ammonium formate at **110°C** yields a remarkable **~18% adenine** — the highest yield from a prebiotically plausible one-pot reaction. This route bypasses the photochemical step entirely, as ammonium formate converts to formamidine in situ.

In all HCN polymerization experiments, adenine is produced at consistently **higher yields than guanine** (10–40× higher; Levy, Miller & Oró, *J. Mol. Evol.* 49, 165–168, 1999), with trace 2,6-diaminopurine also detected (Borquez et al., *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 35, 79–90, 2005).

## 3. Formamide condensation offers a one-pot alternative

Formamide (HCONH₂), the simplest amide containing C, H, O, and N, represents a conceptually elegant alternative feedstock because it connects directly to HCN chemistry through the equilibrium **HCONH₂ ⇌ HCN + H₂O** while offering practical advantages: high boiling point (210°C), low volatility, and demonstrated accumulation via thermophoresis in hydrothermal pores to concentrations sufficient for nucleobase synthesis (Niether et al., *PNAS*, 2016). The formamide program, pioneered by Saladino, Crestini, Costanzo, and Di Mauro beginning in 2001, has produced a remarkably comprehensive body of evidence.

Heating neat formamide at **100–160°C** with various mineral catalysts produces all canonical nucleobases. Key results include: **CaCO₃, silica, alumina, kaolin, and zeolites** yield purine, adenine, and cytosine at up to ~0.9% adenine (Saladino et al., *Bioorg. Med. Chem.* 9, 1249–1253, 2001); **TiO₂ (anatase)** uniquely produces thymine and acyclonucleosides alongside adenine (*ChemBioChem* 4, 514–521, 2003); **montmorillonite** yields purine, adenine, cytosine, uracil, and hypoxanthine (*ChemBioChem* 5, 1558–1566, 2004); and **iron sulfur minerals** (pyrite, pyrrhotite) produce purine and isocytosine at 160°C (*J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 130, 15512, 2008). Borate minerals also catalyze adenine formation from formamide (Saladino et al., *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 41, 317, 2011).

A landmark 2015 study demonstrated that proton irradiation (170 MeV, simulating solar wind/cosmic rays) of liquid formamide at **243 K (−30°C)** in the presence of powdered meteorites produces not just all canonical nucleobases but also sugars (ribose, deoxyribose), amino acids, and remarkably **four nucleosides** (cytidine, uridine, adenosine, thymidine) — the first one-pot synthesis of nucleosides from a single feedstock (Saladino et al., *PNAS* 112, E2746–E2755, 2015). Carbonaceous chondrites were the most effective catalysts. UV irradiation of formamide at 130°C produces adenine, hypoxanthine, and guanine even without mineral catalysts (Barks et al., *ChemBioChem* 11, 1240–1243, 2010), and high-energy laser plasma simulating asteroid impacts on formamide yields all four canonical nucleobases through CN• and NH• radical intermediates, with AICN detected as a key intermediate (Ferus et al., *PNAS* 112, 657–662, 2015).

The mechanism involves thermal decomposition generating HCN, NH₃, and HNCO as reactive intermediates, which then follow pathways analogous to classical HCN oligomerization. Isotope-labeling studies using ¹³C-¹⁵N coupling confirmed that adenine derived from formamide contains C–N units traceable to both HCN-like and formamide-like pathways (Yamada et al., *J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 100, 4617, 1978). Computational studies by Slavova et al. (*Int. J. Quantum Chem.*, 2020) and Šponer et al. (2020) demonstrated complete self-catalytic reaction pathways from pure formamide to all purine bases, with formamide and formimidic acid serving as catalysts.

## 4. Cyanosulfidic chemistry unifies nucleotide, amino acid, and lipid precursors

The cyanosulfidic protometabolism framework, established by Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy & Sutherland (*Nature Chemistry* 7, 301–307, 2015), represents a paradigm shift toward systems chemistry by demonstrating that precursors of ribonucleotides, amino acids, and lipids can all derive from HCN and its derivatives under a single set of conditions: **HCN, H₂S (reductant), UV light, and Cu(I)–Cu(II) photoredox cycling** at ~35°C, anoxic conditions, and neutral pH with phosphate buffer. The geochemical scenario envisions streams flowing downhill, leaching feedstocks from thermally metamorphosed evaporites, with solar UV driving the chemistry.

For purine synthesis specifically, three breakthrough results stand out. First, Xu et al. (*Nature* 582, 60–66, 2020) achieved **selective prebiotic formation of DNA purine nucleosides** (deoxyadenosine and deoxyinosine) through 8-mercaptoadenine — derived from triaminopyrimidine + ammonium thiocyanate — which undergoes photoreduction with H₂S under UV to yield adenine. MgCl₂ as Lewis acid catalyst achieved combined thioadenosine isomer yields of **87%**. Second, Xu et al. (*J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 143, 14482–14486, 2021) extended this to coproduce adenosine, inosine, deoxyadenosine, and deoxyinosine with strict stereo- and furanosyl-selectivity. Third, Stairs, Nikmal, Bučar, Zheng, Szostak & Powner (*Nature Communications* 8, 15270, 2017) demonstrated divergent synthesis of pyrimidine and 8-oxo-purine ribonucleotides from a common precursor (2-thiooxazole from glycolaldehyde + thiocyanic acid), achieving **β-ribo-stereochemistry** matching biological nucleic acids.

The cyanosulfidic approach differs fundamentally from classical HCN oligomerization in that it produces **nucleotides and nucleosides directly** rather than free bases, operates at moderate temperatures and neutral pH, and addresses the glycosidic bond problem that plagues other pathways. Notably, 4,5-dicyanoimidazole — a byproduct of adenine synthesis — serves as an auxiliary that captures cyanoacetylene, solving a concentration problem for this key molecule (Sutherland group, *JACS* 2022).

## 5. Environmental conditions dictate which pathways operate

### Eutectic freezing concentrates HCN by six orders of magnitude

The concentration problem is the central challenge: steady-state oceanic HCN concentrations were likely **7 × 10⁻¹³ M at 100°C** and **2 × 10⁻⁶ M at 0°C** (pH 8) — far below the 0.01–0.1 M threshold for productive polymerization (Miyakawa, Cleaves & Miller, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 32, 195–208, 2002). Eutectic freezing, first proposed by Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel (*Science* 153, 72–73, 1966), provides an elegant solution: as dilute HCN solution freezes, pure ice crystallizes out, concentrating HCN until the **eutectic point at −23.4°C** where the liquid phase contains **74.5 mol% HCN**. This mechanism also suppresses hydrolysis (adenine t₁/₂ ≥ 10⁶ years at 0°C vs. ~1 year at 100°C; Levy & Miller, *PNAS* 95, 7933–7938, 1998).

The most striking experimental validation comes from the 25–27 year freezing experiments: Levy, Miller, Brinton & Bada (*Icarus* 145, 609–613, 2000) recovered **0.035–0.04% adenine** from dilute NH₄CN frozen at −20°C and −78°C, and Miyakawa et al. (2002) detected adenine and guanine even from initial concentrations as low as **0.001 M NH₄CN** after 27 years at −78°C. Orgel (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 34, 361–369, 2004) concluded that eutectic freezing is "the most plausible prebiotic method for concentrating HCN." This scenario is geologically plausible: the early Sun was ~30% less luminous, so global glaciation was possible without significant greenhouse warming.

### Surface pools drive wet-dry cycling and photochemistry

Warm evaporitic pools at **20–80°C** provide UV-driven photochemistry (the early Earth lacked an ozone layer, so UV-C flux was significantly higher) and concentration through evaporation. Wet-dry cycles are critical for the Carell group's formamidopyrimidine (FaPy) pathway, which achieves **~60% nucleoside yield** through dry-state condensation of FaPys with ribose (Becker et al., *Science* 352, 833–836, 2016; *Science* 366, 76–82, 2019). Pearce et al. (*PNAS* 114, 11327, 2017) modeled nucleobase evolution in warm little ponds, concluding that RNA polymers must emerge within a few years of meteorite deposition. The hot spring hypothesis of Damer & Deamer (*Astrobiology* 20, 429–452, 2020) envisions fluctuating volcanic pools cycling through hydrated, gel, and dehydrated phases at temperatures of **50–80°C** with **pH 2–5** (from dissolved volcanic CO₂ and SO₂).

### Hydrothermal vents face a stability paradox

Alkaline vents (Lost City type, **40–90°C, pH 9–11**) are thermodynamically favorable for nucleobase synthesis at lower temperatures (LaRowe & Regnier, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 38, 383–397, 2008), but adenine stability drops precipitously with temperature: t₁/₂ ≈ **1 year at 100°C** and **minutes at 250°C** (Levy & Miller, 1998). Interestingly, Franiatte et al. (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 38, 139–148, 2008) found that adenine reaches an apparent equilibrium at ~4 × 10⁻⁶ mol/L at 300°C under controlled N₂/CO₂/H₂ fugacities after ~200 hours, suggesting thermodynamic buffering by hydrothermal fluid composition. Black smoker conditions (150–400°C, pH 2–3) are almost certainly too harsh for nucleobase accumulation.

## 6. Mineral catalysts lower barriers and direct selectivity

**Iron-sulfur minerals** (pyrite, greigite, mackinawite) are central to Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis, where the reaction FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂ (ΔG = **−9.2 kcal/mol**) provides thermodynamic driving force for coupled organic synthesis (Wächtershäuser, 1988–1992). Experimental validation includes peptide synthesis from amino acids on (Fe,Ni)S surfaces (Huber & Wächtershäuser, *Science* 281, 670, 1998) and pyruvate from carbonylated iron-sulfur compounds (Cody et al., *Science* 289, 1337, 2000). For nucleobase synthesis specifically, pyrite and pyrrhotite catalyze formamide condensation to purines at 160°C (Saladino et al., *JACS* 130, 15512, 2008).

**Clay minerals**, especially **montmorillonite**, play a dual role: catalyzing nucleobase synthesis from formamide (Saladino et al., 2004) and, critically, catalyzing RNA oligomerization from activated mononucleotides. Ferris and colleagues demonstrated that montmorillonite promotes RNA oligomers of **6–14 mers** (*J. Am. Chem. Soc.* 115, 12270, 1993), extendable to **50 mers** with daily monomer feeding (*Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B* 361, 1777, 2006), with **100–1000× rate enhancement** over solution-phase hydrolysis. Na⁺-montmorillonite shows sequence selectivity (~20-fold preference for 5′-purine-pyrimidine over pyrimidine-purine dimers) and homochiral selectivity.

**TiO₂ photocatalysis** operates through a distinct mechanism: UV excitation of the semiconductor (band gap **3.2 eV**, ~388 nm) generates electron-hole pairs that drive radical chemistry on formamide adsorbed to the surface, without directly decomposing the products (Senanayake & Idriss, *PNAS* 103, 1194, 2006). This means **low-energy photons** (below the direct photodissociation threshold of organic molecules) can drive C–C and C–N bond formation. TiO₂ uniquely enables thymine synthesis from formamide — a product not formed with other catalysts (Saladino et al., 2003).

**Borate minerals** are essential for stabilizing ribose (the RNA sugar) through cis-diol complexation (Ricardo et al., *Science* 303, 196, 2004) and also catalyze nucleobase formation from formamide (Saladino et al., 2011). Their availability on the early Earth depends on continental crust differentiation — the oldest tourmaline dates to ~3.65 Ga (Grew et al., 2011).

### Kinetic and thermodynamic parameters

The overall pentamerization 5 HCN → adenine is strongly exothermic: **ΔE = −93.8 kcal/mol; ΔG₂₉₈ = −53.7 kcal/mol** (Roy et al., *PNAS*, 2007, B3LYP/6-311+G**). Adenine appears to be at or near the **global minimum on the Gibbs energy surface** of HCN self-reaction chemistry (*JACS*, 2025/2026). Low experimental yields therefore reflect kinetic, not thermodynamic, barriers.

Key rate parameters: HCN oligomerization rate constant at pH 9.2, 40°C is **0.096 L mol⁻¹ day⁻¹** (Sanchez et al., 1967); the AICN + HCN → adenine step has a rate **<10⁻²² L/(mol·s)** — a severe kinetic bottleneck (Armas-Vázquez et al., *Astrobiology* 23, 705, 2023). NH₃ catalysis reduces the activation barrier from 60.4 to ~27.6 kcal/mol; the 2025/2026 DLPNO-CCSD(T) calculations place the bottleneck barrier at ~29.5 kcal/mol in the AMN pathway mediated by HCN, and note that GGA DFT methods underestimate barriers by ~10 kcal/mol. The optimal pH for HCN chemistry is **8–9** (pKa of HCN = 9.2 at 25°C), where both the electrophilic HCN and nucleophilic CN⁻ coexist.

## 8. Recent advances are reshaping the field (2020–2026)

Several developments have substantially altered the landscape of prebiotic adenine chemistry. The Carell group's unified nucleoside synthesis via formamidopyrimidines achieves **N-9 regioselective purine nucleoside formation at ~60% yield** through wet-dry cycling — solving the long-standing glycosidic bond problem for purines (Becker et al., *Science* 2016, 2019). The Sutherland group's demonstration that RNA pyrimidine and DNA purine nucleosides form selectively under cyanosulfidic conditions (Xu et al., *Nature* 2020) challenges the pure RNA-world model by suggesting **DNA and RNA building blocks may have co-originated**.

Computationally, automated reaction network exploration has revealed new pathways with lower activation energies than classically proposed routes (Zhao, Garimella & Savoie, *JACS* 145, 6135, 2023), and the comprehensive microkinetic modeling of Sandström & Rahm (*JACS*, 2025/2026) represents the first rigorous comparison of competing base-catalyzed pathways in liquid HCN. Network/graph theory approaches (Wołos et al., *Science* 369, eaaw1955, 2020) have mapped the connectivity of the entire prebiotic chemical space, identifying self-regenerating cycles.

Experimentally, the discovery of **mineral-facilitated aqueous synthesis of HCN from prebiotically abundant amino acids** (*PNAS* 2025) provides an alternative HCN supply route independent of a reducing atmosphere — a significant development given ongoing debate about early Earth atmospheric composition. Callahan & Cleaves (*ACS Earth Space Chem.*, 2023) partly resolved the "Miller Paradox" by showing nucleobases can form from NH₄CN even in the presence of formaldehyde, countering the long-held view that HCN/formaldehyde competition (glycolonitrile formation) prevents nucleobase synthesis. Detection of all five canonical nucleobases in asteroid Ryugu samples by Oba et al. (*Nature Astronomy*, 2025) following the Hayabusa2 mission confirms extraterrestrial nucleobase synthesis is a robust process, with purine-to-pyrimidine ratios varying across meteorite types and correlating negatively with ammonia content.

## 9. Seven unresolved challenges define the frontier

**The concentration problem** remains paramount. Early oceanic HCN at ~10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁶ M (Stribling & Miller, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 1987) falls 4–8 orders of magnitude below the ~0.01 M polymerization threshold. Eutectic freezing, evaporitic concentration, ferrocyanide precipitation and release, and thermophoretic accumulation in hydrothermal pores are proposed solutions, but none has been demonstrated in a geologically integrated scenario.

**The asphalt problem** — formation of intractable tars as the dominant product — means adenine is always a minor component. Typical yields of 0.01–0.5% leave >99% of carbon locked in uncharacterized polymers. Rimmer et al. (*Acc. Chem. Res.*, 2024) formalized this as the "arithmetic demon": multi-step syntheses with <100% yield per step suffer exponential product decline. Systems chemistry approaches suggesting overlapping redundant pathways may compensate for individual low yields (Cleaves, 2023) offer a conceptual but not yet quantitative response.

**Selectivity** presents a related challenge. AICN branches to adenine, hypoxanthine, guanine, xanthine, and other purines depending on conditions, and no known prebiotic mechanism produces adenine as the sole or even dominant product. Schwartz & Voet (1984) found that 8-hydroxymethyladenine was the major product — an order of magnitude more abundant than adenine — when HCN polymerized with formaldehyde.

**The pH paradox** arises because HCN oligomerization requires basic conditions (pH 8–10) while some cyclization steps and the Carell FaPy pathway require acidic conditions. Wet-dry cycling with spatially or temporally separated pH regimes is the leading proposed resolution. **The water problem** — water is essential as solvent but drives hydrolysis of intermediates and thermodynamically disfavors condensation — creates tension with all aqueous pathways. Neat formamide has been proposed as an alternative medium, but its geochemical plausibility is disputed (Bada & Cleaves, *PNAS*, 2015).

**The glycosidic bond problem** — directly condensing adenine with ribose yields only ~4% adenosine with poor regio- and stereoselectivity — has seen the most progress, with Carell's FaPy pathway and Sutherland's 8-mercaptoadenine route both achieving high-yielding, selective nucleoside formation. Finally, the **environmental scenario debate** between surface pools (wet-dry cycling, UV), eutectic freezing, hydrothermal vents, and impact events remains unresolved, with each scenario satisfying some requirements while failing others. A multi-environment model — atmospheric HCN synthesis → delivery to diverse surface settings → concentration and reaction → protection and accumulation — is increasingly favored but lacks experimental integration across scales.

## Conclusion

Prebiotic adenine synthesis is thermodynamically among the most favorable self-reactions of HCN, yet kinetically constrained by barriers exceeding 25 kcal/mol for the rate-limiting AICN-to-adenine step. The field has matured from Oró's single-pathway model into a richly interconnected network where HCN oligomerization, formamide condensation, and cyanosulfidic photochemistry converge on shared intermediates — most critically AICN, which serves as the universal branch point to all purines. The most productive recent advances are not in adenine base synthesis per se (which has been well-established since the 1960s) but in connecting base formation to nucleoside and nucleotide assembly: Carell's FaPy pathway and Sutherland's 8-mercaptoadenine route have effectively solved the glycosidic bond selectivity problem that long represented the weakest link.

For synthesis network design in an agentic system, the key architectural insight is that **adenine formation is best modeled not as a linear pathway but as a network with multiple entry points** (HCN, formamide, cyanoacetaldehyde), a **central hub** (AICN), and **environment-dependent branching** (UV for DAMN → AICN photochemistry; thermal for ammonium formate routes; cyanosulfidic for nucleoside-coupled synthesis). The Hill & Orgel ammonium formate route (18% yield at 110°C) and the cyanosulfidic 8-mercaptoadenine pathway (87% thioadenosine yield) represent the highest-yielding experimentally validated nodes. The concentration problem and asphalt problem remain the critical unsolved constraints for any geologically realistic scenario, and computational network exploration (Savoie, Wołos, Sandström & Rahm) is emerging as the most powerful tool for identifying kinetically viable pathways through this complex chemical landscape.
