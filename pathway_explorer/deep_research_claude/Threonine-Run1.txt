# Prebiotic synthesis of threonine: routes, evidence, and unsolved puzzles

**Threonine emerges as one of the most prebiotically accessible yet paradoxically fragile proteinogenic amino acids.** The cyanosulfidic chemistry developed by Sutherland and colleagues demonstrates that threonine's Strecker precursor forms as an "inevitable by-product" of the same HCN photoredox chemistry that generates ribonucleotide building blocks — linking amino acid and RNA origins in a single reaction network (Patel et al., *Nature Chemistry*, 2015). Yet threonine's β-hydroxyl group renders it among the least stable amino acids under prebiotic conditions, unable to persist beyond **1 billion years even at 0°C** in aqueous solution (Truong et al., *Icarus*, 2019). This tension between facile synthesis and rapid degradation defines threonine's unique position in origin-of-life chemistry and frames the central challenge: not how to make it, but how to use it before it decomposes.

---

## Five demonstrated routes from simple molecules to threonine

Multiple independent pathways connect simple prebiotic feedstocks (HCN, NH₃, H₂O, H₂S, CH₄, UV light) to threonine. Each proceeds through distinct intermediates and operates under different environmental conditions, suggesting that threonine could have formed in diverse early Earth settings.

### Route 1: Strecker synthesis via lactaldehyde (cyanosulfidic pathway)

The most completely characterized route proceeds through **lactaldehyde** (2-hydroxypropanal) as the key aldehyde precursor. Ritson and Sutherland (*Angewandte Chemie International Edition*, 2013) demonstrated each step experimentally: UV irradiation of Cu(I) cyanide complexes with H₂S as reductant converts HCN to formaldehyde, glycolaldehyde, and acetaldehyde through photoredox cycling. Acetaldehyde reacts with HCN to form acetaldehyde cyanohydrin, which undergoes photoreduction to lactaldehyde at **19% yield** after 2 hours of irradiation. Lactaldehyde then undergoes classic Strecker chemistry — reaction with NH₃ to form an imine, followed by HCN addition to yield 2-amino-3-hydroxybutyronitrile (the threonine aminonitrile), which hydrolyzes to threonine and allothreonine as a racemic mixture of diastereomers. The complete pathway requires only HCN, H₂O, H₂S, Cu minerals, and UV light — all prebiotically plausible.

Patel et al. (*Nature Chemistry*, 2015) placed this route within the broader cyanosulfidic protometabolism, showing that the threonine aminonitrile is one of four amino acid precursors (alongside those for glycine, alanine, and serine) that arise as "inevitable by-products" of the Kiliani–Fischer reductive homologation of HCN that also produces ribonucleotide precursors. A parallel route through glyceraldehyde (rather than lactaldehyde) provides a second entry point: glyceraldehyde + NH₃ + HCN directly yields the threonine aminonitrile.

### Route 2: Miller-Urey spark discharge synthesis

Electric discharge experiments have confirmed threonine production from simple gas mixtures. **Ring, Wolman, Friedmann, and Miller** (*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 1972) provided the first confirmed detection, using spark discharge on CH₄/N₂/H₂O with traces of NH₃. Both threonine and allothreonine were identified by ion-exchange chromatography and GC-MS, alongside over 20 other amino acids. The mechanism proceeds through in situ generation of HCN, NH₃, and aldehydes (including lactaldehyde) followed by aqueous Strecker synthesis.

Reanalysis of Stanley Miller's archived experimental samples revealed additional threonine detections. Parker, Cleaves, Dworkin, Glavin, and Bada (*PNAS*, 2011) analyzed Miller's unreported 1958 H₂S-containing experiment (CH₄/NH₃/CO₂/H₂S gas mixture) and detected **23 amino acids including threonine** — which appeared in the H₂S experiment but not in the three other discharge configurations (original, volcanic, silent). The presence of H₂S apparently promoted formation of hydroxylated and sulfur-containing amino acids. Johnson et al. (*Science*, 2008) found that Miller's volcanic apparatus (incorporating hot water mist) preferentially synthesized hydroxylated compounds, with **22 amino acids** total. All products were racemic, confirming abiotic origin.

### Route 3: Aldol condensation of glycine and acetaldehyde

The biological retro-aldol cleavage of threonine (catalyzed by threonine aldolases) yields glycine + acetaldehyde. The reverse — aldol condensation of glycine with acetaldehyde — represents a conceptually simple prebiotic route, since both glycine and acetaldehyde rank among the most abundant prebiotic molecules. However, this condensation is **thermodynamically unfavorable** and requires either high glycine concentrations or catalytic assistance to proceed (Fesko, *Applied Microbiology and Biotechnology*, 2016). Both threonine and allothreonine diastereomers form. No purely prebiotic demonstration of this specific aldol condensation has been reported, though the reaction defines the structural relationship between threonine and its simplest precursors.

Koga and Naraoka (*ACS Earth and Space Chemistry*, 2022) demonstrated a related pathway: ¹³C-labeled aldehydes (formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, glycolaldehyde) reacting with ammonia in aqueous solution produced families of **hydroxy amino acids including C₃ and C₄ structural isomers** that match the distribution found in CM and CR carbonaceous chondrites. This ammonia-aldehyde chemistry provides a route to threonine-related compounds without requiring HCN.

### Route 4: Reductive amination of α-keto acids

The keto acid precursor to threonine is **2-oxo-3-hydroxybutyrate** (α-keto-β-hydroxybutyrate). Muchowska, Varma, and Moran (*Nature*, 2019) demonstrated that Fe²⁺ promotes reductive amination of α-keto acids at 70°C, producing glycine, alanine, aspartate, and glutamate from their corresponding keto acids. Kaur et al. (*Chem*, 2024) extended this to H₂-driven reductive amination over nickel catalysts at **22°C and pH 8**. While threonine's specific keto acid precursor was not tested in either study, the principle is established. Huber and Wächtershäuser (*Science*, 2006) demonstrated CO-dependent formation of α-hydroxy and α-amino acids (including the serine analog, bearing a HOCH₂ substituent) at 80–120°C on Ni-Fe precipitates, suggesting that the threonine homolog (bearing a CH₃CHOH substituent) could form via the same chain-elongation mechanism.

### Route 5: Bucherer-Bergs hydantoin pathway

The Bucherer-Bergs reaction (aldehyde + HCN + NH₃ + CO₂ → hydantoin → amino acid) provides a thermodynamic advantage over direct Strecker synthesis because the hydantoin intermediate acts as a thermodynamic sink. Pulletikurti, Yadav, Springsteen, and Krishnamurthy (*Nature Chemistry*, 2022) demonstrated this pathway for several α-keto acids → amino acids, showing that pyruvate converts to alanine via 5-methylhydantoin and that the chemistry parallels extant biosynthetic pathways. For threonine, lactaldehyde would react with HCN and ammonium carbonate to form 5-(1-hydroxyethyl)hydantoin, which hydrolyzes to threonine. Hydantoins have been detected in carbonaceous chondrites (Lerner and Cooper, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*, 2005), consistent with this pathway operating in meteorite parent bodies.

## Critical intermediates and their roles as branch points

The prebiotic synthesis of threonine intersects with several other biosynthetic pathways through shared intermediates, creating a network rather than a linear sequence.

**Lactaldehyde** (2-hydroxypropanal) is the most direct aldehyde precursor specific to threonine via Strecker chemistry. It forms through UV photoreduction of acetaldehyde cyanohydrin at 19% yield (Ritson and Sutherland, 2013). **Glycolaldehyde** serves as the crucial branch point between sugar chemistry and amino acid chemistry — it enters the formose reaction to produce ribose precursors and simultaneously provides the carbon skeleton for serine (via Strecker) and connects to threonine through further homologation. Its detection in the interstellar medium toward IRAS 16293-2422 and in precometary ice analogs (de Marcellus et al., *PNAS*, 2015) confirms its cosmochemical availability. **Glyceraldehyde** occupies the next node: it is central to Sutherland's ribonucleotide synthesis and directly reacts with HCN and NH₃ to form the threonine aminonitrile, making it perhaps the single most important branch point connecting RNA and protein precursor chemistry.

**Acetaldehyde** connects to threonine through two routes — Strecker synthesis (producing alanine directly, or lactaldehyde via cyanohydrin reduction, then threonine) and aldol condensation with glycine. It is among the simplest and most abundant products of spark discharge experiments and interstellar chemistry. **α-Ketobutyrate** represents the metabolic connection: in modern biochemistry, threonine dehydratase converts threonine to α-ketobutyrate, and the reverse (reductive amination) constitutes a plausible prebiotic route. Copley, Smith, and Morowitz (*PNAS*, 2005) proposed that α-keto acids formed esters with dinucleotides in a proto-metabolic amino acid synthesis, providing selectivity through nucleotide-keto acid interactions. **HCN oligomers** represent a less specific but broad-spectrum source: Ruiz-Bermejo, Zorzano, and Osuna-Esteban (*Life*, 2013) reviewed 24 experimental conditions and found that **12 of the 20 proteinogenic amino acids**, including threonine, were detected in HCN polymer hydrolysates, with glycine and aspartic acid consistently the most abundant products.

## Environmental settings and their suitability for each synthetic step

Different prebiotic environments favor different steps in threonine synthesis, and no single setting optimizes the entire pathway. This "conditions compatibility problem" is a central challenge.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (Lost City type, 40–90°C, pH 9–11) provide the most favorable thermodynamic conditions for amino acid synthesis. Shock and Canovas (*Science*, 1998) calculated that autotrophic synthesis of all 20 amino acids is exergonic in hot, moderately reduced hydrothermal solutions. White, Bhartia, and Russell (*Earth and Planetary Science Letters*, 2015) demonstrated that mackinawite (FeS) and greigite (Fe₃S₄) precipitate in simulated Hadean chimney walls at 40–80°C, with maximum greigite yield at 75°C. Greigite's Fe₄S₄ cubane units are structural analogs of active centers in modern hydrogenase and ferredoxin. Lane and Martin (*Journal of Molecular Evolution*, 2012) showed that Fe(Ni)S minerals in alkaline vent micropores could concentrate organics approximately **5,000-fold** via thermophoresis, partially addressing the concentration problem.

**Surface volcanic hot springs and evaporitic pools** favor condensation reactions and UV-driven photochemistry. Damer and Deamer (*Astrobiology*, 2020) proposed that volcanic hot spring pools with wet-dry cycling at 60–80°C drive peptide and RNA-like polymer formation. The cyanosulfidic chemistry of Sutherland operates optimally in surface UV-exposed pools with phosphate buffer at ~35°C. Lahav, White, and Chang (*Science*, 1978) showed that wet-dry cycling plus temperature cycling in clay environments produces longer oligopeptides in higher yields than temperature fluctuations alone. Campbell et al. (*Nature Communications*, 2019) demonstrated that deliquescent minerals can mediate autonomous wet-dry cycling via humidity changes, achieving **over 10-fold higher oligomerization yields**.

**Black smoker hydrothermal vents** (250–400°C) are problematic. Bada, Miller, and Zhao (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 1995) demonstrated that amino acids are **irreversibly destroyed at 240°C**. However, the mixing zones where hot fluid encounters cold seawater (100–150°C) remain viable. Marshall (*Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*, 1994) synthesized amino acids at 200–275°C from aqueous NH₄HCO₃ with hydrocarbons, though no synthesis occurred below 150°C. The synthesis-destruction balance is precarious: Aubrey et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2009) concluded that equilibrium at high temperatures favors net degradation rather than synthesis.

**Ice environments** offer a counterintuitive advantage. Eutectic freezing concentrates solutes in brine channels between ice crystals. Miyakawa et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2002) froze NH₄CN solutions for 25 years at -20°C and -78°C, producing adenine, guanine, and amino acids — demonstrating that cold environments can drive prebiotic chemistry while simultaneously protecting labile products from thermal degradation.

## Mineral catalysts accelerate and direct threonine-relevant reactions

Mineral surfaces play three critical roles in prebiotic amino acid chemistry: catalyzing bond formation, concentrating dilute reactants, and potentially inducing chiral selectivity.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** anchor Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis. Huber and Wächtershäuser (*Science*, 1998) demonstrated peptide formation from amino acids activated by CO on (Ni,Fe)S surfaces at 100°C, achieving dipeptide yields of **0.4–12.4%** and tripeptide yields of ~0.10%. Their 2006 work (*Science*) showed CO-dependent α-amino acid synthesis on Ni-Fe precipitates at 80–120°C using cyano ligands as the C/N source. Pyrite (FeS₂) shows particularly interesting photocatalytic properties: a 2024 study in *Nature Communications* demonstrated that natural pyrite produces D-amino acids with enantiomeric excess of **14.5–42.4%** via asymmetric photocatalytic reductive amination — the first demonstration of a mineral surface generating significant chiral bias in amino acid synthesis. Cody, Boctor, and Hazen (*Science*, 2000) showed that carbonylated iron-sulfur compounds produce pyruvate from CO and FeS at hydrothermal conditions, establishing the availability of key α-keto acid precursors.

**Clay minerals**, particularly montmorillonite, catalyze condensation and polymerization. Ferris et al. (*Nature*, 1996) achieved RNA oligomers up to 50 nucleotides on montmorillonite surfaces. For amino acids, Kitadai et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2015) showed that glycine on montmorillonite at 150°C for 336 hours converts **~13.9% of monomers** to peptides under dry conditions, with no peptides forming without the mineral. Montmorillonite's interlayer structure provides steric effects that enhance condensation while protecting macromolecules from thermal decomposition. Bujdák et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 1995) demonstrated hetero-oligomerization (ala-gly-gly from ala + gly₂) on Ca-montmorillonite during wet-dry cycling at 80°C, with approximately 10% oligomer yields after 3 weeks.

**Photocatalytic minerals** offer UV-driven synthesis pathways. ZnS surfaces drive reversible reductive amination of α-keto acids to amino acids (Wang et al., *Chemical Communications*, 2012), supporting Mulkidjanian's zinc world hypothesis (*Biology Direct*, 2009) that life emerged at photosynthetically active porous ZnS structures. TiO₂ has been shown to catalyze amino acid production from simple precursors under near-UV illumination (Dunn, Aikawa, and Bard, *Journal of the American Chemical Society*, 1981). Both minerals could have driven amino acid synthesis in shallow, UV-exposed prebiotic pools.

## Meteoritic and experimental evidence establishes threonine's abiotic credentials

The detection of threonine in extraterrestrial materials provides independent confirmation that abiotic synthesis occurs under natural conditions, though contamination complicates interpretation.

The strongest evidence for indigenous extraterrestrial threonine comes from the **CR2 chondrite GRA 95229** (Graves Nunataks, Antarctica). Pizzarello et al. (*PNAS*, 2012) positively identified threonine and allothreonine alongside other hydroxy amino acids, with the critical observation that **threonine was racemic** — consistent with abiotic synthesis and ruling out biological contamination. The pristine Antarctic collection conditions and the absence of L-excess make this the most compelling meteoritic detection. In the Murchison meteorite, threonine has been detected at approximately **700 ppb** for L-threonine, but the strong L-enrichment (~99% Lee) is attributed to terrestrial contamination (Kato et al., *Scientific Reports*, 2017; Glavin et al., *Meteoritics and Planetary Science*, 2020). Similarly, Tagish Lake analyses showed ~99% Lee for threonine (Glavin et al., 2012), again flagged as contamination. Only **12 of the 20 proteinogenic amino acids** have been confirmed in meteorites with indigenous signatures.

Interstellar ice analog experiments provide complementary evidence. Nuevo et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2008) tentatively detected threonine in organic residues from vacuum UV irradiation of ice mixtures (H₂O, CO, CO₂, CH₃OH, CH₄, NH₃) at 10–80 K — the first such detection in ice photolysis experiments. Separately, UV/EUV irradiation of naphthalene + H₂O + NH₃ ice at 15 K produced 13 amino acids including threonine (Nuevo et al., *Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society*, 2008). These experiments demonstrate that threonine can form through non-aqueous photochemistry in interstellar environments.

Quantitative yields from laboratory experiments remain low. In spark discharge experiments, threonine appears at trace levels relative to glycine (the dominant product). Weber (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 1998) achieved 0.08% homoserine (threonine's biosynthetic precursor) from formaldehyde + glycolaldehyde + NH₄Cl with thiol catalyst at 40°C over 35 days. The cyanosulfidic pathway achieves 19% yield for the lactaldehyde intermediate, but overall threonine yields through the complete Strecker sequence remain unquantified in the published literature.

## Stereochemistry creates a fourfold selectivity challenge

Threonine's two chiral centers (C2 and C3) generate four stereoisomers — L-threonine (2S,3R), D-threonine (2R,3S), L-allothreonine (2S,3S), and D-allothreonine (2R,3R) — creating challenges beyond those faced by amino acids with a single stereocenter. Abiotic Strecker synthesis from lactaldehyde produces roughly equal amounts of threonine and allothreonine diastereomers, each as a racemic D/L mixture, yielding approximately 25% of the biologically relevant L-threonine. Schroeder and Bada (*Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*, 1977) studied threonine epimerization kinetics in fossil foraminifera and showed that L-threonine epimerizes to D-allothreonine at the α-carbon faster than β-carbon racemization, meaning the diastereomer ratio shifts more rapidly than the enantiomeric ratio.

Potential mechanisms for generating enantiomeric excess include **circularly polarized light (CPL)** from star-forming regions, which can induce 1–2% ee in amino acids through preferential photodestruction (Modica et al., *Astrophysical Journal*, 2014). This small seed can be amplified: Breslow demonstrated that as little as **1% Lee can reach 95:5 L:D ratio** through simple evaporation, exploiting differential solubility of racemic versus enantiopure crystals. Pyrite's asymmetric photocatalysis (14.5–42.4% ee for D-amino acids) represents a mineral-based alternative. However, for threonine specifically, aqueous alteration on meteorite parent bodies promotes racemization of amino acids with α-hydrogen atoms, meaning any original ee would erode over geological timescales.

## Stability, concentration, and the fundamental bottlenecks

Threonine faces several interrelated challenges that constrain its prebiotic accumulation and utilization.

**Thermal instability** is the most severe constraint. Threonine's β-hydroxyl group enables rapid β-elimination yielding dehydroaminobutyric acid, analogous to serine's well-characterized decomposition. Körner (*ChemSusChem*, 2021) noted that **no systematic investigation of threonine decomposition products under hydrothermal conditions had been performed** as of that date — a remarkable gap. What is known places threonine among the most labile proteinogenic amino acids: in dry heating studies (80–180°C), it ranks below most nonpolar and acidic amino acids in stability. Truong et al. (*Icarus*, 2019) established that threonine and aspartic acid cannot persist beyond 1 billion years at 273 K, making them the least durable proteinogenic amino acids — critical for both origin-of-life scenarios and biosignature detection on icy ocean worlds like Europa and Enceladus.

The **concentration problem** applies broadly to prebiotic amino acids but affects threonine acutely given its instability. At estimated prebiotic ocean concentrations of ~10⁻⁴ M glycine, Baú et al. calculated that "tens of thousands of terrestrial prebiotic oceans would not have sufficed to obtain a single Gly₉ molecule at equilibrium." Solutions include mineral surface adsorption (layered double hydroxides can concentrate, align, and template amino acids per Erastova et al., *Nature Communications*, 2017), eutectic freezing in ice channels, evaporitic pool concentration via wet-dry cycling, thermophoretic concentration in hydrothermal micropores (~5,000-fold per Lane and Martin), and aerosol chemistry at air-water interfaces.

The **selectivity problem** — producing threonine specifically rather than complex mixtures — remains largely unsolved. The cyanosulfidic pathway offers the most promising selectivity through its constrained reaction network, but still produces mixtures. Islam, Bučar, and Powner (*Nature Chemistry*, 2017) demonstrated that 2-aminothiazole selectively sequesters glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde from complex mixtures, potentially providing a physicochemical selection mechanism. The **water problem** (condensation reactions disfavored in aqueous solution, with ΔG ≈ +3.5 kcal/mol for glycine dimerization) applies to subsequent peptide incorporation. Proposed solutions include wet-dry cycling, depsipeptide intermediates (ester bonds formed first, then converted to amides), thioester chemistry, and aminonitrile coupling in water (Canavelli, Islam, and Powner, *Nature*, 2019).

## Conclusion

Threonine's prebiotic synthesis is better understood than for most proteinogenic amino acids, primarily thanks to the cyanosulfidic framework that connects its formation to the same chemistry producing RNA precursors. The Ritson-Sutherland pathway through lactaldehyde provides a complete, experimentally validated route from HCN to the threonine aminonitrile using only prebiotically available materials. Spark discharge experiments confirm that electrical energy acting on simple gas mixtures — particularly those containing H₂S — produces threonine alongside dozens of other amino acids. Racemic threonine in the pristine CR2 meteorite GRA 95229 demonstrates that abiotic synthesis occurs naturally in asteroid parent bodies.

Three critical gaps remain. First, threonine's thermal and hydrolytic instability means it must be synthesized and utilized on timescales shorter than its decomposition half-life — a constraint that strongly favors lower-temperature environments (alkaline vents, surface pools, ice) over high-temperature hydrothermal settings. Second, the systematic study of threonine decomposition products under prebiotic conditions has never been performed, leaving the kinetics and products of its degradation poorly constrained. Third, achieving the stereochemical selectivity needed to produce predominantly L-threonine (one of four stereoisomers) from racemic abiotic synthesis remains an open problem, though pyrite's asymmetric photocatalysis and CPL-seeded amplification mechanisms offer partial solutions. The convergence of cyanosulfidic chemistry, meteorite analysis, and mineral catalysis research is steadily narrowing these gaps, positioning threonine as a tractable case study for understanding how biological amino acids emerged from prebiotic chemistry.
