# Prebiotic synthesis of thymine: the hardest nucleobase to make

**Thymine (5-methyluracil) stands as the most challenging canonical nucleobase to synthesize under prebiotic conditions**, requiring a C5-methylation step that no demonstrated abiotic pathway achieves efficiently. Every known route produces thymine as a minor product at roughly one-tenth the yield of uracil, its unmethylated parent. This difficulty is now understood as chemically fundamental: the C5 methyl addition is less energetically favorable than the hydroxyl and amino substitutions that produce uracil and cytosine, and achieving regioselective carbon methylation without enzymatic assistance remains the central bottleneck. The cumulative evidence — from formamide thermolysis experiments and astrophysical ice photochemistry to pristine asteroid sample returns — strongly supports the view that thymine was a late evolutionary addition, emerging through enzymatic methylation within living systems rather than through prebiotic chemistry. This report compiles all demonstrated and proposed synthesis routes, quantitative data, environmental constraints, and open challenges relevant to the prebiotic origin of thymine.

---

## Seven routes to thymine, all with low yields

Multiple prebiotic synthesis pathways have been proposed or demonstrated for thymine. They fall into two broad categories: indirect routes that first produce uracil and then methylate it, and direct routes from simple feedstocks. In every case, thymine yields are low relative to other nucleobases.

### Uracil methylation via formaldehyde

The dominant mechanism across most routes proceeds through three steps: (1) electrophilic addition of formaldehyde at C5 of uracil to produce **5-hydroxymethyluracil (5-HMU)**, (2) esterification of 5-HMU with formic acid, and (3) decarboxylation yielding thymine. This pathway was first demonstrated by Stephen-Sherwood, Oró, and Kimball (*Science* 173:446–447, 1971), who achieved **~0.1% yield** using formaldehyde and hydrazine as reductant in ammoniacal solution. Choughuley, Subbaraman, Kazi, and Chadha (*Biosystems* 9:73–80, 1977) later showed that uracil reacts with formaldehyde and formic acid at **100–140°C** in dilute aqueous solution to produce 5-HMU, methylenebiuracil, and thymine, with thymine favored under acidic conditions. Robertson and Miller (*Science* 268:702–705, 1995) established that formaldehyde adds to uracil at C5 with favorable rates and equilibria, describing 5-substituted uracils as "a bridge between the RNA world and the DNA-protein world." Quantum chemical calculations at the B3LYP/6-31G* level confirm this mechanism with an overall **ΔG of −29.4 kcal/mol** and an activation energy of ~16.7 kcal/mol for the rate-limiting step (Petera et al., *Molecules* 26:2248, 2021).

### Formamide-based one-pot chemistry

Formamide (HCONH₂) provides the most versatile single-precursor route to thymine. When heated at **160°C** with TiO₂ as catalyst, neat formamide produces thymine, 5-HMU, and acyclonucleosides — compounds that do not form without TiO₂ (Saladino, Ciambecchini, Crestini, Costanzo, Negri, and Di Mauro, *ChemBioChem* 4:514–521, 2003). TiO₂ catalyzes partial decomposition of formamide to formaldehyde, which then hydroxymethylates uracil (itself a formamide condensation product). Manganese oxides (MnO, Mn₃O₄) represent a second catalyst family capable of producing thymine from formamide at 100–180°C (Bhushan, Nayak, and Kamaluddin, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 46:203–213, 2016). Remarkably, Petera et al. (2021) demonstrated that pure formamide heated at 160°C for 24 hours produces **uracil (~1 mM) and thymine (~0.1 mM) without any catalyst** — the formic acid hydrolysis byproduct disproportionates to formaldehyde, which completes the methylation in situ.

Under proton irradiation (170 MeV, simulating solar wind) with powdered meteorites as catalysts, formamide produces the complete set of nucleobases, sugars (ribose, 2-deoxyribose), and even nucleosides including **thymidine** — the first demonstration of prebiotic thymidine formation (Saladino, Carota, Botta et al., *PNAS* 112:E2746–E2755, 2015). High-power laser irradiation of formamide simulating asteroid impact plasma (~4,500 K) also produces all canonical nucleobases via •CN and •NH radical chemistry (Ferus et al., *PNAS* 112:657–662, 2015).

### Other demonstrated pathways

The β-alanine/urea pathway of Chittenden and Schwartz (*Nature* 263:350–351, 1976; *Biosystems* 9:87–92, 1977) proceeds through evaporation of β-alanine and urea with clay minerals to form 5,6-dihydrouracil (DHU), UV photodehydrogenation of DHU to uracil, and reaction with acetate to yield thymine — entirely from prebiotically plausible materials compatible with wet-dry cycling pools. UV photolysis of pyrimidine mixed with methanol or methane ices at **~14 K** produces small quantities of thymine alongside more abundant uracil and cytosine (Materese, Nuevo, Bera, Lee, and Sandford, *Astrobiology* 13:948–962, 2013), though thymine is "by far the least efficiently produced nucleobase" because methyl addition requires three alterations to the pyrimidine ring. Most remarkably, Oba, Takano, Naraoka, Watanabe, and Kouchi (*Nature Communications* 10:4413, 2019) showed that UV photolysis of simple ices (H₂O:CO:NH₃:CH₃OH at 10 K) produces **all three pyrimidines and three purines simultaneously** — confirmed by isotope labeling — representing the most fundamental demonstrated route from simple molecules.

The Sutherland cyanosulfidic pathway (Powner, Gerland, and Sutherland, *Nature* 459:239–242, 2009; Patel et al., *Nature Chemistry* 7:301–307, 2015), arguably the most sophisticated prebiotic chemistry framework developed, produces activated pyrimidine ribonucleotides (cytidine and uridine derivatives) but has **not been extended to thymine**. Similarly, Xu et al. (*Nature* 582:60–66, 2020) demonstrated selective co-synthesis of DNA purine nucleosides (deoxyadenosine, deoxyinosine) with RNA pyrimidine nucleosides but did not produce deoxythymidine. HCN oligomerization (Ferris, Joshi, Edelson, and Lawless, *J. Mol. Evol.*, 1970s) and Fischer-Tropsch type synthesis (Yang and Oró, 1971; Pearce and Pudritz, *Astrobiology* 16:853–872, 2016) produce purines and some non-canonical pyrimidines but not thymine directly.

## Critical intermediates form a convergent network

The prebiotic routes to thymine share a set of key intermediates that serve as network branch points, connecting multiple synthesis pathways and linking thymine chemistry to broader prebiotic reaction networks.

**Uracil** is the immediate precursor to thymine in nearly all pathways and represents the most important branch point. It can be synthesized through at least six routes: reaction of cyanoacetaldehyde with concentrated urea (**30–50% cytosine yield**, which hydrolyzes to uracil; Robertson and Miller, *Nature* 375:772–774, 1995), reaction of cyanoacetylene with cyanate (**19% cytosine yield** at 100°C; Ferris, Sanchez, and Orgel, *J. Mol. Biol.* 33:693–704, 1968), formamide condensation, HCN oligomer hydrolysis, β-alanine/urea condensation with clay catalysis, and photodecarboxylation of orotic acid.

**Cyanoacetylene (HC₃N)** is the primary C₃ building block for the pyrimidine ring. It forms by electric discharge in CH₄/N₂ mixtures (Sanchez, Ferris, and Orgel, *Science* 154:784, 1966), is abundant in interstellar molecular clouds and Titan's atmosphere, and hydrolyzes in water to **cyanoacetaldehyde** — itself ~10³ times more stable than cyanoacetylene and capable of reacting with urea to produce cytosine/uracil at high yields. **Formaldehyde (H₂CO)** serves the dual critical role of sugar synthesis (formose reaction) and C5-methylation of uracil to initiate thymine formation. It is produced abundantly by UV photolysis of CO₂/H₂O atmospheres and by electric discharge in methane-containing atmospheres. **Formamide** functions as a "unitary frame" for nucleobase synthesis (Saladino et al., *Chem. Soc. Rev.* 41:5526–5565, 2012), decomposing thermally to yield HCN, CO, NH₃, formaldehyde, and formic acid — precisely the building blocks needed for both purine and pyrimidine ring construction plus the methylation step to thymine.

**Urea**, produced by electric discharge, cyanamide hydrolysis, and HCN hydrolysis via formamide, concentrates in evaporating lagoons due to its high water solubility and thermal persistence, making it a critical co-reactant in virtually all pyrimidine synthesis pathways. **Orotic acid**, robustly produced under both subaerial (180°C, 15 bar) and aerial (80°C, 1 bar) alkaline hydrothermal conditions from cyanide polymerization (Pérez-Fernández et al., *Scientific Reports* 12:15140, 2022), bridges prebiotic chemistry to extant biosynthesis via the de novo pyrimidine pathway.

## Environmental conditions determine which pathways dominate

The synthesis and survival of thymine depend critically on temperature, pH, UV flux, mineral surfaces, and water activity. These constraints effectively partition viable prebiotic thymine chemistry among three environmental settings.

**Surface evaporitic pools** provide the most favorable overall conditions for thymine synthesis. Wet-dry cycling concentrates non-volatile solutes (urea, cyanoacetaldehyde, formamide with its 210°C boiling point), enables dehydration reactions required for ring closure, and exposes reaction mixtures to UV radiation that drives photodehydrogenation (DHU → uracil) and photoanomerization. The Robertson-Miller drying lagoon model achieves **30–50% cytosine yields** from cyanoacetaldehyde and concentrated urea at 100°C. Clay mineral surfaces provide catalytic templates and adsorption sites. Becker, Schneider, Okamura, Crisp, Amatov, Dejmek, and Carell (*Nature Communications* 9:163, 2018) demonstrated that fluctuating temperature, pH, and concentration in simulated geothermal fields enable continuous nucleoside formation, and recent work shows wet-dry cycling can polymerize nucleotide monomers into oligomers up to **53 nucleotides** (*PNAS* 121:e2412784121, 2024).

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (white smokers, 60–90°C, pH 9–11) offer moderate temperatures compatible with nucleobase stability and mineral catalysis by serpentinite, brucite, and iron sulfides. Thermodynamic analysis by LaRowe and Helgeson (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 38:383–397, 2008) shows all five nucleobases are thermodynamically favored for synthesis from **0°C to 150–250°C** at 500 bars with Rainbow hydrothermal system composition. However, reactive nitrogen sources are scarce at deep-sea vents, and **black smoker temperatures (250–400°C) destroy all nucleobases within minutes**.

Thymine's exceptional thermal stability governs accumulation prospects. Levy and Miller (*PNAS* 95:7933–7938, 1998) measured nucleobase half-lives at pH 7:

| Temperature | Thymine | Uracil | Cytosine | Adenine | Guanine |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0°C | ~2 × 10⁹ yr | 3.8 × 10⁸ yr | 1.7 × 10⁴ yr | ~10⁶ yr | ~10⁶ yr |
| 100°C | **56 yr** | 12 yr | 19 days | ~1 yr | ~0.8 yr |
| 250–350°C | Minutes | Minutes | Seconds | Minutes | Minutes |

**Thymine is the most thermally stable of all canonical nucleobases**, with an Arrhenius relationship of log k = −7709/T + 11.24. At 0°C its half-life approaches **2 billion years** — compatible with geological accumulation over hundreds of millions of years. This creates a paradox: thymine is the hardest to make but the easiest to preserve. A scenario featuring low-temperature storage (~0°C) punctuated by brief excursions to ~100°C for synthesis (e.g., shallow hydrothermal pools) optimally balances reactivity and product stability.

## Mineral catalysts show striking selectivity for different nucleobases

The choice of mineral catalyst profoundly affects which nucleobases form from formamide, with **TiO₂ uniquely enabling thymine production**. This catalyst specificity has major implications for understanding where on early Earth thymine could have originated.

Saladino and colleagues systematically tested dozens of mineral catalysts with neat formamide at 160°C. Limestone, kaolin, silica, alumina, and zeolite produce purine, adenine, guanine, and cytosine. Montmorillonite clays produce hypoxanthine and uracil efficiently but actually **enhance thymine degradation** (Saladino et al., *ChemBioChem* 5:1558–1566, 2004). Only TiO₂ — present on early Earth as anatase and rutile — generates thymine and 5-HMU from formamide, because it uniquely catalyzes formamide decomposition to formaldehyde. Iron-sulfur minerals (pyrrhotite, pyrite, chalcopyrite) catalyze purine and adenine synthesis from formamide but not thymine (Saladino et al., *JACS* 130:15512–15518, 2008). Metal(II) octacyanomolybdates represent a distinct catalyst family that produces thymine from formamide in good yields alongside purine, cytosine, and adenine (Iqubal et al., *Astrobiology* 14:769–779, 2014).

Borates play no direct role in nucleobase synthesis but are critical downstream: they selectively stabilize ribose over other aldopentoses (Ricardo et al., *Science* 303:196, 2004), guide regioselective ribose-5'-phosphorylation (Hirakawa et al., *Scientific Reports* 12, 2022), and feature in the Discontinuous Synthesis Model for RNA formation validated by Benner et al. (*PNAS*, 2025). Phosphate minerals provide buffering, nucleotide activation, and polymerization scaffolding. ZnS photocatalysis drives metabolic precursor reactions (reverse Krebs cycle steps at pH ~7, 288 K) but has not been directly linked to nucleobase synthesis (Zhang et al., *PNAS*, 2007).

## Meteorites and asteroids now confirm extraterrestrial thymine delivery

The detection of thymine in pristine extraterrestrial samples represents one of the most significant recent advances in the field and validates astrophysical ice photochemistry as a genuine source of prebiotic nucleobases.

Callahan et al. (*PNAS* 108:13995–13998, 2011) detected purines (guanine, hypoxanthine, xanthine, adenine at 10–500 ppb) in the Murchison meteorite but notably **did not detect thymine or cytosine** (detection limit ~0.02 ppb), while both were found in nearby soil samples — arguing strongly against contamination for the purines. The breakthrough came when Oba et al. (*Nature Communications* 13:2008, 2022) applied gentler extraction conditions (cold water at ~25°C rather than hot formic acid) and more sensitive HPLC/ESI-HRMS analytics. They detected **thymine at 0.02–6 ppb** alongside cytosine for the first time in Murchison, Murray, and Tagish Lake meteorites. The presence of methylated pyrimidines (1-methyluracil, 6-methyluracil) at concentrations similar to non-methylated forms supports an abiological origin.

The definitive confirmation came from **asteroid Ryugu samples** returned by the Hayabusa2 mission. Koga, Oba, Takano et al. (*Nature Astronomy*, 2026) detected all five canonical nucleobases (A, G, C, T, U) in pristine material never exposed to terrestrial contamination, at total concentrations of **1,577 ± 35 pmol/g** (sample C0370) and **507 ± 21 pmol/g** (sample A0480). Isotopic signatures (**δ¹³C = +25.5‰ to +33.9‰; δ¹⁵N = +45.1‰ to +59.8‰**) confirm extraterrestrial origin. Ryugu shows roughly equal purines and pyrimidines, while Murchison is enriched in purines and Bennu/Orgueil in pyrimidines. Carbon isotope measurements of Murchison nucleobases by Martins et al. (*Earth Planet. Sci. Lett.*, 2008) — uracil δ¹³C = +44.5‰ ± 2.3‰ — further confirm non-terrestrial origins.

These findings validate the interstellar ice photochemistry results of Oba et al. (2019) and Materese et al. (2013) and demonstrate that extraterrestrial delivery during the Late Heavy Bombardment (**4.0–3.8 Gya**) could have provided a baseline inventory of all nucleobases, including thymine, to early Earth.

## Why uracil came first and thymine came later

The question of why RNA uses uracil while DNA uses thymine has a compelling evolutionary answer rooted in genome integrity, and this answer has profound implications for whether prebiotic thymine synthesis was ever necessary.

The primary selective advantage of thymine over uracil in DNA is **damage recognition**. Cytosine spontaneously deaminates to uracil with a half-life of ~340 years at 25°C (Shapiro, *PNAS* 96:4396–4401, 1999). If DNA normally contained uracil, the repair enzyme uracil-DNA glycosylase (UDG) could not distinguish legitimate uracils from deamination-damaged cytosines. By methylating uracil to thymine, organisms "tag" correct uracils so that any unmodified uracil in DNA is flagged as damage requiring excision repair. Vértessy and Tóth (*Accounts of Chemical Research* 42:97–106, 2009) termed this the "thymine invention."

Modern biology uses **two non-homologous thymidylate synthase families** to convert dUMP to dTMP. Classical ThyA uses 5,10-methylenetetrahydrofolate as both carbon donor and reducing agent. The more recently discovered ThyX (Myllykallio, Lipowski, Leduc, Filée, Forterre, and Liebl, *Science* 297:105–107, 2002) uses the same folate as carbon donor but FADH₂ as reductant and is ~10× catalytically slower. These enzymes share **no evolutionary homology**, indicating convergent evolution and at least two independent inventions of thymine biosynthesis (Filée, Becker, Mellottee et al., *Nature Communications* 14:838, 2023).

Forterre's two-step model (*Current Opinion in Microbiology* 5:525–532, 2002; *Biochimie* 87:793–803, 2005) proposes that ribonucleotide reductase first converted ribonucleotides to deoxyribonucleotides, creating **uracil-containing DNA (U-DNA)**. Thymidylate synthases evolved later, converting dUMP to dTMP. Some modern phages (PBS1, PBS2 of *Bacillus subtilis*) still use U-DNA — possible relics of this ancient transition. The temporal order is established biochemically: dUMP is the substrate of thymidylate synthase, meaning ribonucleotide reduction must precede thymine biosynthesis.

For prebiotic methylation of uracil to thymine, the most plausible agents are **formaldehyde** (demonstrated by Robertson and Miller, 1995; Choughuley et al., 1977) and **formic acid** as co-reductant. These add to C5 via a Mannich-type reaction that achieves natural regioselectivity at the correct position. Other potential prebiotic methylating agents — methanol, dimethyl sulfide, methylamine — remain largely untested. The biological cofactor SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) has no known prebiotic analog. Notably, free methyl cations generated by β-decay of tritiated methane methylate uracil at multiple sites (C1, O2, N3, O4, C6), demonstrating the **regioselectivity challenge**: simple alkylating agents prefer nitrogen and oxygen over carbon (Korsakov et al., *Biochimie* 71:633–637, 1989).

## The thymine problem and what remains unsolved

The "thymine problem" in prebiotic chemistry encompasses several interconnected challenges that collectively explain why thymine occupies a unique position among the canonical nucleobases.

**The methylation bottleneck** is the core issue. While uracil forms readily through multiple prebiotic routes at yields of 20–50% (from cyanoacetaldehyde + urea), every demonstrated route to thymine produces it at roughly **one-tenth the uracil yield**. Quantum chemical calculations by Bera, Nuevo, Milam, Sandford, and Lee (*J. Chem. Phys.* 144:144308, 2016) show that methyl group addition to the pyrimidine ring in water ice is inherently less energetically favorable than hydroxyl or amino group addition, providing a fundamental physical explanation. The C5 position of uracil, while activated by the conjugated system, is not the most nucleophilic site — nitrogen and oxygen atoms react preferentially with simple alkylating agents.

**The Sutherland gap** represents a major theoretical challenge. The cyanosulfidic chemistry framework (Powner et al., 2009; Patel et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2020) — widely regarded as the most compelling prebiotic nucleotide synthesis system — produces RNA pyrimidine nucleosides (cytidine, uridine) and DNA purine nucleosides (deoxyadenosine, deoxyinosine) but **has not been extended to deoxythymidine**. Closing this gap would be a major advance.

**The asphalt problem** (Benner, Kim, and Carrigan, *Accounts of Chemical Research* 45:2025–2034, 2012) applies with particular force to thymine synthesis: when reactive compounds are mixed without precise control, the result is intractable polymeric tars rather than specific products. Thymine's requirement for multi-step sequential chemistry (ring formation → methylation → reduction) makes it especially vulnerable to side reactions at each stage.

Additional unresolved issues include stereochemistry (all biological nucleosides are β-anomers with D-sugars, but prebiotic syntheses produce anomeric and enantiomeric mixtures; Yadav, Kumar, and Krishnamurthy, *Chemical Reviews* 120:4766–4805, 2020), the compatibility problem (different components of nucleotides require different, often mutually exclusive conditions; Kitadai and Maruyama, 2018, identified at least 8 mutually exclusive reaction conditions for complete chemical evolution), and the concentration problem (many syntheses require reactant concentrations of ≥0.1 M, limiting geographic plausibility to evaporating lagoons or eutectic freezing environments).

## Conclusion: thymine as a biosynthetic invention, not a prebiotic given

The evidence converges on a striking conclusion: **prebiotic thymine synthesis may never have been necessary for life's origin**. If the RNA world preceded DNA, and if the earliest DNA used uracil (as Forterre's U-DNA model proposes), then thymine became biologically important only after thymidylate synthases evolved within already-living systems. The independent, convergent evolution of two non-homologous thymidylate synthase families (ThyA and ThyX) reinforces the view that thymine was "invented" enzymatically, likely under selective pressure for genome integrity.

Nevertheless, the prebiotic chemistry of thymine matters for several reasons. Extraterrestrial delivery is now confirmed as a genuine source — **all five canonical nucleobases exist in pristine Ryugu asteroid samples** at pmol/g concentrations. Formamide-based chemistry, particularly with TiO₂ catalysis, provides a plausible terrestrial route at ~0.1 mM yields without exotic reagents. The formaldehyde-mediated hydroxymethylation pathway (uracil → 5-HMU → thymine, ΔG = −29.4 kcal/mol) is thermodynamically favorable and uses only prebiotically abundant reagents.

For synthesis network design and pathway evaluation in agentic AI systems, the key priorities are: (1) extending the Sutherland cyanosulfidic framework to include thymidine, (2) identifying novel prebiotic reducing agents for the 5-HMU → thymine conversion that avoid the regioselectivity problems of simple alkylating agents, (3) exploring mineral-catalyzed one-pot systems where TiO₂ or manganese oxides could couple uracil formation with in situ methylation, and (4) investigating whether wet-dry cycling with formaldehyde-rich atmospheres could drive the sequential chemistry needed at sufficient yields. The field remains wide open, with thymine's unique position — hardest to synthesize yet most thermally stable — continuing to illuminate fundamental questions about why biology chose the molecules it did.
