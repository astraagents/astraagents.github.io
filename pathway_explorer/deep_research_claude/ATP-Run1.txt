# Prebiotic synthesis of ATP: a comprehensive research review

**No continuous pathway from simple molecules to ATP has been demonstrated under a single geochemical setting, but every individual step — adenine synthesis, ribose formation, nucleoside assembly, phosphorylation, and triphosphate bond formation — now has at least one experimentally validated prebiotic route.** The most striking recent advance is the demonstration that acetyl phosphate with Fe³⁺ converts ADP to ATP at **~15–20% yield** in water at 30°C (Pinna et al., 2022, *PLOS Biology*), providing the first plausible prebiotic route to the complete ATP molecule. The field has converged on two competing macro-frameworks: surface/photochemistry scenarios (Sutherland, Carell, Benner) that exploit UV light and wet-dry cycles, and deep-sea alkaline hydrothermal vent scenarios (Lane, Martin, Russell) that leverage natural proton gradients and iron-sulfur catalysis. Integration of individually successful reaction steps into geochemically coherent sequences remains the field's central unsolved challenge.

## From five molecules of HCN to the universal energy currency

ATP's prebiotic synthesis can be conceptually divided into four stages: (1) adenine base formation, (2) ribose sugar synthesis, (3) nucleoside and nucleotide assembly, and (4) triphosphate bond formation. Each stage has multiple proposed routes, which originate from feedstock molecules — **HCN, formaldehyde, formamide, H₂S, CO₂, H₂, and phosphate** — all plausibly available on the Hadean Earth through volcanic outgassing, atmospheric photochemistry, meteoritic delivery, and serpentinization.

The adenine base (C₅H₅N₅) is formally a pentamer of hydrogen cyanide. Juan Oró demonstrated this synthesis in 1960–1961 by heating concentrated ammonium cyanide (>1 M) at 70°C for several days, obtaining **~0.5% adenine yield** (Oró, *Nature*, 1961). The accepted pathway proceeds through four intermediates: HCN dimerizes to formamidine, trimerizes to aminomalononitrile, and tetramerizes to **diaminomaleonitrile (DAMN)** — the critical bottleneck intermediate first characterized by Sanchez, Ferris, and Orgel (*J. Mol. Biol.*, 1967). Near-quantitative UV photoisomerization converts DAMN to **4-aminoimidazole-5-carbonitrile (AICN)**, which reacts with a fifth HCN equivalent to close the purine ring and yield adenine. Computational work by Roy, Najafian, and Schleyer (*PNAS*, 2007) confirmed the overall pentamerization is thermodynamically favorable (ΔG = −53.7 kcal/mol in the gas phase). A 2025 study by Sandström et al. (*JACS*) applied quantum chemistry and microkinetic modeling to identify four interwoven base-catalyzed pathways to adenine in liquid HCN at 278 K, confirming DAMN as a necessary intermediate.

The HCN route faces a serious **concentration problem**: below ~0.01 M, hydrolysis to ammonium formate dominates over DAMN formation. Solutions include eutectic freezing (concentrating HCN in ice; Levy et al., *Icarus*, 2000, obtained 0.04% adenine from dilute HCN frozen at −78°C for 25 years), alkaline lake evaporation, and an alternative entry point demonstrated by Hill and Orgel (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 2002) where DAMN reacts with ammonium formate to yield adenine without requiring high HCN concentrations.

A second major route uses **formamide (HCONH₂)**, the simplest amide and hydration product of HCN. Saladino, Di Mauro, and colleagues showed that heating neat formamide at ~160°C with mineral catalysts — TiO₂ (*ChemBioChem*, 2003), montmorillonite clay (*ChemBioChem*, 2004), iron-sulfur minerals (*JACS*, 2008), or powdered meteorites (*PNAS*, 2015) — produces all four canonical nucleobases plus amino acids, carboxylic acids, and even nucleosides in a single pot. Proton-beam irradiation of formamide with meteorite powder yielded cytidine, uridine, adenosine, and thymidine directly. The formamide route elegantly avoids the HCN concentration problem because formamide is a stable liquid (bp 210°C) that can accumulate through thermal dissociation of ammonium formate.

## Ribose is prebiotic chemistry's most reluctant building block

Ribose synthesis presents perhaps the most acute challenge in the entire pathway. The classic **formose reaction** — base-catalyzed oligomerization of formaldehyde discovered by Butlerov in 1861 — produces ribose as a **minor product (<1%)** within a complex mixture of over 40 sugars (Shapiro, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 1988). Worse, ribose is the **least stable** of the four aldopentoses, with a half-life of just **73 minutes at pH 7 and 100°C** (Larralde, Robertson & Miller, *PNAS*, 1995). A 2025 study from Krishnamurthy's group (*Chem*) used NMR monitoring to show that under milder conditions (room temperature, pH ~8), the formose reaction produces **only branched-chain sugars — not linear sugars like ribose** — further undermining this route.

The landmark solution came from **borate mineral stabilization**. Ricardo, Carrigan, Olcott, and Benner (*Science*, 2004) demonstrated that borate ions complex with ribose's cis-1,2-diol in its furanose form, locking it in a cyclic structure that prevents ring-opening and subsequent enolization. This complexation reduces decomposition rates by **orders of magnitude** and preferentially stabilizes ribose over other aldopentoses (Furukawa, Horiuchi & Kakegawa, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 2013). Kim, Ricardo, and Benner (*JACS*, 2011) further showed that combined borate and molybdate minerals guide formaldehyde/glycolaldehyde mixtures toward pentoses including ribose at **~20% yield**, with molybdate catalyzing Bilik rearrangements that interconvert pentose isomers. Relevant borate minerals include **colemanite** (Ca₂B₆O₁₁·5H₂O), **ulexite**, and **kernite**, though their availability in Hadean settings remains debated — the oldest confirmed tourmaline (boron-bearing) deposits are >3.7 Ga at Isua, Greenland.

An alternative route bypasses free ribose entirely. **Ritson and Sutherland** (*Nature Chemistry*, 2012) demonstrated that UV irradiation of HCN in the presence of copper(I) cyanide complexes drives a Kiliani-Fischer-type photoredox synthesis of glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde — the C₂ and C₃ sugar fragments that feed directly into nucleotide assembly without ever forming free ribose. This Cu(I)/Cu(II) photoredox cycling operates catalytically and was later integrated into the broader cyanosulfidic protometabolic network (Patel et al., *Nature Chemistry*, 2015).

## Three competing strategies for building the nucleoside

Connecting adenine to ribose through a glycosidic bond is complicated by adenine's multiple nucleophilic nitrogen atoms (N1, N3, N7, N9) and ribose's two ring forms (furanose/pyranose) and two anomers (α/β). Biology exclusively uses β-N9-furanosyl adenosine. Three strategies have emerged to solve this selectivity problem.

**The Sutherland bypass approach** (Powner, Gerland & Sutherland, *Nature*, 2009) is the most radical: it avoids condensing free base with free sugar altogether. Instead, pyrimidine nucleotides are assembled by building the nucleobase stepwise on a sugar scaffold. Glycolaldehyde reacts with cyanamide to form **2-aminooxazole**, which then reacts with glyceraldehyde to yield ribo-aminooxazoline (44% yield, with 30% arabino isomer; Anastasi et al., *Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.*, 2006). Subsequent reaction with cyanoacetylene produces an anhydronucleoside, and regioselective phosphorylation yields **β-ribocytidine-2′,3′-cyclic phosphate** — an activated nucleotide ready for oligomerization. Inorganic phosphate plays multiple roles throughout as acid-base catalyst, nucleophilic catalyst, and pH buffer. For purine nucleosides, Xu et al. (*Nature*, 2020) extended this approach using 8-mercaptoadenine and dry-state tethered glycosylation followed by photochemical reduction, yielding N9-configured deoxyadenosine selectively.

**The Carell formamidopyrimidine (FaPy) approach** (Becker et al., *Science*, 2016) achieved a breakthrough in purine nucleoside selectivity. Formamidopyrimidines — derived from aminopyrimidines (detected on comets by ESA's Rosetta mission) via nitrosation, reduction, and formylation — condense with ribose to give **N9-purine nucleosides in ~60% yield with extreme regioselectivity**. Becker et al. (*Science*, 2019) subsequently demonstrated a unified synthesis of both pyrimidine and purine RNA ribonucleotides in one-pot wet-dry cycling conditions, with phosphate minerals enabling selective 5′-phosphorylation.

**The Benner cyclic phosphate approach** (Kim & Benner, *PNAS*, 2017) uses **ribose-1,2-cyclic phosphate**, which blocks the α-face of ribose and enforces both β-anomeric and N9-regiochemical selectivity. Heating with adenine at 85°C yields **adenosine-2′-phosphate at 15% yield**, exclusively as the β-anomer. The 2′-phosphate can rearrange to the biologically relevant 5′-phosphate using urea and borate.

A fourth approach involves **mineral catalysis**: Ti-containing minerals catalyze direct ribosylation of adenine to give N9-ribosylated products at 14% yield with 13:1 N9:N6 selectivity (*Nature Synthesis*, 2022), representing the highest selectivity achieved through purely mineral-mediated chemistry.

## Phosphorylation converges on acetyl phosphate as ATP's likely precursor

The "phosphorylation problem" has historically been prebiotic chemistry's most stubborn bottleneck. Orthophosphate from apatite — the dominant terrestrial phosphate mineral — has poor aqueous solubility and low reactivity. Condensation reactions forming phosphoester bonds are thermodynamically unfavorable in water. Multiple solutions have been proposed:

**Meteoritic phosphide minerals**, particularly **schreibersite ((Fe,Ni)₃P)**, corrode in water to release reactive reduced phosphorus species — orthophosphate, pyrophosphate, phosphite, and hypophosphite — via free radical reactions (Pasek et al., *Geochim. Cosmochim. Acta*, 2007). Schreibersite corrosion in aqueous ammonia even produces **diamidophosphate (DAP)** (Gibard et al., *Angew. Chem.*, 2019). Gull et al. (*Scientific Reports*, 2015) achieved nucleoside phosphorylation yields of **<6%** using schreibersite at 60–80°C and pH 8–10 — among the highest yields for aqueous reactions below 100°C without condensing agents.

**Diamidophosphate (DAP)** emerged as a remarkably versatile prebiotic phosphorylating agent. Gibard, Bhowmik, Karki, Kim, and Krishnamurthy (*Nature Chemistry*, 2018) showed DAP phosphorylates nucleosides, amino acids, glycerol, and fatty acids in aqueous solution at pH 5.5–10 and room temperature to 50°C. Under paste conditions (low water activity), DAP produces oligonucleotides up to 4-mers and oligopeptides up to 8-mers in the same pot. Cruz, Jiménez, and Krishnamurthy (*JACS*, 2023) later enhanced DAP's regioselectivity for nucleoside phosphorylation.

**Volcanic polyphosphates**, particularly cyclic trimetaphosphate (cTMP), provide another route. Yamagata et al. (*Nature*, 1991) demonstrated volcanic production of polyphosphates. Using trimetaphosphate with Ni(II) catalysis in wet-dry cycles, Yamagata and Inomata achieved **30% total phosphorylation** of adenosine, including **13% 5′-ATP** and 10.4% 2′,3′-cyclic AMP. **Borate-directed phosphorylation** offers exquisite regioselectivity: Kim et al. (*Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.*, 2016) showed that adding borate to adenosine + phosphate + pyrophosphate in formamide at 90°C yields **exclusively 5′-AMP**, because borate blocks the 2′ and 3′ hydroxyl groups.

The critical final step — **triphosphate bond formation** — was demonstrated most convincingly by Pinna, Kunz, Halpern, Harrison, Jordan, Ward, Werner, and Lane (*PLOS Biology*, 2022). Acetyl phosphate (AcP) with **Fe³⁺** converts ADP to ATP at **~15–20% yield** in water at 30°C and pH 5.5–6 within 2–4 hours. The conditions are remarkably specific: Fe³⁺ is uniquely effective among all tested metal ions (Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺, Mn²⁺, Co²⁺, Cr³⁺, Mo³⁺ all failed), and AcP is uniquely effective among phosphorylating agents (carbamoyl phosphate achieved only ~half the yield; pyrophosphate, cTMP, and phosphoenolpyruvate all failed). **Most strikingly, AcP phosphorylated ADP but not GDP, CDP, UDP, or IDP**, potentially explaining ATP's unique status as life's universal energy currency. Fe³⁺ forms a macro-chelate complex interacting with adenine's N7, the AcP phosphate, and ADP's β-phosphate — a geometry unavailable to other nucleotides.

## Hydrothermal vents versus sunlit pools: the environmental debate

Two dominant environmental frameworks compete in the field, each best suited for different synthesis stages.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (exemplified by the Lost City hydrothermal field, discovered in 2000 at the Atlantis Massif, Mid-Atlantic Ridge) operate at **40–90°C, pH 9–11**, and produce **up to 14 mM H₂** through serpentinization of olivine. Nick Lane, William Martin, and Michael Russell have argued that these vents provide natural proton gradients of **3–6 pH units** across thin Fe(Ni)S mineral barriers — gradients equivalent in magnitude and polarity to those driving modern ATP synthase. Herschy et al. (*J. Mol. Evol.*, 2014) built a bench-top reactor simulating these conditions and detected formaldehyde production from H₂ + CO₂ using Ni-doped mackinawite catalysts. Preiner et al. (*Nature Ecology & Evolution*, 2020) demonstrated that greigite, magnetite, and awaruite catalyze CO₂ fixation with H₂ at 100°C under alkaline conditions, producing **formate (up to 200 mM), acetate (up to 100 μM), and pyruvate (up to 10 μM)**. These vents persist for **>100,000 years**, orders of magnitude longer than black smokers.

**Surface photochemistry environments** — volcanic hot springs, evaporitic pools, tidal flats — provide complementary advantages. The Hadean Earth lacked an ozone layer, making UV-C (200–280 nm) and UV-B intensely available at the surface. This radiation drives the Sutherland cyanosulfidic network, DAMN-to-AICN photoisomerization, photoanomerization of nucleosides, and Cu(I)/Cu(II) photoredox cycling. **Wet-dry cycles** in these settings overcome the thermodynamic barrier to condensation reactions by concentrating reactants and removing water. Damer and Deamer (*Astrobiology*, 2020) proposed volcanic hot spring pools specifically, where cycling between hydrated, gel, and dehydrated phases drives lipid-encapsulated polymer formation. Field experiments at Mutnovsky volcano (Kamchatka) demonstrated vesicle self-assembly within minutes of introducing prebiotic reagents into a boiling spring.

**Impact crater hydrothermal systems** offer a third setting, sustaining temperatures of 50–120°C for **>50,000 years** in 23-km craters and up to 10⁶ years for larger structures. These sequestered crater lakes solve the concentration problem of open-ocean scenarios and provide diverse mineral catalysts including illite, shocked glass, and clays.

Key mineral catalysts span all environments. **Iron-sulfur minerals** (greigite Fe₃S₄, pyrite FeS₂, mackinawite FeS, magnetite Fe₃O₄) catalyze CO₂ reduction and contain Fe₄S₄ cubane structures directly analogous to modern ferredoxin cofactors. **Montmorillonite clay** catalyzes RNA oligomerization to **50 nucleotides** from activated monomers (Ferris & Ertem, *Science*, 1992; Ferris et al., *Nature*, 1996), with rate constants **100–1,000× greater** than hydrolysis rates. **TiO₂** photocatalyzes nitrogen fixation (N₂ → NH₃) and CO₂ reduction under UV illumination on early Earth mineral surfaces.

## The integration challenge and whether ATP came first

Despite remarkable progress on individual steps, **no single continuous pathway from simple precursors to ATP under one geochemical setting has been demonstrated**. This "integration challenge" is the field's most formidable open problem. Walton, Rimmer, and Shorttle (2022) quantified it as the "arithmetic demon" problem: for 30 sequential reactions at realistic yields, product concentrations drop exponentially to negligible levels. The individual steps operate under incompatible conditions — HCN oligomerization requires >0.01 M HCN at pH ~9; formose chemistry needs alkaline Ca(OH)₂; phosphorylation often requires acidic pH or anhydrous conditions; UV photochemistry demands surface exposure while many intermediates are UV-labile.

Several specific bottlenecks persist:

- **The ribose problem** remains only partially solved. Borate stabilization extends ribose's lifetime dramatically, but the formose reaction itself may not produce linear ribose under mild conditions (Krishnamurthy et al., 2025). The Sutherland bypass circumvents this entirely by never forming free ribose.
- **Chirality** remains unexplained: prebiotic reactions produce racemic mixtures, but life uses exclusively D-ribose. Template-directed RNA copying is severely inhibited by wrong-enantiomer incorporation.
- **ATP stability under prebiotic conditions** is limited: the hydrolysis half-life is ~2 years at 25°C and pH 8, but drops to **2.4 minutes at 120°C and pH 3**. Mg²⁺ provides partial stabilization (~50% reduction in hydrolysis rate at 120°C). This rules out black smoker vents but permits alkaline vent and surface pool temperatures.
- **Whether ATP was the original energy currency** is actively debated. De Duve's thioester world hypothesis (1991, 1995) proposes that energy-rich thioesters preceded phosphate-based metabolism. Lane and Martin argue that **acetyl phosphate** (ΔG°′ of hydrolysis = −43 kJ/mol, higher than ATP's −31 kJ/mol) served as a proto-ATP. The Pinna et al. (2022) finding that acetyl phosphate uniquely and selectively converts ADP to ATP with Fe³⁺ — and specifically phosphorylates only the adenine nucleotide, not guanine, cytidine, or uridine — suggests that ATP's dominance may reflect intrinsic prebiotic chemistry rather than a frozen accident. Goldford et al. (*Cell*, 2017) computationally demonstrated that thioester-based metabolic networks could sustain significant biosynthetic capability without phosphate.

A plausible integrated scenario is emerging, albeit speculatively. **Alkaline hydrothermal vents** provide CO₂ reduction to formate, formaldehyde, and acetate via iron-sulfur catalysis, generating acetyl phosphate as a proto-energy currency. **Surface/evaporitic settings** receiving vent-derived and atmospheric feedstocks (HCN, formaldehyde, H₂S) drive photochemical nucleotide assembly through Sutherland-type or Carell-type pathways. **Borate-containing evaporites** stabilize ribose and direct 5′-phosphorylation. Wet-dry cycling drives condensation and polymerization. Finally, **acetyl phosphate with Fe³⁺** converts ADP to ATP, bootstrapping the modern energy currency. This multi-environment scenario requires transport between settings — a requirement that some researchers view as a feature (providing sequential processing) and others view as implausible.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of ATP has progressed from a seemingly intractable problem to one where every individual bond-forming step has at least one experimentally validated route. The Carell FaPy pathway achieves **60% purine nucleoside yields** with correct N9 regiochemistry. The Sutherland cyanosulfidic network unifies nucleotide, amino acid, and lipid precursor synthesis from HCN and H₂S. Borate minerals solve both ribose stabilization and 5′-regioselective phosphorylation. And the Pinna et al. discovery that acetyl phosphate with Fe³⁺ uniquely produces ATP from ADP at **15–20% yield** — selectively for adenine over all other bases — provides a compelling terminal step. The field's frontier has shifted from demonstrating individual reactions to understanding how geochemistry connects them. Whether this connection occurs through multi-environment transport, through undiscovered one-pot chemistries, or through a simpler ancestor molecule that preceded ATP altogether remains the defining question for the next decade of origin-of-life research.
