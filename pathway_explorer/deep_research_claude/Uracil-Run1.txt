# Prebiotic synthesis of uracil: routes, evidence, and unsolved puzzles

**Uracil — the simplest pyrimidine in RNA — can form through at least eight distinct prebiotic pathways, yet no single route convincingly produces it from scratch under realistic early-Earth conditions.** The most efficient laboratory synthesis remains the Robertson–Miller evaporating-lagoon route (cyanoacetaldehyde + concentrated urea → **30–50% cytosine**, which hydrolyzes to uracil), while the most prebiotically compelling approach is the Powner–Sutherland bypass strategy that builds pyrimidine nucleotides without ever producing free uracil. Confirmation of uracil at **6–32 ppb in pristine Ryugu asteroid samples** (Oba et al., *Nature Communications*, 2023) — with all five canonical nucleobases now identified in Ryugu (Koga et al., *Nature Astronomy*, 2026) — proves that abiotic uracil synthesis occurs in extraterrestrial environments. Terrestrial and extraterrestrial sources likely both contributed to the prebiotic nucleobase inventory, but bridging the gap from free uracil to functional uridine nucleotides remains one of origin-of-life research's hardest problems.

---

## The cyanoacetylene highway and its branching routes

The dominant family of prebiotic uracil syntheses passes through **cyanoacetylene (HC₃N)**, a C₃N building block produced at up to **8.4% relative to HCN** in spark discharges through CH₄/N₂ atmospheres (Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel, *Science*, 1966). Cyanoacetylene is also one of the most abundant nitrogen-bearing molecules in the interstellar medium and Titan's atmosphere, making it a cosmochemically robust starting material.

The classic Ferris–Sanchez–Orgel pathway (1968, *Journal of Molecular Biology*) reacts HC₃N with cyanate (NCO⁻) to form *trans*-cyanovinylurea, which cyclizes to **cytosine at ~6–19% yield** (0.025 M HC₃N + 0.05 M cyanate, 100°C, 24 hours). Cytosine then undergoes hydrolytic deamination to uracil. The limitation is cyanate's rapid hydrolysis to CO₂ and NH₃, demanding implausibly high steady-state concentrations (>0.1 M).

Robertson and Miller (1995, *Nature*) solved the concentration problem for one reactant by substituting **concentrated urea** — geochemically plausible in evaporating lagoons — for cyanate. Cyanoacetaldehyde (the hydrolysis product of HC₃N, ~1,000× more stable in water) reacts with saturated urea to give cytosine in **30–50% yield**, the highest reported for any prebiotic pyrimidine synthesis. From dilute starting conditions (0.001 M cyanoacetaldehyde + 0.01 M urea), dry-down produces 0.23% cytosine and 0.08% uracil directly. Even the cyanoacetaldehyde dimer — an oligomeric storage form — reacts efficiently: 12.8% cytosine from 0.001 M dimer + 2 M urea at 100°C over three days (Nelson et al., *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2001).

A parallel branch uses **guanidine** instead of urea: cyanoacetaldehyde + guanidine yields 2,4-diaminopyrimidine in **40–85% yield** under drying conditions (Ferris et al., *Journal of Molecular Evolution*, 1974), which hydrolyzes stepwise to cytosine, isocytosine, and uracil. Thiourea substitution produces 2-thiocytosine en route to uracil (Robertson et al., *Journal of Molecular Evolution*, 1996).

## Formamide, impacts, and radical chemistry

Formamide (NH₂CHO), the simplest amide and the hydrolysis product of HCN, serves as both a solvent and a one-carbon building block. Though its steady-state oceanic concentration was negligible (~**10⁻⁹ M** at 0°C, pH 8), its high boiling point (210°C) permits concentration by evaporation in volcanic fumarolic settings. Thermophoresis in hydrothermal rock pores can accumulate formamide from dilute lake concentrations to reactive levels in **45–90 days** (Niether et al., *PNAS*, 2016).

The Saladino–Di Mauro program (2001–2019) systematically demonstrated that heating formamide at **140–160°C** with mineral catalysts produces nucleobases, though pyrimidine yields depend critically on the catalyst. **Montmorillonite** yields hypoxanthine and uracil (Saladino et al., *ChemBioChem*, 2004). **TiO₂ (anatase)** uniquely produces thymine and 5-hydroxymethyluracil via photocatalytic formaldehyde generation (Saladino et al., *ChemBioChem*, 2003; Senanayake & Idriss, *PNAS*, 2006). Iron-sulfur minerals catalyze purine and pyrimidinone formation but also accelerate RNA degradation — covellite gives an RNA half-life of just **1.8 minutes** (Saladino et al., *JACS*, 2008). Borate minerals modulate product distributions, broadening the nucleobase spectrum (Saladino et al., *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2011).

High-energy formamide chemistry provides a distinct mechanism. Ferus et al. (2015, *PNAS*) used laser-induced plasma to simulate meteorite impacts, showing that formamide dissociates into **CN· and NH· radicals** that recombine to form all four canonical RNA nucleobases in a single experiment. Saladino et al. (2015, *PNAS*) extended this with 170 MeV proton irradiation of formamide in the presence of powdered meteorites at 273 K, producing not only nucleobases but remarkably four nucleosides — cytidine, uridine, adenosine, and thymidine — without separate sugar or base synthesis steps.

## Key intermediates and the DAMN branch point

Several molecules serve as critical nodes in the prebiotic synthesis network, connecting simple feedstocks (HCN, H₂O, NH₃, CH₄) to uracil.

**Diaminomaleonitrile (DAMN)**, the HCN tetramer (C₄H₄N₄), forms readily from 0.1–1.0 M HCN at pH ~9 and room temperature. It represents a **major branch point**: UV photoisomerization converts DAMN to 4-aminoimidazole-5-carbonitrile (AICN), which leads to adenine via HCN addition (~18% yield when heated with ammonium formate at 110°C; Hill & Orgel, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2002). Under photothermal conditions, DAMN participates in multicomponent reactions yielding pyrimidine derivatives (Bizzarri et al., *European Journal of Organic Chemistry*, 2022). Energy source determines product selectivity: thermal conditions favor imidazoles, photochemical conditions favor pyrimidines, and combined photothermal conditions favor purines.

**Cytosine** is the most common kinetic precursor to uracil but is itself thermodynamically unstable. Its deamination half-life ranges from **~17,000 years at 0°C** to **19 days at 100°C** to seconds at 350°C (Levy & Miller, *PNAS*, 1998; Wolfenden et al., *PNAS*, 2016). Robert Shapiro's influential critique (*PNAS*, 1999) argued that cytosine was never detected in meteorites or spark discharge experiments, requires implausibly concentrated reactants, and deaminates faster than plausible synthesis rates. Counter-arguments from the Miller group showed that synthesis proceeds even at 4°C and −20°C more efficiently than Arrhenius extrapolation predicts. The Murchison meteorite contains uracil but not cytosine — consistent with cytosine's cosmochemical instability and its role as a transient intermediate.

**2-Aminooxazole**, formed from glycolaldehyde and cyanamide with phosphate catalysis, is the gateway intermediate in the Powner–Sutherland pathway. It reacts with glyceraldehyde to form pentose aminooxazolines (ribose aminooxazoline predominates at pH 7 with phosphate buffer), which subsequently react with cyanoacetylene to form anhydronucleosides. Ribose aminooxazoline crystallizes as a conglomerate with spontaneous enantiomeric enrichment — an elegant built-in chiral purification mechanism. However, 2-aminooxazole has a UV photodestruction half-life of only **6.9 hours** at 0.1 mM, potentially limiting its accumulation under the intense UV flux of the early Earth (Todd et al., *Chemical Communications*, 2019).

## Environments that favor uracil accumulation

Five geochemical settings have demonstrated relevance to prebiotic uracil chemistry, each offering distinct advantages and limitations.

**Evaporitic pools and warm little ponds** provide the most experimentally productive conditions for free uracil synthesis. Wet-dry cycling concentrates dilute reactants, drives condensation reactions, and can achieve the saturated urea conditions (~120 molal) needed for the Robertson–Miller synthesis. Pearce and Pudritz (2017, *PNAS*) modeled nucleobase evolution in warm little ponds, concluding that RNA polymers must have formed within a few years of meteorite deposition. Ross and Deamer showed wet-dry cycles produce oligonucleotides up to 53 nucleotides from uridine monophosphate without chemical activation agents (*PNAS*, 2024). The β-alanine + urea pathway (Chittenden & Schwartz, *Nature*, 1976) operates entirely within this paradigm: evaporation and mild heating with clay minerals yield dihydrouracil, which UV irradiation converts to uracil.

**Ice eutectic environments** achieve high effective concentrations through a different mechanism — freezing excludes solutes into thin liquid films between ice crystals. Miyakawa, Cleaves, and Miller (2002, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*) demonstrated that dilute ammonium cyanide solutions produce both pyrimidines and purines upon eutectic freezing, calling this "the most plausible demonstrated mechanism" for HCN polymerization. Menor-Salván et al. (2009, *Chemistry – A European Journal*) showed frozen urea solutions under reductive atmosphere with spark discharge yield cytosine, uracil, 2,4-diaminopyrimidine, and adenine — while room-temperature controls produced **no pyrimidines at all**, only hydantoins and tholins.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (Lost City type, 40–90°C, pH 9–11) offer microporous compartments, continuous H₂/CO₂ supply from serpentinization, and Fe(Ni)S mineral catalysts structurally analogous to modern enzyme cofactors. Thermophoresis across microporous structures can concentrate molecules up to **~5,000-fold** (Herschy et al., *Journal of Molecular Evolution*, 2014). A 2022 study in *Scientific Reports* showed that cyanide polymerization under simulated alkaline hydrothermal conditions preferentially generates pyrimidines (including uracil, 5-aminouracil, 6-aminouracil, and orotic acid) over triazines in subaerial settings (80°C, 1 bar). Black smoker vents (>300°C) are generally hostile — uracil decomposes within minutes to hours above 200°C.

**Volcanic and fumarolic settings** provide the heat (95–160°C) and mineral surfaces needed for formamide chemistry. Saladino's group tested formamide with thermal waters and meteorites at the Solfatara volcano in the Phlegrean Fields, achieving one-pot synthesis of all natural nucleobases, amino acids, and carboxylic acids spanning most of the Krebs cycle.

## Mineral catalysts shape product distributions

The choice of mineral catalyst dramatically influences which nucleobases emerge from prebiotic reactions.

**Montmorillonite**, a 2:1 layered aluminosilicate with ~250 m²/g surface area, plays dual roles. It catalyzes uracil formation from formamide (Saladino et al., 2004) and from β-alanine + urea (Schwartz & Chittenden, 1977), and it catalyzes oligomerization of activated uridine nucleotides. Ferris's group demonstrated montmorillonite-catalyzed oligonucleotide synthesis up to **40–50 mers for adenosine** and **25–30 mers for uridine** phosphorimidazolides, with rate constants **100–1,000 times greater** than hydrolysis (Ferris, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2002). Selectivity for 3'→5' linkages reaches 10.3:1 over 2'→5'.

**Borate minerals** do not catalyze uracil synthesis directly but solve a downstream problem: ribose stabilization. Ricardo, Carrigan, Olcott, and Benner (2004, *Science*) showed that borate complexation at 2',3'-cis-diols prevents ribose degradation via enediol rearrangements. Furukawa et al. (2013, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*) demonstrated that ribose stability increases preferentially over other aldopentoses with increasing borate concentration. Most recently, Hirakawa et al. (2022, *Scientific Reports*) achieved one-pot borate-guided phosphorylation of ribose to ribose 5'-phosphate — the sugar-phosphate backbone component of uridine monophosphate.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** (pyrite, pyrrhotite, greigite) catalyze formamide condensation to nucleobases but also promote RNA degradation — a double-edged sword for prebiotic scenarios. **TiO₂**, abundant in volcanic ash and beach sands (~0.5 wt% of Earth's crust), uniquely produces thymine and 5-hydroxymethyluracil through photocatalytic mechanisms active under UV wavelengths <380 nm. **Phosphate minerals** are essential for the final nucleotide assembly step: schreibersite [(Fe,Ni)₃P] from meteorites phosphorylates uridine at 60–80°C with yields <6% (Gull et al., *Scientific Reports*, 2015), while diamidophosphate achieves ~**89% conversion** of uridine to 2',3'-cyclic UMP in paste conditions over 18 days (Gibard et al., *Nature Chemistry*, 2018).

## UV photochemistry and interstellar ice synthesis

Ultraviolet radiation drives uracil synthesis in both terrestrial and extraterrestrial settings.

Nuevo et al. (2009, *Astrobiology*) first demonstrated uracil formation from UV irradiation of pyrimidine in H₂O ice at **20–30 K** using a Lyman-α source (~2 × 10¹⁵ photons cm⁻² s⁻¹, 22–23 hours). Oba et al. (2019, *Nature Communications*) achieved simultaneous production of all six nucleobases (cytosine, uracil, thymine, adenine, xanthine, hypoxanthine) from photolysis of a simple ice mixture (H₂O:CO:NH₃:CH₃OH = 5:2:2:2) at **10 K**. Materese et al. (2013, *Astrobiology*) found that uracil production far exceeds thymine in ice photochemistry — a result consistent with RNA preceding DNA in evolutionary history.

On early Earth, UV photochemistry serves multiple functions in uracil-related chemistry. In the Powner–Sutherland pathway, **254 nm irradiation** drives photoanomerization of α-cytidine-2'-phosphate and partial C→U deamination to generate β-uridine-2',3'-cyclic phosphate. Xu et al. (2017, *Nature Chemistry*) showed that α-2-thioribocytidine undergoes extraordinarily efficient UV photoanomerization to the β-anomer. UV also converts dihydrouracil to uracil in the Chittenden–Schwartz surface-pool pathway. However, UV simultaneously destroys nucleobases — creating a kinetic race between synthesis and degradation that depends critically on self-shielding, mineral protection, and cycling between exposed and protected environments.

## The Powner–Sutherland revolution and recent unified syntheses

The 2009 *Nature* paper by Powner, Gerland, and Sutherland fundamentally reframed the problem. Rather than separately synthesizing uracil and ribose and then coupling them — a reaction with an estimated dissociation constant of **~700 M** for pyrimidine glycosylation (Hud et al., *Nature Chemistry*, 2016) — they built the nucleotide from fragments that simultaneously construct base and sugar. The sequence proceeds: glycolaldehyde + cyanamide → 2-aminooxazole → + glyceraldehyde → ribose aminooxazoline → + cyanoacetylene → anhydrocytidine → phosphorylation → **β-ribocytidine-2',3'-cyclic phosphate** → UV → **β-ribouridine-2',3'-cyclic phosphate**. Phosphate participates throughout as a general acid/base catalyst, nucleophilic catalyst, pH buffer, and chemical buffer.

Becker, Schneider, and Carell (2019, *Science*) achieved a **unified synthesis of both pyrimidine and purine ribonucleotides** through wet-dry cycles using hydroxylamine, cyanoacetylene, urea, CaCO₃, and Zn²⁺/Fe²⁺ ions — addressing the longstanding limitation that purine and pyrimidine routes required incompatible conditions. Feldmann and Carell (2023, *ChemistryEurope*) merged the Sutherland–Powner and Carell pathways into a single concept, eliminating the problematic late-stage addition of reactive cyanoacetylene. Xu et al. (2020, *Nature*) demonstrated selective co-production of RNA pyrimidine nucleosides (C, U) and DNA purine nucleosides (dA, dI) from shared intermediates — hinting at the simultaneous emergence of RNA and DNA building blocks.

## From asteroid to laboratory: extraterrestrial uracil is real

The detection of uracil in pristine, uncontaminated samples settles a decades-old debate about meteoritic contamination. Oba et al. (2023, *Nature Communications*) extracted Ryugu particles returned by Hayabusa2 in hot water, identifying uracil at **6–32 ppb** alongside nicotinic acid (49–99 ppb), amino acids, amines, and carboxylic acids. Differences between touchdown sites (A0106 vs. C0107) likely reflect differential UV/cosmic-ray exposure on the asteroid surface.

Koga et al. (2026, *Nature Astronomy*) extended this to detect **all five canonical nucleobases** in Ryugu samples, with isotopic signatures (δ¹³C ≈ +25.5‰ to +33.9‰; δ¹⁵N ≈ +45.1‰ to +59.8‰) confirming extraterrestrial origin. A striking finding: **ammonia concentration negatively correlates with purine-to-pyrimidine ratio** across Ryugu, Murchison, Bennu, and Orgueil samples, suggesting shared formation pathways modulated by parent-body aqueous alteration. Murchison is enriched in purines; Bennu and Orgueil are enriched in pyrimidines — consistent with variable degrees of aqueous processing converting cytosine to uracil at different rates.

## Persistent challenges and the road ahead

Several fundamental problems remain open. The **concentration problem** — achieving reactive concentrations of HCN (~0.01–0.1 M needed) from a prebiotic ocean estimated at ~**2 × 10⁻⁶ M HCN at 0°C** — has multiple partial solutions (eutectic freezing, wet-dry cycling, aerosol chemistry, chemical capture via crystalline DCI adducts; Green et al., *JACS*, 2022) but no demonstrated mechanism operating at geological scale. The **asphalt problem** (Benner, *Accounts of Chemical Research*, 2012) — the tendency of complex prebiotic mixtures to devolve into intractable tars rather than functional molecules — remains genuinely unresolved and may represent the deepest challenge to understanding life's chemical origins.

Uracil's own **stability** is relatively favorable: half-life of **~10⁶ years at 0°C** and **12 years at 100°C** (Levy & Miller, *PNAS*, 1998), making it the most thermally stable pyrimidine. But the **glycosylation problem** persists for any pathway requiring free-base coupling: direct uracil-ribose glycosylation yields <1% even under forcing conditions (Kim & Benner, *Astrobiology*, 2019). Recent progress includes metal-cation/clay-catalyzed direct ribosylation (Zhang et al., *Life*, 2021) and microdroplet synthesis achieving **2.5% uridine yield** in ~50 μs flight time (Nam et al., *PNAS*, 2018), but these approaches have not been integrated into continuous prebiotic scenarios.

Phosphorylation has seen dramatic advances. Walton et al. (2022, *Nature Communications*) overturned the long-held view that phosphate was scarce on early Earth, showing Fe²⁺ actually **increases phosphate mineral solubility** in anoxic systems by 10³–10⁴ fold. Diamidophosphate achieves 89% conversion of uridine to cyclic UMP (Gibard et al., *Nature Chemistry*, 2018), and aerosol droplets accelerate the same reaction >100-fold to **6.5–10% in under one hour** (Castañeda et al., *Scientific Reports*, 2019).

The **atmosphere composition debate** (weakly reducing N₂/CO₂/H₂O versus strongly reducing CH₄/NH₃/H₂) directly affects HCN production rates and thus pyrimidine synthesis efficiency. Current consensus favors a mildly reducing atmosphere, but transient reducing episodes from large impacts could have provided pulses of reactive feedstocks. Ferus et al. (2017, *PNAS*) showed that even modestly reducing mixtures (NH₃ + CO + H₂O) produce all RNA nucleobases via discharge, with formamide as the critical reactive intermediate.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of uracil is not a single-pathway problem but a network problem. At least eight distinct routes produce uracil or its immediate precursors, operating in environments ranging from **10 K interstellar ices** to **160°C volcanic fumaroles**. Three insights have reshaped the field since 2009. First, Sutherland's bypass strategy eliminated the need to solve the intractable free-base glycosylation problem by building sugar and base simultaneously. Second, asteroid sample return missions proved that extraterrestrial environments deliver uracil to planetary surfaces — **6–32 ppb in Ryugu**, with all five canonical bases now confirmed. Third, the systems chemistry paradigm revealed that uracil synthesis cannot be understood in isolation: it is embedded in reaction networks that simultaneously generate amino acid, lipid, and sugar precursors from shared feedstocks like HCN and H₂S.

The central unresolved question is not whether uracil can form prebiotically — it clearly can, through multiple routes — but whether any geologically realistic scenario can produce, concentrate, stabilize, and incorporate uracil (or its nucleotide) into functional RNA polymers without the sequential human interventions that laboratory demonstrations still require. Bridging this gap between demonstrated chemistry and autonomous, self-sustaining prebiotic processes remains the defining challenge of origin-of-life research.
