# Prebiotic synthesis of ribose: pathways, evidence, and unresolved challenges

**Ribose remains one of the most chemically elusive yet biologically essential molecules in origin-of-life research.** No single prebiotic pathway produces ribose selectively under geologically plausible conditions — the central bottleneck in explaining RNA's emergence. Six major synthesis routes have been proposed since 1861, each with distinct advantages and critical limitations: the formose reaction, borate-stabilized formose chemistry, cyanosulfidic (Powner–Sutherland) pathways, photochemical reductive homologation, the Eschenmoser glyoxylate scenario, and phosphate-directed aldomerization. Recent breakthroughs (2020–2026) in chiral symmetry-breaking on magnetite, selective ribose enrichment on metal-doped clays, extraterrestrial ribose detection in asteroid Bennu samples, and systems chemistry network approaches have substantially advanced the field. This report synthesizes the complete literature landscape to support synthesis network design for the GenesisAgents platform.

## 1. Known prebiotic synthesis routes for ribose

### The formose reaction: autocatalytic but uncontrollable

The oldest and most-studied route to prebiotic sugars originated with Aleksandr Butlerow's 1861 observation that formaldehyde treated with Ca(OH)₂ produces a "sugar-like substance" (Butlerow, *C. R. Acad. Sci.* 53, 145–147, 1861). Ronald Breslow elucidated its autocatalytic mechanism in 1959 (*Tetrahedron Letters* 1(21), 22–26), showing that the core cycle proceeds through aldol condensations and retro-aldol fragmentations. The reaction initiates slowly: two formaldehyde molecules dimerize to glycolaldehyde (the rate-limiting step), after which the cycle self-amplifies. Even trace glycolaldehyde (**as low as 3 ppm**) can initiate the autocatalytic phase.

The Breslow cycle proceeds: glycolaldehyde (C2) + HCHO → glyceraldehyde (C3) → isomerization to dihydroxyacetone (C3) → + HCHO to tetrulose (C4) → isomerization to aldotetrose → retro-aldol to 2 × glycolaldehyde (autocatalytic regeneration). Pentoses form through C2 + C3 or C3 + C2 aldol additions: dihydroxyacetone + glycolaldehyde → ribulose (C5), which isomerizes to ribose. Appayee and Breslow (2014, *JACS* 136, 3720–3723) updated the mechanism, showing that aldose–ketose isomerization proceeds via hydride shifts rather than enediolate intermediates, with Ca²⁺ chelation promoting the process.

The formose reaction's fundamental limitation is its lack of selectivity. **Ribose constitutes less than 1% of total sugar products** (Shapiro, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 18, 71–85, 1988). Decker, Schweer, and Pohlmann (1982, *J. Chromatogr.* 244, 281) identified over **30 distinct compounds** by GC-MS, including straight-chain and branched carbohydrates from C2 to C7, organic acids, and degradation products. The Cannizzaro disproportionation of formaldehyde to methanol and formate competes with productive aldol chemistry, and under standard conditions (formaldehyde ≥0.15 M, Ca(OH)₂, 40–65°C, pH 11–12), products rapidly degrade to intractable polymeric tar — the so-called "asphalt problem."

### Borate-stabilized synthesis solves the degradation problem

Ricardo, Carrigan, Olcott, and Benner published a landmark paper in 2004 showing that **borate minerals stabilize ribose** (*Science* 303, 196). Their key insight was that borate (B(OH)₄⁻) forms stable cyclic diesters with *cis*-1,2-diol groups present in the cyclic furanose forms of pentoses. Glyceraldehyde — the first formose intermediate bearing a 1,2-diol — forms an anionic borate complex that suppresses enolization (preventing it from acting as a nucleophile) while remaining reactive as an electrophile. Pentose–borate complexes lack carbonyl groups entirely, blocking the enolization and browning reactions that produce tar.

Without borate, ribose decomposes within **minutes** under alkaline conditions. With borate, ribose remains **stable for days at room temperature**. The minerals tested included **colemanite** (Ca₂B₆O₁₁·5H₂O), **kernite** (Na₂B₄O₇·4H₂O), and **ulexite** (NaCaB₅O₉·8H₂O). Furukawa, Horiuchi, and Kakegawa (2013, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 43, 353–361) demonstrated that borate **selectively increases ribose stability more than other aldopentoses** — a critical finding, since ribose is normally the least stable aldopentose. Kim, Ricardo, and Benner (2011, *JACS* 133, 9457–9468) showed that borate, molybdate, and calcium minerals together guide pentose formation from formaldehyde and glycolaldehyde in "hands-off" experiments. Borate reduces maximum sugar yield but dramatically improves persistence, and Takahashi, Kim, Benner, Kakegawa, and Furukawa (2026, *Astrobiology*) confirmed that borate-rich environments show higher residual ribose accumulation over extended timescales.

### Cyanosulfidic chemistry bypasses free ribose entirely

The most conceptually transformative advance came from Powner, Gerland, and Sutherland (2009, *Nature* 459, 239–242), who demonstrated that activated pyrimidine ribonucleotides can be synthesized under prebiotically plausible conditions **without ever producing free ribose**. Their "systems chemistry" approach builds the sugar and base components simultaneously through shared intermediates.

The pathway begins with glycolaldehyde + cyanamide → **2-aminooxazole**, catalyzed by inorganic phosphate acting as pH buffer and general acid-base catalyst. The 2-aminooxazole then reacts with glyceraldehyde to produce pentose aminooxazolines — a reaction that is **essentially quantitative and highly stereoselective for ribose and arabinose aminooxazolines** (Anastasi, Crowe, Powner, and Sutherland, 2006, *Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.* 45, 6176–6179). Arabinose aminooxazoline reacts with cyanoacetylene to form an anhydronucleoside (2,2′-anhydrocytidine), which undergoes regioselective phosphorylation to yield β-ribocytidine-2′,3′-cyclic phosphate. UV irradiation partially converts cytidine to uridine nucleotides. All starting materials — HCN, cyanamide, glycolaldehyde, glyceraldehyde, cyanoacetylene, and phosphate — are plausible prebiotic feedstocks.

Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy, and Sutherland (2015, *Nature Chemistry* 7, 301–307) extended this framework dramatically, showing that RNA, protein, and lipid precursors all derive from a common **cyanosulfidic protometabolism** — HCN and H₂S as primary feedstocks, with copper photoredox cycling and phosphate catalysis linking all three subsystems. Xu et al. (2017, *Nature Chemistry* 9, 303–309) showed that photoanomerization of α-2-thioribocytidine is "extraordinarily efficient," enabling a route through ribose aminooxazoline to β-ribonucleosides.

### Photochemical routes generate the essential C2 and C3 building blocks

Ritson and Sutherland (2012, *Nature Chemistry* 4, 895–899) demonstrated UV-driven synthesis of glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde from HCN via a Kiliani–Fischer-type reductive homologation. The mechanism proceeds through photoexcitation of cyanocuprate complexes (CuCN/Cu(CN)₃²⁻) at **254 nm**, generating hydrated electrons that reduce HCN to formaldehyde imine (which hydrolyzes to formaldehyde), then reduce glycolonitrile (the cyanohydrin of formaldehyde) to glycolaldehyde, and subsequently glyceraldehyde.

In their 2013 follow-up (*Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.* 52, 5845–5847), replacing HCN with **H₂S as the stoichiometric reductant** avoided problematic cyanate formation and achieved **~42% glycolaldehyde yield** from glycolonitrile after 4 hours of UV irradiation, and **~17% glyceraldehyde yield** from the glycolaldehyde cyanohydrin after 2 hours. Xu, Ritson, Ranjan, Todd, Sasselov, and Sutherland (2018, *Chem. Commun.* 54, 5566–5569) improved the system further using sulfite (SO₃²⁻) plus ferrocyanide [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻, achieving **68% total reduced products** (glyceronitrile 40% + glycolaldehyde sulfite adduct 28%) after just 1 hour of irradiation — versus only 20% without ferrocyanide. Ritson and Sutherland (2023, *Nature Chemistry* 15, 1470–1477) demonstrated thiophosphate as a reductant enabling one-pot synthesis of ribo-aminooxazoline from HCN, with glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde produced in approximately equimolar amounts (~13–19% yields).

### The glyoxylate scenario offers remarkably clean chemistry

Eschenmoser proposed the "glyoxylate scenario" (2007, *Tetrahedron* 63, 12821–12844) as a fundamentally different approach, using glyoxylate (a "carboxylated formaldehyde" equivalent) instead of formaldehyde. Glyoxylate dimerizes to **dihydroxyfumaric acid (DHF)**, which serves as the key reactive intermediate. DHF undergoes aldol condensation with aldehydes followed by carbonyl migration and decarboxylation to produce sugars.

Sagi, Punna, Hu, Meher, and Krishnamurthy (2012, *JACS* 134, 3577–3589) demonstrated that DHF + glyceraldehyde produces **~60% yield of ketosugars** (xylulose + ribulose) with practically no side products — in stark contrast to the formose reaction's intractable mixtures. The reaction exclusively produces ketoses rather than aldoses, representing fundamentally different selectivity. A critical limitation remains: **no validated prebiotic pathway to DHF exists**, and converting the ketose products (ribulose) to the aldose ribose requires isomerization not yet demonstrated prebiotically.

### Phosphate-directed aldomerization channels selectivity toward ribose bisphosphate

Müller, Eschenmoser, and colleagues (*Helv. Chim. Acta* 1990, 73, 1410–1468) showed that **glycolaldehyde phosphate** aldomerization produces pentose-2,4-diphosphates with remarkable selectivity — ribose-2,4-bisphosphate emerged as a main product. Phosphate groups decrease enolization rates, suppressing the nucleophilic reactivity that drives the formose reaction's scrambling. Krishnamurthy, Arrhenius, and Eschenmoser (1999, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*) reported that mangalite double-layered hydroxide mineral afforded **25% yield pentose-2,4-bisphosphates**, with ribose-2,4-bisphosphate constituting **48% of the pentose fraction**.

## 2. Key intermediates and branch-point molecules

### The C1–C3 building blocks that underpin all pathways

**Formaldehyde (HCHO)** is the primordial C1 building block. It was the first organic molecule detected in interstellar space (1969), is ubiquitous in molecular clouds and comets, and can be produced photochemically from CO₂ + H₂O in a primitive atmosphere (Pinto, Gladstone, and Yung, *Science* 1980, 210, 183–185). The equilibrium constant for glycolonitrile formation from HCN + HCHO is remarkably large (K_eq = **4.6 × 10⁵ M⁻¹**), meaning formaldehyde is efficiently scavenged by HCN in mixed prebiotic environments.

**Glycolaldehyde (HOCH₂CHO)**, the simplest sugar, is the most critical C2 branch-point intermediate. It leads to sugars (via aldol with formaldehyde → glyceraldehyde → pentoses), nucleotide precursors (via condensation with cyanamide → 2-aminooxazole → aminooxazolines), and amino acids (via Strecker-type reactions). Multiple prebiotic formation routes exist: formose dimerization of formaldehyde, UV photoreduction of HCN (Ritson and Sutherland pathway), olivine-catalyzed synthesis from formaldehyde in aqueous environments (*Earth Planet. Sci. Lett.* 2024), and interstellar ice photolysis. Glycolaldehyde has been detected in interstellar space by Hollis et al. (*Astrophys. J.* 2004, 613, L45–L48).

**Glyceraldehyde (HOCH₂CH(OH)CHO)**, the simplest aldotriose, forms via aldol addition of glycolaldehyde + formaldehyde. It is the key C3 branch point: it can isomerize to dihydroxyacetone (thermodynamically favored), undergo aldol addition with glycolaldehyde to produce pentoses (including ribose ~20% and arabinose ~14%; Kofoed et al. 2005), or react with 2-aminooxazole to produce aminooxazolines (essentially quantitative). Islam, Bučar, and Powner (2017, *Nature Chemistry* 9, 584–589) showed that **2-aminothiazole** selectively crystallizes the aminal of glyceraldehyde, preventing its thermodynamically favored isomerization to dihydroxyacetone — solving a key kinetic control problem.

### Aminooxazolines: the intermediates that make cyanosulfidic chemistry work

**2-Aminooxazole (2AO)** forms from glycolaldehyde + cyanamide with phosphate catalysis. It is isosteric and isoelectronic with 2-aminoimidazole (2AI), which forms simultaneously at pH 7 in ~equimolar ratio (Li et al., *JACS* 2017). Crucially, 2AO is significantly more reactive with glyceraldehyde than 2AI, allowing selective channeling toward nucleotide synthesis.

The reaction of 2-aminooxazole with glyceraldehyde produces **pentose aminooxazolines** with high stereoselectivity for the ribo and arabino configurations. **Ribose aminooxazoline (RAO)** crystallizes spontaneously from reaction mixtures with enhanced enantiomeric excess — providing a mechanism for homochirality. RAO is at least **70 times more stable** than ribose–borate complexes (Powner, *Chem. Commun.* 2017), making it a far more robust carrier of ribose stereochemistry than free ribose itself. Fernández-García, Grefenstette, and Powner (2017, *Chem. Commun.* 53, 4919–4921) demonstrated an alternative route through glycidaldehyde (from acrolein oxidation, 90% yield) that produces aminooxazoline-5′-phosphate in **36% yield** with improved ribo/arabino selectivity of **2.8:1**.

### Sugar phosphates bridge sugar chemistry and nucleotide assembly

The "phosphate problem" — poor solubility and reactivity of apatite, the dominant terrestrial phosphate mineral — has been addressed through multiple strategies. Meteoritic **schreibersite [(Fe,Ni)₃P]** corrodes in water to produce phosphate, pyrophosphate (10–20% of total P oxyanions), and reactive reduced phosphorus species (Pasek et al., *Geochim. Cosmochim. Acta* 2007, 71, 1721). Gull et al. (2015, *Sci. Rep.* 5, 17198) achieved nucleoside phosphorylation in **<6% yield** from schreibersite analogues at 60–80°C, pH 8–10. A 2025 advance from Pasek (*Commun. Chem.* 8, 187) achieved nucleotide formation from nucleosides up to **65%** at 80°C using amidophosphite intermediates.

Hirakawa, Kakegawa, and Furukawa (2022, *Sci. Rep.* 12, 11828) demonstrated **borate-guided regioselective phosphorylation**: ribose + phosphate + urea + borate → ribose 5′-phosphate by simple thermal evaporation, with phosphorylation occurring preferentially on ribose compared to other aldopentoses, exclusively in the presence of borate. The borate complexes the 2′- and 3′-oxygens, directing phosphorylation to the desired 5′-position.

## 3. Environmental settings and their compatibility with ribose chemistry

### Alkaline hydrothermal vents provide reducing chemistry but threaten sugar stability

Black smokers (300–400°C, pH 2–3, 200–300 atm) are definitively too hot for sugar chemistry — ribose's half-life at 100°C is already only **73 minutes** (Larralde, Robertson, and Miller, 1995, *PNAS* 92, 8158–8160), and sugars would be destroyed within seconds at black smoker temperatures. Alkaline hydrothermal vents of the Lost City type (40–90°C, pH 9–11; Russell, 1988; Martin and Russell, 2003) offer a more moderate environment. Sharp pH gradients across thin Fe(Ni)S mineral barriers — equivalent in magnitude to the proton-motive force in modern cells — can drive CO₂ fixation (Sojo, Herschy, Whicher, Camprubí, and Lane, *Astrobiology* 2016, 16, 181–197). CaCO₃-based chemical gardens catalyze the formose reaction and produce glucose and ribose, with pH gradients allowing dynamic sugar accumulation in specific membrane layers (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 2020). However, Lane lab results showed only **~0.06% ribose** from formaldehyde under simulated alkaline vent conditions, and the alkaline pH accelerates ribose degradation.

### Volcanic hot springs and evaporitic pools emerge as the most favorable setting

The Damer and Deamer hot spring hypothesis (2020, *Astrobiology* 20, 429–452) proposes fluctuating volcanic hot spring pools as the site of protocell emergence. Three alternating phases — hydrated (free-floating protocells), moist/gel (condensation), and dehydrated (layered films enabling condensation chemistry) — drive polymer synthesis kinetically by removing water. Water temperatures of 60–90°C with cycling to ambient provide the energy for condensation while remaining below destructive temperatures. RNA-like polymers up to hundreds of nucleotides have been formed at 80°C through wet-dry cycling (*Nature Scientific Reports* 2022).

The **3.5 Ga Pilbara Craton** in Western Australia provides geological precedent: an anoxic hot spring setting with demonstrated capacity to mobilize and concentrate C, H, N, O, P, S, B, Zn, Mn, and K (Van Kranendonk et al., 2020). Critically, volcanic and evaporitic settings concentrate **borate** — tourmaline (boron-rich silicate) is found in >3.7 Ga metasediments at Isua, Greenland (Grew, Bada, and Hazen, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 2011, 41, 307–316). The combination of borate availability, wet-dry cycling, moderate temperature, mineral diversity, and UV exposure makes volcanic hot springs the most favorable single environment for prebiotic ribose chemistry.

### Ice eutectic environments stabilize and concentrate

Ice eutectic phases concentrate solutes by factors of **100–1,000×** in interstitial liquid channels (0.1–0.2 mm diameter). At 0°C, ribose's half-life extends to **44 years** — a 350,000-fold improvement over 100°C conditions. Attwater, Wochner, and Holliger (2013, *Nature Chemistry* 5, 1011–1018) demonstrated RNA polymerase ribozyme activity at temperatures as low as **−19°C**. Menor-Salván and Marín-Yaseli (2012, *Chem. Soc. Rev.* 41, 5404–5415) showed that ice-water matrices efficiently produce nucleobases at high rates while protecting labile products. Freeze-thaw cycling facilitates nucleotide activation, bridged dinucleotide synthesis, and template-directed primer extension in a single reaction mixture.

## 4. Mineral catalysts that guide ribose synthesis and stability

### Borate minerals are the single most important class for ribose selection

The mechanism of borate stabilization involves complexation with *cis*-1,2-diols on cyclic sugar forms. Šponer et al. (2008, *Chem. Eur. J.* 14, 9990–9998) used DFT calculations to show that the ribose–borate 2:1 complex is stabilized by a strong hydrogen bond between the ribose 3-OH and a borate oxygen, plus favorable solvation of the 5-CH₂OH group. Geologically, tourmaline-group minerals are documented at 3300–3450 Ma in the Barberton belt, South Africa, and at >3650 Ma in the Isua complex, Greenland. However, whether Hadean continental crust had differentiated sufficiently to concentrate boron remains debated (Grew, Bada, and Hazen, 2011). A 2026 review proposed the "Boron Theorem" and a **Boron Availability Index (BAI)** as a heuristic metric for assessing planetary habitability with respect to RNA world scenarios (*Frontiers* 2026).

### Oxometalate catalysts (molybdate, tungstate) direct pathway selectivity

**Molybdate (MoO₄²⁻)** catalyzes the Bílik reaction — rearrangement of branched carbohydrates to linear carbohydrates at near-neutral pH (Ziegler, Kim, and Benner, *Astrobiology* 2018, 18, 1159–1170). This prevents dead-end products from Cannizzaro reduction of branched heptoses. **Tungstate (WO₄²⁻)** selectively promotes ribose over xylose: Nishijima et al. (2026, *ChemSystemsChem*) showed that Na₂WO₄ catalysts **favor ribose formation**, whereas NaOH catalysts show the opposite trend. DFT calculations confirmed that WO₄²⁻ preferentially stabilizes the transition state leading to ribose due to interactions with its tetrahedral structure.

### Iron-sulfur minerals enable redox chemistry and surface catalysis

**Pyrite (FeS₂)** is the centerpiece of Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis (1988, *Microbiol. Rev.* 52, 452): the reaction FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂ provides **ΔG = −9.2 kcal/mol** of energy to drive carbon fixation. Huber and Wächtershäuser (1997, *Science* 276, 245–247) demonstrated (Fe,Ni)S-catalyzed synthesis of methyl mercaptan and acetic acid from CO. **Greigite (Fe₃S₄)**, an inverse thiospinel structurally resembling [4Fe-4S] protein clusters, facilitates CO₂ reduction in the alkaline vent model. **Mackinawite (FeS)**, a layered iron sulfide, is proposed to catalyze CO₂ reduction across nanocrystals via vectorial proton gradients in vent systems. Colón-Santos et al. (2019, *ChemSystemsChem*) showed that **chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂)** in the recursive formose reaction preferentially produces uracil and ribose.

### Montmorillonite catalyzes downstream RNA polymerization

Ferris and colleagues demonstrated that Na⁺-montmorillonite catalyzes formation of RNA oligomers containing **6–14 monomer units** from 5′-phosphorimidazolide mononucleotides (Ferris and Ertem, 1993, *JACS* 115, 12270–12275; Ferris, Hill, Liu, and Orgel, 1996, *Nature* 381, 59–61). Through daily "feeding" of activated monomer over 14 days, oligomers up to **40–50 mers** were achieved (Huang and Ferris, 2006, *JACS* 128, 8914–8919), with rate enhancement of **100–1,000× over hydrolysis**. Optimal conditions require 1 M NaCl — matching estimated ancient ocean concentrations of 0.9–1.2 M. Montmorillonite forms from weathering of volcanic ash and has been detected on Mars.

## 5. Experimental evidence: yields, selectivities, and conditions

The quantitative landscape of prebiotic ribose chemistry spans five orders of magnitude in yield, from the formose reaction's <1% ribose to the glyoxylate scenario's ~60% ketosugars:

- **Formose reaction**: Ribose yield **<1%** of total sugars (Shapiro, 1988); >30 identified products (Decker et al., 1982); formaldehyde ≥0.15 M, Ca(OH)₂, 40–65°C, pH 11–12. Na₂WO₄ selectively enhances ribose over xylose (Nishijima et al., 2026).

- **Borate-stabilized formose**: Ribose half-life extended from minutes to **days** at room temperature (Ricardo et al., 2004). Aldopentose yields up to **65%** relative to initial glycolaldehyde with NaOH + H₃BO₃ catalyst at pH 9, 5 mM glycolaldehyde, 50–100 mM formaldehyde (Pestunova group, *Kinetics & Catalysis* 2013). Borate reduces maximum yield but dramatically increases persistence; the net effect over geological timescales favors ribose accumulation.

- **Photochemical synthesis**: Glycolaldehyde **~42%** from glycolonitrile (CuCN + H₂S, 254 nm, 4 h; Ritson and Sutherland, 2013). Glyceraldehyde **~17%** from glycolaldehyde cyanohydrin (2 h, 254 nm). With sulfite + ferrocyanide: **68% total reduced products** in 1 h (Xu et al., 2018). Thiophosphate system: glycolaldehyde **~19%**, glyceronitrile **~9%** (Ritson and Sutherland, 2023).

- **Cyanosulfidic nucleotide synthesis**: Aminooxazoline formation essentially quantitative and highly stereoselective (Anastasi et al., 2006). Aminooxazoline-5′-phosphate **36% yield** over two steps via glycidaldehyde route, ribo/arabino selectivity **2.8:1** (Fernández-García et al., 2017). Telescoped synthesis from HCN to ribo-aminooxazoline achieved in **~1% overall yield** over 15 steps (Ritson and Sutherland, 2023).

- **Purine nucleosides (Carell)**: Formamidopyrimidines (FaPys) + ribose → N-9 purine nucleosides in **~60% yield** with **extreme regioselectivity** (exclusively N-9; Becker et al., *Science* 2016, 352, 833–836). Becker et al. (2019, *Science* 366, 76–82) demonstrated a unified synthesis of all four Watson-Crick ribonucleotides via wet-dry cycling.

- **Glyoxylate scenario**: DHF + glyceraldehyde → **~60% yield** of C5 ketosugars (xylulose + ribulose; Sagi et al., 2012, *JACS* 134, 3577). Reaction is remarkably clean — practically no side products.

- **Phosphate-directed aldomerization**: **25% pentose-2,4-bisphosphates** from mineral-catalyzed glycolaldehyde phosphate reaction, with ribose-2,4-bisphosphate as **48% of pentose fraction** (Krishnamurthy, Arrhenius, Eschenmoser, 1999).

- **Aldol route**: Glycolaldehyde + glyceraldehyde → ribose **~20%**, arabinose **~14%** (Kofoed et al., 2005). Ribose → 2-deoxyribose photoreduction **52%** yield (Ritson and Sutherland, 2014).

## 6. Open questions and the fundamental bottlenecks

### The asphalt problem remains the central obstacle for formose-based routes

The formose reaction's autocatalytic, self-amplifying nature means that once initiated, sugar production rapidly outpaces any selectivity control. Cross-aldol reactions produce straight-chain and branched sugars from C2 to C7+; the Cannizzaro reaction diverts formaldehyde to methanol and formate; and sugars undergo browning reactions to form polymeric tar. Above the "yellowing point" (observed after ~28 days in mechanochemical experiments; Trapp group, 2020), hexoses accumulate (~66%), followed by heptoses and eventually intractable tar. No single additive — borate, phosphate, molybdate, or tungstate — converts the formose reaction into a selective ribose synthesis. The most promising approaches combine multiple mineral modifiers (borate + molybdate + Ca²⁺; Kim et al., 2011) or divert formose intermediates into nucleotide precursors via cyanamide (*Chemical Science* 2023, 14, 9589–9599).

### Selectivity for ribose requires multiple coincident selection pressures

Ribose is the **least stable** of the four aldopentoses (Larralde et al., 1995), and free sugar equilibration at pH 7, 25°C yields only **19% ribose** versus 75% arabinose and 6% ribulose within 13 days. Multiple factors have been proposed to collectively favor ribose:

- Borate complexation selectively stabilizes ribose over other aldopentoses (Furukawa et al., 2013)
- Fatty acid membranes exhibit diastereoselectivity favoring ribose permeation (Szostak group, *PNAS* 2005)
- Aminooxazoline formation provides thermodynamic selectivity for ribo-stereochemistry at C2′ (Powner group)
- Tungstate catalysis preferentially forms ribose in the formose reaction (Nishijima et al., 2026)
- Borate-guided phosphorylation occurs preferentially on ribose versus other aldopentoses (Hirakawa et al., 2022)
- Downstream selection: β-D-ribofuranose maximizes nucleic acid duplex stability for information storage

No single mechanism alone produces ribose dominance; the most plausible scenario involves a **cascade of selection pressures** operating at different stages.

### Homochirality remains an active frontier with a promising new solution

All prebiotic chemistry produces racemic mixtures, yet life uses exclusively D-ribose. The most significant recent breakthrough came from Ozturk, Liu, Sutherland, and Sasselov (2023, *Science Advances* 9, eadg8274), who achieved **~60% enantiomeric excess** of ribose aminooxazoline via spin-selective crystallization on magnetite (Fe₃O₄) surfaces through the **chiral-induced spin selectivity (CISS) effect**. Subsequent crystallization yielded **100% ee homochiral crystals**. The reciprocal effect — enantiopure RAO uniformly magnetizes Fe₃O₄ surfaces — creates a positive feedback loop (*Nature Communications* 2023, 14, 6351). Ozturk, Sasselov, and Sutherland (*PNAS* 2025) proposed that RAO homochirality propagates to RNA → peptides → metabolites via successive stereoselective processes.

Blackmond and colleagues provided complementary advances: heterochiral preference in prebiotically plausible peptide ligation reactions paradoxically provides enantioenrichment in homochiral chains (*Nature* 2024, 626, 1019–1024), and an autocatalytic feedback network combining amino acid synthesis and ligation demonstrates spontaneous symmetry breaking (Higgs and Blackmond, *PNAS* 2025, 122, e2423683122). Earlier work by Breslow and Cheng (*PNAS* 2010, 107, 5723) showed L-amino acids catalyze preferential D-glyceraldehyde formation (D/L ratios 51:49 to 61:39), providing a cross-chirality transfer mechanism.

### Ribose stability constrains all scenarios to specific conditions

Ribose's measured half-lives (Larralde, Robertson, and Miller, 1995) define hard thermodynamic boundaries on plausible prebiotic environments. At **pH 7, 100°C: t₁/₂ = 73 minutes**. At **pH 7, 0°C: t₁/₂ = 44 years**. Stability maximizes near pH 5 (Levy and Miller, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 2011), suggesting high-pCO₂ environments may have favored RNA precursor accumulation. The implication is clear: ribose must either be stabilized (by borate, phosphate, mineral surfaces, or aminooxazoline formation) or consumed rapidly by downstream reactions (nucleoside formation, phosphorylation) before it degrades. The cyanosulfidic pathway elegantly addresses this by never producing free ribose at all — the sugar stereochemistry is "locked in" at the aminooxazoline stage, where stability is **≥70× greater** than ribose–borate complexes.

## 7. Recent advances redefining the field (2020–2026)

### Metal-doped clays and oxometalate catalysts open new selectivity avenues

Zhao and Wang (2021, *Chem* 7, 3292–3308) demonstrated that metal cations and clay minerals **synergistically and selectively bind ribose** from complex formose mixtures. Using a continuous-flow fixed-bed microreactor simulating prebiotic conditions, ribose was the major sugar adsorbed on Cu²⁺-doped clays. DFT calculations confirmed Cu(β-ribopyranose)(silica)Cl₂ has the lowest free energy among all pentose complexes. A follow-up (Xu, Zhao, and Wang, 2025, *Chem. Commun.* 61, 1701–1704) achieved **selective non-enzymatic ribose synthesis** from formaldehyde, metal salts, and clays.

### Extraterrestrial ribose confirmed in meteorites and asteroid Bennu

Furukawa et al. (2019, *PNAS* 116, 24440–24445) provided the **first direct detection of extraterrestrial ribose** in the NWA 801 and Murchison meteorites (2.3–180 ppb, ¹³C-enriched). In December 2025, Furukawa et al. (*Nature Geoscience*) detected **ribose and glucose in pristine OSIRIS-REx samples from asteroid Bennu** — the first glucose in an extraterrestrial sample. All five nucleobases, phosphates, amino acids, and now sugars have been found in Bennu, meaning **all molecular components needed to form RNA are present** in a single extraterrestrial body. Deoxyribose was absent in both studies, supporting RNA-first hypotheses.

### Systems chemistry and network approaches gain momentum

A cyanamide-mediated formose reaction (2023, *Chemical Science* 14, 9589–9599) demonstrated that cyanamide diverts formose sugars into aminooxazoline nucleotide precursors while delaying the autocatalytic exponential phase — coupling sugar autocatalysis with nucleotide production in a manner consistent with Gánti's chemoton model. Matreux, Aikkila, Mast, Braun, and Scheu (2024, *Nature*) showed that **heat flows through thin rock fractures** create "geo-microfluidic" systems where thermophoresis selectively accumulates and sorts **over 60 different prebiotic building blocks** into distinct spatial locations. Krishnamurthy and Liotta (2023, *Chem* 9, 784) formalized the glyoxylose reaction framework, and Yadav, Kumar, and Krishnamurthy (2022, *Nature Chemistry* 14, 170–178) demonstrated a cyanide-enabled reductive glyoxylate protometabolic pathway.

The Carell group unified previously separate pyrimidine nucleoside pathways (Feldmann et al., 2023, *ChemistryEurope* 1, e202300013) and continued demonstrating that wet-dry cycles drive concurrent formation of all four canonical ribonucleotides. Haas, Lamour, Christ, and Trapp (2020, *Commun. Chem.* 3, 140) showed mechanochemical formose conditions in ball mills produce more stable, less branched sugars than aqueous reactions. Ono, Furukawa, Benner et al. (2024, *Astrobiology* 24, 489–497) systematically mapped quantitative effects of pH, temperature, cation, and concentration on ribose synthesis, establishing parameter spaces for pathway modeling.

## Conclusion: toward integrated network models

The prebiotic synthesis of ribose is not a single-pathway problem but a **multi-route, multi-environment, multi-selection network challenge**. The field has evolved from seeking one "correct" pathway to recognizing that different chemical routes likely operated in concert across spatial and temporal gradients on early Earth. Three strategic principles emerge for synthesis network design:

**First, the cyanosulfidic pathway's strategy of avoiding free ribose appears chemically superior to any route that requires isolating ribose from a mixture.** Aminooxazoline intermediates are orders of magnitude more stable than free ribose, offer built-in stereoselectivity, and connect directly to nucleotide products. For network modeling, the HCN → glycolonitrile → glycolaldehyde → 2-aminooxazole → aminooxazoline → nucleotide chain should be treated as the primary productive pathway.

**Second, mineral-mediated selection pressures are not optional extras but essential network components.** Borate stabilization, tungstate/molybdate pathway direction, metal-doped clay enrichment, and magnetite-mediated chirality selection each address a distinct failure mode. A realistic network model must incorporate these mineral interactions as filters and catalysts operating on the chemical mixture at each stage.

**Third, the 2023 CISS/magnetite result from Ozturk, Sutherland, and Sasselov represents a potential solution to the longest-standing open question — homochirality.** The positive feedback loop between enantiopure RAO and magnetized Fe₃O₄ surfaces, combined with conglomerate crystallization amplification, offers the first prebiotically plausible route from racemic chemistry to homochiral RNA precursors. This should be incorporated as a symmetry-breaking node in any complete prebiotic network model.
