# Prebiotic synthesis of lysine remains one of origin-of-life chemistry's hardest unsolved problems

**Lysine has never been reliably synthesized under prebiotic conditions**, making it one of the most conspicuously absent proteinogenic amino acids from the abiotic chemical inventory. Across seven decades of spark discharge experiments, meteorite analyses, hydrothermal simulations, UV photolysis studies, and HCN polymerization reactions, lysine appears in at most one or two tentative detections—none confirmed by modern high-resolution techniques until a single 2021 borosilicate-catalyzed experiment. This gap matters because lysine's ε-amino group provides irreplaceable cationic functionality for nucleic acid binding, enzyme catalysis, and protein structure. The consensus view treats lysine as a **"biological invention"**—an amino acid that entered the genetic code only after enzymatic biosynthetic pathways evolved, not one drawn from the prebiotic environment (Thoma & Powner, *JACS*, 2023).

---

## The structural challenge: a 6-carbon diamine defeats standard prebiotic mechanisms

Lysine (2,6-diaminohexanoic acid) possesses two features that collectively make prebiotic synthesis extraordinarily difficult: a **6-carbon backbone** and **two amino groups** separated by four methylene units. Every major prebiotic amino acid synthesis mechanism fails at one or both of these requirements.

**The Strecker synthesis**, the dominant route to α-amino acids in prebiotic chemistry, converts an aldehyde + HCN + NH₃ into an α-aminonitrile, then an α-amino acid. For lysine, the required starting aldehyde would be **5-aminopentanal** (H₂N–(CH₂)₄–CHO). This molecule is itself complex with no known prebiotic source, and the Strecker mechanism can only generate the α-amino group—it cannot explain the ε-amino group's origin. The Strecker route is thus a theoretical dead-end for lysine (Miller & Van Trump, 1981, *Origin of Life*, Springer).

**Cyanosulfidic chemistry** (the Powner–Sutherland pathways) achieves remarkable selectivity in producing precursors for ~12 proteinogenic amino acids from HCN, H₂S, phosphate, and UV light (Patel et al., *Nature Chemistry*, 2015, 7:301–307). However, Patel et al. explicitly noted that **cyclization events during homologation of β-aminopropionaldehyde make further chain extension to the acyclic Strecker precursor of lysine "appear unlikely."** The pathway defaults to proline (cyclic C5) instead of achieving the linear C6 diamine chain.

**HCN polymerization** offers the only historical claim of lysine detection from truly prebiotic chemistry. Moser & Matthews (*Experientia*, 1968, 24:658–659) reported "small amounts of lysine" from hydrolysis of aminoacetonitrile-derived polymers. However, Cleaves's high-resolution LC-MS re-examination (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2010, 40:587) confirmed only glycine, serine, alanine, and N-carbamyl-2,3-diaminosuccinic acid from HCN oligomer hydrolysis—**lysine was not among confirmed products**. Ruiz-Bermejo et al. (*Life*, 2013, 3:421) reviewed all HCN polymerization experiments and found that while 12 of 20 protein amino acids have been detected across various conditions, lysine's inclusion is based solely on the unconfirmed 1968 report.

## Experimental evidence: one tentative detection across decades of work

### Miller-Urey experiments consistently fail to produce lysine

The original Miller experiment (1953, *Science*, 117:528–529) detected five amino acids; lysine was absent. Ring et al. (*PNAS*, 1972, 69:765–768) expanded the inventory to ~20 amino acids using CH₄/N₂/H₂O/NH₃ discharge—still no lysine. Johnson et al.'s reanalysis of Miller's archived volcanic apparatus samples using modern LC-FD/ToF-MS (*Science*, 2008, 322:404) found **22 amino acids and 5 amines**—lysine absent. Parker et al.'s reanalysis of Miller's 1958 H₂S experiment (*PNAS*, 2011, 108:5526–5531) identified 23 amino acids including 7 organosulfur compounds—again, no lysine.

**The sole exception** is Criado-Reyes et al. (*Scientific Reports*, 2021, 11:21009), who conducted spark discharge experiments comparing **borosilicate glass** and **Teflon** reactors using CH₄/NH₃/N₂ mixtures. They detected **56 organic products** by GC-MS after TMS derivatization, including lysine (compound #14), identified with a NIST library similarity index ≥98%. Total amino acid yields were dramatically higher in borosilicate (**159 µg/mg crude** buffered at pH 8.7) versus Teflon (**27 µg/mg**). The authors attributed this to silicate surface catalysis leaching boron and silicon species into solution. This remains the **only published report of lysine detection in a Miller-Urey-type experiment**, though the identification is described as "tentatively assigned" and no specific quantitative yield for lysine alone was reported in the main text.

### Lysine is absent from all analyzed meteorites

Lysine has not been detected in the Murchison meteorite or any other carbonaceous chondrite. This absence is so consistent that it serves as a **contamination diagnostic**: Engel & Macko (*Precambrian Research*, 2001) and Glavin et al. (*Meteoritics & Planetary Science*, 2020) both noted that the absence of lysine, histidine, arginine, tyrosine, and phenylalanine confirms that detected amino acids are indigenous rather than terrestrial contaminants. However, shorter-chain diamino acids **have** been found: Meierhenrich et al. (*PNAS*, 2004, 101:9182–9186) identified DL-2,3-diaminopropanoic acid and DL-2,4-diaminobutanoic acid at parts-per-billion concentrations in Murchison—both racemic, confirming abiotic origin. The pattern is clear: **diamino acid abundance drops sharply with carbon chain length**, and the C6 lysine falls below detection limits.

### UV photolysis produces isomers but not lysine itself

Muñoz Caro et al. (*Nature*, 2002) irradiated H₂O:CH₃OH:NH₃:CO:CO₂ ices at 12 K with UV light and detected 16 amino acids—plus, notably, **"two diaminohexanoic acids"** with the same molecular formula as lysine but different structural arrangements. Lysine itself was not confirmed. Nuevo et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2008) reported a chromatographic peak at lysine's retention time in UV-irradiated ice residues, but co-injection experiments revealed it was **likely ethanolamine (NH₂CH₂CH₂OH)** coeluting with lysine. The authors concluded: "the presence of lysine in our residues cannot be totally ruled out" but remained unconfirmed.

## Key intermediates and the carbon chain elongation bottleneck

The chemical path from simple prebiotic molecules (HCN, NH₃, H₂CO, H₂O) to lysine requires solving two nested problems: building a linear C6 chain and installing amino groups at both termini.

**Potential precursor molecules** include aminoacetonitrile (H₂NCH₂CN, which oligomerizes to HCN polymers), acrylonitrile (CH₂=CHCN, a C3 building block accessible from HCN chemistry), β-aminopropionaldehyde (from acrylonitrile + NH₃), and various α-keto acids. In modern biochemistry, lysine arises through either the **diaminopimelate (DAP) pathway** starting from aspartate semialdehyde + pyruvate (bacteria, plants; 7–10 enzymatic steps), or the **α-aminoadipate (AAA) pathway** starting from α-ketoglutarate + acetyl-CoA (fungi; ~8 enzymatic steps). Neither pathway has any demonstrated prebiotic equivalent—both require sophisticated enzymatic catalysis with no known abiotic substitutes.

The shorter diamino acids **α,γ-diaminobutyric acid** (C4) and **α,β-diaminopropionic acid** (C3) have been detected in spark discharge experiments (Ring et al., 1972; Wolman et al., 1972) and meteorites (Meierhenrich et al., 2004). These are lysine's shorter homologues, demonstrating that the chemistry for diamino acid formation exists prebiotically—but only for chains up to C4–C5. The yield of diamino acids drops precipitously with each additional carbon, and the **C6 threshold has never been crossed** in confirmed prebiotic synthesis.

A particularly informative intermediate is **diaminopropionic acid (Dpr)**, which Thoma & Powner (*JACS*, 2023, 145:3121–3130) showed can be synthesized prebiotically by ammonia addition to dehydroalanine nitrile with **81–85% yield**. Dpr can serve as a functional lysine surrogate in peptide catalysis, though its lower sidechain pKa (~6.3 vs. lysine's ~10.8) limits its functional equivalence.

## Environmental conditions and thermodynamic barriers paint a harsh picture

**Thermodynamic calculations** by Amend & Shock (*Science*, 1998, 281:1659–1662) showed that amino acid synthesis from CO₂, NH₄⁺, and H₂ is endergonic under surface seawater conditions (18°C, 1 atm) for all 20 amino acids, but becomes exergonic for 11 amino acids at hydrothermal conditions (100°C, 250 bar). The "late" amino acids, including lysine, have mean Gibbs free energies of formation of **~285 ± 94 kJ/mol** at the surface—significantly higher than the ~169 ± 42 kJ/mol for the 10 "early" amino acids. Higgs & Pudritz (*Astrobiology*, 2009, 9:483–490) demonstrated that prebiotic amino acid abundance correlates strongly (**r = 0.96**) with free energy of formation, and lysine sits at the thermodynamically unfavorable extreme.

**Hydrothermal environments** present a double-edged sword. While moderate-temperature alkaline vents (Lost City type, 40–90°C, pH 9–11) provide thermodynamically favorable conditions for amino acid synthesis, amino acids also decompose rapidly at elevated temperatures. Bada, Miller & Zhao (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 1995, 25:111–118) showed that amino acids are **irreversibly destroyed above ~240°C**, ruling out black smoker environments. Lysine's ε-amino group provides an additional decomposition pathway through lactam formation and cross-linking reactions, making it **more thermally labile** than simpler amino acids. Sato et al. (*Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research*, 2004, 43:3217–3222) demonstrated that amino acid degradation rates increase with functional group complexity at 200–340°C.

**Wet-dry cycling** in evaporitic pools at 65–85°C promotes peptide bond formation but has not been shown to synthesize lysine de novo. Intriguingly, Frenkel-Pinter et al. (*PNAS*, 2019, 116:16338) found that proteinogenic basic amino acids (Lys, Arg, His) **preferentially polymerize** into linear depsipeptides compared to their non-proteinogenic analogues, and the resulting oligomers enhance RNA duplex thermal stability. This suggests lysine has superior polymerization properties once available—but does not solve the prior synthesis problem.

## Mineral catalysts concentrate lysine but cannot yet create it

**Montmorillonite clay** shows remarkable selectivity for lysine. A 2024 study in *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta* demonstrated that cationic amino acids (lysine, arginine) are **selectively adsorbed** with Langmuir constants far exceeding those of neutral amino acids at pH 5–7 in NaCl solution. Lysine's protonated ε-amino group drives cation exchange into montmorillonite's interlayer space, increasing d-spacing from **1.2 to 2.1 nm** (Cuadros et al., *Colloids and Surfaces A*, 2009). Bezaly et al. (*Scientific Reports*, 2026) showed that lysine intercalation creates diverse nano-environments that may facilitate polymerization. However, Mg²⁺ competition reduces adsorption ~10-fold, and these interactions only concentrate pre-existing lysine—they do not generate it.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** catalyze reductive amination of α-keto acids to α-amino acids (Huber & Wächtershäuser; Barge et al., *PNAS*, 2019, 116:4828–4833), but only simple amino acids (alanine, glycine) have been produced. The reaction requires freshly precipitated FeS with strict metal specificity—replacing FeS with other metal sulfides eliminates amino acid production (Kitadai et al., *Life*, 2018, 8:46). Pyrite photocatalysis can produce amino acids with **14.5–42.4% enantiomeric excess** for D-amino acids (*Nature Communications*, 2024), but no iron-sulfur system has produced lysine or any C6 diamino acid.

**Borosilicate glass** (silicate minerals) may represent the most promising catalytic avenue, given the Criado-Reyes et al. (2021) result. Their experiment yielded **~6× more amino acids** in borosilicate versus Teflon reactors, with some amino acids forming exclusively in the presence of silicate surfaces. A follow-up study by Jenewein & García-Ruiz (conference abstract, *Macla*, 2025) extends this work to pure quartz versus borosilicate systems, with a manuscript reportedly under review at *PNAS*.

## Lysine occupies a unique evolutionary and chemical niche

**Genetic code chronology** places lysine as a **mid-to-late addition**, ranking approximately 10th in Trifonov's consensus temporal order (*Gene*, 2000, 261:139–151): G/A → V/D → P → S → E/L → T → R → N → **K** → Q → I → C → H → F → M → Y → W. Makarov et al. (*JACS*, 2023, 145:3840–3850) showed the early amino acid alphabet **completely lacked positively charged residues**—Lys, Arg, and His were all absent, with metal ion cofactors likely compensating for the missing positive charges in primitive proteins.

The existence of **two completely independent biosynthetic pathways** for lysine (DAP and AAA)—sharing no homologous enzymes in their later steps—represents remarkable convergent evolution and underscores the intense selective pressure for this amino acid (Fondi et al., *BMC Evolutionary Biology*, 2007, 7(Suppl 2):S3). The biosynthetic cost of lysine (~30 ATP equivalents) is among the highest of any amino acid, reflecting the thermodynamic difficulty that also blocks prebiotic synthesis.

Thoma & Powner's 2023 work provides a compelling **chemical rationale for lysine's selection**. In direct competition experiments, lysine thioacids underwent peptide coupling with aminonitriles in **96% yield** at pH 7, while ornithine (C5 homologue) and diaminobutyric acid (C4 homologue) cyclized to lactams at **>90% yield**, completely blocking peptide formation. Only lysine's four-methylene spacer is long enough to prevent intramolecular cyclization while maintaining an electronically isolated, fully protonated sidechain amine (pKa ~10.8). This creates an extraordinarily sharp chemical selection: among all possible ω-amino-α-amino acids, **only lysine is compatible with peptide chain elongation**.

## What remains unsolved and why it matters

The prebiotic synthesis of lysine remains fundamentally unresolved. The core bottleneck is **carbon chain elongation to C6 with regioselective ε-amination**—no known prebiotic mechanism achieves this. The cyclization barrier identified by Sutherland's group means that standard HCN homologation chemistry defaults to cyclic products (proline) rather than linear C6 diamine chains. Extraterrestrial delivery offers no solution, as lysine has not been detected in any meteorite.

Several paths forward deserve investigation. The borosilicate-catalyzed spark discharge result (Criado-Reyes et al., 2021) suggests that **mineral surface catalysis** may enable more complex amino acid synthesis than solution-phase chemistry alone—but this remains a single, tentative detection requiring independent confirmation. Computational reaction network exploration (Wołos et al.; RTIP methods in *JACS Au*, 2024) has not yet been applied to lysine, representing a significant frontier. The possibility of **multi-step, systems-chemistry approaches** combining different prebiotic environments (hydrothermal synthesis → mineral concentration → wet-dry polymerization) remains largely unexplored for lysine specifically.

The field faces a paradox: lysine is chemically the **optimal amino acid for peptide chain elongation** and functionally essential for nucleic acid binding, yet it appears to be among the last amino acids biology could access. Whether this means early life operated without positive charges (using metal ions instead), employed simpler surrogates like diaminopropionic acid, or accessed lysine through yet-undiscovered prebiotic chemistry remains one of origin-of-life research's most consequential open questions.

## Conclusion

Lysine's prebiotic absence is not simply a gap in our knowledge—it is a **structurally informative constraint** on origin-of-life scenarios. The thermodynamic cost (ΔG ~285 kJ/mol at surface conditions), the Strecker synthesis dead-end, the cyclization barrier in cyanosulfidic chemistry, and the blank across meteorite inventories collectively point to a genuine chemical boundary. The Criado-Reyes et al. (2021) borosilicate result and the diaminohexanoic acid isomers in UV ice experiments (Muñoz Caro et al., 2002) suggest the boundary is not absolute—C6 diamino compounds can form abiotically under specific conditions—but the particular regiochemistry of 2,6-diaminohexanoic acid remains elusive. The most productive research directions likely involve mineral-catalyzed spark or photochemistry, systematic exploration of HCN polymer hydrolysis products with modern mass spectrometry, and computational pathway discovery. Until a robust prebiotic route to lysine is found, origin-of-life models must account for how early biology functioned without—or eventually invented—one of its most indispensable building blocks.
