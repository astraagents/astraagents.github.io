# Prebiotic synthesis of pyruvate: pathways, catalysts, and unsolved problems

**Pyruvate sits at the crossroads of prebiotic chemistry — a three-carbon α-keto acid that bridges simple volcanic gases (CO₂, H₂) and the core metabolic networks of all living cells.** At least eight distinct abiotic routes to pyruvate have been demonstrated or proposed in the literature, spanning iron-sulfur geochemistry, cyanosulfidic photochemistry, glyoxylate condensation, and mineral photoelectrochemistry. The most striking recent advances show that native Ni-Fe alloys can fix CO₂ to pyruvate at **25°C** (Beyazay et al., *Nature Communications*, 2023), and that pyruvate plus glyoxylate with Fe²⁺ spontaneously generates **9 of 11 TCA cycle intermediates** (Muchowska, Varma & Moran, *Nature*, 2019). These findings strengthen the case that pyruvate emerged naturally on the early Earth, but the field still faces serious bottlenecks in yield (typically ≤10 µM from CO₂), selectivity (pyruvate partitions among five competing reaction pathways simultaneously), and stability (rapid decarboxylation above ~150°C).

---

## CO₂ fixation routes mirror the acetyl-CoA pathway

The most geochemically grounded pathway to pyruvate follows the logic of the biological Wood-Ljungdahl (acetyl-CoA) pathway — the most ancient CO₂ fixation route in both bacteria and archaea. Three landmark experimental programs have demonstrated this sequence abiotically:

**Huber & Wächtershäuser (1997, *Science*)** first showed that coprecipitated NiS and FeS convert CO and CH₃SH into the activated thioester methyl thioacetate (CH₃-CO-SCH₃) at 100°C over 7 days — a direct analog of the acetyl-CoA synthase reaction. Yields were modest (~0.5% based on CH₃SH input), but the mechanism — methyl migration to a carbonyl center on a Ni-Fe surface — precisely mirrors the enzymatic A-cluster mechanism. The thioester can then undergo reductive carboxylation to pyruvate: **CH₃CO-SR + CO₂ + 2e⁻ → CH₃COCOO⁻ + RSH**, mimicking pyruvate:ferredoxin oxidoreductase (PFOR).

**Varma, Muchowska, Chatelain & Moran (2018, *Nature Ecology & Evolution*)** demonstrated that native metallic iron (Fe⁰) reduces CO₂ in water at 30–100°C and 1–40 bar to produce formate (dominant), acetate (near-mM), and pyruvate (detected transiently). This was transformative: it showed the core reaction sequence of the acetyl-CoA pathway unfolds spontaneously with simple native metals, replacing the function of over 120 enzymes.

**Preiner et al. (2020, *Nature Ecology & Evolution*)** extended this to three naturally occurring hydrothermal minerals — **awaruite (Ni₃Fe)**, **greigite (Fe₃S₄)**, and **magnetite (Fe₃O₄)** — catalyzing CO₂ + H₂ → formate (up to **200 mM**), acetate (up to **100 µM**), pyruvate (up to **10 µM**), and methanol at 100°C, 25 bar, under alkaline conditions over 16 hours. Awaruite proved most effective overall. Beyazay et al. (2023, *Nature Communications*) then pushed this to **ambient temperature (25°C)** using Ni₃Fe nanoparticles under 25 bar CO₂/H₂, producing formate (**55.5 mM**), acetate (**0.2 mM**), and pyruvate (**0.04 mM**) at 100°C in 24 hours — and even detecting products at room temperature.

The complete abiotic sequence thus reads: **CO₂ → formate (C₁) → acetate/acetyl thioester (C₂) → pyruvate (C₃)**, with each carbon–carbon bond-forming step showing progressively lower yields. The C₁→C₂→C₃ chain extension remains the principal bottleneck.

## Reverse TCA analogs build a metabolic network from pyruvate

Once pyruvate exists, it becomes a hub for constructing broader metabolic networks. The non-enzymatic reverse (reductive) TCA cycle has received intense experimental attention:

**Muchowska et al. (2017, *Nature Ecology & Evolution*)** demonstrated that Fe⁰, Zn²⁺, and Cr³⁺ together promote **6 of 11 rTCA cycle reactions** in acidic aqueous solution at 140°C, achieving two consecutive three-step sequences (oxaloacetate → malate → fumarate → succinate; oxalosuccinate → isocitrate → aconitate → citrate). The **Muchowska, Varma & Moran (2019, *Nature*)** paper then showed that pyruvate plus glyoxylate with only Fe²⁺ at 70°C builds up 9 of 11 TCA cycle intermediates — including all five universal metabolic precursors (acetate, pyruvate, oxaloacetate, succinate, α-ketoglutarate) — within 3 hours. Adding hydroxylamine and Fe⁰ produced four biological amino acids (glycine, alanine, aspartate, glutamate) in parallel with their biosynthetic routes.

A critical shortcut in this network is the spontaneous **decarboxylation of oxaloacetate to pyruvate + CO₂**, which proceeds non-enzymatically under essentially all prebiotic conditions. Oxaloacetate is thermally unstable; its conversion to pyruvate follows the kinetics **log(k) = 11.74 − 4956/T** (Guzman & Martin, 2009). This means any pathway that generates oxaloacetate — whether through glyoxylate chemistry, aldol condensations, or the rTCA cycle — automatically produces pyruvate as well.

**Keller, Turchyn & Ralser (2014, *Molecular Systems Biology*)** showed that Fe²⁺ at concentrations plausible for the Archean ocean catalyzes 29 reactions constituting glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathway at 70°C, including pyruvate formation from sugar phosphates. This demonstrates that once simple sugars and pyruvate coexist, iron alone can drive interconversion among core metabolic intermediates.

## Cyanosulfidic and glyoxylate pathways offer complementary routes

Two chemically distinct programs provide alternative paths to pyruvate that do not require CO₂ fixation under hydrothermal conditions:

**The cyanosulfidic network**, developed by Sutherland's group at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology, starts from HCN and H₂S under UV irradiation at ~35°C and neutral pH. The key sequence proceeds through glycolonitrile (HCN + formaldehyde) → glycolaldehyde → glyceraldehyde, with acetaldehyde produced via thiocyanate-mediated deoxygenation (Patel et al., 2015, *Nature Chemistry*). **Coggins & Powner (2017, *Nature Chemistry*)** demonstrated that α-phosphorylation of glyceraldehyde with diamidophosphate (DAP) produces glyceraldehyde-2-phosphate, which dehydrates at 60°C to phosphoenolpyruvaldehyde and then oxidizes to **phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP)** — the high-energy glycolytic intermediate whose hydrolysis yields pyruvate directly. This elegant pathway recapitulates triose glycolysis prebiotically and links nucleotide precursor chemistry to metabolic pyruvate production.

**The glyoxylate scenario**, originating with Eschenmoser (2007, *Chemistry & Biodiversity*), positions glyoxylate (OHCCO₂⁻) as a central hub molecule — formally "carboxylated formaldehyde." Cope et al. (2013, *JACS*) showed that cyanide catalyzes glyoxylate dimerization to tartrate at **80% yield** under alkaline conditions; tartrate then dehydrates to oxaloacetate, which spontaneously decarboxylates to pyruvate. Springsteen et al. (2018, *Nature Communications*) demonstrated linked cycles of oxidative decarboxylation using glyoxylate and H₂O₂ at 50°C in bicarbonate buffer, producing TCA cycle analogs. Strikingly, Springsteen et al. (2020, *Nature Chemistry*) showed that glyoxylate + pyruvate in water alone — **no metals required** — generates an α-ketoacid network analogous to the rTCA cycle. Yadav et al. (2022, *Nature Chemistry*) further demonstrated that cyanide serves as a selective primordial reductant, reducing oxaloacetate to malate and fumarate to succinate while leaving pyruvate and α-ketoglutarate intact — a selectivity pattern that parallels biology.

Glyoxylate itself has multiple plausible prebiotic sources: photo-oxidation of acetylene, HCN polymer hydrolysis (Marín-Yaseli et al., 2016, *Chemistry — A European Journal*), mineral-mediated CO₂ reduction, and it has been detected in the **Murchison meteorite** alongside pyruvic acid (Cooper et al., 2011, *PNAS*).

## Photochemical and HCN-based pathways provide additional channels

**ZnS mineral photoelectrochemistry** offers perhaps the highest-yield prebiotic route to pyruvate from a single precursor. Guzman & Martin (2009, *Astrobiology*) showed that UV-irradiated ZnS colloids (200–400 nm, 15°C, circumneutral pH) photooxidize lactate to pyruvate at **70% yield** with a quantum efficiency of 0.009. The reverse reaction (pyruvate → lactate photoreduction) proceeds at 30% yield. Furthermore, pyruvate undergoes ZnS-catalyzed photooxidative carboxylation to α-ketoglutarate at **50% yield** (quantum efficiency 0.0036). Zhang & Martin (2006, *JACS*) had earlier demonstrated that ZnS drives three of five reductive steps in the rTCA cycle, including fumarate → succinate at **95% yield**. Wang et al. (2012, *Chemical Communications*) extended this to reversible photocatalytic amination of pyruvate, oxaloacetate, glyoxylate, and α-ketoglutarate on ZnS surfaces — connecting keto acid and amino acid pools through a single mineral photocatalyst.

**HCN oligomerization** provides a distinct atmospheric/aqueous route. While Oró (1960) focused on adenine synthesis, hydrolysis of HCN polymers produces a range of α-keto acids including pyruvate (Ferris et al., 1978, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*). Formaldehyde accelerates HCN oligomerization (Schwartz & Goverde, 1982, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*), and the resulting polymer hydrolysis products include glyoxylic acid, amino acids, and nucleobases — connecting HCN chemistry to the glyoxylate and pyruvate networks.

**The formose reaction** (Butlerov, 1861; Breslow, 1959) connects formaldehyde to pyruvate through sugar intermediates: formaldehyde → glycolaldehyde → glyceraldehyde → pyruvaldehyde (methylglyoxal, via dehydration) → pyruvate (via oxidation). Under alkaline formose conditions, glyceraldehyde also rearranges to lactic acid through the benzilic acid rearrangement of methylglyoxal — lactate being the reduced form of pyruvate, interconvertible via ZnS photochemistry. UV irradiation of aqueous formaldehyde produces acetaldehyde and glycolaldehyde directly (Pestunova et al., 2011, *Advances in Space Research*), while UV photolysis of acetaldehyde with ammonium salts yields pyruvic acid and alanine.

## Mineral catalysts bridge geochemistry and biochemistry

The mineral catalysts relevant to prebiotic pyruvate synthesis fall into four functional categories:

**Iron-sulfur minerals** are central. **Greigite (Fe₃S₄)** possesses the inverse spinel structure nearly identical to biological [4Fe-4S] cubane clusters in ferredoxins. Roldan et al. (2015, *Chemical Communications*) demonstrated electrochemical CO₂ reduction on greigite nanoparticles at room temperature and atmospheric pressure, producing formate, acetate, methanol, and pyruvate — with pyruvate forming selectively at **pH 6.5** but not at pH 4.5 or 10.5. DFT studies by Santos-Carballal et al. (2018, *Philosophical Transactions A*) confirmed strong CO₂ activation on greigite {001} and {111} surfaces. **Mackinawite (FeS)**, the layered iron(II) sulfide structurally analogous to [2Fe-2S] clusters, is a precursor to greigite in geological settings. Kitadai et al. (2019, *Science Advances*) showed that electrochemically reduced mackinawite (FeS_PERM) catalyzes reductive amination of pyruvate to alanine but critically **did not observe CO₂ → organic molecule formation** even at 100°C — suggesting native metals are more effective than sulfides for the initial CO₂ fixation step. **Pyrite (FeS₂)** remains the cornerstone of Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis, with the reaction FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂ (ΔG° ≈ **−38.4 kJ/mol**) providing reducing equivalents for CO₂ fixation (Drobner et al., 1990, *Nature*).

**Native metals and alloys** have emerged as the most effective catalysts. **Awaruite (Ni₃Fe)**, naturally occurring in serpentinizing systems, produced the highest yields in the Preiner et al. (2020) study. The complementary roles of Ni and Fe — Ni for H₂ dissociation, Fe for CO₂ binding — were confirmed by Beyazay et al. (2023, *Angewandte Chemie*), who showed that bimetallic Ni₃Fe outperforms either metal alone.

**Semiconductor sulfides** drive photochemical pyruvate synthesis. ZnS (sphalerite) is the most studied, with its bandgap (~3.7 eV) well-matched to the early Earth's UV flux. MnS provides comparable photocatalytic reduction of rTCA intermediates (Zhang et al., 2004, *JACS*). TiO₂ (rutile) catalyzes photochemical synthesis of citric acid cycle intermediates (Saladino et al., 2011, *Astrobiology*).

**Clay minerals** (montmorillonite) serve primarily as concentrating agents and surface catalysts. Following Bernal's 1951 proposal, clays adsorb and orient organic molecules on 2D surfaces — a geometric advantage captured by Pólya's theorem: on a 2D surface molecules always meet in finite time, while in a 3D ocean they may never encounter each other.

## Environmental settings each favor different steps

No single environment supports all steps of prebiotic pyruvate synthesis optimally. The evidence favors a **gradient of environments**, each contributing distinct capabilities:

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (Lost City type, discovered 2000) provide the strongest setting for de novo CO₂ fixation. Serpentinization of olivine generates H₂ (up to **15 mM** in vent fluids), creating a powerful thermodynamic drive for CO₂ reduction. Vent temperatures of **40–90°C** at pH **9–11**, with naturally occurring awaruite, greigite, and magnetite catalysts, reproduce the conditions of the Preiner et al. and Beyazay et al. experiments. The **~3 pH unit gradient** across thin (~1 µm) inorganic vent walls mimics the proton motive force used by all cells for energy coupling — a point emphasized by Russell & Hall (1997, *Journal of the Geological Society*), Martin & Russell (2007, *Philosophical Transactions B*), and Lane & Martin (2012). Lost City chimneys persist for over **120,000 years**, providing sustained reaction conditions. The principal limitation is dilution: pyruvate yields are ~10 µM, and products disperse into the ocean.

**Surface/evaporitic pools** excel at concentration and photochemistry. Evaporation can increase solute concentrations by orders of magnitude, overcoming the dilution problem. UV radiation — far more intense on the ozone-free early Earth — drives ZnS photochemistry (70% yield lactate → pyruvate) and cyanosulfidic reactions. Wet-dry cycling promotes condensation reactions thermodynamically disfavored in bulk water. The Damer & Deamer (2020, *Astrobiology*) hot spring hypothesis specifically invokes this setting for polymer assembly and protocell formation, with field experiments at Hells Gate, New Zealand, producing RNA-like strands of **100–200 nucleotides** via wet-dry cycling.

**Shallow alkaline vents** (analogous to Prony Bay, New Caledonia, at <50 m depth) may bridge these settings, providing serpentinization-driven CO₂ fixation with proximity to surface UV and tidal/wave-driven wet-dry cycling.

## Pyruvate's reactivity is both its strength and its greatest challenge

Novikov & Copley (2013, *PNAS*) mapped the **complete reactivity landscape of pyruvate** under simulated prebiotic conditions (50 mM pyruvate with various transition metal sulfides, CO₂, H₂, H₂S, NH₄⁺, at 25°C and 110°C). They identified five simultaneous reaction pathways:

- **Decarboxylation** → acetaldehyde + CO₂ (the dominant degradation pathway)
- **Reduction** → lactate (by H₂ on mineral surfaces)
- **Reductive amination** → alanine (with NH₄⁺)
- **Aldol condensation** → parapyruvate and higher oligomers
- **Thiolation** → thioester intermediates (with H₂S)

Different minerals shift branching ratios dramatically: pyrite, pyrrhotite, and troilite each favor different product distributions. This selectivity problem means that even when pyruvate is synthesized, it rapidly partitions into competing pathways. The **stability problem** compounds this: gas-phase thermal decomposition follows Arrhenius kinetics with activation energy **41.25 kcal/mol** (Taylor, 1987), and at temperatures above ~150°C, decarboxylation to acetaldehyde is extremely rapid. Cody et al.'s paradoxical synthesis of pyruvate at 250°C (2000, *Science*) was possible only because high pressure (**50–200 MPa**) shifted equilibrium and the synthesis rate exceeded the decomposition rate.

Recent work on **coacervate protocells** offers a partial solution: amino acid-based coacervate microdroplets preserve **60.4%** of pyruvate at 90°C over 8 hours versus only **29.8%** in free aqueous solution (*JACS*, 2025). Baaske et al. (2007) demonstrated extreme accumulation of nucleotides in simulated hydrothermal pore systems via thermophoresis, a mechanism that could similarly concentrate pyruvate.

## Key intermediates form a connected network

The essential precursor molecules for prebiotic pyruvate fall into a hierarchical network where multiple pathways converge:

**Formate (HCOO⁻)** is the universal first product of CO₂ reduction, produced at up to 200 mM by awaruite — three orders of magnitude above pyruvate yields. It is the C₁ building block for all downstream carbon chain extension. **Acetaldehyde (CH₃CHO)** connects multiple worlds: it arises from pyruvate decarboxylation (degradation), cyanosulfidic deoxygenation of glycolaldehyde, UV photolysis of formaldehyde, and atmospheric photochemistry. Its oxidation and carboxylation can regenerate pyruvate, creating a potentially cycling system. **Glycolaldehyde (HOCH₂CHO)** is the simplest sugar and a key node linking formose chemistry, cyanosulfidic chemistry (via HCN + formaldehyde → glycolonitrile → glycolaldehyde), and the Coggins-Powner route to PEP and pyruvate. **Glyoxylate (OHCCO₂⁻)** serves as a branch point connecting CO₂ fixation products to the TCA cycle network, the Eschenmoser scenario, and amino acid synthesis. **Oxaloacetate** spontaneously decarboxylates to pyruvate under all prebiotic conditions and can be generated by glyoxylate dimerization → tartrate → oxaloacetate, or via rTCA cycle chemistry. **Lactate** is the reduced form of pyruvate, interconvertible via ZnS photochemistry (70% yield) or Ni/zeolite catalysis (Song et al., 2025, *Angewandte Chemie*), and arises independently from glyceraldehyde rearrangement in the formose reaction. **Acetyl thioesters** (CH₃CO-SR) are the direct precursors to pyruvate via reductive carboxylation and the key output of the Huber-Wächtershäuser reaction.

## Critical open problems and the path forward

Despite remarkable experimental progress, several fundamental challenges remain unresolved:

**The yield gap** is the most pressing quantitative problem. The C₁→C₂→C₃ chain extension shows a steep yield decline: formate (200 mM) → acetate (100 µM) → pyruvate (10 µM) in the Preiner et al. system — a thousand-fold drop at each step. No integrated experiment has yet achieved continuous, self-sustaining production of pyruvate from CO₂ in a single geochemically realistic reactor. The full pathway from H₂ + CO₂ through pyruvate to downstream metabolic products remains fragmented across separate studies using different conditions.

**The selectivity problem** reflects the inherent reactivity of α-keto acids. Pyruvate simultaneously undergoes decarboxylation, reduction, amination, condensation, and thiolation — and the relative rates depend sensitively on mineral type, pH, temperature, and co-reactant concentrations. Whether environmental conditions can "select" for productive pathways over degradative ones is an active area of investigation (Moran group, *Nature Chemistry*, 2022).

**The stability-synthesis tension** defines a narrow window of viable conditions. Pyruvate requires continuous resupply (steady-state flux) rather than static accumulation — a constraint that favors flow-through environments like hydrothermal vents over closed pools. The discovery that coacervate compartments enhance pyruvate preservation under thermal stress suggests that proto-compartmentalization may have been essential from the earliest stages.

**Bridging demonstrated chemistry to self-sustaining protometabolism** remains the field's central goal. Mrnjavac et al. (2025) are attempting to demonstrate gradual assembly of metabolism at a model phosphorylating hydrothermal vent — but no experiment has yet closed the loop from CO₂ fixation through pyruvate to a self-reinforcing metabolic cycle in a single system. The most promising convergence point appears to be the pyruvate + glyoxylate + Fe²⁺ system of Moran's group, which generates nearly the complete TCA cycle and amino acids from just two small organic molecules and one metal ion — but those starting organics must themselves be supplied.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of pyruvate is no longer a theoretical speculation but an experimentally grounded reality, with at least eight demonstrated routes spanning temperatures from 25°C to 250°C and pressures from 1 to 2,000 bar. The field has moved decisively beyond "can pyruvate form abiotically?" to "under what conditions does it form selectively and sustainably?" The acetyl-CoA pathway analog over Ni-Fe catalysts (Varma, Preiner, Beyazay) provides the most geochemically continuous route from CO₂, while the cyanosulfidic (Coggins-Powner) and glyoxylate (Eschenmoser-Springsteen) pathways offer higher yields through more complex precursor chemistry. The ZnS photoelectrochemical system delivers the highest single-step conversion (70%) but requires a pre-existing lactate source. The critical next frontier is demonstrating a **complete, integrated, self-sustaining pathway** from CO₂ to pyruvate to downstream metabolism in a single geochemically plausible setting — likely requiring the coupling of alkaline vent CO₂ fixation with surface concentration and photochemistry. Shallow serpentinizing systems exposed to UV may ultimately prove to be the most promising cradle for this chemistry.
