# Prebiotic synthesis of nicotinamide: from spark discharge to complete NAD⁺ assembly

**Nicotinamide—the redox-active heterocycle at the heart of NAD⁺—can be assembled prebiotically through at least three independent routes from simple feedstocks, and has been detected in pristine asteroid samples.** The field has progressed from Orgel's foundational propiolaldehyde–cyanoacetaldehyde condensation (1970) to Trapp's complete prebiotic NAD⁺ synthesis (2024), while meteoritic detections in Ryugu and Bennu confirm extraterrestrial vitamin B₃ formation as a robust cosmochemical process. Two principal pathways to the pyridine ring dominate the literature: (1) a Chichibabin-type cyclization from C₃ aldehyde precursors and ammonia, producing nicotinonitrile, and (2) a non-enzymatic recapitulation of the bacterial aspartate–DHAP pathway through quinolinic acid. Both operate under prebiotically plausible conditions, but the glycosidic bond instability of nicotinamide riboside and the concentration problem remain fundamental bottlenecks.

## Two convergent routes to the pyridine ring

The first prebiotic access to the nicotinamide heterocycle was demonstrated by Dowler, Fuller, Orgel, and Sanchez (*Science* 169, 1320–1321, 1970). Their route combines **propiolaldehyde** (HC≡C–CHO, produced by electric discharge on CH₄/H₂O mixtures) with **cyanoacetaldehyde** (NCCH₂CHO, formed by hydration of cyanoacetylene) and ammonia. The two C₃ units condense to form **2-cyanoglutaconic dialdehyde**, a linear intermediate that undergoes Chichibabin-type ring closure with ammonia to yield **nicotinonitrile** (3-cyanopyridine). Stepwise hydrolysis then gives nicotinamide (partial) or nicotinic acid (complete). The 2-cyanoglutaconic dialdehyde intermediate has proven to be a critical branch-point molecule: in the presence of ribosylamine (from ribose + ammonia), it cyclizes directly to nicotinonitrile riboside, coupling heterocycle formation with glycosylation in a single step.

Friedmann, Miller, and Sanchez (*Science* 171, 1026–1027, 1971) complemented this work by showing that electric discharge on **ethylene + ammonia** generates pyridine and HCN, which recombine to produce all three cyanopyridine isomers—nicotinonitrile (3-position), 2-cyanopyridine, and 4-cyanopyridine. The preferential formation of the 3-isomer under certain conditions partially addresses the regioselectivity question of why biology uses the 3-carboxamide rather than the 2- or 4-isomer, though selectivity remains incomplete. Miyakawa, Cleaves, and Miller (*PNAS* 99, 14628–14631, 2002) later demonstrated that even mildly reducing **CO–CO₂–N₂–H₂O atmospheres**—more geochemically plausible than strongly reducing CH₄-rich mixtures—produce nicotinic acid alongside amino acids and nucleobases under electric discharge and proton irradiation.

The second major pathway mirrors the most ancient biosynthetic route to NAD⁺. Cleaves and Miller (*J. Mol. Evol.* 52, 73–77, 2001) showed that the entire six-step bacterial de novo pathway from **aspartic acid + dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP)** proceeds non-enzymatically through **quinolinic acid** (pyridine-2,3-dicarboxylic acid) to nicotinic acid. Yields reached **up to 7%**—remarkably high for prebiotic chemistry. Phosphorylated substrates (DHAP, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate) gave significantly higher yields than their non-phosphorylated analogs, suggesting catalytic roles for phosphate. When asparagine replaced aspartate, nicotinamide was produced directly. This result established that NAD biosynthesis could have originated as "an inevitable consequence of life based on carbohydrates and amino acids," with enzymes added later to each step independently.

## From nicotinamide to NAD⁺: the complete prebiotic pathway

The assembly of the full NAD⁺ dinucleotide requires three additional transformations beyond heterocycle synthesis: N-glycosylation, phosphorylation, and pyrophosphate coupling. Each presents distinct prebiotic challenges.

**Glycosylation.** Kim and Benner (*Chem. Eur. J.* 24, 581–584, 2018) achieved the first prebiotically plausible ribosylation by reacting nicotinamide with **ribose-1,2-cyclic phosphate** in the presence of Mg²⁺ or Ca²⁺ at **85°C** under evaporative conditions. The cyclic phosphate blocks the α-face of ribose, enforcing stereoselective β-glycoside formation. However, the product is the **2′-phosphate regioisomer** rather than the biologically relevant 5′-NMN. Critically, the same chemistry produces adenosine 2′-phosphate, unifying RNA nucleotide and NAD⁺ precursor synthesis under shared conditions.

**The Trapp group breakthrough.** Bechtel, Kurrle, and Trapp (*Chem. Eur. J.* 30, e202402055, 2024) reported the **first complete prebiotic synthesis of NAD⁺** from simple feedstocks. Their pathway proceeds through four stages: (1) cyanoacetaldehyde + propiolaldehyde form 2-cyanoglutaconic dialdehyde; (2) this intermediate cyclizes with ribosylamine (pre-formed from ribose + ammonia) to yield **nicotinonitrile riboside**, which hydrolyzes to nicotinamide riboside; (3) **regioselective 5′-phosphorylation** using ITO-P (isothiatriazine-3-one phosphate, formed under Strecker-type conditions with H₂S) at pH 6, 23°C over 7 days gives **NMN at ~2% yield**; (4) photochemical coupling of NMN with ITO-AMP (light-activated adenylate) under **278 nm UV irradiation** forms the pyrophosphate bond to yield NAD⁺. A key procedural insight was that ammonia must be pre-mixed with ribose before addition of the aldehyde precursors; reversing the order produces only free nicotinonitrile without ribosylation. The photochemical activation step draws directly on Sutherland's methyl isocyanide phosphorimidazolide chemistry (Mariani, Russell, Javelle, and Sutherland, *JACS* 140, 8657–8661, 2018).

## Connection to cyanosulfidic protometabolism

Neither the Sutherland group (MRC LMB Cambridge) nor the Powner group (UCL) has published a paper directly targeting nicotinamide synthesis. However, the cyanosulfidic protometabolic network described in Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy, and Sutherland (*Nature Chemistry* 7, 301–307, 2015) generates exactly the feedstocks required. **Cyanoacetylene** (HC₃N), produced from HCN + acetylene via Cu(I)-catalyzed Nieuwland-type coupling, hydrates in water to form cyanoacetaldehyde—a direct substrate for pyridine ring formation. **Glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde** emerge from HCN photoredox chemistry (Ritson and Sutherland, *Nature Chemistry* 4, 895–899, 2012), providing both the sugar precursor for ribosylation and the C₃ carbonyl for the Cleaves–Miller pathway. The cyanosulfidic conditions—HCN, H₂S, Cu(I)/Cu(II) photoredox cycling, UV irradiation, phosphate buffering at ~pH 7, ~35°C—are broadly compatible with nicotinamide ring closure, making cyanoacetaldehyde the critical nexus molecule connecting Sutherland-type chemistry to NAD⁺ assembly.

## Meteoritic and extraterrestrial evidence validates prebiotic synthesis

Nicotinic acid (niacin) is now established as an indigenous component of carbonaceous chondrites and pristine asteroid samples. Smith, Callahan, Gerakines, Dworkin, and House (*Geochim. Cosmochim. Acta* 136, 1–12, 2014) systematically surveyed eight Antarctic CM2 meteorites and detected nicotinic acid at **30–600 ppb** in all samples, alongside picolinic acid, isonicotinic acid, and—for the first time in meteorites—pyridine dicarboxylic acids including quinolinic acid. Abundance **negatively correlated** with the degree of pre-terrestrial aqueous alteration, suggesting parent-body processing degrades these compounds. Pizzarello and Huang (2004, 2005) provided isotopic proof of extraterrestrial origin for Murchison nicotinic acid: **δD = +129‰ and δ¹³C = +20‰**, values far outside terrestrial ranges.

The Hayabusa2 mission delivered the most compelling evidence. Oba et al. (*Nature Communications* 14, 1292, 2023) detected nicotinic acid at **49–99 ppb** in pristine, hermetically sealed Ryugu samples—more abundant than uracil (6–32 ppb). The OSIRIS-REx analysis of asteroid Bennu (Glavin et al., *Nature Astronomy*, 2025) found nicotinic acid alongside all five canonical nucleobases, 14 amino acids, and ~10,000 N-bearing molecular species, with N-heterocycle concentrations **5–10× higher than Ryugu**.

**Nicotinamide itself** (not just nicotinic acid) was first detected in meteorites by Oba et al. (*Nature Communications* 13, 2008, 2022) using gentle room-temperature extraction and HPLC/ESI-HRMS on Murchison, Tagish Lake, and Murray samples. Previous studies missed nicotinamide because harsh acid hydrolysis conditions (6M HCl, 150°C) converted it entirely to nicotinic acid. This analytical insight resolved a long-standing gap and confirmed that both the amide and acid forms are present extraterrestrially. Isonicotinamide was co-detected, and the complete isomer suite (all positional isomers plus alkylated homologs) argues strongly against terrestrial contamination.

## Interstellar ice chemistry as a formation mechanism

Smith, Gerakines, and Callahan (*Chem. Commun.* 51, 11787–11790, 2015) demonstrated that proton irradiation of astrophysical ice analogs (H₂O:CO₂:pyridine = 100:20:1) produces nicotinic acid, quinolinic acid, and other pyridine carboxylic acids. In water-rich ices mimicking interstellar grain mantles, **nicotinic acid becomes the favored monocarboxylic acid isomer** (preferential 3-position carboxylation), and the product ratio (picolinic:isonicotinic:nicotinic ≈ 0.89:0.35:1.00) closely matches the least aqueously altered CM2 chondrites. This correspondence supports cosmic ray processing of N-heterocyclic ices as the primary formation mechanism, with subsequent parent-body aqueous alteration modifying the initial distribution.

Paschek, Lee, Semenov, and Henning (*ChemPlusChem* 89, e202300508, 2024) modeled vitamin B₃ synthesis inside **carbonaceous planetesimals** using the Cleaves–Miller pathway: Strecker-derived aspartate + formose-derived glyceraldehyde/DHA at 0–100°C, ~100 bar, in anaerobic aqueous solution. Their thermochemical equilibrium predictions match measured meteorite abundances, and the mechanism correctly predicts the absence of picolinic acid (consistent with observed meteorite data).

Neither pyridine nor nicotinic acid has been definitively detected in the interstellar medium via radio/millimeter-wave spectroscopy, despite extensive searches. The non-detection is attributed to rapid photodestruction, chemical conversion, and dilution across many structural isomers. However, the 2018 detection of benzonitrile (McGuire et al., *Science*) and subsequent identifications of cyanonaphthalene isomers in TMC-1 confirm that six-membered aromatic rings are present in the ISM.

## Environmental conditions and mineral catalysis

The three principal prebiotic environments each favor different steps in nicotinamide and NAD⁺ synthesis. **Volcanic/surficial settings** provide electric discharge (volcanic lightning) for generating HCN, cyanoacetylene, and propiolaldehyde, plus UV irradiation for the photochemical NAD⁺ coupling step. The Bechtel/Kurrle/Trapp pathway is most compatible with this environment. **Evaporitic/tidal settings** support Kim–Benner ribosylation chemistry, which requires evaporative drying at 80–90°C with divalent metal ion catalysis, and borate minerals that stabilize ribose. **Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (serpentinizing systems) excel at NAD⁺ reduction: Henriques Pereira et al. (*Communications Chemistry* 8, 318, 2025) showed that Ni-Fe mineral surfaces reduce NAD⁺ specifically to **1,4-NADH** using H₂ gas at 40°C, pH 8.5, 5 bar H₂, while NMN lacking the AMP moiety is overreduced to biologically useless products. The AMP "handle" of NAD⁺ thus serves a functional prebiotic role—protecting against overreduction on mineral surfaces—suggesting the dinucleotide structure predates enzymes.

Mineral catalysts relevant to specific steps include:

- **Ni-Fe alloys** (awaruite, taenite): Catalyze stereospecific NAD⁺ → 1,4-NADH reduction with H₂; NiFe₃ nanopowder outperforms pure metals by ~300%
- **Iron-sulfur minerals** (pyrite, pyrrhotite): Catalyze pyruvate and related α-keto acid transformations, generating aspartate precursors via reductive amination
- **Phosphate minerals**: Catalytic role in Cleaves–Miller pathway (phosphorylated substrates yield significantly more nicotinic acid)
- **Borate minerals**: Stabilize ribose for glycosylation chemistry
- **Cu(I)/Cu(II)**: Drive photoredox cycling in cyanosulfidic networks, generating cyanoacetylene and sugar precursors

## Critical bottlenecks and unresolved challenges

**The glycosidic bond problem** remains the most severe. The N-glycosidic bond in nicotinamide riboside is intrinsically labile because the positively charged pyridinium ring acts as an excellent leaving group. Free nicotinamide riboside hydrolyzes within hours at room temperature. Kim and Benner showed that 2′-phosphorylation stabilizes the nucleoside, while Bechtel et al. required carefully controlled mild conditions (pH 6, 23°C) to preserve the bond during 5′-phosphorylation. Whether nicotinamide riboside can persist long enough under realistic geological conditions for downstream chemistry—or whether all steps must be telescoped into one-pot sequences—is unresolved.

**Regiochemistry** presents a dual challenge. Electric discharge produces all three cyanopyridine isomers, not exclusively the biologically relevant 3-isomer. And while the Kim–Benner ribosylation produces the 2′-phosphate (not the biologically relevant 5′-NMN), the Trapp group's ITO-P achieves 5′-selective phosphorylation at the cost of low yields (~2%). No prebiotic equivalent of the biological PRPP-mediated 5′-ribosylation has been demonstrated.

**The concentration problem** constrains multi-component condensation reactions. The Orgel/Chichibabin cyclization requires simultaneous presence of propiolaldehyde, cyanoacetaldehyde, and ammonia at concentrations sufficient for efficient ring closure. Evaporative concentration in ponds or tidal flats offers a solution but restricts viable geological settings. **The water problem**—thermodynamic unfavorability of condensation reactions in dilute aqueous solution—affects glycosylation, phosphoester, and pyrophosphate bond formation. Bechtel et al. overcome the pyrophosphate step with photochemical energy input, but this requires surficial UV access incompatible with deep-sea hydrothermal vent models.

Sebastianelli, Kaur, Chen, Krishnamurthy, and Mansy (*Chem. Eur. J.* 30, e202400411, 2024) demonstrated that **free nicotinamide cannot accept electrons from pyruvate**—only NMN and NAD⁺ support non-enzymatic redox cycling. The anomeric carbon (sugar attachment point) is essential for electron transfer. This places severe demands on prebiotic chemistry: the heterocycle alone is metabolically inert, and at minimum the complete nucleotide (NMN) must form before any useful redox chemistry can occur.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of nicotinamide has matured from isolated heterocycle formation to a complete, demonstrated pathway to NAD⁺ within five decades. The convergence of three independent lines of evidence—laboratory synthesis, meteoritic detection, and astrophysical ice chemistry—establishes nicotinic acid/nicotinamide as among the most prebiotically accessible cofactor precursors. The Orgel/Chichibabin route through 2-cyanoglutaconic dialdehyde and the Cleaves–Miller biomimetic aspartate pathway represent genuinely distinct chemical strategies to the same heterocycle, operating under different but overlapping environmental conditions. The Trapp group's 2024 NAD⁺ synthesis marks a watershed, yet the field remains constrained by the fundamental tension between the lability of the nicotinamide glycosidic bond and the thermodynamic demands of building the full dinucleotide in water. The emerging functional evidence—that the AMP handle protects against overreduction on mineral surfaces, and that free nicotinamide lacks redox activity—reframes the problem: prebiotic chemistry must produce not merely the heterocycle, but the complete nucleotide, before NAD⁺ can participate in the protometabolic networks that origin-of-life models require. Whether this was achieved through one-pot telescoped sequences in geochemically structured environments, or through exogenous delivery of nicotinic acid followed by terrestrial ribosylation, remains the central open question.
