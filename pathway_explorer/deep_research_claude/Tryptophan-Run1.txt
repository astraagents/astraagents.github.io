# Prebiotic synthesis of tryptophan remains the hardest unsolved problem in amino acid origins

**Tryptophan has never been synthesized from simple precursors under plausible prebiotic conditions in any laboratory experiment.** The bicyclic indole ring—a benzene ring fused to a pyrrole ring—represents the single most chemically complex side chain among the 20 canonical amino acids, and no demonstrated terrestrial prebiotic route to this scaffold exists. Among all proteinogenic amino acids, tryptophan consistently ranks last in chronological models of genetic code evolution (Trifonov, 2000, *Gene* 261:139–151), is absent from every Miller-Urey experiment ever conducted, and has never been confirmed in any meteorite until a tentative 2025 detection in pristine asteroid Bennu samples. Only two lines of direct evidence for abiotic tryptophan exist: its identification in serpentinized oceanic lithosphere at the Atlantis Massif (Ménez et al., 2018, *Nature* 564:59–63) and the 2024 demonstration that indole forms from acetylene and ammonia in interstellar ices (Kaiser et al., 2024, *JACS* 146:28437–28447). The central bottleneck is clear: **prebiotic indole ring formation on Earth has not been demonstrated**, making tryptophan the defining test case for whether the genetic code's full complement could arise without enzymatic biosynthesis.

## Two pathways show real promise, but neither is complete

The landscape of proposed prebiotic tryptophan routes spans at least seven distinct approaches, yet only two have produced tangible experimental evidence. The rest remain theoretical or have been tried and failed.

**The Ménez hydrothermal Friedel-Crafts pathway** represents the strongest single piece of evidence for abiotic tryptophan. Using synchrotron deep-UV fluorescence imaging and TOF-SIMS, Ménez et al. (2018, *Nature* 564:59–63) identified tryptophan preserved within **iron-rich saponite clay nanopores** in serpentinized peridotite recovered 173 meters below the seafloor at the Lost City hydrothermal field (Atlantis Massif, Mid-Atlantic Ridge). The proposed mechanism involves three steps: serpentinization produces H₂ and NH₃ alongside iron-rich saponite clay; the indole ring forms from these gases plus inorganic carbon within the serpentinization environment; and finally, a **Friedel-Crafts alkylation** attaches an alanyl side chain to the indole C-3 position, catalyzed by the Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺-substituted saponite acting as an acidic nanoreactor. Conditions were moderate hydrothermal temperatures (<200°C), alkaline pH, and a strongly reducing (H₂-rich) environment. Also detected were indole, skatole, and hydroxyanthranilic acid. No biomarkers were present, arguing against biological contamination. However, the finding has drawn skepticism from Jim Cleaves (Tokyo Institute of Technology) regarding whether the spectroscopic evidence definitively excludes biological contamination via fluid flow, and the chirality of the tryptophan was not determined.

**The interstellar ice photochemistry pathway** provides the first bottom-up demonstration that indole can form from the simplest possible starting materials. Kaiser et al. (2024, *JACS* 146:28437–28447) irradiated low-temperature ices (~5–10 K) of acetylene (C₂H₂) and ammonia (NH₃) with energetic particles simulating galactic cosmic rays. Using resonance-enhanced multiphoton ionization (REMPI) and tunable vacuum-UV photoionization mass spectrometry—isomer-selective techniques—they identified **indole (C₈H₇N), pyrrole (C₄H₅N), and aniline (C₆H₅NH₂)** in the gas phase during temperature-programmed desorption. Computational pathways indicate that acetylene oligomerization builds the aromatic ring, ammonia supplies nitrogen, and ring fusion of pyrrole with C₄ fragments yields indole through radical intermediates on ice surfaces. While these are interstellar conditions, the results imply indole could reach early Earth via meteorites, comets, and interplanetary dust particles. This pathway produces the indole precursor but not tryptophan itself.

The remaining proposed routes have significant limitations:

- **Strecker synthesis from indole-3-acetaldehyde**: Theoretically sound (indole-3-acetaldehyde + HCN + NH₃ → α-aminonitrile → hydrolysis → tryptophan), but requires pre-formed indole-3-acetaldehyde, which has no known prebiotic source and is itself unstable (Kirschning, 2022, *Chemistry – A European Journal* 28:e202201419).
- **Cyanosulfidic chemistry** (Patel et al., 2015, *Nature Chemistry* 7:301–307): Produces Strecker precursors for ~12 amino acids (Gly, Ala, Ser, Thr, Val, Leu, Ile, Pro, Asp, Glu, Asn) via HCN/H₂S/Cu(I)-Cu(II) photoredox cycling, but **has not been extended to any aromatic amino acid**, including tryptophan.
- **HCN oligomerization** (Ferris et al., 1978, *J. Mol. Evol.* 11:293–311; Sanchez et al., 1967, *J. Mol. Biol.* 30:223–253): Produces adenine, purines, pyrimidines, and simple amino acids at pH ~9.2, but the indole ring is too complex to arise from direct HCN polymerization.
- **Fischer indole synthesis under prebiotic conditions**: Requires phenylhydrazine, which is not a known prebiotic molecule. No researcher has proposed this as prebiotically viable.
- **Formamide chemistry** (Saladino et al., 2012, *Phys. Life Rev.* 9:84–104): Produces purines, pyrimidines, and simple amino acids when heated with mineral catalysts (CaCO₃, TiO₂, etc.), but **indole has never been reported** as a product.

## The indole ring is the central bottleneck

The question of how the bicyclic indole ring (C₈H₇N) forms prebiotically is the single most critical unsolved problem on the path to tryptophan. Indole consists of a six-membered benzene ring fused to a five-membered pyrrole ring, and forming this structure requires either sequential ring construction or fusion of pre-formed ring precursors.

**Pyrrole** can form prebiotically. Seel et al. (2021, *Life* 11:980) demonstrated that acetylene (C₂H₂) reacts with ammonia (NH₃) over NiS and CoS catalysts at **105°C** under aqueous anaerobic conditions to yield pyrrole—conditions consistent with volcanic hydrothermal environments and Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis. The Kaiser et al. (2024) interstellar ice experiments also produce pyrrole from the same feedstocks under cryogenic energetic processing. Additionally, thermal decomposition of serine yields pyrrole under certain conditions (Choughuley, referenced in prebiotic literature). However, **no direct pyrrole-to-indole conversion has been demonstrated under prebiotic conditions.** The ring annulation step—appending a benzene ring onto pyrrole—remains experimentally unverified in any geochemically plausible scenario.

**Benzene** formation from simple precursors is also documented. Acetylene trimerization to benzene occurs under UV/electrical discharge or at high temperatures (~400°C). Kaiser's group demonstrated photochemical benzene formation from acetylene ices on Pluto-like surfaces (Kleimeier et al., 2022, *Phys. Chem. Chem. Phys.* 24:1424–1436). Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) are ubiquitous in carbonaceous chondrites and the interstellar medium, confirming that aromatic ring systems form readily in astrophysical environments.

**Anthranilic acid (2-aminobenzoic acid)**, the biological branch-point from chorismate toward tryptophan, has no demonstrated prebiotic synthesis. Its formation requires a pre-existing benzene ring with both amino and carboxyl groups at ortho positions—a challenging selectivity target without enzymatic control. The entire shikimate pathway (phosphoenolpyruvate + erythrose-4-phosphate → chorismate, 7 enzymatic steps) that produces anthranilic acid in modern biology has no known abiotic equivalent (Richards et al., 2006, *Eukaryotic Cell*).

**Serine**, the co-substrate that condenses with indole in modern tryptophan synthase, can form prebiotically through multiple routes: Strecker synthesis from glycolaldehyde or glyceraldehyde + HCN + NH₃; computational RTIP pathway sampling identifies low-energy routes from CO, NH₃, H₂O, and formaldehyde with barriers of ~36–50 kcal/mol (Li et al., 2024, *JACS Au* 4:4757–4768); and it appears in trace amounts in Miller-Urey spark discharge experiments. Glyceraldehyde, a serine precursor, forms via the formose reaction (Butlerow, 1861; Breslow mechanism, 1959) from formaldehyde catalyzed by Ca(OH)₂, though with poor selectivity.

The modern tryptophan synthase reaction (indole + L-serine → L-tryptophan + H₂O) uses sophisticated **substrate channeling** through a 25 Å intramolecular tunnel between α and β subunits, preventing the hydrophobic indole from escaping through cell membranes (Palazzolo & Bhatt, 2020, *Int. J. Mol. Sci.*, PMC7699789). This highlights a fundamental problem: even biology struggles to retain free indole. In an open prebiotic system, indole would rapidly partition out of aqueous solution or evaporate (bp 254°C), making the condensation with serine kinetically challenging without some form of molecular confinement.

## Environmental conditions constrain which routes are viable

The three major prebiotic environments—alkaline hydrothermal vents, black smokers, and surface evaporitic pools—offer starkly different prospects for aromatic amino acid synthesis.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents (Lost City type)** provide the most favorable conditions. These off-axis systems operate at pH **9–11** and moderate temperatures of **40–90°C**, driven by serpentinization of olivine-rich mantle rock. H₂ concentrations reach 1–15 mmol/kg (Proskurowski et al., 2008, *Science*), and methane is produced geochemically. Chimney walls in a Hadean context would contain iron-bearing minerals: ferrobrucite, mackinawite (FeS), and greigite (Fe₃S₄) due to the anoxic, Fe²⁺-rich early ocean (Russell & Hall, 1997, *J. Geol. Soc. London* 154:377–402; Martin & Russell, 2007, *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B*). Critically, Amend & Shock (1998, *Science* 281:1659–1662) calculated that amino acid synthesis from CO₂, NH₄⁺, and H₂ is **thermodynamically favorable** at moderate hydrothermal temperatures—the reaction becomes exergonic. The Ménez et al. finding of abiotic tryptophan in Lost City-type serpentinites directly supports this environment as the most plausible site for aromatic amino acid genesis.

**Black smoker conditions** (300–407°C, pH 2–3) are largely destructive. Amino acid half-lives at 250°C range from seconds to minutes; glycine's half-life is ~2 minutes (Bada, Miller & Zhao, 1995, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 25:111–118). Phenylalanine rapidly decarboxylates at 275°C. While Wächtershäuser's pyrite-formation energy hypothesis (FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂, ΔG = −10.0 kcal/mol) and (Ni,Fe)S catalyzed thioester and peptide synthesis (Huber & Wächtershäuser, 1997, 1998, *Science* 281:670–672) are important for simple carbon fixation, black smoker temperatures would rapidly destroy any tryptophan formed. Cooler gradient zones (40–150°C) where hot and cold fluids mix represent more plausible micro-environments.

**Surface evaporitic pools** ("warm little ponds," Damer & Deamer, 2020, *Astrobiology* 20:429–452) offer critical concentration advantages through wet-dry cycling. Evaporation concentrates dilute organic solutes and drives dehydration-condensation reactions thermodynamically unfavorable in bulk water. Mineral surfaces (clays, volcanic glass) provide catalytic sites. UV radiation—roughly **1,000× higher** at the surface than today due to the absence of an ozone layer (Cockell, 2000; Cnossen et al., 2007, *J. Geophys. Res. Planets*)—serves as both an energy source and a destructive force. For tryptophan specifically, its strong UV absorption at ~280 nm makes it **photolabile** under early Earth surface conditions, meaning any tryptophan formed at the surface would be rapidly destroyed.

The early Earth atmosphere was **weakly reducing to neutral** (CO₂-N₂ dominated), not the strongly reducing (CH₄-NH₃) atmosphere Miller originally assumed (Zahnle et al., 2010, *Cold Spring Harb. Perspect. Biol.*). This less favorable atmosphere reduces spark-discharge amino acid yields, though transient reducing atmospheres after large impacts could provide windows for organic synthesis.

## Mineral catalysts that could drive key steps

**Iron-sulfur minerals** are the most prebiotically relevant catalyst family for tryptophan-related chemistry. **Pyrite (FeS₂)** photocatalyzes reductive amination of α-keto acids to amino acids under UV light, with enantiomeric excesses of **14.5–42.4%** (Nature Communications, 2024), leveraging its semiconductor properties (bandgap ~0.95 eV). **Greigite (Fe₃S₄)**, with its inverse spinel structure containing Fe₄S₄ cubane units analogous to ferredoxin active sites, shows maximal yields at 75°C in chimney simulation experiments (White et al., 2015, *Earth Planet. Sci. Lett.* 430:105–114). **Mackinawite (FeS)**, with its layered tetragonal structure, is proposed as the dominant catalyst in ancient alkaline vent chimneys and supports CO₂ activation (Santos-Carballal et al., 2018, *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. A*). Ni-doped variants like **violarite (FeNi₂S₄)** show enhanced CO₂ reduction efficiency. Collectively, these minerals catalyze carbon fixation, thioester synthesis, peptide bond formation, and reductive amination—but **none has been shown to catalyze indole ring formation**.

**Iron-rich saponite clay** stands out as the only mineral directly implicated in abiotic tryptophan formation. In the Ménez et al. (2018) work, Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺-substituted saponite nanopores provided surface acidity analogous to industrial Friedel-Crafts catalysts (Lewis acids), confinement effects that concentrated reactants, and a reducing environment conducive to aromatic ring alkylation. **Montmorillonite**, the most extensively studied prebiotic clay, catalyzes RNA oligomerization up to 50-mers (Ferris et al., 1996, *Nature* 381:59–61) and amino acid polymerization during wet-dry cycling at 80°C, but functions primarily as a concentrator and polymerization catalyst rather than a de novo amino acid synthesizer.

**Zinc sulfide (ZnS)**, with a bandgap of ~3.7 eV, drives UV-photocatalytic reductive amination of α-keto acids to amino acids (Wang et al., 2012, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*; Mulkidjanian's "Zinc World" hypothesis, 2009, *Biol. Direct* 4:26). **TiO₂** (anatase, bandgap ~3.2 eV) catalyzes nucleobase synthesis from formamide under UV light (Saladino et al., 2003) and has been shown to facilitate C-C bond formation on its surface. **Transition metal ions** (Cu²⁺, Ni²⁺, Co²⁺) drive transamination reactions between amino acids and α-keto acids without redox changes in the metal.

**No mineral-catalyzed prebiotic indole synthesis has been demonstrated.** This represents perhaps the most critical experimental gap in the field. Potential candidates include acidic clay surfaces (for Fischer-type cyclizations), Cu²⁺-exchanged clays (which can incorporate aniline guests), and ZnBr₂ (a known laboratory catalyst for indole cyclization), but all remain untested under geochemically plausible conditions.

## Experimental evidence is sparse but growing

The experimental record for prebiotic tryptophan is dominated by negative results, with two landmark positive findings.

**Miller-Urey experiments have consistently failed to produce tryptophan.** The original 1953 experiment yielded only 5 amino acids (Miller, 1953, *Science* 117:528–529). The 2008 reanalysis of Miller's sealed vials using HPLC-FD and TOF-MS found up to **22 amino acids** from the "volcanic" apparatus configuration (Johnson et al., 2008, *Science* 322:404), and the 2011 reanalysis of 1958 H₂S-containing experiments found **23 amino acids** including 7 organosulfur compounds (Parker et al., 2011, *PNAS* 108:5526–5531). In every case, the detected amino acids were exclusively **C₂–C₆ aliphatic structures**. The 10 amino acids consistently produced in these experiments correspond precisely to the "early" amino acids in genetic code evolution: Gly, Ala, Val, Asp, Glu, Pro, Ser, Leu, Thr, and Ile (Higgs & Pudritz, 2009, *Astrobiology* 9:483–490).

**Meteorite analyses tell a similar story of absence.** The Murchison meteorite (CM2 carbonaceous chondrite) contains **86+ amino acids** spanning C₂–C₉ structures (Cronin, Pizzarello, and colleagues, 1980s–1990s; Koga et al., 2017, *Scientific Reports*), but all are simple aliphatic, branched, or small cyclic structures. Tryptophan has never been detected. Ryugu asteroid samples (Hayabusa2 mission) yielded 13 amino acids, all C₂–C₆ range (Naraoka et al., 2023; Parker et al., 2023, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*). The amino acid suite in meteorites shows an **exponential decline in concentration with increasing carbon number**, and tryptophan (C₁₁H₁₂N₂O₂) far exceeds the size range of confirmed meteoritic amino acids.

The most exciting recent finding is the **tentative first detection of tryptophan in asteroid Bennu samples** returned by the OSIRIS-REx mission (Mojarro et al., 2025, *PNAS*). Earlier analyses by Glavin et al. (2025, *Nature Astronomy*) had identified 14 protein amino acids and all 5 nucleobases. Mojarro et al. reported "trace signals of a fifteenth α-amino acid, tryptophan, which has not been detected previously in extraterrestrial materials." José Aponte (NASA Goddard) noted this is "a big deal, because tryptophan is one of the more complex amino acids." Importantly, Bennu samples were collected and returned in sealed capsules, avoiding the atmospheric entry heating that Sara Russell suggests destroys fragile molecules in meteorites. The detection awaits independent confirmation.

**UV irradiation experiments** on interstellar ice analogues (Muñoz Caro et al., 2002, *Nature* 416:403–406; Bernstein et al., 2002, *Nature* 416:401–403) produced up to 16 amino acids from H₂O:CH₃OH:NH₃:CO:CO₂ ices at 12–15 K, but exclusively simple structures—no aromatic amino acids. In aqueous solution, tryptophan is **photodegraded** by UV, not synthesized (Scappini et al., 2007, *International Journal of Astrobiology* 6:269).

An interstellar detection of tryptophan was claimed by Iglesias-Groth (2023, *MNRAS* 523:2876–2886) using archived Spitzer Space Telescope mid-IR spectra of the IC 348 star cluster, but this was **effectively refuted** by JWST MIRI follow-up (Dhariwal et al., 2024, *ApJL* 968:L9), which found no tryptophan features and attributed several of the original spectral assignments to instrumental artifacts. The only confirmed amino acid in any extraterrestrial in-situ measurement remains glycine at Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko (Altwegg et al., 2016, *Science Advances*).

## Why tryptophan came last—and a new paradox

Tryptophan's status as the last amino acid incorporated into the genetic code rests on converging lines of evidence. **Trifonov's consensus chronology** (2000, *Gene* 261:139–151; updated 2004, *J. Biomol. Struct. Dyn.* 22:1–11), compiled from 40–82 independent criteria, places tryptophan unambiguously at position 20 of 20: G/A → V/D → P → S → E/L → T → R → N → K → Q → I → C → H → F → M → Y → **W**. Wong's coevolution theory (1975, *PNAS* 72:1909–1912) classifies tryptophan as a "Phase 2" amino acid that entered the code only after enzymatic biosynthetic pathways evolved. Higgs & Pudritz (2009, *Astrobiology* 9:483–490) showed that the "early 10" amino acids match those produced prebiotically, while the "late 10" (including Trp) were never observed in any prebiotic experiment—with concentrations predicted to fall below detection limits based on exponential decline with Gibbs free energy of formation.

The thermodynamic and kinetic barriers are substantial. Amino acid formation from CO₂, NH₄⁺, and H₂ under surface conditions is **endergonic for all amino acids**, but tryptophan's ΔG_surf is among the highest (Amend & Shock, 1998, *Science* 281:1659–1662). Modern tryptophan biosynthesis requires **~78 ATP equivalents**—the highest of any amino acid (Akashi & Gojobori, 2002, *PNAS* 99:3695–3700). The indole ring's formation requires precise molecular geometry; industrial syntheses typically demand 200–500°C with specialized catalysts. Tryptophan is encoded by a single codon (UGG)—the only amino acid besides methionine with zero codon degeneracy—suggesting late capture of remaining codon space.

Tryptophan is also selectively vulnerable to degradation. While thermally stable under anoxic conditions at 90–140°C for 24 hours, it is **highly susceptible to oxidation** by reactive oxygen species, UV light, and metal-catalyzed reactions. Hydrothermal degradation studies (Sheehan & Savage, subcritical water at 265–310°C) show that the aromatic indole ring itself is remarkably resistant, but the α-carbon backbone degrades via decarboxylation (to tryptamine) and deamination. This stability profile creates a paradox: tryptophan is stable enough to persist in certain environments but is destroyed by the very conditions (UV, oxidants) that drive much prebiotic chemistry.

A provocative 2024 finding challenges the simple "late addition" narrative. **Wehbi et al. (2024, *PNAS*, doi:10.1073/pnas.2410311121)** analyzed protein domains attributable to the Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA) and found that **pre-LUCA protein sequences show surprisingly high frequencies of aromatic amino acids, including tryptophan**. The authors note that "early life seems to have liked rings" and that small amino acid size predicts ancient enrichment better than Trifonov's consensus ranking. Metal-binding amino acids (Cys, His) appear to have been incorporated much earlier than previously thought. The highest tryptophan-frequency pre-LUCA domain is PF00133 (core catalytic domain of aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases), where conserved Trp residues regulate binding pocket geometry. This raises an unresolved tension: if tryptophan was truly the last amino acid added, why do the most ancient reconstructed protein domains contain it at relatively high frequency?

## Conclusion: critical gaps define the research frontier

The prebiotic synthesis of tryptophan distills the hardest open problems in origin-of-life chemistry into a single molecule. The field has converged on several key insights. First, **terrestrial prebiotic indole ring formation is the make-or-break problem**—no abiotic route to indole under geochemically plausible surface or hydrothermal conditions has been experimentally demonstrated on Earth. Second, **extraterrestrial delivery of indole is now experimentally supported** (Kaiser et al., 2024), creating a viable exogenous source, but the subsequent aqueous-phase coupling of indole with serine or an equivalent amino acid backbone remains undemonstrated. Third, the **Ménez et al. (2018) finding of abiotic tryptophan in serpentinite** is the strongest single piece of evidence that the molecule can form without enzymes, but it relies on Fe-saponite Friedel-Crafts catalysis whose mechanism has not been replicated in laboratory synthesis. Fourth, the tentative Bennu detection (Mojarro et al., 2025) hints that pristine extraterrestrial samples may contain tryptophan that atmospheric entry heating destroys in meteorites.

The most productive experimental directions are: (1) attempting mineral-catalyzed indole synthesis from pyrrole + C₄ fragments or from aniline + acetaldehyde on acidic clay or metal sulfide surfaces at 100–200°C; (2) replicating the Ménez Friedel-Crafts pathway in laboratory hydrothermal reactors with Fe-saponite, H₂, NH₃, and CO₂; (3) testing whether indole delivered to warm aqueous pools with serine, HCN, and mineral catalysts can produce tryptophan through Strecker or condensation chemistry; and (4) systematic screening of HCN polymer and formamide thermolysis products for indole using the isomer-selective mass spectrometric techniques Kaiser's group pioneered. The Wehbi et al. paradox of high pre-LUCA tryptophan frequency demands resolution—either through discovery of efficient abiotic routes or through revision of our models of early protein composition. Tryptophan remains, as Kirschning (2022) noted, the amino acid whose prebiotic provenance "must be considered speculative," and closing this gap is essential for any complete account of life's chemical origins.
