# Prebiotic synthesis of tyrosine: the hardest amino acid to make from scratch

**Tyrosine is among the most difficult proteinogenic amino acids to synthesize under prebiotic conditions, and no single-step prebiotic experiment has ever produced it directly from simple molecules.** The fundamental bottleneck is de novo aromatic ring formation — assembling a hydroxylated benzene ring from C₁–C₂ feedstocks without enzymatic catalysis. Across six decades of prebiotic chemistry experiments, tyrosine has been absent from every standard spark discharge, hydrothermal simulation, and HCN polymerization experiment. Only two experimental approaches have yielded tyrosine: Friedmann and Miller's (1969) multi-step pathway via phenylacetylene intermediates, and Chen et al.'s (2008) UV photolysis of PAH-containing ices — both requiring preformed aromatic precursors. Thermodynamic analysis reveals why: tyrosine synthesis is strongly endergonic under surface conditions (**ΔG = +334 kJ/mol** at 18°C, 1 atm) but becomes exergonic (**ΔG = −60 kJ/mol**) at hydrothermal temperatures and pressures. This thermodynamic duality, combined with recent detection of tyrosine in pristine asteroid Bennu samples (2025) and abiotic aromatic amino acids in serpentinized ocean crust, suggests that hydrothermal and asteroidal environments are the most plausible settings for prebiotic tyrosine formation.

---

## Spark discharge experiments consistently fail to produce tyrosine

The foundational Miller-Urey experiment (Miller, 1953, *Science*) and all subsequent standard spark discharge experiments have failed to detect tyrosine among their products. The original 1953 experiment using CH₄/NH₃/H₂/H₂O detected only glycine, alanine, β-alanine, aspartic acid, and α-aminobutyric acid. Modern reanalysis of Miller's archived samples using HPLC and LC-ToF-MS (Johnson et al., 2008, *Science*; Parker et al., 2011, *PNAS*) expanded the detected amino acid inventory to **22–23 species** — but tyrosine remained conspicuously absent. The comprehensive Ring, Wolman, Friedmann, and Miller (1972, *PNAS*) electric discharge study confirmed 19+ amino acids with no aromatic amino acids detected whatsoever.

The single landmark positive result is Friedmann and Miller (1969, *Science*), who demonstrated phenylalanine and tyrosine synthesis through a deliberate **multi-step pathway** rather than one-pot discharge chemistry. Their route proceeded through: (1) generation of phenylacetylene from hydrocarbon pyrolysis/discharge; (2) radical addition of H₂S to phenylacetylene followed by hydrolysis to yield phenylacetaldehyde; (3) Strecker synthesis (phenylacetaldehyde + HCN + NH₃ → phenylalanine nitrile → phenylalanine). Tyrosine was obtained as a "small yield" side product, likely via hydroxylation during the H₂S-mediated step. Miller himself explicitly stated in subsequent reviews (1974, 1987) that "the prebiotic synthesis of phenylalanine, tyrosine and tryptophan involves **pyrolysis reactions combined with plausible solution reactions**" — acknowledging these aromatic amino acids require fundamentally different chemistry from the simple amino acids that form spontaneously in discharge experiments.

Even experiments with different atmospheric compositions — neutral CO₂/N₂ atmospheres with oxidation inhibitors (Cleaves et al., 2008, *Origins Life Evol. Biosph.*), H₂S-rich volcanic gas mixtures (Parker et al., 2011, *PNAS*), and cyanamide-enriched systems (Parker et al., 2014, *Angewandte Chemie*) — consistently produced only aliphatic amino acids. The yield hierarchy in all spark discharge experiments follows: **glycine >> alanine > aspartic acid > aminobutyric acids > valine, leucine >> aromatic amino acids (not detected)**.

## UV photolysis and the PAH precursor requirement

Standard UV photolysis experiments using simple ice mixtures (H₂O, CH₃OH, NH₃, CO, HCN) at 10–15 K produce glycine, alanine, serine, and a handful of other simple amino acids, but never tyrosine. Bernstein et al. (2002, *Nature*) and Muñoz Caro et al. (2002, *Nature*) — the two seminal studies on amino acid formation in interstellar ice analogs — reported no aromatic amino acids. When Nuevo et al. (2008, *Origins Life Evol. Biosph.*) tentatively identified a tyrosine peak in VUV-irradiated ice residues, they retracted the claim, noting it was "probably due to other compounds eluting from the column with the same retention times (coelution)" and that "the photoproduction of aromatic rings from aliphatic compounds is not an efficient process."

The critical exception is Chen et al. (2008, *Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society*), who irradiated **naphthalene** (C₁₀H₈, a polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon) mixed with H₂O and NH₃ ice at 15 K using synchrotron UV/EUV photons (4–20 eV). This experiment detected **13 amino acids including tyrosine and phenylalanine**. The key distinction is that the starting material already contained aromatic rings — the PAH served as a structural template, requiring only fragmentation and functionalization rather than de novo ring construction. Alanine was the most abundant product at **2.2 nmol**, with phenylalanine the dominant aromatic amino acid. This result demonstrates that wherever PAHs and ammonia coexist in icy environments exposed to UV radiation (interstellar clouds, cometary surfaces, outer solar system ices), aromatic amino acid formation is chemically feasible.

## Meteorite detections confirm extraterrestrial tyrosine formation

The history of tyrosine detection in meteorites traces a revealing arc from confident absence to confirmed presence. Early analyses of the Murchison meteorite (Kvenvolden et al., 1970, *Nature*; Engel and Nagy, 1982, *Nature*) explicitly reported tyrosine as absent, and its absence was used as evidence against terrestrial contamination — the logic being that biological contamination would introduce all common protein amino acids. Through the 1990s, meteorite amino acid inventories focused on C₂–C₇ species, and tyrosine (a C₉ amino acid) fell outside the analytical window.

As methods improved and analyses extended to higher carbon numbers, tyrosine entered the comprehensive meteorite amino acid inventory. Glavin et al. (2018, Elsevier chapter) listed tyrosine among C₉ amino acids detected across multiple studies, and Elsila et al. (2016, *ACS Central Science*) confirmed that **12 of 20 protein amino acids have been found in meteorites, including tyrosine**. However, no individual concentration measurement for tyrosine in Murchison has been published — it is presumably at **sub-ppb to very low ppb levels**, orders of magnitude below glycine (~3,000 ppb) and α-aminoisobutyric acid (~1,000–6,000 ppb).

The strongest evidence for extraterrestrial tyrosine came in 2025 from NASA's OSIRIS-REx mission. Mojarro et al. (2025, *PNAS*) explicitly detected tyrosine in asteroid Bennu aggregates using pyrolysis-GC-MS with MRM mass spectrometry, confirmed by three independent transitions and analytical standards. Separately, Glavin et al. (2025, *Nature Astronomy*) reported tentative detection among **33 total amino acids** identified in Bennu samples, with overall racemic (D ≈ L) composition supporting abiotic origin. Crucially, the Bennu team proposed that aromatic amino acids formed not via Strecker synthesis but through **Friedel-Crafts alkylation** of aromatic hydrocarbons during aqueous alteration on the parent body — a mechanism consistent with Ménez et al.'s (2018) findings in terrestrial serpentinites.

Ryugu samples (Hayabusa2) did not yield tyrosine, with amino acid detection limited to C₂–C₆ species (Parker et al., 2023, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*; Naraoka et al., 2023, *Science*). No enantiomeric excess data exists specifically for tyrosine in any extraterrestrial sample.

## Hydrothermal vents offer thermodynamic viability but no experimental proof

**No published hydrothermal vent simulation experiment has produced tyrosine.** Marshall (1994, *GCA*), Hennet et al. (1992, *Naturwissenschaften*), and Huber and Wächtershäuser (2006, *Science*) all produced only simple amino acids (glycine, alanine, serine analogs). Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world framework, while demonstrating activated acetic acid, peptide formation, and simple amino acid synthesis on (Ni,Fe)S catalysts, explicitly does not extend to aromatic amino acid synthesis.

However, thermodynamic calculations tell a strikingly different story. Amend and Shock (1998, *Science*) showed that amino acid synthesis from CO₂, H₂, NH₃, and H₂S becomes **thermodynamically favorable under hydrothermal conditions**. Higgs and Pudritz (2009, *Astrobiology*) compiled these data, showing tyrosine's Gibbs energy of formation shifts from **+334.20 kJ/mol at surface conditions (18°C, 1 atm)** to **−59.53 kJ/mol at hydrothermal conditions (100°C, 250 atm)**. This ~400 kJ/mol swing makes hydrothermal environments the only known setting where tyrosine synthesis is thermodynamically downhill.

The most compelling evidence for hydrothermal aromatic amino acid synthesis comes from Ménez et al. (2018, *Nature*), who discovered abiotic **tryptophan** preserved 173 meters below the seafloor in serpentinized rocks at the Atlantis Massif — the same geological setting as the Lost City hydrothermal field. The synthesis was attributed to **Friedel-Crafts reactions catalyzed by iron-rich saponite clay** during a late alteration stage. While tyrosine was not specifically identified, the same catalytic mechanism could plausibly generate it through analogous alkylation chemistry. Lost City conditions — **40–116°C, pH 9–11**, rich in H₂ (up to 15 mM) and CH₄, with abundant iron-bearing minerals — appear optimal for both synthesis and preservation.

Black smoker vents (**300–400°C, pH 2–5**) are far less favorable. Tyrosine decomposes rapidly above 200°C through decarboxylation (forming tyramine) and deamination. Sato et al. (2004, *Industrial & Engineering Chemistry Research*) measured phenylalanine (structurally analogous) decomposition at 200–340°C/20 MPa with activation energy of **~154 kJ/mol**. At 250°C, aromatic amino acid half-lives are on the order of **minutes to hours**. The hydroxyl group on tyrosine's ring weakens the aromatic backbone compared to phenylalanine, making tyrosine particularly degradation-prone among aromatic amino acids (Körner et al., 2021, *ChemSusChem*). One mitigating factor: dissolved H₂ suppresses amino acid decomposition (Lee et al., 2014), and dissolved metal salts at extreme pH may further inhibit degradation (Aspin et al., 2025, *GCA*).

## Mineral catalysts and the path through aromatic intermediates

The mineral catalysis landscape for tyrosine synthesis involves three distinct challenges: aromatic ring formation, functionalization to a phenylalanine/tyrosine precursor, and hydroxylation.

**Iron-rich saponite clay** emerges as the most important catalyst for aromatic amino acid synthesis. Ménez et al. (2018, *Nature*) demonstrated it catalyzes Friedel-Crafts reactions producing tryptophan abiotically in serpentinized ocean crust. Saponite is a trioctahedral smectite clay abundant in serpentinites and carbonaceous chondrite parent bodies. Montmorillonite K10, a related acid clay, is a known industrial Friedel-Crafts catalyst, though it has not been specifically tested for prebiotic aromatic amino acid synthesis.

**Iron oxyhydroxide minerals** (mixed-valence Fe(II)/Fe(III)) catalyze reductive amination of α-keto acids to amino acids under alkaline, moderate-temperature conditions. Barge et al. (2019, *PNAS*) demonstrated alanine synthesis from pyruvate at **50–70°C**, but this has not been extended to aromatic keto acid substrates like 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate. If 4-hydroxyphenylpyruvate could be generated, its transamination to tyrosine on iron oxyhydroxide surfaces would be a plausible final step.

**For aromatic ring formation**, acetylene cyclotrimerization (3 C₂H₂ → C₆H₆, ΔG = −142 kcal/mol) is catalyzed by multiple prebiotically available metals. Fe⁺ cations drive barrierless gas-phase cyclotrimerization (Shuman et al., Takahashi et al., 2022, *Molecules*). Ag(111) surfaces achieve **100% selective cyclotrimerization at temperatures as low as ~90 K** (2024, *Chemical Science*). Ni, Co, Cu, and Pd also catalyze this reaction. Acetylene itself is a plausible prebiotic molecule — it forms from methane pyrolysis and is explicitly included as a starting material in Patel et al.'s (2015) cyanosulfidic chemistry.

**For hydroxylation** (benzene → phenol, or phenylalanine → tyrosine), the most relevant mineral systems are:

- **TiO₂ photocatalysis**: Generates hydroxyl radicals (OH•) under UV irradiation, well-established for aromatic ring hydroxylation in environmental chemistry but not yet demonstrated for Phe → Tyr specifically
- **Fenton chemistry on iron minerals**: Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → OH• can hydroxylate aromatic rings. Mixed-valence iron minerals (magnetite, green rust) in vent systems could generate H₂O₂ via partial oxygen reduction
- **ZnS photocatalysis**: Wang et al. (2012, 2013, *Origins Life Evol. Biosph.*; *Int. J. Astrobiology*) demonstrated UV-irradiated ZnS catalyzes reversible amination of α-keto acids, relevant to amino acid/keto acid interconversion but not tested for aromatic substrates

Pyrite (FeS₂) and mackinawite (FeS), while central to iron-sulfur world chemistry, have not demonstrated catalytic activity for aromatic ring formation. Their primary prebiotic roles remain electron transfer, surface adsorption, and simple carbon fixation (CO₂ → CO, N₂ → NH₄⁺).

## The aromatic ring problem and cyanosulfidic chemistry's limits

The central unsolved problem in prebiotic tyrosine synthesis is constructing the aromatic ring from simple feedstocks. Modern biology uses the shikimate pathway — seven enzymatic steps converting phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) and erythrose-4-phosphate (E4P) to chorismate, then additional steps to tyrosine via prephenate or arogenate. **No non-enzymatic version of this pathway has been demonstrated**, though individual steps show prebiotic potential. The chorismate → prephenate conversion is a Claisen rearrangement (a pericyclic reaction that proceeds thermally), and chorismate mutase merely accelerates it **~10⁶-fold**. Coggins and Powner (2016, *Nature Chemistry*) achieved prebiotic synthesis of PEP — the highest-energy phosphate in biology and a shikimate pathway substrate — from glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde, providing one entry point.

Sutherland's cyanosulfidic chemistry (Patel et al., 2015, *Nature Chemistry*) represents the most sophisticated prebiotic synthesis network yet demonstrated, producing precursors for RNA, lipids, and amino acids from HCN, H₂S, and phosphate under UV irradiation with Cu(I)/Cu(II) photoredox cycling. However, it generates Strecker precursors for only **glycine, alanine, serine, and threonine** — all derivable from C₁–C₃ aldehydes. **Neither Sutherland nor Powner has published a cyanosulfidic route to any aromatic amino acid.** The network provides PEP and sugar phosphates that could theoretically feed into a shikimate-like pathway, but the connection to aromatic ring assembly remains a critical gap.

HCN polymerization produces complex mixtures containing some aromatic carbon (evidenced by ¹³C NMR resonances at 135–142 ppm), but free aromatic amino acids have not been convincingly released from HCN polymer hydrolysis (Ferris et al., 1960s–1980s; Ruiz-Bermejo et al., 2021, *Processes*). The Diels-Alder [4+2] cycloaddition is a powerful ring-forming reaction (1,3-butadiene + dienophile → cyclohexene), but its application to prebiotic aromatic compound formation remains largely theoretical.

Based on current evidence, the most plausible complete prebiotic pathway to tyrosine combines atmospheric/surface chemistry with aqueous processing:

1. **CH₄ → C₂H₂** (acetylene) via spark discharge or pyrolysis
2. **3 C₂H₂ → C₆H₆** (benzene) via metal-catalyzed cyclotrimerization
3. **C₆H₆ → C₆H₅OH** (phenol) via radical hydroxylation (OH• from Fenton chemistry or UV photolysis)
4. **Phenol + C₂ unit → 4-hydroxyphenylacetaldehyde** via Friedel-Crafts alkylation or radical addition
5. **4-HPA + HCN + NH₃ → tyrosine** via Strecker synthesis

This is essentially the pathway implied by Friedmann and Miller (1969), combining high-energy atmospheric chemistry with aqueous solution chemistry. An alternative route proceeds through phenylalanine with a final hydroxylation step.

## Tyrosine ranks among the last amino acids in genetic code evolution

Tyrosine's prebiotic scarcity is mirrored in its late incorporation into the genetic code. Trifonov's consensus chronology (2000, *Gene*; 2004, *J. Biomol. Struct. Dynam.*), built from 40–82 independent criteria, ranks tyrosine **18th of 20** — preceded only by tryptophan. Higgs and Pudritz (2009, *Astrobiology*) classified tyrosine among the 10 "late" amino acids (Lys, Phe, Arg, His, Asn, Gln, Cys, Tyr, Met, Trp), with an observational concentration of **Crel = 0** — meaning it was never detected in any prebiotic synthesis experiment they surveyed. The 10 "early" amino acids (Gly, Ala, Asp, Glu, Val, Ser, Ile, Leu, Pro, Thr) have a mean formation energy of **169 ± 42 kJ/mol**, while the late 10 average **285 ± 94 kJ/mol**. Tyrosine at +334 kJ/mol exceeds even the late-group average.

The correlation between prebiotic availability and genetic code chronology is reinforced by biosynthetic cost data. Tyrosine requires **~50 ATP equivalents** for biological synthesis (Akashi and Gojobori, 2002, *PNAS*), compared to ~11.7 for glycine. Only tryptophan is more expensive at 74.3 ATP. Wong's coevolution theory (1975, 2005) proposes that late amino acids were added to the code as biosynthetic pathways evolved — consistent with tyrosine requiring the complex shikimate pathway, which could only emerge after sophisticated enzyme catalysis existed. Philip and Freeland (2011, *Astrobiology*) showed that 12 standard amino acids (including tyrosine) are not found in the Murchison meteorite and likely entered biology through metabolic innovation rather than environmental availability.

Racemization rates present an additional challenge. Tyrosine, with its aromatic β-substituent facilitating α-proton abstraction, is among the most readily racemized amino acids (Bada and Schroeder, early 1970s). At 25°C and pH 7.6, racemization half-lives are **~10⁵–10⁶ years**, with Cu²⁺ ions accelerating rates ~100-fold. Any prebiotic enantiomeric excess from circularly polarized light or other asymmetric influences would be rapidly erased on geological timescales.

## Environmental synthesis map and the most plausible formation settings

Integrating all evidence, different prebiotic environments contribute to different steps of a hypothetical tyrosine synthesis network:

| Synthesis step | Most favorable environment | Key evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Acetylene formation (CH₄ → C₂H₂) | Atmospheric discharge, volcanic lightning | Miller experiments, pyrolysis chemistry |
| Benzene formation (3 C₂H₂ → C₆H₆) | Metal-rich mineral surfaces | Fe⁺-catalyzed cyclotrimerization (Takahashi et al., 2022) |
| Phenol formation (C₆H₆ → C₆H₅OH) | UV-exposed surface pools, Fenton chemistry | Radical hydroxylation at mineral-water interfaces |
| Aromatic amino acid assembly | Serpentinite-hosted hydrothermal systems | Friedel-Crafts on Fe-rich saponite (Ménez et al., 2018) |
| Strecker amino acid synthesis | Warm alkaline pools (~50–70°C) | Friedmann & Miller (1969) |
| PAH → amino acid conversion | Asteroidal parent bodies, cometary ices | Chen et al. (2008); Bennu detection (Mojarro et al., 2025) |

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents (Lost City type)** emerge as the thermodynamically optimal environment: moderate temperatures (40–116°C), alkaline pH (9–11), H₂-rich fluids (suppressing decomposition), iron-bearing clay minerals for Friedel-Crafts catalysis, and favorable ΔG for amino acid synthesis. **Exogenous delivery via carbonaceous meteorites and comets** provides a complementary source, with PAH-rich interstellar material undergoing UV processing and aqueous alteration to yield trace tyrosine alongside other amino acids. The detection of tyrosine in Bennu samples via Friedel-Crafts alkylation during aqueous alteration on the parent body validates this pathway.

Volcanic/fumarolic environments contribute primarily to precursor generation — methane pyrolysis to acetylene, H₂S for thiol-mediated chemistry, and metal sulfide mineral surfaces. Surface evaporitic pools offer UV exposure for photochemistry and wet-dry cycling for concentration, but amino acid stability under UV is limited (Scappini et al., 2007, *Int. J. Astrobiology*).

## Conclusion: what remains unknown and what matters most

The prebiotic synthesis of tyrosine remains one of the outstanding challenges in origin-of-life chemistry, with several critical steps undemonstrated. **No experiment has achieved tyrosine synthesis from simple molecules (H₂O, CO₂, NH₃, HCN, CH₄) in a single continuous process.** The three most urgent open problems are: (1) demonstrating prebiotic benzene/phenol formation from C₁–C₂ molecules under plausible conditions, not just thermodynamic favorability; (2) achieving regioselective para-hydroxylation of an aromatic ring without enzymatic catalysis; and (3) connecting cyanosulfidic or hydrothermal network chemistry to aromatic ring assembly.

The field has converged on two most-plausible formation scenarios. First, the **Friedmann-Miller atmospheric pathway**: methane discharge → acetylene → benzene (cyclotrimerization) → phenol (hydroxylation) → Strecker synthesis → tyrosine. Second, the **hydrothermal/asteroidal Friedel-Crafts pathway**: PAHs + ammonia + water undergo iron-clay-catalyzed alkylation to yield aromatic amino acids directly, as evidenced by Ménez et al.'s (2018) findings in serpentinites and the Bennu detection. These two routes are not mutually exclusive — atmospheric processing could generate aromatic precursors that are subsequently functionalized in aqueous hydrothermal systems.

The thermodynamic data provide perhaps the most actionable insight for future experimental work: tyrosine synthesis shifts from strongly endergonic to exergonic between surface and hydrothermal conditions, a **~400 kJ/mol swing** that represents the single largest thermodynamic leverage point. Experiments targeting tyrosine synthesis should therefore focus on **moderate hydrothermal conditions (50–120°C, alkaline pH, H₂-rich) with iron-bearing clay catalysts** — the conditions where thermodynamics, mineral catalysis, and empirical evidence for abiotic aromatic amino acid formation most strongly converge. The recent demonstration that all 20 amino acid residues can be incorporated into prebiotically synthesized peptides (Foden et al., 2020, *Nature*) shows that once tyrosine is available, even at trace levels, it can be integrated into prebiotic protopeptide chemistry.
