# Prebiotic synthesis of guanine: routes, evidence, and unsolved puzzles

**Guanine (C₅H₅N₅O) is among the hardest canonical nucleobases to synthesize under plausible prebiotic conditions**, consistently produced at 10–40× lower yields than adenine across virtually all known pathways. Yet extraterrestrial samples tell a different story: recent meteorite analyses reveal guanine as the most abundant purine nucleobase in some carbonaceous chondrites, reaching **649 ppb in Murchison** (Koga et al. 2024, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*). This paradox — difficult terrestrial synthesis but abundant extraterrestrial occurrence — frames one of prebiotic chemistry's central puzzles. Multiple synthesis routes now exist, from Oró's classic HCN oligomerization to Carell's high-yielding formamidopyrimidine (FaPy) pathway, but each carries constraints on environmental context, reagent availability, and selectivity. This report covers the full landscape of known pathways, intermediates, conditions, catalysts, experimental evidence, astrochemical context, and open challenges relevant to building synthesis network models for guanine.

---

## HCN oligomerization: the foundational pathway from five molecules of HCN

The prebiotic synthesis of purines from hydrogen cyanide remains the most thoroughly studied route, originating with John Oró's landmark 1960 demonstration that concentrated ammonium cyanide produces adenine. Guanine synthesis via this route was definitively established only in 1999, when Levy, Miller, and Oró showed that NH₄CN polymerization yields guanine at both **+80°C and −20°C**, albeit at **10–40× lower yields** than adenine (*Journal of Molecular Evolution* 49, 165–168).

The stepwise mechanism, refined by Ferris and Orgel through the 1960s, proceeds through four oligomerization steps before a critical branching point:

**Step 1–4 (shared trunk):** HCN dimerizes to iminoacetonitrile, adds a third HCN to form aminomalononitrile (AMN), then a fourth to produce **diaminomaleonitrile (DAMN)** — a stable, crystalline HCN tetramer (C₄H₄N₄). DAMN formation requires HCN concentrations above ~0.01 M, below which hydrolysis to formamide dominates. Under UV irradiation (~350 nm), DAMN photoisomerizes to its trans-isomer (diaminofumaronitrile, DAFN), which thermally cyclizes to **4-aminoimidazole-5-carbonitrile (AICN)** — with conversion efficiencies of **77–82%** (Ferris & Orgel 1966, *Journal of the American Chemical Society* 88, 1074). Quantum yields for the DAMN→DAFN step are approximately 0.045, and for DAFN→AICN approximately 0.0034 (Szabla et al. 2014, *Chemistry – A European Journal*).

**Step 5 — the critical divergence:** AICN is the central branch point determining whether adenine or guanine forms. If AICN reacts with HCN or formamidine before hydrolysis, it yields **adenine** via ring closure. If AICN undergoes hydrolysis first, the nitrile group converts to an amide, producing **AICA (4-aminoimidazole-5-carboxamide)**. AICA then reacts with cyanogen (NCCN), cyanate (OCN⁻), or urea to close the pyrimidine ring and yield **guanine** (Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel 1968, *Journal of Molecular Biology* 38, 121–128). This hydrolysis requirement is the fundamental reason guanine yields trail adenine — aqueous conditions favor hydrolysis but also compete with the HCN addition that leads to adenine.

The overall pentamerization 5 HCN → adenine is highly exothermic (ΔG₂₉₈ = **−53.7 kcal/mol**; Roy, Najafian & Schleyer 2007, *PNAS*). For guanine, the thermodynamics are similarly favorable but the kinetic pathway is longer and requires an oxygen-incorporating step. Computational analysis by Jung and Choe (2018, *Chemical Physics Letters*) mapped the potential energy surface for guanine formation from AICN + H₂O + NCCN, finding high gas-phase barriers that are significantly reduced by water molecule catalysis.

## Formamide routes produce guanine through a self-catalytic network

Formamide (HCONH₂) serves as both solvent and reactant — a liquid between 4–211°C that thermally decomposes to HCN + H₂O, generating the same oligomerization intermediates as aqueous HCN chemistry but in a concentrated, non-aqueous medium. The Italian school led by Raffaele Saladino and Ernesto Di Mauro systematically explored formamide-based nucleobase synthesis beginning in 2001.

Heating neat formamide at **160°C for 48 hours** with mineral catalysts (TiO₂, CaCO₃, montmorillonite, iron-sulfur minerals, meteorite powders) produces panels of nucleobases, amino acids, and carboxylic acids whose composition depends on the specific catalyst (Saladino et al. 2001, *Bioorganic & Medicinal Chemistry*; 2003, *ChemBioChem*; 2008, *JACS* 130, 15512). Guanine yields from thermal-only formamide experiments are generally trace to sub-percent, with adenine and hypoxanthine more abundant. However, **UV irradiation dramatically enhances guanine production**. Barks, Hud, Orlando, and Di Mauro (2010, *ChemBioChem* 11, 1240–1243) demonstrated that neat formamide irradiated at **254 nm and 130°C** produces adenine, hypoxanthine, and guanine even without mineral catalysts, effectively "relaxing the requirements" for purine nucleobase formation.

Proton irradiation experiments simulating cosmic ray bombardment represent another energy source. Saladino et al. (2015, *PNAS* 112, E2746–E2755) irradiated formamide with **165 MeV proton beams** in the presence of powdered meteorites and detected an extraordinarily rich product panel: amino acids, carboxylic acids, nucleobases (including guanine), sugars, and four nucleosides. High-energy laser-induced plasma experiments by Ferus et al. (2015, *PNAS* 112, 657–662) similarly produced all four canonical nucleobases from formamide, establishing radical cyanide (CN•) pathways as a "unified mechanism" for nucleobase formation under impact conditions.

Computational work by Slavova et al. (2020, *International Journal of Quantum Chemistry*) mapped complete reaction pathways from pure formamide to all six major purines (adenine, guanine, hypoxanthine, xanthine, isoguanine, 2,6-diaminopurine), showing that all needed reactants and catalysts — HCN, NH₃, H₂O, formic acid, urea, formimidic acid — arise from partial decomposition of formamide itself, constituting a self-catalytic system.

## Carell's FaPy pathway achieves 60% nucleoside yield with strict regioselectivity

The most significant recent advance in prebiotic guanine chemistry comes from Thomas Carell's group at LMU Munich. In 2016, Becker, Thoma, Deutsch, Gehrke, Mayer, Zipse, and Carell reported that **formamidopyrimidines (FaPys) condense with ribose to produce N9-purine nucleosides with ~60% yield and extreme regioselectivity** (*Science* 352, 833–836). This addressed the long-standing "glycosylation problem" — the difficulty of attaching purine bases to ribose with correct regiochemistry.

The FaPy precursors are themselves prebiotically accessible: HCN oligomers react with guanidine and formic acid to yield aminopyrimidines, which are formylated to produce FaPy intermediates. For guanine specifically, the FaPy derived from **2,5,6-triamino-4-hydroxypyrimidine** yields guanosine with correct N9-regiochemistry upon dry-state condensation with ribose, followed by rehydration under basic conditions.

The Carell group extended this work in two landmark papers. Becker et al. (2018, *Nature Communications* 9, 163) demonstrated that **wet-dry cycles** — a regular feature of geothermal field environments — drive the entire sequence: aminopyrimidines react with sodium nitrite in the dry state to form nitroso-pyrimidines (stable, high-melting, insoluble compounds that physically enrich by precipitation). Reduction with elemental iron and nickel in dilute formic acid yields FaPys, which condense with ribose upon drying to produce canonical and non-canonical purine nucleosides. Three sequential physical enrichment steps driven by solubility differences provide a natural purification mechanism.

In 2019, Becker et al. (*Science* 366, 76–82) connected the purine pathway with pyrimidine synthesis, demonstrating the first **unified prebiotically plausible synthesis of all four Watson-Crick nucleosides** (adenosine, guanosine, cytidine, uridine) plus their 5′-mono- and diphosphates under consistent geochemical conditions. Hydroxylamine — formed from nitrite and sulfite at ~70°C in 27–37% yield — serves as the key starting material linking all pathways. This represents the current state of the art in prebiotic nucleotide synthesis.

## Cyanosulfidic chemistry produces adenine but not guanine

The elegant cyanosulfidic network developed by John Sutherland's group at MRC Cambridge has attracted enormous attention since the landmark Patel et al. (2015) paper demonstrating common origins of RNA, protein, and lipid precursors from HCN derivatives, H₂S, and UV-driven Cu(I)–Cu(II) photoredox cycling (*Nature Chemistry* 7, 301–307). However, **this network does not produce guanine or guanosine**. The purine branch yields adenosine, deoxyadenosine, inosine (hypoxanthine base), and deoxyinosine, but not guanine-containing species (Xu et al. 2020, *Nature* 582, 60–66; Xu et al. 2021, *JACS*). Guanine incorporation into the cyanosulfidic framework remains an open challenge.

Matthew Powner's contributions include chemoselective multicomponent assembly of purine precursors in water (2011, *JACS*), demonstrating pH-dependent reactions using 2-aminooxazole and 5-aminoimidazoles that produce only N9-purination — a key regiochemical requirement. Stairs et al. (2017, *Nature Communications* 8, 15270) showed divergent synthesis of pyrimidine and **8-oxo-purine ribonucleotides** from common precursors, offering an alternative where 8-oxo-guanine nucleotides, which are well-tolerated in Watson-Crick base pairs with cytosine, might have served as primitive guanine surrogates.

## Key intermediates form a branching network connecting all purines

The pathway from simple molecules to guanine passes through a network of intermediates that also connect to adenine, hypoxanthine, xanthine, and non-canonical purines. Understanding these branch points is essential for synthesis network modeling.

| Intermediate | Formula | Role | Branch point? |
|---|---|---|---|
| HCN | CHN | C₁N₁ building block | — |
| Aminomalononitrile (AMN) | C₃H₃N₃ | HCN trimer; highly reactive | Minor |
| **DAMN** (diaminomaleonitrile) | C₄H₄N₄ | HCN tetramer; stable crystalline solid | **Major**: UV → AICN; thermal → polymers; + formamidine → pentamer |
| **AICN** (4-aminoimidazole-5-carbonitrile) | C₄H₄N₄ | Central purine precursor; photostable | **Major**: + HCN → adenine; + H₂O → AICA → guanine |
| **AICA** (4-aminoimidazole-5-carboxamide) | C₄H₆N₄O | Hydrolysis product of AICN | **Major**: + cyanogen → guanine; + cyanate → hypoxanthine |
| Formamidine | CH₄N₂ | One-carbon N-donor | Directs to adenine |
| Cyanamide | CH₂N₂ | Condensing agent; detected in ISM | Multiple heterocycles |
| Guanidine | CH₅N₃ | From HCN polymer hydrolysis | Annulation to guanine derivatives |
| Hypoxanthine | C₅H₄N₄O | 6-oxopurine; deamination product of adenine | → xanthine or guanine |
| Xanthine | C₅H₄N₄O₂ | 2,6-dioxopurine | → guanine via amination |
| FaPy (formamidopyrimidines) | Variable | Ring-opened purine precursor | Carell pathway to A, G nucleosides |

The adenine-guanine interconversion pathway — adenine → hypoxanthine (deamination) → xanthine (oxidation) → guanine (amination) — is thermodynamically characterized but kinetically extremely slow under ambient conditions. Adenine deamination has a half-life of ~80 years at 37°C, pH 7 (Shapiro 1995). Computational modeling shows the gas-phase barrier for the full adenine→guanine transition is prohibitive (k ≈ 5.59 × 10⁻⁴⁰ s⁻¹), though individual steps like deamination are thermodynamically favorable in water (ΔG = −9.63 kcal/mol).

A noteworthy recent contribution from Menor-Salván, Hud, and colleagues (2022, *Chemistry – A European Journal*) demonstrated guanine formation from **2,5,6-triamino-4-hydroxypyrimidine** in urea-ammonium-formate-water solvent dried at 65°C over 3 days. They also produced **2,4-diaminopurine in 60% yield** from tetraaminopyrimidine, establishing a unified prebiotic context linking pyrimidines, pterins, and purines.

## Environmental conditions that favor each synthesis step

Three principal environments have been experimentally validated for different stages of the guanine synthesis network, and each carries distinct advantages and limitations.

**Eutectic freezing (−2 to −78°C)** resolves the critical concentration problem for HCN chemistry. The water-HCN eutectic at **−23.4°C** concentrates HCN from dilute solution into liquid channels between ice crystals, reaching the ≥0.01 M threshold required for polymerization. Miyakawa, Cleaves, and Miller (2002, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 32, 209–218) demonstrated this definitively: NH₄CN frozen at −78°C for **27 years** yielded 11 purines and pyrimidines upon hydrolysis, including adenine, guanine, hypoxanthine, xanthine, and 2,6-diaminopurine. Even initial concentrations as low as **0.001 M** proved sufficient under eutectic conditions. Schwartz, Joosten, and Voet (1982, *BioSystems*) and Levy et al. (2000) independently confirmed purine production from frozen HCN. This environment also maximizes nucleobase stability: at 0°C, guanine's half-life exceeds **10⁶ years** versus ~1 year at 100°C (Levy & Miller 1998, *PNAS* 95, 7933).

**Surface evaporitic pools with wet-dry cycling (20–80°C)** provide the environment best suited for the Carell FaPy pathway and for nucleoside formation. Evaporation concentrates reactants by factors of ~**1,400×** (Ross & Deamer 2016). The dry state enables condensation reactions (glycosidic bond formation, phosphorylation) that are thermodynamically unfavorable in bulk water. UV-C radiation — approximately **10× more intense** on the ozone-free early Earth — drives the DAMN→AICN photochemical conversion essential for purine synthesis while simultaneously providing a selection pressure favoring photostable nucleobases. A 2025 study (*ACS Central Science*) showed 2′,3′-cyclic nucleotides polymerize under wet-dry cycling at room temperature with yields of ~70% for guanosine monomers at mildly alkaline pH 10–11.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents (40–90°C, pH 9–11)** offer sustained chemical energy from serpentinization and mineral catalysts (FeS, Fe₃S₄, Ni-mackinawite) at the vent-ocean interface. The Martin and Russell model proposes that the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway supplies all intermediates for purine and pyrimidine biosynthesis. However, nucleobase stability constrains this setting: at 100°C, adenine and guanine half-lives are approximately **1 year**, and cytosine's is only **19 days** (Levy & Miller 1998). Black smoker temperatures (300–400°C) are incompatible with nucleobase accumulation entirely.

## Mineral catalysts steer product selectivity in formamide and HCN chemistry

**Iron-sulfur minerals** — pyrite (FeS₂), greigite (Fe₃S₄), mackinawite (FeS), pyrrhotite, and chalcopyrite (CuFeS₂) — catalyze nucleobase synthesis from formamide at 160°C (Saladino et al. 2008, *JACS* 130, 15512). Pyrite gives the highest yields of purine, adenine, and 2-aminopurine. Different iron-sulfur minerals produce distinct selectivity profiles, with some favoring purines and others pyrimidines. An important caveat: these same minerals catalyze **RNA degradation** with half-lives as short as 1.8 minutes in formamide, raising questions about polymer survival.

**Clay minerals**, especially Na⁺-montmorillonite, play dual roles: concentrating reactants through adsorption and catalyzing oligomerization. James Ferris's group at RPI demonstrated that montmorillonite catalyzes RNA oligomer formation up to **50-mers** from activated mononucleotides with addition of fresh monomer over 14 days (Huang & Ferris 2006, *JACS* 128, 8914). Purine nucleotides preferentially form the biologically relevant **3′,5′-phosphodiester bonds** on clay surfaces, while pyrimidines favor 2′,5′-linkages. Relative reactivity of activated monomers follows A > G > C > U (ratio 8.2:4.8:1.3:1).

**Titanium dioxide (TiO₂)** acts as a UV-responsive semiconductor photocatalyst. Senanayake and Idriss (2006, *PNAS* 103, 1194) showed that formamide adsorbed on TiO₂(001) single-crystal surfaces produced mass spectrometric signals consistent with adenine (m/z 135), **guanine (m/z 151)**, uracil (m/z 112), and cytosine (m/z 111) upon **3.2 eV** illumination — band-gap excitation of the semiconductor rather than direct molecular photolysis, allowing product accumulation without photodestruction.

**Borate minerals** primarily stabilize ribose rather than directly catalyzing purine ring formation. Ricardo, Carrigan, Olcott, and Benner (2004, *Science* 303, 196) showed borate complexation at the 2′,3′-hydroxyl positions selectively stabilizes ribose over other aldopentoses, an essential enabler for nucleoside formation. Furukawa et al. (2022, *Scientific Reports*) achieved high-yield regioselective ribose 5′-phosphate synthesis from ribose, phosphate, urea, and borate via thermal evaporation — phosphorylation greatest for ribose and **only in borate's presence**. Tourmaline, a boron-rich mineral, has been found in >3.7 Ga metasediments in Isua, Greenland, confirming early geological availability.

## Quantitative experimental evidence across six decades

The experimental record for prebiotic guanine synthesis spans from Oró's 1960 experiments to 2026 asteroid sample analyses:

| Experiment | Conditions | Guanine outcome | Citation |
|---|---|---|---|
| NH₄CN polymerization (warm) | 1 M, 80°C, days | ~0.01–0.05% yield (10–40× less than adenine) | Levy, Miller & Oró 1999, *J. Mol. Evol.* |
| NH₄CN frozen 27 years | 0.15 M, −78°C | Detected by GC/MS after hydrolysis | Miyakawa, Cleaves & Miller 2002, *OLEB* |
| AICN → AICA → guanine | UV + aqueous + cyanogen | Demonstrated stepwise | Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel 1968, *J. Mol. Biol.* |
| Formamide + UV (254 nm) | 130°C, 48 h, neat | Detected without catalyst | Barks et al. 2010, *ChemBioChem* |
| Formamide + TiO₂ surface | UHV, 3.2 eV light | m/z 151 detected | Senanayake & Idriss 2006, *PNAS* |
| Formamide + proton beam | 165 MeV, meteorite catalyst | Part of rich product panel | Saladino et al. 2015, *PNAS* |
| Laser plasma (impact sim.) | High-energy, formamide | All 4 canonical bases by GC-MS | Ferus et al. 2015, *PNAS* |
| FaPy + ribose (dry state) | 100°C dry, then basic aqueous | **~60% N9-nucleoside yield** | Becker et al. 2016, *Science* |
| Unified wet-dry synthesis | Geothermal model, Fe/Ni | All 4 nucleosides + phosphates | Becker et al. 2019, *Science* |
| Murchison meteorite | Extraterrestrial | **649 ± 103 ppb** (6M HCl extract) | Koga et al. 2024, *GCA* |

Oró's original 1960–1961 papers (*Biochemical and Biophysical Research Communications* 2, 407; *Archives of Biochemistry and Biophysics* 94, 217) did not specifically report guanine — the **6N HCl hydrolysis** protocol used to maximize adenine yield actually decomposes guanine. Detection of guanine from HCN chemistry required milder hydrolysis conditions, achieved only decades later. Meteorite analyses provide the strongest quantitative evidence for prebiotic guanine abundance: Callahan et al. (2011, *PNAS* 108, 13995) found guanine at **244 ppb** in CM2 meteorite LON 94102 — the most abundant canonical nucleobase in that sample. Three terrestrially rare analogs (purine, 2,6-diaminopurine, 6,8-diaminopurine) found alongside canonical bases but absent from surrounding soil provided the definitive contamination control. Carbon isotope ratios (δ¹³C = +44.5‰ for meteoritic nucleobases vs. −10.6‰ for soil; Martins et al. 2008, *Earth and Planetary Science Letters*) independently confirm extraterrestrial origin.

## Guanine precursors pervade the interstellar medium and comets

HCN — the ultimate starting material for purine synthesis — is one of the most abundant carbon-containing molecules in the interstellar medium, detected ubiquitously in molecular clouds, star-forming regions, comets, and planetary atmospheres. **Formamide (NH₂CHO)** was first detected in the dense cloud Sgr B2 (Rubin et al. 1971, *Astrophysical Journal*) and has since been found across the Galactic Habitable Zone (Adande et al. 2013, *Astrobiology* 13, 439). Additional detected precursors include cyanamide (NH₂CN), cyanomethanimine (HNCHCN — the HCN dimer; Rivilla et al. 2019), glycolonitrile (HOCH₂CN; Zeng et al. 2019), and aminoacetonitrile (Belloche et al. 2008). Over 256 molecular species have been identified in the ISM to date, though no nucleobase has been directly detected there.

Cometary inventories are equally rich. The Rosetta mission detected HCN, NH₃, formaldehyde, methanol, formic acid, glycine, methylamine, ethylamine, and phosphorus in comet 67P's coma (Altwegg et al. 2016, *Science Advances*). Ammonium cyanide (NH₄CN) — the precise reagent of Oró's purine synthesis — was identified in 67P dust particles. Formamide is present at ~0.01% relative to water in comets. However, **guanine itself has not been directly detected in any comet**; mass spectrometer resolution aboard spacecraft is currently insufficient for unambiguous nucleobase identification.

Extraterrestrial delivery estimates from Chyba and Sagan (1992, *Nature* 355, 125) suggest that interplanetary dust particles may have delivered **≥10⁶–10⁷ kg/yr** of intact organics to the early Earth, with the caveat that organics do not survive impacts above ~10 km/s. Late Heavy Bombardment (~4.5–3.8 Ga) both delivered and destroyed organics, creating a complex temporal window. The detection of all five canonical nucleobases in Ryugu asteroid return samples (Oba et al. 2026, *Nature Astronomy*) — with purine-to-pyrimidine ratios varying systematically with ammonia content across different parent bodies — confirms that nucleobase synthesis occurs in asteroidal aqueous alteration environments and delivery to Earth was plausible.

## Four persistent challenges define the frontier of prebiotic guanine research

**The selectivity problem** remains largely unresolved. No single prebiotic scenario consistently produces guanine in high yield relative to other purines. In HCN oligomerization, the product hierarchy follows adenine >> hypoxanthine > purine > 2,6-diaminopurine > guanine > xanthine (Callahan et al. 2011, normalized to adenine = 1: guanine ≈ 0.02). This reflects the additional hydrolysis and ring-closure steps required for guanine. Mineral catalysts, UV irradiation, and specific temperature regimes shift product distributions but do not achieve selective guanine production. Whether selectivity is even necessary remains debated — Wächtershäuser proposed an all-purine precursor to nucleic acids, and hypoxanthine can base-pair with cytosine as a guanine surrogate.

**The concentration problem** has been partially resolved through eutectic freezing. Miyakawa et al. (2002) calculated steady-state HCN concentration in a warm (100°C, pH 8) primitive ocean at only **7 × 10⁻¹³ M** — far below the ≥0.01 M needed for polymerization. Eutectic freezing at −23.4°C concentrates HCN by >100-fold in liquid veins between ice crystals. Evaporitic pools, mineral adsorption on clays and hydroxylapatite, and thermophoretic concentration of formamide in hydrothermal pores (Niether et al. 2016, *PNAS*) offer complementary concentration mechanisms. Whether sufficient frozen or evaporitic environments existed on the Hadean Earth remains debated.

**Stability constraints** set a temperature ceiling. Guanine's half-life at 100°C and pH 7 is approximately **1 year**, decomposing to xanthine via deamination (Levy & Miller 1998, *PNAS*). At 0°C, the half-life extends to ~1.3 × 10⁶ years — marginally adequate for low-temperature origin scenarios. For comparison, cytosine is far less stable (t₁/₂ = 19 days at 100°C, 17,000 years at 0°C), making cytosine the most vulnerable component of the Watson-Crick base-pair system. Guanine's remarkable **UV photostability** — arising from ultrafast internal conversion via peaked conical intersections — may have provided a selection advantage on the UV-drenched early Earth.

**The glycosylation challenge is arguably the hardest remaining problem for guanine specifically.** Direct coupling of free guanine with ribose gives extremely low yields (Kim & Benner 2017, *PNAS*: only **0.34% guanosine 2′-phosphate** from ribose 1,2-cyclic phosphate plus guanine), primarily because of guanine's very low aqueous solubility. Multiple N-nucleophilic sites create regiochemistry problems (N7 vs. N9), and anomeric (α vs. β) and ring-size (furanose vs. pyranose) selectivity must also be controlled. The Carell FaPy pathway circumvents these issues by building the purine ring onto a pre-attached sugar, achieving ~60% N9-selective nucleoside formation. Powner's tethered glycosylation approach produces 8-oxo-purine ribonucleotides as potential guanine surrogates. The Sutherland cyanosulfidic network avoids the glycosylation problem for pyrimidines entirely but has not yet incorporated guanine.

## Conclusion: converging pathways and the road ahead

The prebiotic synthesis of guanine is no longer a matter of "if" but "how, where, and how much." At least five experimentally demonstrated routes exist — HCN oligomerization, formamide thermolysis/photolysis, FaPy condensation, impact/plasma chemistry, and extraterrestrial parent-body aqueous processing — each operating under different environmental conditions and producing guanine through distinct intermediate networks that share common early steps. The field is converging on integrated scenarios combining multiple environments: eutectic freezing for HCN concentration, UV irradiation for the DAMN→AICN conversion, wet-dry cycling for nucleoside formation via FaPy intermediates, and mineral catalysis for selectivity tuning.

Three insights stand out for synthesis network modeling. First, **AICN is the master branch point** — its fate (HCN addition vs. hydrolysis) determines adenine vs. guanine selectivity, making water activity the critical control parameter. Second, the **FaPy pathway represents a paradigm shift** from "base-first" to "base-on-sugar" assembly, achieving yields and regioselectivity unmatched by any other prebiotic purine route. Third, meteorite data consistently show guanine as the dominant purine in certain parent bodies, suggesting that aqueous alteration chemistry on asteroids may be more efficient at guanine production than any terrestrial laboratory simulation to date — a finding that should inform model calibration. The principal remaining bottleneck is not guanine synthesis per se but rather its efficient, selective incorporation into nucleotides under a unified, geochemically coherent scenario.
