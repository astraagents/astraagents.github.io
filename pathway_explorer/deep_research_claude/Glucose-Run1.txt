# Prebiotic synthesis of glucose: routes, evidence, and unresolved challenges

**No single demonstrated pathway selectively produces glucose from simple prebiotic molecules — making it one of the hardest unsolved problems in origin-of-life chemistry.** The formose reaction, the classical route from formaldehyde to sugars discovered in 1861, generates over 50 products with glucose comprising a small and variable fraction, and a landmark 2025 NMR study revealed it fundamentally produces branched rather than linear sugars. Alternative routes — cyanosulfidic photochemistry, the glyoxylate scenario, mineral-guided cycles, and extraterrestrial delivery — each solve part of the problem but none yet achieve selective glucose synthesis under plausible prebiotic conditions. The first detection of glucose in pristine asteroid Bennu samples (Furukawa et al., *Nature Geoscience*, 2025) confirms that hexoses existed in the early solar system, but the mechanism of their formation remains debated. This report synthesizes the current state of knowledge across all proposed pathways, intermediates, environmental settings, catalysts, and experimental evidence to guide synthesis network design.

## 1. The formose reaction remains the canonical but deeply flawed pathway

The formose reaction, discovered by Aleksandr Butlerov in 1861 (*Comptes rendus de l'Académie des Sciences*, 53:145–147), converts formaldehyde into a mixture of sugars under alkaline conditions. Ronald Breslow elucidated the autocatalytic mechanism in 1959 (*Tetrahedron Letters*, 1(21):22–26), proposing a cycle in which glycolaldehyde — the simplest sugar — catalyzes its own production from formaldehyde through a sequence of aldol additions, aldose-ketose isomerizations, and retroaldol fragmentations. The cycle proceeds: formaldehyde → glycolaldehyde (C2) → glyceraldehyde (C3) → dihydroxyacetone (C3) → tetrulose (C4) → aldotetrose (C4) → two molecules of glycolaldehyde, with branching pathways producing C5 and C6 sugars at each stage. Appayee and Breslow updated the mechanism in 2014 (*JACS*, 136:3720–3723), showing via deuterium labeling that the isomerization steps proceed through a **hydride shift** rather than the originally proposed enediolate intermediate.

The reaction exhibits three kinetic phases described by Tran, Yi, and Fahrenbach (*Chemical Science*, 2023, 14:9589–9599): a lag phase during which only Cannizzaro disproportionation occurs, an exponential phase once glycolaldehyde exceeds a critical concentration (**≥3 ppm** per Socha, Weiss, and Sakharov, 1980), and a degradation phase in which sugars polymerize into intractable tars. Recent computational work (2025, *J. Chem. Phys.*, 163(8)) quantified the fundamental kinetic problem: the Cannizzaro disproportionation barrier is **16.5 kcal/mol**, lower than both formaldehyde dimerization (**26.9 kcal/mol**) and the productive formose cycle (**18.0 kcal/mol**), meaning the parasitic side reaction is always kinetically favored over sugar formation.

Typical experimental conditions use **0.1–1.8 M formaldehyde** with Ca(OH)₂ catalyst at **50–80°C** and pH ≥ 12. Total sugar yields are dismal: Omran et al. (*Life*, 2020, 10:125) reported only **0.003%** of starting formaldehyde remained as sugars under hydrothermal conditions (120°C, pH 12.5, 1 hour), with the thermodynamic minimum being carboxylic acids rather than sugars. Nishijima et al. (*Angewandte Chemie Int. Ed.*, 2026) found that non-target product molecular formulas outnumber monosaccharide species by **4–7 fold** at all reaction stages. One notable finding from Omran et al. (2020) is that under NaOH-only catalysis (no divalent cation) at 80°C, glucose constitutes approximately **80 mol%** of detectable sugars — remarkable selectivity, though overall sugar yields remain below 1%.

A critical 2025 finding fundamentally challenges the formose reaction's viability. Pulletikurti, Lin, Sutton, Liotta, and Krishnamurthy (*Chem*, April 2025) used NMR spectroscopy under mild conditions and demonstrated that the formose reaction produces **only branched sugars, not linear sugars like glucose or ribose**. They concluded that "controlling the aldol reaction of formaldehyde to selectively produce linear aldoses appears unfeasible," calling for the community to "search for alternative solutions."

## 2. Alternative synthesis routes each address different aspects of the problem

### Cyanosulfidic photochemistry bypasses formaldehyde

John Sutherland's group at the MRC Laboratory of Molecular Biology developed a fundamentally different approach. Ritson and Sutherland (*Nature Chemistry*, 2012, 4:895–899) demonstrated that glycolaldehyde and glyceraldehyde form directly from HCN through UV-driven Cu(I)↔Cu(II) photoredox cycling — a Kiliani-Fischer-type reductive homologation that bypasses the problematic formaldehyde dimerization entirely. The key 2015 paper by Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy, and Sutherland (*Nature Chemistry*, 7:301–307) showed that precursors for ribonucleotides, amino acids, and lipids all derive from **reductive homologation of HCN** using H₂S as reductant and UV light as energy source — a unified "cyanosulfidic protometabolism."

The most recent advance is Ritson and Sutherland's 2023 work (*Nature Chemistry*, 15:1470–1477) showing that **thiophosphate (PSO₃³⁻)** — formed from meteorite-derived phosphite oxidized by H₂S under UV — is a far more effective photoreductant than H₂S alone. Specific yields include glycolaldehyde at **~34%** (with phosphate buffer, pH 6.5) and glyceraldehyde at **~14%** overall after evaporation. Tetroses and pentoses are accessible through continued Kiliani-Fischer homologation **without progressing to higher sugars**, potentially solving the selectivity problem for smaller sugars. However, this pathway has not been extended to selective hexose or glucose production.

### The glyoxylate scenario offers cleaner chemistry

Albert Eschenmoser proposed glyoxylate — a "carboxylated formaldehyde" — as an alternative C1 building block (*Tetrahedron*, 2007, 63:12821–12844). Experimental validation by Sagi, Krishnamurthy, and colleagues (*JACS*, 2012, 134:3577–3589) showed that dihydroxyfumarate (DHF, the formal glyoxylate dimer) reacts with glyceraldehyde to produce xylulose and ribulose in **~60% yield** — with near-quantitative conversion and clean, selective formation of ketoses with no detectable aldoses. This stands in stark contrast to the messy formose reaction. Krishnamurthy's group further developed the "glyoxylose reaction" framework (*Chem*, 2023), proposing repetitive aldol condensations with glyoxylate followed by carbonyl migrations and decarboxylations. The challenge remains that no known prebiotic pathway produces DHF itself in sufficient quantities, and selective glucose formation has not been demonstrated through this route.

### Hydrothermal vent chemistry and extraterrestrial delivery

Alkaline hydrothermal vents (Lost City type, 40–90°C, pH 9–11) provide favorable conditions for formaldehyde production from CO₂ reduction by H₂ across Fe(Ni)S membranes, followed by formose-type sugar synthesis at mild alkaline conditions. Nick Lane's group at UCL demonstrated proof-of-principle CO₂ reduction to formaldehyde and subsequent ribose formation (~0.06%) in simulated vent reactors. Thermophoretic concentration in microporous vent structures can amplify organic molecule concentrations up to **5,000-fold**. However, sugar stability remains problematic at vent temperatures (see Section 5).

Extraterrestrial delivery provides a complementary source. Furukawa et al. (*PNAS*, 2019, 116:24440–24445) first detected ribose, arabinose, xylose, and lyxose in the Murchison and NWA 801 meteorites at **2.3–180 ppb**. Their landmark 2025 finding of **glucose in pristine Bennu asteroid samples** (*Nature Geoscience*, 19:19–24) confirmed that hexoses — including the primary biological energy source — existed in the early solar system. Glycolaldehyde, detected in interstellar space by Hollis, Lovas, and Jewell (*Astrophysical Journal Letters*, 2000) and in the solar-type protostar IRAS 16293-2422 by Jørgensen et al. (*Astrophysical Journal Letters*, 2012), could have survived cometary delivery to early Earth in quantities up to **10²³ kg**.

## 3. Formaldehyde and glycolaldehyde are the critical C1 and C2 entry points

**Formaldehyde (HCHO)** is the foundational C1 building block for nearly all proposed prebiotic sugar pathways. Pinto, Gladstone, and Yung (*Science*, 1980, 210:183–185) estimated atmospheric photochemical production rates of **~10¹¹ mol/year** on early Earth, yielding a steady-state oceanic concentration of approximately **10⁻³ M** — likely an overestimate given Cannizzaro losses and other decomposition channels. Formaldehyde is abundant in the interstellar medium and cometary ices, thermally stable below 300°C, and hydrates to methanediol in aqueous solution. Weber (2002) argued it may be the only C1 CHO molecule capable of generating complex organics for life's origin.

**Glycolaldehyde (HOCH₂CHO)**, the simplest sugar, serves as the autocatalytic "linchpin" of the Breslow cycle. Its formation from formaldehyde is the critical bottleneck: the uncatalyzed dimerization barrier is **45.3 kcal/mol** (Kua et al., *J. Phys. Chem. A*, 2013, 117:12658), requiring "umpolung" of formaldehyde's electrophilic carbon. Once present, glycolaldehyde enables aldol addition with formaldehyde to produce glyceraldehyde (C3) through much lower barriers. Atmospheric glycolaldehyde production on early Earth was estimated at only ~10⁷ mol/yr from direct photochemistry, with the formose reaction potentially contributing ~2 × 10⁸ mol/yr.

**Glyceraldehyde**, the simplest chiral sugar and first aldotriose, is the central branch point in the network. It isomerizes to **dihydroxyacetone (DHA)** via the Lobry de Bruyn–Alberda van Ekenstein transformation, with equilibrium strongly favoring the ketose form under basic conditions. The C3 stage is where pathways diverge: C3 + C2 → pentoses (ribose for RNA); C3 + C3 → hexoses (including glucose for metabolism); C3 + C1 → tetroses. Hexose formation specifically proceeds through multiple routes — C3 + C3 aldol condensation (primarily yielding ketohexoses like fructose and sorbose), C4 + C2, and C5 + C1 — with interconversion between glucose, fructose, and mannose occurring through enediol intermediates.

**Glucose is the most thermodynamically stable aldohexose** because β-D-glucopyranose adopts the ⁴C₁ chair conformation in which all hydroxyl groups and the CH₂OH substituent occupy equatorial positions — unique among the 16 aldohexose stereoisomers. Its open-chain aldehyde form constitutes only **~0.002–0.02%** of the equilibrium mixture, making it the least reactive hexose toward degradation. This thermodynamic and kinetic stability may explain glucose's biological selection, but the formose reaction primarily produces fructose (via C3 + C3 condensation), which must undergo Lobry de Bruyn–Alberda van Ekenstein rearrangement to yield glucose and mannose.

## 4. Minerals serve as both catalysts and stabilizers across distinct environmental settings

### Borate minerals are the most important stabilizing agents

The seminal work by Ricardo, Carrigan, Olcott, and Benner (*Science*, 2004, 303:196) demonstrated that borate ions form reversible complexes with 1,2-diol groups on sugars, locking them in their cyclic furanose forms and suppressing base-catalyzed enolization and degradation. Ribose, which decomposes within minutes under alkaline conditions, **remained stable for days** with borate at room temperature. Furukawa et al. (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2013, 43:353–361) showed borate **selectively stabilizes ribose** over other aldopentoses. Kim, Ricardo, and Benner (*JACS*, 2011, 133:9457–9468) demonstrated that combined **borate, molybdate, and calcium minerals** guide formation of tetroses, pentoses, and heptoses in "hands-off" experiments at 65°C. Molybdate catalyzes the Bílik rearrangement converting branched pentoses to linear forms — essential for producing biologically relevant sugars.

For glucose specifically, Furukawa et al. (*Scientific Reports*, 2023) showed that borate-rich evaporative environments enable **regioselective phosphorylation** of glucose to glucose 6-phosphate and gluconate to 6-phosphogluconate using thermal evaporation — establishing a non-enzymatic proto-glycolysis and proto-pentose phosphate pathway. Borate minerals (colemanite, ulexite, kernite) are enriched in volcanic/hydrothermal settings, and their geological availability on Hadean Earth is supported by tourmaline deposits dating to **3,300–3,450 Ma** in the Barberton belt, South Africa.

### Iron-sulfur minerals catalyze carbon fixation and sugar-relevant reactions

**Greigite (Fe₃S₄)**, a structural analog of [Fe₄S₄] clusters in ferredoxin enzymes, forms abiotically in alkaline hydrothermal chimneys at 40–80°C (maximum yield at **75°C**, Roldan et al., *Astrobiology*, 2015) and catalyzes CO₂ reduction. **Pyrite (FeS₂)** is central to Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis and drives production of most TCA cycle intermediates. Most relevant for hexose synthesis, **iron(III) hydroxide oxide (goethite, Fe(OH)O)** efficiently catalyzes condensation of glyceraldehyde to ketohexoses at just 25°C and pH 5–6: Weber (*J. Mol. Evol.*, 1992, 35:1–6) reported yields of **sorbose (15.2%), fructose (12.9%), psicose (6.1%), and tagatose (5.6%)** with no hexose decomposition observed even after 96 days. This iron hydroxide pathway is notable for its mild conditions, reasonable yields, and product stability.

### Environmental settings create distinct reaction zones

Three primary settings have been proposed, each with different advantages:

- **Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (40–90°C, pH 9–11): Moderate temperatures allow longer sugar survival; CaCO₃ chemical garden membranes create steep pH gradients with formose-active alkaline zones and cooler protective zones; thermophoretic concentration in micropores amplifies reactants; activity persists for >30,000 years. Sugar stability is limiting: Larralde, Robertson, and Miller (*PNAS*, 1995, 92:8158) measured ribose half-life at **73 minutes at 100°C** and **~44 years at 0°C** at pH 7. Glucose and other hexoses have comparable half-lives.

- **Volcanic hot spring pools with wet-dry cycling**: Damer and Deamer (*Astrobiology*, 2020, 20:429–452) proposed that fluctuating hot spring pools drive condensation during drying, encapsulation during rehydration, and provide access to borate and silicate minerals. The 3.48 Ga Dresser Formation in Western Australia confirms such environments existed on early Earth. Evaporation concentrates reactants and strengthens borate-sugar ester bonds, while rehydration releases substrates.

- **Photochemically active surface environments**: Essential for cyanosulfidic chemistry (UV at **254 nm** principal emission; early Earth had orders-of-magnitude higher UV flux at 200–300 nm due to absence of the ozone layer). ZnS (band gap ~3.6 eV, absorbing UV <345 nm) and TiO₂ serve as mineral semiconductor photocatalysts. Montmorillonite clay catalyzes RNA polymerization with rate constants **100–1,000× greater** than hydrolysis rates (Ferris, *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B*, 2006, 361:1777–1786).

## 5. Experimental evidence spans meteorites, photochemistry, and mineral catalysis

The experimental landscape for prebiotic glucose synthesis spans several complementary approaches:

**Formose reaction experiments** demonstrate the range of possible products. Omran et al. (2020) showed that CaCO₃-based chemical gardens (hydrothermal vent analogs) catalyze the formose reaction to produce glucose, ribose, and other monosaccharides, with glucose dominating (~80 mol% of sugars) under NaOH-only conditions. Lamour et al. (*Life*, 2019) demonstrated mechanochemical formose conditions (ball milling) producing hexoses as **66%** of the sugar mixture after 28 days, with **~95% selectivity** toward aldoses/ketoses versus ~50–60% in aqueous solution. Hashidzume (*RSC Advances*, 2023) achieved nearly 100% hexose + heptose yield using microwave-irradiated Ca(OH)₂ + formaldehyde at 150°C for 1 minute — though under conditions far from prebiotically plausible.

**Mineral catalyst studies** provide some of the most promising results for hexose formation. Weber's 1992 iron hydroxide experiments remain among the best for selective hexose production, yielding ~28% combined ketohexoses from glyceraldehyde at 25°C with no decomposition over 96 days. Jong et al. (*Angewandte Chemie*, 2025) showed that different mineral environments (hydroxyapatite, goethite, montmorillonite, TiO₂) produce distinct formose product distributions, with environmental mineral history effectively "transferred" to sugar product profiles. The Camprubi, Harrison, and Jordan experiment (*Astrobiology*, 2022) demonstrated that acetyl phosphate stabilizes ribose **10-fold** in the formose reaction, though the mechanism was product stabilization rather than directed synthesis.

**Electrochemical approaches** represent an emerging frontier. Cestellos-Blanco et al. (*Joule*, 2022, 6:2503–2521) converted CO₂ electrochemically to glycolaldehyde (on Cu nanoparticles) and formaldehyde (on boron-doped diamond electrodes, **~62% Faradaic efficiency**), with subsequent formose reaction producing C4–C8 sugars including glucose. A 2025 *PNAS* study achieved **60–80% carbon conversion** for pentose/tetrose/triose mixtures and >20% for hexoses through combined electro-, photo-, and organo-catalysis.

**Meteoritic evidence** provides ground truth for what abiotic chemistry actually produces. Furukawa et al.'s detection of ribose and other sugars in Murchison and NWA 801 meteorites (2019) at **2.3–180 ppb**, confirmed by δ¹³C extraterrestrial signatures, and the subsequent detection of glucose in Bennu samples (2025), demonstrate that formose-like chemistry operates in asteroid parent bodies. The absence of deoxyribose in all samples supports the RNA world hypothesis. Yi et al. (*JACS Au*, 2023) noted that C6 aldonates (stable sugar acid forms) are abundant in the Murchison meteorite while canonical sugars are absent, suggesting aldonates may serve as stable reservoirs that convert back to pentoses through decarboxylation.

## 6. The selectivity, stability, and chirality problems remain the central bottlenecks

### The selectivity problem is more severe than previously recognized

The formose reaction was already known to produce **50+ different sugar species** (Shapiro, *Origins of Life and Evolution of the Biosphere*, 1988). Orgel's influential 2004 review (*Critical Reviews in Biochemistry and Molecular Biology*, 39:99–123) acknowledged that directed formose chemistry would be "ideal" but declared the evidence insufficient for prebiotic ribose availability. The 2025 Krishnamurthy NMR study deepens this crisis by showing the reaction **fundamentally produces branched rather than linear sugars** — meaning glucose cannot be a direct formose product under the conditions studied. Among 16 possible aldohexose stereoisomers plus 8 ketohexose isomers plus branched isomers, obtaining glucose specifically requires either remarkable selectivity or post-synthesis selection mechanisms.

Benner's "asphalt problem" (*Accounts of Chemical Research*, 2012, 45:2025–2034) captures the broader challenge: prebiotic systems "DEVOLVE to give uselessly complex mixtures" unless reactants are "presented in a specific order in large amounts." Possible solutions include mineral-guided selectivity (borate constraining products, molybdate converting branched to linear forms), environmental compartmentalization (different reaction steps in different microenvironments), and selective stabilization (thermodynamic and kinetic trapping of glucose in its exceptionally stable β-pyranose form).

### Sugar stability constrains viable environments

Larralde, Robertson, and Miller's 1995 study remains definitive: sugars decompose rapidly under prebiotic conditions, with ribose half-life of **73 minutes at 100°C** at pH 7. Alkaline conditions (needed for the formose reaction) accelerate decomposition further through enolization, retro-aldol cleavage, and saccharinic acid formation. Acidic conditions promote dehydration to 5-hydroxymethylfurfural and levulinic acid. This creates a fundamental tension: sugar synthesis requires conditions that simultaneously destroy the products.

Borate stabilization partially resolves this, but borate also inhibits the formose reaction by sequestering reactive intermediates — suggesting that **sugar formation and stabilization must occur in separate locations** connected by transport. Wet-dry cycling in borate-rich pools offers one resolution: synthesis during the wet alkaline phase, stabilization during drying as borate esters strengthen, and selective release upon rehydration.

### Chirality amplification has plausible mechanisms but remains undemonstrated for glucose

Biology uses exclusively D-sugars. Cooper and Rios (*PNAS*, 2016) detected D-enantiomeric excesses of **33–55%** for sugar acids in carbonaceous meteorites, demonstrating that abiotic processes can produce chiral bias. Recent work on the Chiral-Induced Spin Selectivity (CISS) effect (Ozturk et al., *PNAS*, 2022, 119:e2204765119) proposes that spin-polarized electrons from magnetite deposits under solar UV could break chiral symmetry. Blackmond's group demonstrated that **1% excess of L-amino acids can produce 100% chiral purity** in sugar-amino acid cross-chirality transfer experiments. A 2025 *PNAS* framework proposes that homochirality propagates from a single nucleic acid precursor (ribose aminooxazoline) through genome → peptides → metabolites — but analysis of pristine Bennu and Ryugu samples challenges the hypothesis that solar system amino acid bias drove biological homochirality, suggesting it originated during terrestrial prebiotic chemistry.

## Conclusion: toward a multi-pathway, multi-environment synthesis network

The prebiotic synthesis of glucose cannot be explained by any single pathway. The evidence points toward a **convergent, multi-source model** in which several mechanisms contribute glucose and its precursors:

The most promising near-term experimental directions are **Weber's iron hydroxide catalysis** (selective ketohexose formation at 25°C with subsequent isomerization to glucose), **Benner's mineral-guided borate-molybdate cycles** (with potential extension from pentoses to hexoses), **extraterrestrial delivery** of glycolaldehyde and pre-formed sugars (now confirmed by Bennu glucose detection), and **electrochemical CO₂-to-sugar pathways** that mimic prebiotic energy flows. The cyanosulfidic approach, while powerful for C2–C5 intermediates, has not been extended to selective hexose production.

For an agentic AI synthesis network, the critical design implications are threefold. First, **formaldehyde → glycolaldehyde initiation** is the primary bottleneck (barrier 26.9–45.3 kcal/mol) and should be treated as a separate module requiring either photochemical, electrochemical, or delivery-based solutions. Second, **the C3 → C6 pathway** should branch through both direct C3 + C3 aldol condensation (yielding fructose, then glucose via isomerization) and stepwise C1 addition through C4 and C5 intermediates, with mineral catalysts (goethite, borate, molybdate) guiding selectivity at each stage. Third, **environmental cycling** — alternating between alkaline synthesis conditions and neutral/borate-stabilized storage — is essential to balance production against degradation. The field remains highly divided between terrestrial hot-spring advocates (Benner, Deamer), submarine vent proponents (Russell, Lane), and cyanosulfidic systems chemists (Sutherland), with Krishnamurthy's 2025 results forcing reconsideration of the most fundamental assumptions about formose chemistry's role in prebiotic sugar synthesis.
