# Prebiotic synthesis of cytosine: pathways, challenges, and the stability paradox

**Cytosine is the most problematic of the four canonical RNA nucleobases to synthesize and accumulate under prebiotic conditions.** Its rapid hydrolytic deamination to uracil (half-life ~340 years at 25°C) creates a fundamental tension: any plausible origin-of-life scenario must either produce cytosine faster than it degrades, stabilize it through incorporation into larger structures, or bypass free cytosine entirely. Five major synthesis routes have been demonstrated in the laboratory — HCN oligomerization, cyanoacetaldehyde–urea condensation, cyanoacetylene–cyanate reaction, formamide thermal condensation, and the Sutherland cyanosulfidic pathway — each with distinct strengths and limitations. The Sutherland pathway, which constructs the pyrimidine ring directly on the sugar backbone and never requires free cytosine, represents the most significant conceptual breakthrough in the field. Recent detection of all five canonical nucleobases in Ryugu asteroid samples (Koga et al., 2026, *Nature Astronomy*) confirms that abiotic cytosine synthesis occurs in nature, lending renewed credibility to prebiotic chemistry models.

## 1. Classical synthesis routes and their quantitative performance

### HCN oligomerization and the DAMN pathway

Hydrogen cyanide polymerization, first characterized by Sanchez, Ferris, and Orgel in the 1960s, proceeds through a well-defined stepwise mechanism: HCN dimerizes to iminoacetonitrile, which adds another CN⁻ to form aminomalononitrile (the trimer), and a fourth HCN unit yields **diaminomaleonitrile (DAMN)** — the HCN tetramer (Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel, *JACS*, 1966; *J. Mol. Biol.*, 1967). DAMN is isolable at room temperature from 0.1–1.0 M HCN solutions and occupies a critical branch point: UV photoisomerization converts it to diaminofumaronitrile (DAFN), which leads to 4-aminoimidazole-5-carbonitrile (AICN) and eventually adenine via the classic Oró pathway. However, **direct HCN oligomerization does not efficiently produce cytosine** because cytosine (C₄H₅N₃O) requires oxygen that pure HCN chemistry cannot provide. Hydrolysis of HCN polymers at pH 8.5 yields primarily noncanonical pyrimidines — 4,5-dihydroxypyrimidine, orotic acid, and 5-hydroxyuracil — rather than cytosine (Ferris, Joshi & Lawless, *BioSystems*, 1977, 9:81–86; Ferris et al., *J. Mol. Evol.*, 1978, 11:293–311).

Conditions for the Ferris HCN oligomerization experiments were 0.1 M HCN (as NH₄CN) at pH 9.2, room temperature, over 4–12 months in the dark. Adenine yields reached only **~0.04%** from the supernatant. Miyakawa, Cleaves, and Miller (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 2002, 32:209–218) dramatically extended this approach by storing 0.15 M NH₄CN frozen at −78°C for 27 years; acid hydrolysis of the resulting polymer yielded 11 purines and pyrimidines including trace cytosine (~0.007%), uracil, adenine, and guanine. This experiment demonstrated that **eutectic freezing** — concentrating HCN to ~74.5 mol% at the HCN–H₂O eutectic point of −23.4°C — provides the most plausible mechanism for reaching the ≥0.01 M concentrations required for polymerization.

### Cyanoacetaldehyde plus urea: the highest-yielding route

The most efficient direct synthesis of free cytosine was reported by Robertson and Miller (*Nature*, 1995, 375:772–774). Cyanoacetaldehyde (NCCH₂CHO, the hydrolysis product of cyanoacetylene) reacts with concentrated urea to produce cytosine in remarkable yields: **~20% in 5 molal urea, ~33% in 20 molal urea, and up to 53% in saturated (120 molal) urea** at 100°C. The reaction follows mixed first- and second-order kinetics in urea, with an Arrhenius activation energy of **28.2 kcal/mol**. Nelson, Robertson, Levy, and Miller (*Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 2001, 31:221–229) extended this to dry-down conditions, finding that cyanoacetaldehyde dimer (0.001 M) with 2 M urea at 100°C for 3 days yields **12.8% cytosine**. Cleaves, Nelson, and Miller (*Naturwissenschaften*, 2006, 93:228–231) demonstrated that even dilute solutions produce cytosine upon freezing through eutectic concentration.

Cyanoacetaldehyde forms prebiotically from **cyanoacetylene hydrolysis** (half-life ~11 days at pH 9, 30°C), and cyanoacetaldehyde itself is remarkably stable (hydrolysis half-life ~31 years at 30°C, pH 9). Cyanoacetylene is the second most prevalent product of spark discharges in CH₄/N₂ mixtures (Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel, *Science*, 1966, 154:784–785) and is abundant in interstellar molecular clouds and Titan's atmosphere.

### Cyanoacetylene plus cyanate: the Sanchez–Ferris–Orgel route

The original prebiotic pyrimidine synthesis, reported by Sanchez, Ferris, and Orgel (*Science*, 1966, 154:784–785; *J. Mol. Biol.*, 1968, 33:693–704), reacts cyanoacetylene with cyanate to form *trans*-cyanovinylurea, which cyclizes to cytosine. At 0.025 M HC₃N + 0.05 M cyanate at 100°C for 24 hours, **6% cytosine** is obtained; the maximum reported yield across all conditions is **19%**. Conversion of isolated *trans*-cyanovinylurea to cytosine in 1.0 M NaOH proceeds at **98% yield**. The limitations are severe: both cyanoacetylene and cyanate are unstable in water, cyanoacetylene reacts rapidly with common nucleophiles (NH₃, thiols, HCN), and concentrations ≥0.1 M cyanate are required.

### Cyanoacetaldehyde plus guanidine

Ferris et al. (*J. Mol. Evol.*, 1974, 3:301–309) showed cyanoacetaldehyde condenses with guanidine to form **2,4-diaminopyrimidine**, which hydrolyzes to cytosine, isocytosine, and uracil. The key advantage: guanidine has extraordinary hydrolytic stability (half-life **10⁵–10⁸ years** at pH 9). Robertson, Levy, and Miller (*J. Mol. Evol.*, 1996, 43:543–550) achieved **40–85% yields** of 2,4-diaminopyrimidine from guanidine hydrochloride + cyanoacetaldehyde under concentrated (drying lagoon) conditions.

## 2. The cyanosulfidic revolution: building nucleotides without free cytosine

### Powner–Sutherland pathway bypasses the glycosidic bond problem

The landmark paper by Powner, Gerland, and Sutherland (*Nature*, 2009, 459:239–242) fundamentally reframed prebiotic pyrimidine chemistry. Rather than attempting to join a preformed base to a preformed sugar — which fails completely for pyrimidines — the pathway constructs the nucleotide through intermediates containing **fragments of both sugar and base simultaneously**. Ribose is never a free intermediate, eliminating the ribose instability problem, the formose reaction selectivity problem, and the glycosidic bond formation problem in a single stroke.

The pathway proceeds in five key steps. First, glycolaldehyde condenses with cyanamide in phosphate buffer to form **2-aminooxazole** in **>80% yield** — a "chimeric" intermediate containing half a pentose and half a nucleobase. Second, 2-aminooxazole reacts with glyceraldehyde to give pentose aminooxazolines in essentially quantitative yield, with stereoselectivity favoring **ribo-aminooxazoline (~44%)** and **arabinose aminooxazoline (~30%)** (Anastasi, Crowe, Powner & Sutherland, *Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.*, 2006, 45:6176–6179). Third, arabinose aminooxazoline reacts with cyanoacetylene at pH 6.5 in phosphate buffer to form **arabino-2,2′-anhydrocytidine** in near-quantitative yield. Fourth, heating the anhydronucleoside with inorganic phosphate in urea phosphorylates the C3′-hydroxyl, and the resulting phosphodiester undergoes intramolecular SN2 displacement at C2′, inverting arabino to ribo stereochemistry and yielding **β-ribo-cytidine-2′,3′-cyclic phosphate**. Fifth, UV irradiation partially converts cytidine to uridine while selectively destroying stereoisomeric contaminants — a "photochemical sanitization" step providing both activated pyrimidine nucleotides needed for RNA.

**Phosphate plays four critical roles**: pH buffer, general acid-base catalyst, nucleophilic catalyst in the cyanoacetylene addition step, and chemical reactant incorporated into the final product. As the authors noted: "Although inorganic phosphate is only incorporated into the nucleotides at a late stage of the sequence, its presence from the start is essential."

### The cyanosulfidic network unifies all building blocks of life

Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy, and Sutherland (*Nature Chemistry*, 2015, 7:301–307) demonstrated that precursors of ribonucleotides, amino acids, and lipids all emerge from a single chemical network driven by **HCN reductive homologation** with H₂S as reductant and UV light as energy source. The sugar feedstocks (glycolaldehyde, glyceraldehyde) form through Kiliani–Fischer-type photoreduction catalyzed by Cu(I)–Cu(II) cycling (Ritson & Sutherland, *Nature Chemistry*, 2012, 4:895–899). Amino acid precursors (Strecker α-aminonitriles for glycine, alanine, serine, threonine, valine, leucine, proline, arginine, asparagine, aspartic acid, glutamine, glutamic acid) arise as inevitable by-products. Lipid precursors (glycerol-1-phosphate, **31% yield**; glycerol-2-phosphate, **40% yield**) form from photoreduction of dihydroxyacetone by H₂S followed by phosphorylation with urea. Cyanoacetylene — required for the nucleotide pathway — is produced via Cu(II)-catalyzed oxidative cross-coupling of HCN and acetylene.

The proposed geochemical scenario involves post-impact delivery of HCN and schreibersite, evaporite formation concentrating ferrocyanide salts, thermal metamorphosis producing cyanamide (from calcium ferrocyanide), acetylene (from calcium carbide), and ammonia (from magnesium nitride), followed by rainfall-driven flow chemistry sequentially leaching these feedstocks into streams where solar UV irradiation drives synthesis (Sasselov, Grotzinger & Sutherland, *Science Advances*, 2020, 6:eaax3419). Xu et al. (*Chemical Communications*, 2018, 54:5566–5569) showed that sulfite + ferrocyanide — more geochemically abundant than copper — can alternatively drive the reductive homologation.

## 3. Formamide, UV photolysis, and extraterrestrial delivery pathways

### Formamide: a one-carbon building block for all nucleobases

Raffaele Saladino, Ernesto Di Mauro, and colleagues demonstrated that neat formamide (HCONH₂, the HCN hydrolysis product) heated at **160°C for 48 hours** with 1–2% w/w mineral catalysts produces purine, adenine, cytosine, and 4(3H)-pyrimidinone (Saladino et al., *Bioorg. Med. Chem.*, 2001, 9:1249–1253). The product distribution depends strongly on catalyst identity: CaCO₃ favors purines, **TiO₂ uniquely enables thymine synthesis** (Saladino et al., *ChemBioChem*, 2003, 4:514–521), montmorillonite clays produce uracil and cytosine alongside biosynthetic intermediates (Saladino et al., *ChemBioChem*, 2004, 5:1558–1566). Senanayake and Idriss (*PNAS*, 2006, 103:1194–1198) achieved UV-driven conversion of formamide on TiO₂(001) single-crystal surfaces to all five nucleobases, with an estimated **~32%** of desorbed carbon products corresponding to nucleobases plus HCN tetramer.

Under more extreme conditions, Ferus et al. (*PNAS*, 2015, 112:657–662) showed laser-induced dielectric breakdown of formamide — simulating asteroid impact plasma — produces all four RNA canonical nucleobases without any catalyst. Saladino et al. (*PNAS*, 2015, 112:2746–2755) demonstrated that **165 MeV proton irradiation** of formamide with powdered meteorites yields nucleosides (cytidine, uridine, adenosine, thymidine), amino acids, sugars, and carboxylic acids.

### UV ice photolysis: nucleobases from interstellar conditions

The NASA Ames group (Nuevo, Sandford, Materese) showed that UV irradiation of pyrimidine in H₂O + NH₃ ices at **20–30 K** produces both uracil and cytosine (Nuevo, Milam & Sandford, *Astrobiology*, 2012, 12:295–314). The critical advance came from Oba et al. (*Nature Communications*, 2019, 10:4413), who demonstrated that UV irradiation of simple interstellar ice analogs (H₂O:CO:NH₃:CH₃OH) at **10 K** — without any preformed heterocyclic precursors — simultaneously produces all three pyrimidine and three purine nucleobases.

### Meteoritic evidence confirms extraterrestrial cytosine synthesis

The detection history of meteoritic nucleobases culminated in 2026 with Koga et al. (*Nature Astronomy*, March 2026) reporting **all five canonical nucleobases in Ryugu asteroid samples**, with roughly equal purine-to-pyrimidine ratios. Earlier work detected purines in Murchison (Callahan et al., *PNAS*, 2011, 108:13995–13998), uracil in Ryugu (Oba et al., *Nature Communications*, 2023, 14:1292), and all five bases in Bennu (2025, *PNAS*). A striking finding: the purine-to-pyrimidine ratio correlates negatively with ammonia concentration across different meteorite classes, suggesting an as-yet-unknown formation mechanism dependent on parent body physicochemistry. OSIRIS-REx Bennu samples additionally contain **isocytosine, 5-methylcytosine, xanthine**, and 20 proteinogenic amino acids including tryptophan (first extraterrestrial detection).

## 4. Environmental conditions and mineral catalysts shape reaction outcomes

### Hydrothermal vents versus surface pools

The thermal instability of cytosine places severe constraints on viable environments. Levy and Miller (*PNAS*, 1998, 95:7933–7938) measured cytosine's half-life at **19 days at 100°C, ~340 years at 25°C, and ~17,000 years at 0°C** (pH 7). Wolfenden et al. (*PNAS*, 2016) determined the activation enthalpy for deamination as **23.4 ± 0.5 kcal/mol** with Q₁₀ ≈ 4. These data effectively rule out black smoker hydrothermal vents (300–400°C) for cytosine chemistry. Even alkaline vents (Lost City type, 45–90°C, pH 9–11) pose challenges: at pH 10 and 75°C, cytosine half-lives fall below one day.

Surface evaporitic pools offer more favorable conditions. Wet-dry cycling experiments have demonstrated remarkable capabilities: Song et al. (*PNAS*, 2024) showed single wet-dry cycles link mononucleotides into polymers up to **53 nucleotides** long without chemical activation. Damer and Deamer (*Astrobiology*, 2020, 20:429–452) proposed fluctuating volcanic hot spring pools, supported by the discovery of 3.48 Ga geyserite with stromatolites in Western Australia's Dresser Formation (Djokic et al., *Nature Communications*, 2017, 8:16149). The concentration-by-evaporation mechanism bridges the gap from dilute prebiotic solutions (~10⁻⁴ M) to the molar concentrations required for productive chemistry.

### The mineral catalyst toolkit

**Montmorillonite clays** catalyze oligonucleotide formation with extraordinary efficiency. Ferris and Ertem (*Science*, 1992, 257:1387–1389) showed montmorillonite catalyzes oligomerization of activated adenosine to RNA oligomers up to **11-mers** with 3′,5′-linkage selectivity (2:1 over 2′,5′). Daily feeding extends chains to **40–50 mers** (Ferris, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.*, 2002, 32:311–332). The catalytic rate enhancement is **100–1,000×** over hydrolysis rates, with interlayer sites providing sequence, regio, and homochiral selectivity (Joshi et al., *JACS*, 2009, 131:13369–13374).

**Borate minerals** solve the ribose problem by stabilizing ribose's furanose form through cis-1,2-diol complexation, reducing decomposition rates by **orders of magnitude** (Ricardo, Carrigan, Olcott & Benner, *Science*, 2004, 303:196). Borate additionally channels the formose reaction toward pentose amplification (Kim et al., *JACS*, 2011, 133:9457–9468) and enhances regioselective phosphorylation of ribonucleosides (Kim et al., *Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.*, 2016, 55:15816–15820). The presence of tourmaline in the 3.8 Ga Isua Supracrustal Belt confirms borate mineral availability on the earliest Earth.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** — pyrite, greigite, mackinawite — are central to Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis. The pyrite formation reaction (FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻) is exergonic and provides reducing power for organic synthesis (Wächtershäuser, *PNAS*, 1990, 87:200–204). Greigite (Fe₃S₄) contains cubane [Fe₄S₄] units essentially identical to biological ferredoxin clusters, and is now known to be thermodynamically stable rather than metastable (Navrotsky et al., *PNAS*, 2020). A 2024 study in *Nature Communications* showed FeS catalyzes gaseous CO₂ reduction to methanol under simulated hot spring conditions, with Mn-doping increasing production **five-fold**.

**Schreibersite** [(Fe,Ni)₃P], a meteoritic iron-nickel phosphide, provides reactive phosphorus through aqueous corrosion, yielding orthophosphate, pyrophosphate, phosphite, and hypophosphate (Pasek & Lauretta, *Astrobiology*, 2005, 5:515–535). It phosphorylates nucleosides at 60–80°C with yields up to **~6%** — among the highest for aqueous reactions below 100°C (Gull et al., *Scientific Reports*, 2015, 5:17198). An estimated **1–10% of crustal phosphorus** was in schreibersite form during the Hadean.

## 5. The cytosine stability paradox and the nucleotide assembly challenge

### Shapiro's critique remains the field's central challenge

Robert Shapiro's devastating 1999 analysis (*PNAS*, 96:4396–4401) articulated four problems that still define the field's agenda. First, cytosine had never been detected in meteorites or spark discharge experiments (though both have now been achieved — Ferus et al., *PNAS*, 2017 for spark discharge; Koga et al., 2026 for meteorites). Second, all syntheses require implausibly high reactant concentrations. Third, cyanoacetaldehyde reacts faster with common prebiotic nucleophiles (glycine, cyanide, thiols) than with urea. Fourth, no known reaction produces cytosine fast enough to compensate for deamination. Shapiro calculated that maintaining even **10⁻⁵ M cytosine** in the prebiotic ocean requires a synthesis rate of ~10⁻¹⁵ M·s⁻¹ — vanishingly difficult to achieve.

Several strategies have been proposed to overcome the stability problem: **eutectic freezing** at ≤−23°C dramatically extends half-lives and concentrates reactants simultaneously; **mineral adsorption** on montmorillonite (adsorption energies −27 to −50 kcal/mol) may protect cytosine from hydrolysis; **rapid incorporation into double-stranded polymers** slows deamination by a factor of **~140×**; and low-temperature environments consistent with the faint young Sun (30% less luminous) could maintain surface temperatures permitting half-lives of ~17,000 years. Most powerfully, the Sutherland pathway **eliminates the need for free cytosine entirely**, building the nucleobase directly onto the sugar-phosphate scaffold.

### The glycosidic bond problem and its solutions

Direct condensation of cytosine with ribose fails under prebiotic conditions — no comparable glycosylation to Orgel's **1–5% yield** for adenosine has been demonstrated for pyrimidine nucleosides. This "nucleoside problem" was the primary motivation for the Sutherland approach. Kim and Benner (*Astrobiology*, 2019) achieved a partial solution: direct coupling of cytosine with ribose 1,2-cyclic phosphate in the presence of Ca²⁺ yields cytidine 2′-phosphate at **~7%** at 85–125°C, but this requires activated sugar phosphates. Becker et al. (*Science*, 2019, 366:76–82) demonstrated wet-dry cycle-driven synthesis of both pyrimidine and purine ribonucleotides from hydroxylamine derivatives and small molecules with ribose — a unified pathway yielding 5′-mono- and diphosphates.

### Phosphorylation: diamidophosphate emerges as the leading agent

**Diamidophosphate (DAP)** has emerged as the most versatile prebiotic phosphorylating agent. Gibard et al. (*Nature Chemistry*, 2018, 10:212–217) demonstrated that DAP phosphorylates nucleosides, amino acids, and glycerol under aqueous conditions, generating oligonucleotides (up to 4-mers), peptides (up to 8-mers), and vesicles in a single pot. Pyrimidine ribonucleosides form **2′,3′-cyclic phosphates** with particular efficiency. Aerosol-phase phosphorylation is dramatically accelerated: Castañeda et al. (*Scientific Reports*, 2019, 9:13741) achieved **~6.5–10% conversion** of uridine to uridine-2′,3′-cyclic phosphate in **less than one hour**. DAP with 2-aminoimidazole phosphorylates deoxynucleosides yielding **~60% 5′-amidophosphate nucleotides** for pyrimidines with concomitant oligomerization of **18–31%** producing predominantly 3′,5′-linked DNA (Jiménez et al., 2021). Pasek and Krishnamurthy (*Communications Chemistry*, 2025, 8:187) established DAP's geochemical plausibility: ammonolysis of pyrophosphite produces amidophosphite, which spontaneously oxidizes to DAP at room temperature in yields up to **48%**.

## 6. Unresolved bottlenecks and the path forward

### Five critical open problems

The field confronts several persistent challenges. The **selectivity problem** remains acute: HCN polymerization produces intractable tar alongside trace nucleobases (~0.04% adenine), and formamide condensations yield complex mixtures of canonical and noncanonical heterocycles. How nature selected cytosine from mixtures containing isocytosine, diaminopyrimidine, triazines, and dozens of other products is unclear. The Hud group's exploration of alternative nucleobases (melamine, barbituric acid) that form glycosidic bonds more readily suggests the canonical bases may have been evolutionarily selected from initially simpler systems.

The **homochirality problem** is unresolved: the Sutherland pathway requires enantiopure glyceraldehyde, and while ribo-aminooxazoline selectively crystallizes with potential enantiomeric enrichment (Hein, Tse & Blackmond, *Nature Chemistry*, 2011, 3:704–706), the ultimate source of symmetry breaking remains debated. The **compatibility problem** — purine and pyrimidine nucleotide syntheses require geochemically distinct conditions — challenges single-environment models, though Xu et al. (*Nature*, 2020, 582:60–66) demonstrated co-synthesis of RNA pyrimidine and DNA purine nucleosides from a unified network. The **concentration problem** persists: steady-state HCN concentrations at 100°C reach only **7×10⁻¹³ M**, insufficient for any productive chemistry; even at 0°C, concentrations reach only ~2×10⁻⁵ M (Miyakawa et al., 2002). Finally, the **"do-nothing chemistry" criterion** (Ranjan & Sasselov group, 2024–2025) demands that laboratory syntheses be shown to proceed without chemist intervention under geochemical conditions — a standard few current pathways fully meet.

### The cytosine–uracil relationship may hold the key

Cytosine deamination to uracil, while problematic for accumulation, could be a feature rather than a bug. The Sutherland pathway explicitly exploits this: only cytidine synthesis is needed, with UV-mediated partial conversion providing uridine. Levy and Miller suggested that a **two-letter genetic code** (A-U or G-U only) may have preceded a four-letter code; Reader and Joyce (2002) demonstrated a functional ribozyme entirely lacking C-G base pairs. The growing consensus favors a **heterogeneous nucleic acid system** — possibly RNA/DNA chimeras or polymers incorporating non-canonical bases — preceding pure RNA.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of cytosine has progressed from Shapiro's 1999 declaration that cytosine "quite unlikely played a role in the origin of life" to a landscape where multiple viable pathways exist and extraterrestrial cytosine has been confirmed. Three insights have transformed the field. First, the Sutherland cyanosulfidic approach demonstrated that the entire nucleotide — not just the base — should be the synthetic target, eliminating the glycosidic bond and free-cytosine stability problems simultaneously. Second, DAP chemistry has solved the phosphorylation bottleneck with geochemically plausible reagents and remarkable efficiency. Third, the detection of all five nucleobases in pristine Ryugu and Bennu samples confirms that abiotic nucleobase synthesis is not merely a laboratory curiosity but a natural planetary process.

For the GenesisAgents network modeling system, the most productive pathway to implement would be the Sutherland cyanosulfidic network as the primary route, with the Robertson–Miller cyanoacetaldehyde–urea pathway and formamide routes as alternative branches. Critical network nodes include **2-aminooxazole** (the central chimeric intermediate), **cyanoacetylene** (the key C₃N unit feeding both nucleotide and amino acid branches), **glycolonitrile** (the entry point for sugar synthesis via Kiliani–Fischer homologation), and **DAP** (the phosphorylation hub). Environmental parameters should model wet-dry cycling at 25–60°C with phosphate buffering (pH 6.5–7), UV irradiation at 200–300 nm, and eutectic freezing episodes as concentration mechanisms. The network should incorporate cytosine deamination kinetics (k ≈ 10⁻¹⁰ s⁻¹ at 25°C, Eₐ = 23.4 kcal/mol) as a fundamental constraint, treating uracil production as a downstream feature of cytidine synthesis rather than an independent pathway.
