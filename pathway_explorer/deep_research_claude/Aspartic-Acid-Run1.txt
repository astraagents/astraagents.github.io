# Prebiotic synthesis of aspartic acid: routes, evidence, and open challenges

**Aspartic acid ranks among the most metabolically central yet prebiotically elusive amino acids.** At least 10 distinct prebiotic routes have been proposed or demonstrated, ranging from HCN oligomer hydrolysis — where aspartate emerges as a major product — to metal-catalyzed transamination of oxaloacetate, which mimics the biological pathway but battles the rapid decarboxylation of its precursor. The core tension in prebiotic aspartate chemistry is that the most biologically relevant pathways (via oxaloacetate) face severe kinetic competition from degradation, while the most productive abiotic routes (HCN oligomerization, spark discharge) operate through entirely different mechanisms. This report synthesizes the full landscape of published research on prebiotic aspartate formation, organized to guide synthesis network design and pathway evaluation in an agentic AI system for prebiotic chemistry.

---

## Known prebiotic routes span at least 10 distinct pathways

### Strecker synthesis

The classical Strecker route proceeds through aldehyde + NH₃ → imine (Schiff base), then imine + HCN → α-aminonitrile, and finally hydrolysis of the aminonitrile to the amino acid. For aspartate, this requires a **C3 aldehyde bearing a carboxyl group** (such as 3-oxopropanoic acid or a related functionalized aldehyde), making the Strecker route to aspartate less straightforward than for glycine or alanine. Miller (1957, *Biochim. Biophys. Acta* 23, 480) established that spark discharge amino acid synthesis proceeds via this Strecker mechanism in the aqueous phase. Conditions are aqueous solution, **pH 7–9, room temperature to moderate heat**, with reaction times of hours to days. The prebiotic availability of the specific C4 aldehyde precursor for aspartate remains poorly constrained, representing a significant gap in this pathway.

### Miller-Urey spark discharge

Miller's original 1953 experiment (*Science* 117, 528) used CH₄ (200 torr), NH₃ (200 torr), H₂ (100 torr), and H₂O with Tesla coil spark discharge for one week. Aspartic acid was tentatively identified as spot #1 on the 2D paper chromatogram, though Miller's more rigorous 1955 analysis (*JACS* 77, 2351) reclassified this spot as likely **iminodiacetic acid**. The yield of aspartate, when confirmed, was approximately **0.024% relative to carbon input** — far below glycine at ~2.1%.

Modern reanalysis of Miller's archived samples transformed our understanding. Johnson et al. (2008, *Science* 322, 404) reanalyzed volcanic apparatus vials using HPLC-ToF-MS, detecting **22 amino acids and 5 amines** including confirmed D,L-aspartate. Parker et al. (2011, *PNAS* 108, 5526) reanalyzed Miller's unpublished 1958 H₂S experiment (CH₄/NH₃/H₂S/CO₂ atmosphere), finding **23 amino acids and 4 amines** including 7 organosulfur compounds. Aspartate was present at relatively low abundances, and the H₂S experiment produced yields an **order of magnitude higher** than the classic apparatus for many amino acids. Cleaves et al. (2008, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 38, 105) showed that adding oxidation inhibitors (pyrite, ascorbic acid, FeSO₄) to neutral atmosphere (CO₂/N₂) spark discharge experiments boosted amino acid yields by factors of **2–100×**.

### HCN oligomerization — the most productive route for aspartate

HCN oligomerization followed by hydrolysis is the single most productive prebiotic route specifically for aspartate. Ferris, Donner & Lobo (1974, *J. Mol. Evol.* 3, 225) demonstrated that the **major amino acids** released on hydrolysis of HCN oligomers were **glycine, aspartate, and diaminosuccinic acid** — making aspartate a dominant product rather than a trace component. Ferris et al. (1978, *J. Mol. Evol.* 11, 293) confirmed this using 0.1 M HCN solutions at pH 9.2, where oligomers form over days to weeks and yield amino acids upon acid hydrolysis (6N HCl, 110°C, 24h). Matthews & Moser (1966, *PNAS* 56, 1087) independently detected glycine, alanine, and aspartic acid from HCN polymerization in aqueous ammonia. Frozen HCN solutions (−20 to −78°C) concentrated at the eutectic point (~10 M HCN at −23.4°C) also produce aspartate upon oligomer hydrolysis (Miyakawa, Cleaves & Miller, 2002, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 32, 195). The mechanism likely involves **5-carboxymethylidenehydantoin**, a cyclic intermediate specifically related to aspartate identified in HCN oligomer mixtures.

### Cyanoacetylene route

Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel (1966, *Science* 154, 784) showed that cyanoacetylene (HC≡C-C≡N) — a major nitrogen-containing product of electric discharge in CH₄/N₂ mixtures (up to 8.4% of HCN yield) — reacts with HCN and NH₃ in aqueous solution at **pH ~9, room temperature** to produce **aspartic acid, asparagine, and cytosine**. This C₃ + C₁ coupling pathway is distinct from Strecker chemistry. However, cyanoacetylene production is suppressed when NH₃ or H₂S are present in the discharge, and Shapiro (1999, *PNAS*) questioned its abundance under more realistic CO₂/N₂ atmospheres.

### Bucherer-Bergs pathway via α-keto acids — a key recent discovery

Pulletikurti, Yadav, Springsteen & Krishnamurthy (2022, *Nature Chemistry* 14, 1142) demonstrated that α-keto acids react with cyanide and ammonia sources via the **Bucherer-Bergs pathway** (not classical Strecker) to form amino acids. Specifically, **oxaloacetate + NaCN + NH₄HCO₃** produces aspartate via an **N-carbamoyl aspartate** intermediate. This route simultaneously generates **dihydroorotate**, directly paralleling biochemical pyrimidine nucleobase synthesis and elegantly linking amino acid and nucleotide pathways. Conditions are aqueous solution at room temperature to mild heating, neutral to slightly basic pH. This discovery bridges the gap between prebiotic Strecker chemistry (aldehyde-based) and biological metabolism (α-keto-acid-based).

### Transamination of oxaloacetate

Harrison, Webb, Rammu & Lane (2023, *Life* 13, 1177) published the most focused study on prebiotic aspartate synthesis via transamination. Cu²⁺-catalyzed transamination of oxaloacetate by pyridoxamine achieved **~5% yield of aspartate within 1 hour** (160–258 µM), operating across pH 6–10, 25–70°C, and ambient to high pressure. Metal ions tested included Cu²⁺ (best), Fe³⁺, Mn²⁺, and Co²⁺, at **5–10 mM reactants, 50 mM NH₄HCO₃ buffer, 1 mM catalyst**. Mayer, Kaur, Rauscher & Moran (2021, *JACS* 143, 19099) and Dherbassy, Mayer, Muchowska & Moran (2023, *JACS* 145, 13357) provided kinetic and mechanistic details for non-enzymatic metal-pyridoxal transamination. The critical finding: Cu²⁺-catalyzed transamination can initially **outcompete** OAA decarboxylation, but sustained aspartate production requires continuous OAA regeneration.

### Reductive amination of oxaloacetate

This biologically parallel route (OAA + NH₄⁺ + [H] → aspartate) has been tested with multiple reducing systems. Hafenbradl, Keller, Wächtershäuser & Stetter (1995, *Tetrahedron Lett.* 36, 5179) used FeS + H₂S at 100°C under anaerobic conditions but obtained low yields. Critically, Barge, Flores et al. (2019, *PNAS* 116, 4828) tested reductive amination with mixed-valence Fe(II)/Fe(III) oxyhydroxides and found that **aspartic acid was NOT obtained from oxaloacetate** — the starting material converted entirely to pyruvate (via decarboxylation) and subsequently yielded alanine and lactate. Mayer & Moran (2022, *Angew. Chem. Int. Ed.* 61, e202212237) showed OAA has intermediate reactivity for reductive amination with NaBH₃CN, with a rate 1.7× faster than glutamate formation from α-ketoglutarate — but OAA decarboxylation remains the dominant competing pathway.

### Michael addition of ammonia to fumarate

The biological reaction (aspartase, EC 4.3.1.1) reversibly adds NH₃ to fumarate yielding L-aspartate (K_m ≈ 15–20 mM for NH₄⁺). Non-enzymatically, this reaction is thermodynamically feasible under **alkaline conditions with high NH₃ concentrations** at elevated temperatures, but yields are very low without enzymatic catalysis. Fumarate itself can be produced prebiotically through several routes: photocatalytic reduction of oxaloacetate to malate on ZnS surfaces followed by dehydration (Guzman & Martin, 2008, *Int. J. Astrobiol.* 7, 271); non-enzymatic rTCA cycle analogs (Muchowska, Varma & Moran, 2020, *Chem. Rev.* 120, 7708); and detection in the Murchison meteorite.

### Additional routes

Several other pathways contribute to the prebiotic aspartate inventory. **Photochemical synthesis** by Sagan & Khare (1971, *Science* 173, 417) produced aspartate from UV irradiation of CH₄/C₂H₆/NH₃/H₂O/H₂S mixtures. Muñoz Caro et al. (2002, *Nature* 416, 403) produced 16 amino acids including aspartate from UV irradiation of interstellar ice analogs. Fox & Windsor (1970, *Science* 170, 984) obtained aspartate from heated formaldehyde and ammonia mixtures. **Hydrothermal vent synthesis** has been demonstrated by Marshall (1994, *GCA* 58, 2099) and Hennet, Holm & Engel (1992), and Amend & Shock (1998, *Science* 281, 1659) calculated that amino acid synthesis including aspartate is thermodynamically favorable in **100°C moderately reduced hydrothermal solutions**. Varma, Muchowska & Moran detected aspartate from Fe⁰-mediated CO₂ reduction at 30–100°C, 35 bar with hydroxylamine as nitrogen source.

## Critical intermediates and the oxaloacetate instability problem

### Oxaloacetate is the central but unstable nexus

Oxaloacetate (OAA) sits at the heart of multiple aspartate synthesis routes (transamination, reductive amination, Bucherer-Bergs) but is **notoriously unstable**. It decarboxylates to pyruvate + CO₂ with a half-life of approximately **1–2 hours at 25°C, neutral pH** (rate constant k = 8.9 × 10⁻⁷ s⁻¹; Guzman & Martin, 2008). This decarboxylation follows Arrhenius behavior: log(k) = 11.74 − 4956/T. Divalent metal cations **accelerate** decarboxylation following the Irving-Williams series (Mg²⁺ < Mn²⁺ < Fe²⁺ < Co²⁺ < Ni²⁺ < Cu²⁺ < Zn²⁺), creating a paradox where the same metals that catalyze transamination also promote degradation.

Prebiotic OAA can form through several routes: pyruvate + glyoxylate reactions generating 4-hydroxy-2-ketoglutarate intermediates that undergo oxidative decarboxylation (Eschenmoser & Krishnamurthy, *Nat. Commun.* 2017); iron-mediated CO₂ fixation pathways (Muchowska et al., 2019, *Nature* 569, 104); and as a transient intermediate in non-enzymatic rTCA cycle analogs. CO₂-saturated Hadean ocean conditions might partially suppress decarboxylation by Le Chatelier's principle, though this remains experimentally unvalidated.

### HCN oligomers, fumarate, and branching pathways

**HCN oligomers** form the alternative major pathway to aspartate. The oligomerization cascade proceeds: HCN dimer (iminoacetonitrile) → trimer (aminomalononitrile, AMN) → tetramer (diaminomaleonitrile, DAMN) → higher polymers. The tetramer DAMN is a key branch-point molecule leading to both purines and amino acids. Oligomerization requires **[HCN] > 10⁻² M at pH ~9.2**, achievable through eutectic freezing concentration at −23.4°C.

**Fumarate** connects to aspartate via Michael addition and serves as a node in the rTCA cycle network. It can form from malate dehydration, ZnS-photocatalyzed reduction products (Zhou & Guzman, 2016), and succinate oxidation. **Cyanoacetylene** provides a C₃ building block that couples with HCN and NH₃ to produce both aspartate and cytosine, representing a branch point linking amino acid and nucleobase synthesis. **Glyoxylate** serves as the central C₂ feedstock in protometabolic TCA analogs, connecting to OAA via aldol chemistry with pyruvate.

Aspartate itself is a critical **nodal metabolite** in modern biochemistry — the precursor for asparagine, lysine, methionine, threonine, isoleucine, all pyrimidine nucleotides, and contributions to purine synthesis. Harrison et al. (2023) describe it as essential to any "minimal autotrophic protometabolism."

## Environmental conditions favor alkaline vents and surface pools

### Alkaline hydrothermal vents offer the best thermodynamic window

**Lost City-type alkaline vents** operate at **40–90°C, pH 9–11**, with copious H₂ from serpentinization, along with CH₄, formate, NH₃, and H₂S. The Russell/Martin hypothesis (Russell 1993; Martin & Russell 2007, *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. B*) proposes that FeS/NiS precipitate compartments at alkaline mounds created **pH gradients of ~3 units** — mimicking the proton-motive force in modern cells — where cool, slightly acidic CO₂-rich Hadean ocean (pH 5–6) met hot, reduced alkaline fluid. Kitadai (2015, *Orig. Life Evol. Biosph.* 45, 377) calculated that mixing CO₂-rich seawater with H₂-rich alkaline fluid creates energetically favorable conditions for amino acid synthesis at an optimum of **~50°C**. Preiner et al. (2020, *Nature Ecol. Evol.* 4, 534) demonstrated H₂-dependent primordial carbon metabolism using greigite and magnetite catalysts.

**Black smoker vents** (up to 350–400°C, pH 2–3) provide rich transition metal catalysis (pyrite, chalcopyrite, sphalerite) and the pyrite-pulled energy source (FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂; ΔG = −9.2 kcal/mol). However, amino acids are **irreversibly destroyed above ~240°C** (Miller & Bada, 1988, *Nature* 334, 609; Bada et al., 1995). Aspartate is among the more oxidized amino acids (high Z_C value), making its synthesis **endergonic even at 100°C** in many hydrothermal fluid compositions according to Amend & Shock's calculations — though Kitadai showed that alkaline vent mixing zones create sufficiently steep chemical gradients to overcome this.

### Surface pools and atmospheric considerations

Wet-dry cycling in volcanic hot spring pools (Damer & Deamer, 2020, *Astrobiology* 20, 429) provides **concentration through evaporation** (up to ~1000× enrichment), UV energy input (much higher flux on early Earth without ozone, λ > 200 nm), and thermal energy for condensation reactions during drying (20–120°C). Deliquescent minerals mediate effective wet-dry cycles, achieving **21.2% oligomerization yields** versus 3.5% for non-deliquescent controls after 10 cycles (Campbell & Bracher, 2019, *Nat. Commun.* 10, 4508).

The early Earth atmosphere composition remains debated. Current consensus favors a **weakly reducing to neutral atmosphere** (CO₂ + N₂ dominant, with smaller amounts of H₂O, H₂, and traces of CH₄/NH₃) rather than the strongly reducing mixture used in Miller's original experiment. Transient H₂-rich atmospheres after large asteroid impacts could periodically create conditions conducive to Miller-Urey synthesis. Localized reducing environments near volcanic plumes can drive amino acid synthesis even under a weakly reducing global atmosphere.

## Mineral catalysts play distinct roles at each reaction step

### Iron-sulfur minerals bridge geochemistry and biochemistry

**Greigite (Fe₃S₄)** has an inverse thiospinel structure with cubic Fe₄S₄ units that are structurally identical to [4Fe-4S] clusters in ferredoxins and CODH/ACS enzymes. It is thermodynamically stable (confirmed by Navrotsky group, *PNAS* 2020), catalyzes CO₂ reduction to small organics, and can promote the CO₂ fixation steps that generate oxaloacetate precursors. Santos-Carballal et al. (2018, *Phil. Trans. R. Soc. A*) modeled CO₂ reactivity on greigite surfaces by DFT.

**Pyrite (FeS₂)** provides the thermodynamic driving force for Wächtershäuser's surface metabolism (FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂; ΔG = −9.2 kcal/mol). Hafenbradl et al. (1995) demonstrated amino acid synthesis by reductive amination of α-oxo acids coupled to pyrite formation. A remarkable recent finding: natural pyrite **photocatalytically drives reductive amination** of keto acids with **14.5–42.4% enantiomeric excess for D-amino acids** via wavy surface atom arrangements (*Nat. Commun.* 2024).

**Mackinawite (FeS)** — a layered iron sulfide and precursor to greigite — reduces CO₂ to CO at >300°C and catalyzes H₂-driven CO₂ reduction to methanol at **80–120°C** when doped with Mn (5× enhancement) or illuminated with UV-visible light (*Nat. Commun.* 2024). Its semiconductor properties enable photo-assisted catalysis relevant to the earliest steps of carbon fixation.

### Clay minerals concentrate and catalyze

**Montmorillonite** catalyzes oligomerization of activated nucleotides to 40–55-mers (Ferris & Ertem, 1992, *Science* 257, 1387), amino acid polymerization under wet-dry cycling (Lahav et al., 1978, *Science* 201, 67), and specifically the formation of glycyl-aspartic acid dipeptides from glycine and aspartate at **85 ± 5°C** (Kalra/Pant et al., *Int. J. Astrobiol.*). Notably, linear and cyclic aspartate-only peptides were NOT formed, indicating distinct reactivity for aspartate on clay surfaces. Montmorillonite interlayers create **nano-confined environments** distinct from bulk solution, providing locally anhydrous conditions that protect from hydrolysis.

### Photocatalysts and transition metal ions

**ZnS** (sphalerite, band gap ~3.6 eV) photocatalyzes **reversible amination of α-keto acids** under UV (~360 nm) irradiation, including oxaloacetate → aspartate (Wang et al., 2011–2013; Guzman & Martin, 2008). Chiral mesostructured ZnS can drive CO₂ + NH₃ → amino acids with **>96% enantiomeric excess** via spin-polarized electron transfer. **TiO₂** (rutile/anatase, band gap 3.0–3.2 eV) enhances amino acid polymerization (23% increase with anatase) and converts formamide to nucleoside bases under UV.

**Cu²⁺** is the most efficient non-enzymatic transamination catalyst (Harrison et al., 2023). **Fe⁰** reduces CO₂ to pyruvate and acetate at 30–100°C, 35 bar, generating aspartate when hydroxylamine is present as nitrogen source (Varma et al., 2018, *Nat. Ecol. Evol.*). **Ni⁰** catalyzes a prebiotic Krebs cycle analog generating amino acids from H₂ + NH₃ (Kaur et al., 2024, *Chem* 10, 1528). FeS/NiS co-catalyzes thioester synthesis (Huber & Wächtershäuser, 1997).

## Meteoritic and extraterrestrial evidence confirms abiotic aspartate formation

Aspartate is consistently detected in carbonaceous chondrites. In the **Murchison meteorite**, modern analyses by Glavin et al. report **L-aspartate at 1.7 ± 0.1 nmol/g** and **D-aspartate at 0.95 ± 0.04 nmol/g**. The **Tagish Lake meteorite** shows remarkably large L-enantiomeric excesses of **~60% for aspartate**, confirmed as extraterrestrial by δ¹³C enrichments (+24‰ D, +29‰ L). The Aguas Zarcas meteorite shows **~28% L-ee** for aspartate. Chimiak et al. (2022, *Geochim. Cosmochim. Acta* 321, 78) used position-specific carbon isotope analysis of Murchison aspartate and found signatures consistent with Strecker synthesis origins. Over **90 amino acids** have been identified in Murchison, with glycine most abundant and aspartate present at lower but significant concentrations. Muñoz Caro et al. (2002, *Nature* 416, 403) produced 16 amino acids including aspartate from UV irradiation of interstellar ice analogs (H₂O/CO₂/CO/CH₃OH/NH₃), demonstrating that extraterrestrial aspartate formation is experimentally reproducible.

## Six major unresolved challenges constrain prebiotic aspartate synthesis

### The oxaloacetate decarboxylation race

The single greatest bottleneck is that **OAA decarboxylates to pyruvate faster than it can be aminated to aspartate** under most tested conditions. Barge et al. (2019) showed this definitively: iron oxyhydroxide experiments yielded alanine (from pyruvate, the OAA decomposition product) rather than aspartate. The ~5% transamination yield achieved by Harrison et al. (2023) requires the reaction to outpace decarboxylation in the first hour, but sustained production demands continuous OAA regeneration — something not yet demonstrated in a cyclic, self-sustaining manner. The glyoxylate-mediated OAA generation pathway (Eschenmoser & Krishnamurthy, *Nat. Commun.* 2017) works but requires relatively high concentrations and H₂O₂ as oxidant.

### Chirality remains without a definitive solution

All major prebiotic routes produce **racemic aspartate**. Known symmetry-breaking mechanisms include circularly polarized UV light (~1–2% ee in aminonitrile precursors), chiral mineral surfaces, and kinetic resolution via dipeptide-mediated transamination (Dherbassy/Moran group, *PNAS* 2024). The most aspartate-specific mechanism is **differential adsorption on calcite**: Hazen et al. (2001, *PNAS* 98, 5487) showed D-aspartate forms 3 strong bonds on calcite (214) surfaces (~8 kcal/mol lower energy than L-aspartate with 2 bonds), enabling chiral separation on mirror-related scalenohedral faces. Aspartate's ability to form **conglomerate enantiopure crystals** (unlike alanine's racemic crystals) provides a plausible amplification mechanism. Racemization half-lives are approximately **3,500–15,000 years at 25°C** (Bada & Schroeder), with aspartate racemizing faster than most amino acids via a succinimide intermediate.

### The concentration problem

Estimated steady-state amino acid concentrations in the prebiotic ocean are ~10⁻⁹ M — orders of magnitude below the mM concentrations used in most published experiments. Strecker synthesis requires [HCN] > 10⁻² M; peptide bond formation needs mM amino acid concentrations. Solutions include evaporative concentration in pools (up to 1000×), eutectic freezing (HCN concentrates to ~10 M at −23.4°C), mineral adsorption, aerosol enrichment, and submarine vent gradients. Whether protometabolic cycles can operate at ocean-relevant dilutions remains largely untested.

### Thermal stability limits viable environments

Aspartate decomposes via deamination (→ fumarate + NH₃) and decarboxylation (→ β-alanine + CO₂). At **240–250°C**, decomposition is rapid and irreversible (Miller & Bada, 1988). Activation energies range from **88.5–137.4 kJ/mol** for amino acid decomposition at 160–240°C (Hou et al., 2024, *Sci. Rep.*). At 100°C, half-lives are on the order of thousands of years, making alkaline vents (40–90°C) far more favorable than black smokers (>300°C). Solid-state decomposition occurs at ~270°C (ΔH_f = −973 kJ/mol). Protection by mineral adsorption, vesicle encapsulation, and peptide incorporation can extend stability.

### Competing reactions and pathway selectivity

HCN polymerization converts the vast majority of starting material to intractable "tar" rather than useful amino acids. Maillard-type reactions between amino acids and sugars produce complex brown polymers. Aspartate undergoes cyclization to succinimide intermediates, leading to β-aspartate isomers and accelerated racemization. In aqueous solution, hydrolysis of peptide bonds overwhelmingly outcompetes condensation at hydrothermal temperatures.

### Key experimentally undemonstrated steps

The following remain as the weakest links in proposed pathways:

- Sustained OAA production from CO₂ fixation under prebiotically plausible conditions without enzymes or ATP
- A complete, self-sustaining non-enzymatic rTCA cycle with turnover (9 of 11 intermediates demonstrated individually by Muchowska et al. 2019, but not as a running cycle)
- Prebiotic synthesis of pyridoxamine, required for the most efficient transamination route
- Operation of any protometabolic aspartate synthesis at ocean-relevant dilute concentrations
- Experimental validation that Hadean CO₂ saturation suppresses OAA decarboxylation

## Conclusion: converging pathways, persistent gaps

The prebiotic synthesis landscape for aspartic acid is richer than often appreciated, with HCN oligomer hydrolysis and the Bucherer-Bergs pathway from α-keto acids standing out as the most productive and most elegant routes, respectively. **HCN oligomerization uniquely yields aspartate as a major product** alongside glycine — not a trace component — making it the most efficient single route. The **Bucherer-Bergs pathway** (Pulletikurti et al., 2022) simultaneously generates aspartate and dihydroorotate, linking amino acid and pyrimidine nucleotide synthesis in a single reaction network. For network design in an agentic AI system, these two pathways should be prioritized as primary aspartate sources.

The protometabolic routes via oxaloacetate (transamination, reductive amination) face the fundamental kinetic barrier of OAA decarboxylation but achieve biological relevance through Cu²⁺/pyridoxamine catalysis at ~5% yields in one hour. The most plausible integrated scenario combines **alkaline vent CO₂ fixation** (Fe/Ni catalysts, 40–90°C) to generate keto acid intermediates, **rapid transamination or Bucherer-Bergs conversion** before OAA degrades, and **transport to lower-temperature environments** (surface pools, clay surfaces) for concentration via wet-dry cycling and chiral selection on calcite. The three unresolved challenges that most constrain pathway viability are OAA stabilization, operation at realistic dilutions, and the origin of homochirality — each representing a frontier where new experimental work could transform our understanding of how this metabolically essential amino acid first emerged on the prebiotic Earth.
