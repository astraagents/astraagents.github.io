# Prebiotic synthesis of decanoic acid: routes, evidence, and open challenges

**Fischer-Tropsch-type (FTT) synthesis on iron-nickel catalysts is the most experimentally supported route to prebiotic decanoic acid (C10:0)**, producing it as part of a continuous homologous series from C₂ to >C₃₅. Decanoic acid sits at a critical intersection in origin-of-life chemistry: it is the shortest fatty acid that forms stable vesicles at practical concentrations (~20–40 mM), making it the minimum viable building block for protocell membranes. Three major sources have been quantitatively assessed — post-impact FTT synthesis on iron-rich surfaces contributes roughly **100× more total fatty acid mass** than either meteoritic delivery or spark-discharge electrochemistry. Yet accumulating enough C10 to exceed its critical vesicle concentration remains a central unsolved problem. Mixed amphiphile systems and concentration mechanisms (evaporation, mineral adsorption, thermophoresis) offer partial solutions, but no experiment has yet demonstrated a complete, continuous pathway from CO₂ + H₂ to membrane-forming concentrations of decanoic acid under mild prebiotic conditions.

---

## 1. Fischer-Tropsch-type synthesis dominates the experimental landscape

FTT synthesis — the catalytic reduction of CO or CO₂ with H₂ over metal surfaces — is the most thoroughly investigated and productive pathway for abiotic fatty acid generation. The reaction builds carbon chains by sequential CO insertion at a metal surface, producing a homologous series of n-alkanoic acids, n-alkanols, n-alkanes, and n-alkanones that follow an Anderson-Schulz-Flory (ASF) distribution.

**The landmark experiments** were performed by McCollom, Ritter, and Simoneit (1999, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*), who heated formic acid and oxalic acid solutions to **175°C for 2–3 days** in stainless steel vessels. The steel walls (Fe-Ni-Cr alloy) served as both container and catalyst. Products spanned **C₂ to >C₃₅**, comprising n-alkanoic acids, n-alkanols, n-alkenes, n-alkanes, and alkanones, confirmed by ¹³C-labeled precursors. No odd-even carbon number preference was observed — a diagnostic signature of abiotic synthesis. Rushdi and Simoneit (2001, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*) extended this across **100–400°C**, finding maximum lipid yields at **150–250°C**, with n-alkanols dominant at lower temperatures shifting to n-alkanoic acids above 200°C. Above 300°C, cracking and reforming reactions compete with synthesis.

The gas-phase FTT experiments of Nooner and Oró (1979, *ACS Advances in Chemistry*) are particularly significant for decanoic acid. Using **Canyon Diablo meteoritic iron** as catalyst with K₂CO₃ promoter, D₂/CO at 1:1, and **400°C for 24–48 hours**, they produced normal saturated fatty acids from **C₅ to C₁₈** with a unimodal Gaussian distribution **peaking at C₉–C₁₀** — directly at the decanoic acid chain length. Fatty acid yields exceeded aliphatic hydrocarbon yields, and no odd-even carbon preference was observed.

A critical constraint emerged from McCollom, Sherwood Lollar, Lacrampe-Couloume, and Seewald (2010, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*): **CO and formic acid rapidly generate hydrocarbons** via FTT at 250°C on native iron, but **dissolved CO₂ and CH₄ produce no detectable hydrocarbons** under the same conditions. This means the presence of CO (or CO-generating intermediates like formate) rather than just dissolved CO₂ may be essential for prebiotic FTT synthesis — a significant geochemical constraint.

### The ASF distribution and C10 yield predictions

The ASF mole fraction for chain length n is: m_n = (1 − α) · α^(n−1), where α is the chain growth probability. For C10 specifically:

- At **α = 0.5**: C10 mole fraction ≈ 0.1% (negligible)
- At **α = 0.7**: C10 mole fraction ≈ 1.2%
- At **α = 0.85**: C10 mole fraction ≈ 3.4%
- At **α = 0.9**: C10 mole fraction ≈ 3.9%

Appreciable C10 production thus requires **α ≥ 0.7**. The aqueous FTT experiments of McCollom and Rushdi/Simoneit, which produced lipids up to C₃₅, imply relatively high α values on stainless steel surfaces. The Gaussian distribution observed by Nooner and Oró — peaking at C₉–C₁₀ — deviates from simple ASF behavior, suggesting their meteoritic iron/K₂CO₃ system optimally favored medium-chain products.

## 2. Alternative synthesis routes and extraterrestrial delivery

### Meteoritic delivery

Carbonaceous chondrites contain monocarboxylic acids (MCAs) from C₂ to C₁₂ as the **most abundant class of extractable organic compounds** — 10–100× more abundant than amino acids (Lawless and Yuen, 1979, *Nature*). Yuen and Kvenvolden (1973, *Nature*) first identified straight- and branched-chain MCAs in the Murchison and Murray meteorites. Abundances decrease roughly log-linearly with carbon number: acetic acid (C₂) dominates at ~330 nmol/g in Murchison, while C₈–C₁₂ acids are present at trace levels (<1 ppm). Fatty acids up to **C₁₂** have been confirmed in CM, CR, CV, and Tagish Lake meteorites.

Huang et al. (2005, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*) identified >50 MCAs in Murchison using SPME techniques, with **δ¹³C up to +32.5‰ and δD up to +2,024‰** — unambiguously extraterrestrial. The systematic decrease of δ¹³C with increasing carbon number (Yuen et al., 1984, *Nature*) provides direct evidence for **sequential chain growth from simpler precursors**, consistent with FTT-like synthesis on the meteorite parent body.

Deamer (1985, *Nature*) made the groundbreaking discovery that Murchison organic extracts spontaneously form **membranous vesicles**, later characterized by Deamer and Pashley (1989, *Origins of Life and Evolution of the Biosphere*) as containing amphiphilic components including branched C₁₀ acids such as 5-methylnonanoic acid. However, Pearce et al. (2019, *Icarus*) calculated that a single meteorite cannot deliver sufficient C₁₀ to exceed the critical vesicle concentration without subsequent concentration by evaporation.

### UV photolysis of interstellar ices

Dworkin, Deamer, Sandford, and Allamandola (2001, *PNAS*) demonstrated that UV irradiation of simulated interstellar ices (H₂O, CH₃OH, NH₃, CO at ~10 K) produces **amphiphilic molecules that spontaneously self-assemble into vesicles** capable of encapsulating dyes. Mass spectrometry revealed compounds up to **~22 carbon atoms** (Dworkin et al., 2004, *Advances in Space Research*). While specific C₁₀ fatty acid identification was not confirmed, the product mixture likely includes decanoic acid or structural analogs. Ammonia proved crucial for amphiphile formation.

### Spark-discharge experiments

Yuen, Lawless, and Edelson (1981, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*) showed Miller-Urey spark discharge produces MCAs from **C₂ to C₇** with a distribution remarkably similar to Murchison — supporting common abiotic synthesis mechanisms. More recent GC-MS analyses (2023) detected fatty acid fragments and longer-chain products, but **specific C₁₀ production from spark discharge has not been confirmed**. Electrochemical synthesis appears limited to shorter chains without supplementary chain-building mechanisms.

### Post-impact synthesis — the quantitative winner

Cohen et al. (2023, *ACS Earth and Space Chemistry*) performed the most comprehensive quantitative comparison of prebiotic fatty acid sources, concluding that **FTT synthesis on iron-rich surfaces from massive impactors** — operating in a post-impact reducing atmosphere of H₂, CO, and CO₂ at >100°C — could produce approximately **10² times more total fatty acid mass** than either meteoritic delivery or electrochemical synthesis. After extremely large impacts (~Vesta-sized, ~10²⁰ kg), Earth's surface could have functioned as a **global Fischer-Tropsch reactor** for thousands of years.

## 3. Mineral catalysts and their influence on chain length

The choice of mineral catalyst critically determines chain growth probability (α) and hence the yield of C₁₀ products. Prebiotically relevant catalysts include:

**Native metals and alloys** represent the most effective FTT catalysts. **Awaruite (Ni₃Fe)**, a common serpentinization product, achieves efficient CH₄ production from CO₂ (Horita and Berndt, 1999) and is among the most effective natural FTT catalysts. Metallic iron (Fe⁰) works well with CO but not dissolved CO₂ (McCollom et al., 2010). Stainless steel (Fe-Ni-Cr), used as reactor walls in many seminal experiments, functions as an effective multi-metal FTT catalyst — though this dual role of container and catalyst complicates interpretation.

**Iron oxides** play a central role. **Magnetite (Fe₃O₄)** — the primary product of serpentinization — catalyzes the water-gas shift reaction and shows limited FTT activity. Cobalt-bearing magnetite produced C₁–C₅ alkanes following ASF distribution at 300°C and 30 MPa (Ji et al., 2008, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*). A 2024 paper (Purvis et al., *Communications Earth & Environment*) claiming fatty acid production up to C₁₈ from magnetite at 90°C was **retracted in August 2025** due to GC-MS peak misidentification — a cautionary example of contamination risks in this field.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** — greigite (Fe₃S₄), pyrite (FeS₂), and mackinawite (FeS) — are abundant at hydrothermal vents. Greigite is a structural analog of magnetite (inverse spinel) and has been studied computationally for CO₂ activation (Santos-Carballal et al., 2018, *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society A*). These minerals can catalyze CO₂ reduction to short-chain organics, but production of C₁₀+ fatty acids on iron-sulfide catalysts specifically has not been convincingly demonstrated.

**Clay minerals**, particularly **montmorillonite**, serve primarily as concentrators and assembly catalysts rather than synthesis catalysts. One ton of montmorillonite can adsorb up to **150 kg of fatty acids**, preferentially binding longer-chain species. Hanczyc, Fujikawa, and Szostak (2003, *Science*) showed montmorillonite accelerates micelle-to-vesicle conversion by **100×** and enables RNA-loaded clay encapsulation within resulting vesicles. Mißbach et al. (2018, *Organic Geochemistry*) added montmorillonite to FTT experiments but did not observe dramatically different product distributions compared to steel-only controls.

## 4. Environmental settings for synthesis and assembly

### Alkaline hydrothermal vents offer continuous chemistry

Lost City-type alkaline hydrothermal vents operate at **40–116°C, pH 9–11**, with H₂ concentrations up to **15 mmol/kg** and formate up to **158 μmol/kg** — driven by serpentinization of ultramafic rock. Proskurowski et al. (2008, *Science*) provided compelling isotopic evidence for abiogenic C₁–C₄ hydrocarbon production at Lost City. The vent environment provides continuous H₂ supply, mineral catalysts (magnetite, awaruite), and **pH/redox gradients** between alkaline vent fluid and the mildly acidic Hadean ocean (pH ~5–6) that could drive organic synthesis.

Jordan et al. (2019, *Nature Ecology & Evolution*) directly addressed a long-standing criticism of the vent hypothesis by showing that **mixed C₁₀–C₁₅ single-chain amphiphiles** (fatty acids + 1-alkanols) form vesicles at pH 6.5 to >12, in seawater-like NaCl and divalent cation concentrations, and optimally at **~70°C**. This demonstrated that protocell assembly is compatible with alkaline vent conditions — previously thought impossible due to the divalent cation problem.

However, alkaline vents face a **carbon limitation**: dissolved inorganic carbon in end-member fluids is extremely low (0.1–0.6 μmol/L) due to rapid carbonate precipitation at high pH. Additionally, experimental FTT synthesis at <100°C from CO₂ + H₂ on prebiotically relevant minerals has primarily produced only C₁–C₃ compounds, leaving a gap between vent conditions and C₁₀ synthesis requirements.

### Hot springs enable concentration through wet-dry cycling

Damer and Deamer (2020, *Astrobiology*) championed the "hot spring hypothesis," arguing that **subaerial volcanic hot springs** provide wet-dry cycling for concentration, dehydration-driven condensation reactions, and fresh water that avoids the divalent cation problem plaguing marine settings. Milshteyn et al. (2018) confirmed that amphiphiles assemble into vesicles in hot spring water but **not in seawater**. Van Kranendonk et al. (2020) identified **3.5 Ga hot spring deposits** in the Pilbara Craton (Western Australia) capable of mobilizing all biologically essential elements.

### A multi-environment sequential scenario may be most realistic

The optimal environment differs for each step in the pathway to protocell membranes:

- **CO₂ reduction to CO/formate**: Deep subsurface serpentinization (200–400°C)
- **FTT chain growth to C₁₀**: 150–250°C with Fe/Ni catalysts (subsurface or post-impact surface)
- **Concentration**: Surface pools with evaporation, mineral adsorption, or thermophoresis
- **Vesicle self-assembly**: 20–70°C, pH 7–9, low ionic strength

This suggests a **transport-dependent pathway**: hydrothermal synthesis at depth, followed by transport to mixing zones or surface environments where concentration and self-assembly occur.

## 5. Decanoic acid's unique role in protocell membranes

Decanoic acid occupies an optimal position in the **solubility-vs-membrane stability tradeoff**. Octanoic acid (C₈) forms vesicles only above ~100–145 mM (Monnard and Deamer, 2003, *Methods in Enzymology*) — an improbably high prebiotic concentration. Lauric acid (C₁₂) has a much lower CVC (~1–5 mM) but its high melting point and low solubility limit spontaneous assembly. Decanoic acid's CVC of **~20–40 mM** (pH 7–8.5, room temperature; Groen et al., 2012, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*; Budin et al., 2014, *Biophysical Journal*) represents the practical minimum for prebiotically accessible vesicle formation from a pure single-chain amphiphile. The apparent pKₐ shifts from 4.9 in solution to **~7** in membranes, creating a vesicle-stable window at **pH 6.8–7.8** (Namani and Walde, 2005, *Langmuir*).

Mixed amphiphile systems dramatically improve the picture. Budin, Prywes, Zhang, and Szostak (2014, *Biophysical Journal*) showed that adding just **5% oleic acid (C₁₈:₁)** to decanoic acid reduces the CVC from ~40 mM to **~0.5 mM** — a nearly 100-fold decrease. This is prebiotically significant because abiotic synthesis inherently produces chain-length distributions. The Szostak group's standard prebiotically relevant composition — **4:1:1 decanoic acid:decanol:glycerol monodecanoate** — forms vesicles at ~20 mM total amphiphile, is thermostable from 0–100°C (Mansy and Szostak, 2008, *PNAS*), and supports non-enzymatic RNA copying when Mg²⁺ is chelated by citrate (Adamala and Szostak, 2013, *Science*).

Key functional properties of decanoic acid vesicles for protocell models include:

- **Permeability to activated nucleotides**, enabling template-directed synthesis inside vesicles without transport machinery (Mansy et al., 2008, *Nature*)
- **Growth by micelle incorporation**, producing thread-like filaments that divide under mild shear (Zhu and Szostak, 2009, *JACS*)
- **Competitive dynamics**: vesicles with encapsulated RNA or phospholipids grow at the expense of empty vesicles (Chen, Roberts, and Szostak, 2004, *Science*)
- **Thermocycling resilience**: only 0.5–1% content leakage per 25–90°C cycle, while enabling DNA strand separation (Mansy and Szostak, 2008, *PNAS*)

The **Mg²⁺ problem** — fatty acid membranes disrupt at low divalent cation concentrations, while RNA chemistry requires ≥50 mM Mg²⁺ — was resolved by Adamala and Szostak (2013, *Science*) using **200 mM sodium citrate**, which chelates Mg²⁺ sufficiently to protect membranes while still permitting primer extension at ~0.2 h⁻¹. Decylamine/decanoic acid (1:1) vesicles offer an alternative solution, tolerating up to **0.1 M Mg²⁺/Ca²⁺** (Namani and Deamer, 2008, *Origins of Life and Evolution of the Biosphere*) with a CVC of only **~0.5–1 mM**.

## 6. Key intermediates and the chain elongation problem

The pathway to decanoic acid proceeds through a homologous series of progressively longer fatty acids. In meteorites, each step from C₂ to C₁₀ shows roughly an order-of-magnitude decrease per 2-carbon increment. The essential intermediates are:

- **C₁ (formate/CO)**: The carbon feedstock; formate reaches 36–158 μmol/kg at Lost City
- **C₂ (acetic acid)**: The dominant MCA in meteorites; 13–91% of total MCAs
- **C₄ (butyric acid)**: Present in meteorites and FTT products
- **C₆–C₈ (hexanoic to octanoic acid)**: The transition zone; C₈ is the minimum for vesicle formation
- **C₁₀ (decanoic acid)**: The practical minimum for protocell membranes at reasonable concentrations

The FTT chain growth mechanism adds one carbon per CO insertion step: R-CH_x* + CO → R-CH₂-CH_x*. Getting from C₂ to C₁₀ requires **8 sequential insertions**. At α = 0.7 per step, the probability of completing all 8 is 0.7⁸ ≈ 5.7%. At α = 0.5, it drops to 0.5⁸ ≈ 0.4%. This **exponential attruation** is the fundamental thermodynamic-kinetic bottleneck.

An important finding from Pearce et al. (2023, *ACS Earth and Space Chemistry*) states explicitly: "We are unaware of any abiotic mechanisms by which carbon chains shorter than 8 can be extended to at least 8 carbons" outside of FTT-type synthesis. Kolbe electrolysis can couple two carboxylate radicals (e.g., 2 × C₅ → C₈ alkane), but produces hydrocarbons rather than carboxylic acids, requiring subsequent oxidation. The cyanosulfidic protometabolism of Patel et al. (2015, *Nature Chemistry*) generates C₂–C₃ building blocks and glycerol but has **not demonstrated** a pathway to C₁₀ fatty acids.

## 7. Unresolved challenges and the path forward

Five major bottlenecks persist in prebiotic decanoic acid synthesis:

**First, the CO₂-to-CO kinetic barrier.** Dissolved CO₂ — the dominant carbon species in hydrothermal fluids — is extremely resistant to reduction under aqueous conditions below 300–400°C, even with catalysts. McCollom and Seewald (2001, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta*) found the reaction thermodynamically favorable but kinetically inhibited. Most successful C₁₀-producing experiments used CO or CO-generating precursors (formic acid, oxalic acid), not dissolved CO₂. Whether sufficient CO exists in prebiotic hydrothermal systems remains debated.

**Second, the concentration gap.** Even the most optimistic FTT synthesis scenarios produce decanoic acid at concentrations far below its CVC. Cohen et al. (2023) estimated ~15 mM is needed for membrane formation. Solutions include chain-length heterogeneity (Budin et al., 2014), evaporative concentration in surface pools, thermophoretic accumulation in hydrothermal gradients (Budin, Bruckner, and Szostak, 2009, *JACS*), and mineral surface adsorption. No single mechanism has been shown sufficient alone.

**Third, the synthesis-assembly temperature paradox.** Optimal FTT synthesis occurs at 150–250°C, while vesicle assembly and stability favor 20–70°C. This requires either spatial separation (synthesis at depth, assembly at surface) or temporal separation (post-impact cooling). Jordan et al. (2019) showed mixed amphiphile vesicles form optimally at ~70°C, partially bridging this gap.

**Fourth, contamination and artifact risks.** The retraction of Purvis et al. (2024/2025, *Communications Earth & Environment*) — where GC-MS peaks were misidentified as fatty acids produced from magnetite at 90°C — highlights persistent analytical challenges. The catalytic role of stainless steel reactor walls in the McCollom and Rushdi/Simoneit experiments also complicates interpretation of mineral-specific catalysis.

**Fifth, selectivity.** FTT synthesis produces complex mixtures of alkanes, alkenes, alcohols, aldehydes, and ketones alongside fatty acids. Only fatty acids and fatty alcohols are membrane-forming amphiphiles. The fraction of useful amphiphilic products among total organics is typically modest.

Recent advances offer cautious optimism. A 2026 *Nature Communications* paper reported an **organocatalytic cascade** starting from acetaldehyde with prebiotic imidazolidine-4-thione that produces lipid-like molecules up to C₂₀ that spontaneously self-assemble into protocells — a novel non-FTT pathway. Lane and colleagues (2023) noted that FTT synthesis on steel carbide bonds produced a **peak spectrum at C₁₀–C₁₅**, matching protocell membrane requirements. Cornell et al. (2019, *PNAS*) showed prebiotic amino acids bind to and stabilize decanoic acid membranes, supporting co-evolutionary scenarios.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of decanoic acid is best understood not as a single pathway problem but as a **systems chemistry challenge** requiring the integration of synthesis, transport, concentration, and self-assembly across multiple environmental settings. FTT synthesis on iron-nickel catalysts — particularly in post-impact scenarios — remains the most productive demonstrated route, with the Nooner and Oró experiments showing optimal yields precisely at C₉–C₁₀. The field's central insight over the past decade is that **pure compound thinking is misleading**: prebiotic chemistry would have produced heterogeneous amphiphile mixtures, and these mixtures form vesicles far more readily than any pure component. The most critical experimental gap is the absence of a demonstrated continuous pathway from CO₂ + H₂ to C₁₀ fatty acids at temperatures below 100°C on prebiotically relevant mineral catalysts — the retracted Purvis et al. claim having left this question squarely open. For an agentic AI system designing prebiotic synthesis networks, the key design parameters are: chain growth probability α ≥ 0.7 on the chosen catalyst, availability of CO rather than just CO₂ as carbon source, a concentration mechanism achieving ≥15 mM total amphiphile, and mixed amphiphile compositions that exploit the dramatic CVC reduction from chain-length heterogeneity.
