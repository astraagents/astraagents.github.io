# Prebiotic synthesis of methionine: sulfur's hardest puzzle in origin-of-life chemistry

**Methionine can be synthesized under prebiotic conditions, but only through a narrow mechanistic corridor requiring the co-availability of methanethiol, acrolein, HCN, and ammonia.** The most robust demonstrated route — a Strecker synthesis proceeding through the intermediate methional — was first confirmed by Van Trump and Miller in 1972 and validated by Parker, Cleaves, and Bada's 2011 reanalysis of Miller's archived 1958 H₂S experiment, which identified racemic methionine alongside six other organosulfur compounds. Despite this experimental success, methionine remains one of the most challenging proteinogenic amino acids to produce prebiotically. Its thioether bond demands a methylated sulfur source (methanethiol) whose abiotic availability on early Earth is actively contested, its thermal lability constrains viable geological settings, and it has never been conclusively detected as indigenous in any meteorite. Whether methionine entered the genetic code early or late remains a live debate reshaped by a landmark 2024 PNAS study.

---

## The acrolein-methional pathway is methionine's only proven prebiotic route

All experimentally verified prebiotic syntheses of methionine converge on a single mechanistic sequence, first elucidated by Van Trump and Miller (*Science*, 1972). The pathway proceeds in three steps. First, **acrolein** (CH₂=CH-CHO) forms via aldol condensation of formaldehyde and acetaldehyde — both abundant products of spark discharge or photochemistry. Cleaves demonstrated in 2003 (*Monatshefte für Chemie*) that acrolein forms from very dilute aldehyde solutions under neutral to basic aqueous conditions, establishing its prebiotic plausibility. Second, methanethiol undergoes **Michael addition** (1,4-conjugate addition) to acrolein's activated double bond, yielding 3-methylthiopropionaldehyde (methional, CH₃-S-CH₂-CH₂-CHO). This reaction proceeds readily in mild aqueous conditions because the thiolate anion is an excellent soft nucleophile. Third, methional enters the **Strecker synthesis** — reacting with HCN and ammonia to form an α-aminonitrile that hydrolyzes to methionine.

This pathway is remarkably robust. It mirrors the industrial synthesis of racemic DL-methionine and operates without enzymatic catalysis. The thioether (C-S-C) bond characteristic of methionine forms specifically at the Michael addition step, distinguishing it mechanistically from cysteine synthesis (which requires thiol addition to dehydroalanine or direct H₂S incorporation). Acrolein serves as a pivotal hub intermediate — reacting with H₂S to produce homocysteine precursors, with water to yield homoserine precursors, and with amines to form diaminobutyric acid, making it a branch-point molecule in prebiotic sulfur amino acid chemistry.

## Spark discharge experiments provide the strongest experimental evidence

The experimental record for prebiotic methionine synthesis rests primarily on two landmark studies. Van Trump and Miller (1972, *Science* 178:859–860) applied electric spark discharge to a gas mixture of CH₄, N₂, NH₃, H₂O, and H₂S (or CH₃SH), detecting methionine at a yield of **~3 × 10⁻³ relative to glycine**. They identified acrolein as a discharge product and proposed it as the key intermediate. This was the first definitive demonstration that methionine could form under plausible prebiotic conditions.

The second critical dataset emerged from Parker, Cleaves, Bada, and colleagues (2011, *PNAS* 108:5526–5531; *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 41:201–212), who reanalyzed archived samples from a previously unpublished 1958 Miller experiment. That experiment used **CH₄ (258 mm Hg), CO₂ (87 mm Hg), H₂S (100 mm Hg), and NH₃ (250 mm Hg)** with electric spark discharge over three days. Modern HPLC-fluorescence and time-of-flight mass spectrometry revealed **23 amino acids and 4 amines**, including **7 organosulfur compounds**: racemic methionine, S-methylcysteine (reported for the first time from a prebiotic experiment), methionine sulfoxide, methionine sulfone, homocysteic acid, cysteamine, and tentative ethionine. The racemic D/L ratio (1.0 ± 10%) confirmed authentic prebiotic synthesis rather than biological contamination. Bada noted this H₂S experiment produced "a lot richer" amino acid yields than any other Miller experiment.

Beyond spark discharge, several other approaches have been explored with more limited success:

- **UV irradiation of ammonium thiocyanate** (Steinman, Smith, and Silver, 1968, *Science* 159:1108–1109) reportedly produced methionine at <1% yield, though later attempts at reproduction raised doubts about its reliability.
- **Hydrothermal Strecker synthesis** (Hennet, Holm, and Engel, 1992, *Naturwissenschaften* 79:361–365) using KCN, formaldehyde, NH₄Cl, NaSH, and a pyrite-pyrrhotite-magnetite mineral assemblage at **150°C and 10 bar** detected methionine at trace levels (0.050–0.058 mM).
- **Iron-sulfur world experiments** by Huber and Wächtershäuser (2006, *Science* 314:630–632) produced simple α-amino acids from CO, cyanide, and methylthio ligands on Fe-Ni precipitates at 80–120°C, but **methionine itself was not among the reported products**.
- **Cyanosulfidic chemistry** (Patel et al., 2015, *Nature Chemistry* 7:301–307) demonstrated integrated synthesis of amino acid precursors (Gly, Ala, Ser, Thr) from HCN and H₂S with UV-driven Cu photoredox catalysis, but **did not produce methionine**.

Methionine has **never been conclusively identified as indigenous in any carbonaceous chondrite meteorite**. Pizzarello and Shock (2010, *Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology*) explicitly noted this absence, attributed to methionine's oxidative instability during parent body alteration and terrestrial weathering. When trace methionine appears in Murchison meteorite extracts, it is classified as probable terrestrial contamination. Similarly, neither cometary samples (Rosetta at Comet 67P; Stardust from Comet Wild 2) nor interstellar detections have yielded methionine — only glycine has been confirmed extraterrestrially among amino acids.

## Methanethiol availability is the critical bottleneck

The entire prebiotic methionine pathway hinges on methanethiol (CH₃SH) — and its abiotic availability on early Earth is the most contested question in this field. Three potential sources have been investigated.

**Iron sulfide-mediated synthesis** was first demonstrated by Heinen and Lauwers (1996, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 26:131–150), who reacted FeS with H₂S and CO₂ under anaerobic aqueous conditions at ≥75°C, producing methanethiol at ~0.06% yield based on H₂S input. This reaction — essentially 3H₂ + H₂S + CO₂ → CH₃SH + 2H₂O — established that hydrothermal FeS systems could generate this key precursor. A 2024 study in *Communications Earth & Environment* significantly advanced this finding by using **MoS₂ as catalyst with supercritical CO₂**, achieving up to **7.9% conversion of H₂S to methanethiol** at 200°C over 14 days — the highest abiotic yield reported to date.

However, a sobering challenge came from Reeves, McDermott, and Seewald (2014, *PNAS* 111:5474–5479), who measured methanethiol in diverse modern mid-ocean ridge hydrothermal fluids and found concentrations **far below thermodynamic predictions** for abiotic synthesis. They concluded that observed CH₃SH derives primarily from thermal decomposition of preexisting biological organic matter, not from CO₂ reduction. While this does not invalidate prebiotic scenarios (early Earth lacked biogenic contamination), it underscores that **plausible prebiotic methanethiol concentrations were likely nanomolar to low micromolar** — orders of magnitude below laboratory experiment conditions.

The third source — **spark discharge and volcanic lightning** — directly generates CH₃SH from CH₄ and H₂S through radical recombination (•CH₃ + HS• → CH₃SH). This is the most straightforward route and the one operative in Miller-type experiments where methionine was successfully produced.

## Volcanic lightning environments outperform hydrothermal vents for methionine

The geological setting most favorable for methionine synthesis is **volcanic eruption plumes with associated lightning**, not deep-sea hydrothermal vents. This conclusion follows from both experimental evidence and thermodynamic/kinetic considerations.

Volcanic environments provide the ideal reducing gas mixture (CH₄, H₂S, NH₃, H₂O) and activation energy (electrical discharge from volcanic lightning) needed for the acrolein-methional-Strecker pathway. The 2008 "volcanic apparatus" reanalysis by Johnson, Cleaves, and Bada (*Science* 322:404) — simulating a volcanic steam jet with lightning — produced 22 amino acids, more diverse than any previous Miller experiment. Adding H₂S to such systems, as in Miller's 1958 experiment, further generates sulfur amino acids including methionine.

**Black smoker hydrothermal vents** (250–400°C) present a paradox: while rich in H₂S and mineral catalysts, their extreme temperatures rapidly decompose amino acids. Methionine is among the **most thermally labile** amino acids — **86% decomposed after 24 hours at 180°C** in dry heating studies. Bada and Miller argued that amino acid half-lives of minutes at black smoker temperatures make net accumulation implausible.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (Lost City type, 40–90°C) offer more moderate conditions. Thermodynamic calculations by Amend and Shock (1998, *Science* 281:1659–1662) showed amino acid synthesis is energetically favorable in moderately reduced submarine hydrothermal solutions. Russell and Martin proposed that FeS/NiS membranes in alkaline vent chimney walls could catalyze organic synthesis at the vent-ocean interface. However, alkaline vents have lower H₂S concentrations than black smokers, limiting sulfur amino acid precursors, and **no methionine synthesis has been demonstrated** in alkaline vent simulations.

Mineral catalysts relevant across these environments include:

- **Nickel-iron sulfides** — (Fe,Ni)S coprecipitates catalyze thioester formation from CO + CH₃SH (Huber and Wächtershäuser, 1997) and amino acid synthesis from CO, CN⁻, and SCH₃⁻ (2006), directly manipulating C-S bonds
- **Mackinawite (FeS)** — generates methanethiol from CO₂ + H₂S; Mn-doped FeS increases methanol production 5-fold
- **Pyrite (FeS₂)** — strongly adsorbs methionine via sulfur-vacancy defect sites; photocatalytic reductive amination of α-keto acids reported on natural pyrite with **14.5–42.4% enantiomeric excess** for D-amino acids
- **ZnS** — photocatalyzes reversible amination of α-keto acids under UV, potentially converting methionine's keto acid precursor (2-oxo-4-methylthiobutyrate) to methionine
- **MoS₂** — most effective catalyst for abiotic methanethiol synthesis from supercritical CO₂

## Sulfur's "thorniest" element creates cascading challenges

Sonny Harman of NASA Ames characterized sulfur as "perhaps the thorniest" of the biogenic elements in prebiotic chemistry. Multiple interlocking challenges explain why methionine is harder to synthesize than most amino acids.

**The reduced sulfur supply constraint.** On early Earth, volcanic outgassing provided H₂S and SO₂ in ratios determined by magma oxidation state. Mass-independent fractionation of sulfur isotopes (S-MIF) preserved in Archean rocks older than 2.3 Ga — first demonstrated by Farquhar et al. (2000, *Science* 289:756–758) — provides definitive evidence for an anoxic atmosphere. Archean seawater sulfate was extraordinarily low: **<2.5 μM**, roughly 10,000× below modern ocean levels. Sulfide concentrations in prebiotic surface waters, constrained by Ranjan et al. (2023, *AGU Advances*), were likely **<100 μM for sulfite** with variable but limited sulfide. These low concentrations mean sulfur amino acid precursors competed with iron precipitation (FeS formation) and photolytic destruction.

**The multi-precursor co-location problem.** Methionine's Strecker synthesis requires four reactants — methanethiol, acrolein, HCN, and ammonia — to co-occur at sufficient concentrations. Each is produced by different processes (mineral catalysis, aldol condensation, atmospheric chemistry, volcanic outgassing), making their simultaneous availability in one location a significant challenge. Proposed solutions include evaporitic concentration in closed-basin lakes, mineral surface adsorption, and eutectic freezing.

**Thermal lability constrains geological settings.** Methionine decomposes via C-S and C-C bond cleavage at elevated temperatures, with **86% loss at 180°C over 24 hours**. This effectively eliminates black smoker environments and constrains synthesis to settings below ~120°C — or requires very short residence times with rapid quenching.

**Comparison with cysteine highlights distinct difficulties.** Cysteine's free thiol is incompatible with nitriles (the thiol attacks the nitrile intramolecularly), making standard Strecker synthesis impossible for cysteine. Foden et al. (2020, *Science* 370:865–869) solved this with an elegant dehydroalanine + H₂S route. Methionine, paradoxically, may be **easier** than cysteine through the Strecker pathway because its thioether is inert to nitriles. However, methionine requires the additional challenge of methanethiol availability, while cysteine needs only H₂S — a far more abundant prebiotic sulfur species. Youssef-Saliba and Vallée (2021, *Synthesis* 53:2798–2808) noted that "partly because of the high reactivity of the thiol function, it could well be that the prebiotic synthesis of methionine was easier than that of cysteine."

## Homocysteine may have preceded methionine in prebiotic evolution

A compelling hypothesis positions **homocysteine** — not methionine — as the primordial sulfur amino acid. Homocysteine (HSCH₂CH₂CH(NH₂)COOH) forms via H₂S addition to acrolein followed by Strecker synthesis, requiring only H₂S rather than the scarcer methanethiol. Its intramolecular cyclization product, **homocysteine thiolactone**, is a stable five-membered ring thioester that can aminoacylate the N-terminus of existing peptide chains — potentially explaining methionine's role as the universal start codon amino acid.

Shalayel and Vallée (2019, *Life* 9:40; 2022, *Biomolecules* 12:555) demonstrated that homocysteine thiolactone forms readily from the corresponding aminonitrile precursor in water and is stable under mildly acidic to neutral conditions. This connects to de Duve's "thioester world" hypothesis and to the observation that methionyl-tRNA synthetase generates homocysteine thiolactone from misactivated homocysteine — a possible evolutionary relic.

The **norleucine hypothesis** offers another angle. Norleucine (2-aminohexanoic acid) is isosteric with methionine but lacks sulfur. It is abundantly produced in Miller-Urey experiments and present in meteorites. Methionyl-tRNA synthetase can mischarge norleucine onto tRNA^Met, and all 13 methionine residues in human cytochrome P450 can be replaced with norleucine without destroying enzymatic activity. Weber and Miller (1981, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*) noted norleucine's absence from the genetic code as "most striking," and Lazcano and colleagues proposed that methionine may have replaced norleucine when S-adenosylmethionine (SAM)-dependent methylation became biochemically important.

## Was methionine an early or late addition to the genetic code?

The traditional consensus, consolidated by Trifonov (2000, *Gene* 261:139–151) from 40–60 independent criteria, places methionine as the **~16th of 20 amino acids** to enter the genetic code — firmly "late." Higgs and Pudritz (2009, *Astrobiology* 9:483–490) identified 10 "early" amino acids (Gly, Ala, Asp, Glu, Val, Ile, Leu, Pro, Ser, Thr) produced consistently across Miller experiments, hydrothermal simulations, and meteorites. Methionine was explicitly excluded from this early set.

However, a provocative 2024 study by Wehbi, Masel, and colleagues (*PNAS*) challenges this consensus. By analyzing amino acid frequencies in protein domains dating to the **Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA)**, they found that **methionine, cysteine, and histidine are all enriched in LUCA proteins** — despite their classification as "late" additions. Their revised order reclassifies methionine as **early**, arguing that the traditional consensus suffers from circular reasoning: Miller's original 1953 experiment contained no sulfur source, so sulfur amino acids were necessarily absent, yet their absence was cited as evidence for lateness. The strongest predictor of recruitment order in the Wehbi study was simply **molecular weight**, with Trifonov's consensus contributing no additional predictive power.

This debate remains unresolved and represents one of the most active frontiers in origin-of-life research. If methionine entered the genetic code early, it implies that sulfur-rich, reducing environments were critical from the very beginning of biochemical evolution — strengthening scenarios involving volcanic or hydrothermal settings over neutral-atmosphere models.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of methionine is experimentally demonstrated but geochemically constrained. The acrolein-methional-Strecker pathway is the only verified mechanism, operating through a thiol Michael addition that forms the characteristic thioether bond. Spark discharge experiments with H₂S-containing reducing atmospheres produce methionine at ~0.3% of glycine yield — low but significant given glycine's own modest efficiency. Three critical insights emerge from this research. First, methanethiol availability is the rate-limiting factor for prebiotic methionine, and the 2024 discovery that MoS₂-catalyzed supercritical CO₂ systems achieve 7.9% H₂S-to-CH₃SH conversion may substantially ease this bottleneck. Second, homocysteine — accessible from the more abundant H₂S — likely preceded methionine and may explain the deep connection between sulfur amino acids and translation initiation. Third, the traditional classification of methionine as a "late" amino acid is now contested by phylogenomic evidence from LUCA protein domains, suggesting the field's understanding of sulfur's role in early biochemistry may need fundamental revision. The most promising path forward involves integrated "systems chemistry" approaches that test whether methionine synthesis can operate within the same reaction networks that produce other prebiotic building blocks — a challenge no laboratory has yet met.
