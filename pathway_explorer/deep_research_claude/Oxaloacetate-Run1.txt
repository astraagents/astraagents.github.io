# Prebiotic synthesis of oxaloacetate: routes, evidence, and unsolved challenges

**Oxaloacetate (OAA) sits at the metabolic crossroads of life, yet no single abiotic experiment has produced it efficiently from CO₂ alone.** This paradox—a molecule central to the citric acid cycle that is chemically unstable and prebiotically elusive—defines one of the hardest problems in origin-of-life chemistry. Multiple laboratories have now demonstrated fragments of the reverse tricarboxylic acid (rTCA) cycle and produced OAA as a transient intermediate using iron salts, glyoxylate feedstocks, and ZnS photocatalysis, but the direct reductive carboxylation of pyruvate to OAA remains unachieved under bulk aqueous prebiotic conditions. Recent breakthroughs—particularly Muchowska et al.'s iron-promoted network generating 9 of 11 TCA intermediates (Nature, 2019), Springsteen and Krishnamurthy's glyoxylate-driven protometabolic cycles (Nature Communications, 2018), and microdroplet-accelerated carboxylation (2025)—have dramatically narrowed the gap but not yet closed it. OAA's notorious instability (half-life ~7 hours at 25°C) means any prebiotic scenario must produce and consume it rapidly, making it a fleeting hub rather than an accumulated product.

## Eight routes to oxaloacetate under prebiotic conditions

Research spanning four decades has identified at least eight distinct pathways that could generate OAA abiotically, ranging from mineral-surface catalysis to interstellar ice chemistry.

**Glyoxylate-based pathways represent the most experimentally validated routes.** Butch, Krishnamurthy and colleagues (Journal of the American Chemical Society, 2013) demonstrated that cyanide-catalyzed dimerization of glyoxylate produces tartrates in **80% yield** under alkaline aqueous conditions at room temperature; simple dehydration of tartrate yields OAA. Building on this, Springsteen, Krishnamurthy and co-workers (Nature Communications, 2018) established two linked protometabolic cycles—the HKG cycle and the malonate cycle—operating at **pH 7.0–8.5, 23–50°C**, with no metal catalysts required. In the HKG cycle, OAA reacts with glyoxylate to form oxalomalate (complete within minutes at 23°C), which decarboxylates to 4-hydroxy-2-ketoglutarate (HKG) in ≥98% yield over 3 hours. Oxidation with H₂O₂ then regenerates malate and subsequently OAA, completing the cycle. The in situ generation of OAA from pyruvate through this pathway was described as "a long-sought and elusive goal in prebiotic chemistry." Stubbs, Krishnamurthy, and Springsteen (Nature Chemistry, 2020) further showed that pyruvate and glyoxylate alone produce a series of α-ketoacid rTCA analogues under mild aqueous conditions without any metal catalyst.

**Metal-catalyzed rTCA networks provide the broadest intermediate coverage.** Muchowska, Varma, and Moran demonstrated in 2017 (Nature Ecology & Evolution) that Fe⁰, Zn²⁺, and Cr³⁺ promote **6 of 11 rTCA reactions** in consecutive sequence, including the OAA → malate → fumarate → succinate three-step reduction-dehydration-reduction pathway. Their landmark 2019 study (Nature) showed that simply combining pyruvate and glyoxylate with Fe²⁺ in water at 70°C generates **9 of 11 TCA cycle intermediates**, including all five universal metabolic precursors. OAA formed as a key network intermediate, confirmed by detection of its downstream products (aspartate upon addition of hydroxylamine, malonate from oxidative decarboxylation). This network simultaneously runs anabolic build-up and catabolic breakdown—a "life-like regime."

**The iron-sulfur world hypothesis** proposed by Wächtershäuser (Microbiological Reviews, 1988; PNAS, 1990) envisions an archaic rTCA cycle on pyrite/pyrrhotite surfaces powered by the exergonic reaction FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂ (ΔG = **−10.0 kcal/mol**). Huber and Wächtershäuser experimentally demonstrated activated acetic acid synthesis from CO on (Fe,Ni)S surfaces (Science, 1997), and Cody et al. synthesized pyruvate from CO + FeS under hydrothermal conditions at 250°C and 50–200 MPa (Science, 2000). However, **the specific pyruvate → OAA carboxylation step on FeS minerals has never been experimentally confirmed**—it remains the major unfulfilled prediction of this theory.

**Cyanosulfidic chemistry** developed by Sutherland's group offers complementary routes. Patel et al. (Nature Chemistry, 2015) showed HCN homologation with H₂S and UV-driven Cu(I)/Cu(II) photoredox cycling produces amino acid, nucleotide, and lipid precursors, with α-oxo carboxylates including OAA identified as key connecting intermediates. Ritson (Science Advances, 2021) demonstrated photochemical synthesis of Krebs cycle intermediates from cyanosulfidic starting materials, with OAA serving as a central intermediate from which citrate is produced via NaSH/UV photolysis. Yadav, Krishnamurthy and co-workers (Nature Chemistry, 2022) showed HCN acts as a mild reducing agent that efficiently reduces OAA to malate.

**CO₂ fixation to pyruvate**—the essential upstream step—has been achieved through multiple routes. Varma et al. (Nature Ecology & Evolution, 2018) demonstrated native iron reduces CO₂ to formate, acetate, and pyruvate at 30–100°C. Preiner et al. (Nature Ecology & Evolution, 2020) showed greigite (Fe₃S₄), magnetite (Fe₃O₄), and awaruite (Ni₃Fe) catalyze H₂-dependent CO₂ fixation at 100°C producing formate (up to **200 mM**), acetate (~100 μM), and pyruvate (~10 μM). Beyazay et al. (Nature Communications, 2023) pushed this to **ambient temperature (25°C)** using Ni₃Fe nanoparticles. ZnS photocatalysis (Zhang and Martin, JACS, 2006) drives OAA → malate with 75% yield and fumarate → succinate with 95% yield under UV irradiation at 15°C. Finally, Kaiser et al. (PNAS, 2025) detected OAA among Krebs cycle intermediates synthesized abiotically in irradiated interstellar ice analogs, and recent work on aqueous microdroplets (2025) demonstrated spontaneous pyruvate + CO₂ carboxylation at room temperature—a potential breakthrough for the most challenging step.

## Key intermediates form an interconnected prebiotic network

OAA does not exist in isolation prebiotically—it is embedded in a web of interconvertible C₂–C₅ carboxylic acids, three of which serve as critical branch-point molecules.

**Glyoxylate (C₂) is the most versatile prebiotic node.** The simplest α-keto acid, it connects to sugars through dihydroxyfumarate (Eschenmoser, Tetrahedron, 2007), to amino acids through transamination, to pyrimidines through orotate, and to all TCA intermediates through aldol condensation with pyruvate. Sources include HCN polymer hydrolysis (Ruiz-Bermejo, Chemistry—A European Journal, 2016), CO₂ electrochemical reduction, and ZnS photochemistry. Glyoxylate effectively replaces CO₂ as the carbon-fixation substrate in the most successful prebiotic TCA experiments.

**Pyruvate (C₃) bridges linear and cyclic metabolism.** It forms from CO₂ via native iron reduction (Varma et al., 2018), from CO + FeS under hydrothermal conditions (Cody et al., 2000), from sugar phosphate degradation with Fe²⁺ at 70°C (Keller et al., Molecular Systems Biology, 2014), and from glycolaldehyde through Sutherland's phosphorylation-controlled triose glycolysis (Nature Chemistry, 2017). Pyruvate has been detected in carbonaceous meteorites (Cooper et al., PNAS, 2011) and is the immediate precursor to OAA via carboxylation.

**Malate, fumarate, and succinate** form the reduction-dehydration-reduction segment downstream of OAA, and this sequence is among the best-demonstrated prebiotic rTCA fragments. Malate forms from OAA photoreduction on ZnS (75% yield), from FeS/H₂S reduction (6% yield at 100°C, pH 7–10), and from cyanide-mediated reduction (Yadav et al., 2022). Fumarate derives from Zn²⁺-promoted dehydration of malate (Muchowska et al., 2017). The uncatalyzed malate → fumarate dehydration, however, has a **half-life of approximately 10,000 years** (noted by Orgel)—a significant kinetic barrier. Succinate forms readily from fumarate reduction, including **95% yield** via ZnS photoreduction (Zhou and Guzman, Journal of Physical Chemistry C, 2016). Green rust (iron oxyhydroxides) catalyzes all hydration reactions of the rTCA cycle (Zimmermann et al., Angewandte Chemie, 2025).

**Citrate cleavage** to OAA + acetyl-CoA equivalent represents one of the most difficult steps. The retro-aldol reaction has been demonstrated only under harsh conditions—1 M HCl, 140°C, with Cr³⁺ (Muchowska et al., 2017). The discovery of bacteria with reversible citrate synthase requiring reduced ferredoxin (Mall et al., Science, 2018) suggests this reaction may have originally operated reversibly, but its prebiotic analog remains poorly constrained.

## Mineral catalysts act as primitive metalloenzymes

The mineral catalysts relevant to prebiotic OAA synthesis fall into four functional classes, each mimicking specific enzymatic activities found in modern metabolism.

**Iron-sulfur minerals serve as electron-transfer agents and CO₂-fixation catalysts.** Greigite (Fe₃S₄) contains Fe₄S₄ cubane units structurally identical to [4Fe-4S] clusters in ferredoxins and carbon monoxide dehydrogenase/acetyl-CoA synthase (CODH/ACS). Navrotsky et al. (PNAS, 2020) confirmed greigite is thermodynamically stable near ambient temperature, contradicting earlier assumptions of metastability. In Preiner et al.'s experiments, greigite catalyzed CO₂ + H₂ fixation producing formate, acetate, and pyruvate. Roldan et al. demonstrated greigite electrodes reduce CO₂ to methanol, formate, acetate, and pyruvate under ambient conditions, though at low Faradaic efficiencies (<0.02% at −1.3V vs. SHE). Ni-doping to form violarite (FeNi₂S₄) enhanced CO₂ reduction selectivity **~85-fold** and lowered onset potential to −0.5V vs. SHE. Mackinawite (FeS), with its layered [2Fe-2S] rhomb structure analogous to ferredoxin clusters, forms the inorganic membranes at alkaline hydrothermal vents. Ni-doped mackinawite membranes demonstrated CO₂-to-formate fixation at room temperature requiring a pH gradient ≥5 units (Hudson et al., 2020).

**Native metal alloys are the most effective CO₂-fixation catalysts discovered.** Awaruite (Ni₃Fe), the most common Ni-Fe alloy in serpentinizing systems, produced the highest yields in Preiner et al.'s study—**formate up to 200 mM** from H₂/CO₂ at 100°C. Beyazay et al. (2023) showed Ni₃Fe nanoparticles catalyze CO₂ fixation to pyruvate at 25°C, with ¹³C-labeled pyruvate further converting to acetate, parapyruvate, and citramalate (a C₅ product) at room temperature within one hour. Pentlandite ((Fe,Ni)₉S₈), a structural analogue to CODH and hydrogenase active sites, achieves **87% Faradaic efficiency** for CO₂ → CO electroreduction (Piontek et al., Chemical Science, 2019).

**ZnS (sphalerite) is the only demonstrated photocatalyst for rTCA reductive steps.** With a bandgap of ~3.5 eV (effective wavelength ~345 nm), ZnS under UV irradiation drives three of five reductive rTCA steps: OAA → malate, fumarate → succinate (95%), and oxoglutarate → oxalosuccinate (2.5%). Guzman and Martin (Astrobiology, 2008) derived the kinetic competition between OAA photoreduction (quantum efficiency φ = 0.13[OAA]/(2.1 × 10⁻³ + [OAA])) and thermal decarboxylation (log k = 11.74 − 4956/T), finding **90% reduction efficiency at 280 K** with a forward velocity of ~3 yr/cycle. This photochemistry requires shallow hydrothermal environments within the photic zone.

**Dissolved metal ions and iron oxyhydroxides complete the catalytic toolkit.** Fe²⁺ promotes the broadest range of TCA network reactions (Muchowska et al., 2019). Zn²⁺ catalyzes dehydration of malate to fumarate and isocitrate to aconitate. Cr³⁺ promotes hydration of aconitate to citrate/isocitrate. Green rust (fougerite, a mixed-valence Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ layered double hydroxide) was proposed by Russell as an essential membrane catalyst at alkaline vents; it promotes methane oxidation, CO₂ reduction, nitrate reduction to ammonium, and all hydration reactions of the rTCA cycle (Zimmermann et al., 2025).

## Alkaline vents emerge as the most plausible environment

Three geochemical settings have been proposed for prebiotic OAA synthesis, each with distinct advantages and limitations.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents (Lost City type) offer the most sustained and thermodynamically favorable conditions.** These systems operate at **40–90°C, pH 9–11** in the vent fluids, with **10–15 mM H₂** generated by serpentinization of ultramafic olivine. The Hadean ocean, rich in dissolved CO₂ (possibly 1–10 bar atmospheric pCO₂), would have had pH ~5–6, creating a natural proton gradient of **~5 pH units** across thin Fe(Ni)S mineral barriers—equivalent in magnitude and polarity to the proton-motive force in modern cells (~200 mV, as noted by Lane and Martin, 2012). The microporous labyrinth structure enables thermophoretic concentration of organics by up to **~5,000-fold** (Herschy et al., Journal of Molecular Evolution, 2014). Lost City has been active for ≥30,000 years; such systems likely persisted on the Hadean Earth for millions of years, providing sustained far-from-equilibrium chemistry. The mineral assemblage—mackinawite, greigite, magnetite, awaruite, fougerite—encompasses the full catalytic toolkit for acetyl-CoA pathway chemistry and rTCA steps.

**Black smoker vents (350–400°C, pH 2–3)** provide abundant metal sulfide catalysts and pyrite-formation energy but are far too hot for OAA stability; the half-life at such temperatures would be seconds or less. They are more plausible for initial C₁–C₂ chemistry (CO + CH₃SH → thioester) than for multi-step metabolic cycles. Wächtershäuser's original proposals targeted these environments, but the thermodynamic arguments have been questioned (Kalapos, 2007).

**Surface evaporitic pools and hot springs** enable wet-dry cycling that drives condensation reactions (peptide bonds, phosphodiester bonds) and cyclic anhydride formation from 1,4-diacids like succinate. Campbell et al. (Nature Communications, 2019) showed deliquescent minerals yield 10-fold higher oligomerization than non-deliquescent controls. These environments also permit ZnS photocatalysis if within the photic zone. However, they lack the sustained H₂ supply needed for reductive CO₂ fixation and are subject to UV degradation of sensitive intermediates. The Damer and Deamer "hot spring hypothesis" (Astrobiology, 2020) emphasizes these settings for polymerization rather than carbon fixation.

An emerging fourth environment—**aqueous microdroplets in atmospheric aerosols**—has recently shown promise for the problematic pyruvate carboxylation step, with reaction rates 10⁴–10⁶ times faster than in bulk solution. This setting may resolve the most difficult gap in prebiotic OAA synthesis.

## The five unresolved bottlenecks

Despite remarkable progress, five interconnected challenges prevent closure of the prebiotic OAA problem.

**The carboxylation gap remains the central obstacle.** The direct reductive carboxylation of pyruvate to OAA is thermodynamically unfavorable (endergonic) and kinetically sluggish without enzymes. In biology, pyruvate carboxylase requires ATP and biotin as cofactor. Ross (Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres, 2007) showed that pyrophosphate coupling makes this reaction too endergonic even under pyrite-pulled conditions. No bulk aqueous abiotic demonstration exists. The most successful workarounds—glyoxylate aldol condensation followed by oxidation (Springsteen et al., 2018) and microdroplet-accelerated carboxylation (2025)—effectively bypass or modify this step rather than achieving it directly. Moran has acknowledged that "the conversion of pyruvate to oxaloacetate" remains one of the trickiest reactions.

**OAA's intrinsic instability imposes a kinetic race condition.** With a half-life of **~6.9 hours at 25°C, pH 6.8** (k = 2.8 × 10⁻⁵ s⁻¹; Wolfenden, Lewis, and Yuan, JACS, 2011) and activation parameters ΔG‡ = 23.6 kcal/mol, ΔH‡ = 17.2 kcal/mol, OAA decarboxylates rapidly to pyruvate via a concerted six-membered cyclic transition state characteristic of β-keto acids. Divalent metal ions accelerate this: Ni²⁺ > Co²⁺ > Mg²⁺ > Mn²⁺. At hydrothermal temperatures (100°C+), the half-life drops to minutes. This means any prebiotic scenario must generate OAA faster than it degrades—or, more likely, must operate through OAA as a transient, rapidly consumed intermediate rather than an accumulated product. Guzman and Martin's analysis (2008) showed that ZnS photoreduction at 280 K achieves 90% OAA → malate conversion efficiency precisely because lower temperatures slow decarboxylation while photoreduction is temperature-independent.

**No complete self-sustaining abiotic rTCA cycle has been demonstrated.** The reduction and (de)hydration steps are well established, but the **C–C bond-forming carboxylation steps** (pyruvate → OAA, succinate → α-ketoglutarate via succinyl-CoA) and **citrate cleavage** (retro-aldol to OAA + acetyl-CoA) remain the principal gaps. As Harrison et al. noted (Life, 2023): "No prebiotic synthesis of oxaloacetate, in an autotrophic direction, has been shown." Orgel's posthumous critique (PLoS Biology, 2008) emphasized that finding isolated reactions is "necessary but not sufficient"—the challenge is running them simultaneously and selectively.

**Selectivity against parasitic side reactions is underappreciated.** Orgel argued that non-specific mineral catalysts would reduce pyruvate and α-ketoglutarate (disrupting the cycle) alongside desired reductions of OAA and oxalosuccinate. Competing reactions include OAA decarboxylation to pyruvate, isocitrate splitting to succinate + glyoxylate, malate fragmentation to glyoxylate + acetate, non-selective reductions, and tar/polymer formation. The Moran group's counter-evidence is encouraging—their Fe²⁺ network shows approximately **2× more material moving on-cycle than off-cycle**—but this selectivity may be insufficient for a self-sustaining autocatalytic cycle without continuous substrate feeding.

**The concentration problem pervades all dilute-ocean scenarios.** Estimated steady-state HCN concentration in the prebiotic ocean: ~2 × 10⁻⁶ M at pH 8, 0°C, dropping to 7 × 10⁻¹³ M at 100°C. Proposed solutions include eutectic freezing (10³–10⁶-fold concentration in ice veins), mineral surface adsorption, hydrothermal vent micropore trapping (5,000-fold thermophoretic concentration), wet-dry cycling in evaporitic pools, and microdroplet chemistry (10⁴–10⁶× faster rates). Each has experimental support, but none has been integrated into a complete OAA synthesis demonstration.

## Conclusion: a convergent picture with critical gaps

The prebiotic synthesis of oxaloacetate has progressed from pure speculation to a well-constrained experimental problem. Three independent research programs—Moran's metal-catalyzed networks, Krishnamurthy and Springsteen's glyoxylate protometabolic cycles, and Martin's mineral photoelectrochemistry—have each produced OAA or its functional equivalents under plausible prebiotic conditions. The field has converged on **glyoxylate rather than direct CO₂ carboxylation** as the most likely carbon source for the first OAA molecules, with Fe²⁺ and ZnS as the most effective abiotic catalysts. The upstream CO₂-to-pyruvate pathway is now well established through awaruite and native iron catalysis at temperatures as low as 25°C.

The critical remaining challenge is not whether OAA can form abiotically—it clearly can—but whether it can form and be consumed within a self-sustaining, autocatalytic network operating under a single coherent set of environmental conditions. The metabolism-first versus genetics-first debate increasingly appears to be a false dichotomy; Moran's group frames prebiotic metabolism as a "chemical path of least resistance" that could have been gradually pruned by emerging organic catalysts and eventually genetic systems. The instability of OAA, long seen as a liability, may actually be a feature: its rapid decarboxylation to pyruvate and its facile reduction to malate create a kinetic pull that drives the entire network forward. The molecule's fleeting existence in modern enzymatic catalysis—where it rarely accumulates—may faithfully reflect its ancient prebiotic character.
