# Prebiotic glycine synthesis: a complete pathway atlas for origin of life chemistry

**Glycine, the simplest amino acid, emerges through at least ten distinct prebiotic routes spanning every plausible early Earth environment — from spark-discharge atmospheres to alkaline hydrothermal vents to interstellar ices.** This convergence from multiple independent pathways explains why glycine is consistently the most abundant amino acid in Miller-Urey experiments, carbonaceous meteorites, and cometary samples alike. The synthesis network is anchored by three master precursors — HCN, formaldehyde, and ammonia — whose reactions with each other and with mineral catalysts generate glycine through Strecker synthesis, HCN polymer hydrolysis, reductive amination, photochemistry, and Fischer-Tropsch-type catalysis. Understanding these interconnected pathways, their environmental requirements, kinetic bottlenecks, and shared intermediates is essential for computationally modeling prebiotic chemistry networks.

---

## The Strecker synthesis remains the dominant pathway to prebiotic glycine

The Strecker synthesis — formaldehyde + HCN + ammonia → aminoacetonitrile → glycine — is the single most important route to prebiotic glycine, operating as the principal mechanism in Miller-Urey experiments, meteorite parent bodies, and potentially in interstellar ices. Stanley Miller himself identified this as the mechanism underlying his 1953 spark discharge results (Miller, 1953, *Science* 117:528–529; Miller, 1957, *Biochimica et Biophysica Acta*).

The mechanism proceeds through four steps with well-characterized energetics. First, ammonia attacks formaldehyde to produce aminomethanol (NH₂CH₂OH), which dehydrates to methanimine (CH₂=NH). Cyanide then adds to the imine, yielding aminoacetonitrile (NH₂CH₂CN). Finally, hydrolysis proceeds through glycinamide (NH₂CH₂CONH₂) to glycine. Ab initio molecular dynamics simulations by Magrino, Pietrucci, and Saitta (2021, *Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters* 12:2630–2637) mapped the complete free energy landscape in explicit aqueous solution: glycinonitrile formation has a rate-limiting barrier of **15 kcal/mol**, nitrile hydration to glycinamide requires **23 kcal/mol**, and the final amide hydrolysis to glycine faces the highest barrier at **31 kcal/mol**. The overall reaction is strongly exergonic, with glycine at **−37 kcal/mol** relative to initial reactants.

A critical alternative exists through the **Bucherer-Bergs pathway**, where aminoacetonitrile reacts with CO₂ (abundant in the early atmosphere) to form hydantoin, which hydrolyzes to N-carbamoyl-glycine, then to glycine. Taillades, Commeyras, and colleagues (1998, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 28:61–77) demonstrated that under realistic prebiotic conditions — [HCN] and [HCHO] ~1 g/L, pH 8, 25°C, low [NH₃] — the system produces 99.9% glycolonitrile and only 0.1% aminoacetonitrile. However, CO₂ catalyzes conversion of that small aminoacetonitrile fraction to hydantoin, shifting equilibrium productively. Pulletikurti et al. (2022, *Nature Chemistry*) achieved **80% hydantoin yield** from pyruvate + cyanide + ammonia in bicarbonate buffer at room temperature, demonstrating this pathway bridges prebiotic aldehyde chemistry and modern metabolism.

## Miller-Urey experiments and their modern reanalyses reveal far richer chemistry than originally appreciated

Miller's original 1953 experiment — continuous spark discharge through CH₄/NH₃/H₂O/H₂ for one week — identified five amino acids, with **glycine as the most abundant at ~1.05% of total carbon** (Miller, 1955, *Journal of the American Chemical Society*). The mechanism was purely chemical: sparks produced HCN and aldehydes in the gas phase, which dissolved and underwent Strecker synthesis in the aqueous flask.

The 2008 reanalysis by Jeffrey Bada's group transformed our understanding. After Miller's death in 2007, Bada inherited sealed sample vials from three distinct experimental setups. Using modern HPLC-MS and UPLC-ToF-MS, Johnson et al. (2008, *Science* 322:404) found that the classic experiment actually produced **14 amino acids and 5 amines** — far more than Miller's 1950s-era paper chromatography could detect. A previously unpublished volcanic spark discharge apparatus, using a steam-aspirating nozzle simulating volcanic lightning, yielded **22 amino acids and 5 amines**, many hydroxylated. Most remarkably, Parker et al. (2011, *PNAS* 108:5526–5531) reanalyzed Miller's 1958 H₂S-containing experiment and found **23 amino acids, 4 amines, and 7 organosulfur compounds** — with amino acid distributions that correlated remarkably well with carbonaceous chondrite abundances.

The atmospheric composition debate profoundly affects these yields. Under strongly reducing conditions (CH₄/NH₃/H₂), glycine yields reach several percent of total carbon. Under neutral CO₂/N₂/H₂O atmospheres, yields plummet by orders of magnitude — though Cleaves et al. (2008, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 38:105–115) showed that adding ferrous iron as an oxidation inhibitor during hydrolysis substantially increased detected amino acids, suggesting previous low yields from neutral atmospheres were partly analytical artifacts. Schlesinger and Miller (1983, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*) demonstrated that **CO-containing atmospheres yield bioorganic compounds comparable to strongly reducing atmospheres**, an important finding since CO is geochemically more plausible than CH₄ as a long-lived atmospheric constituent.

## Hydrothermal vents, serpentinization, and iron-sulfur mineral catalysis provide submarine routes to glycine

Two fundamentally different hydrothermal environments offer glycine synthesis potential, with alkaline vents dramatically more favorable than black smokers.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (Lost City type) operate at **40–90°C, pH 9–11**, with H₂ concentrations up to **15 mmol/kg** generated by serpentinization of olivine. The Russell-Martin-Lane model proposes these vents as the cradle of prebiotic chemistry: natural pH gradients of ~5 units across thin Fe(Ni)S mineral barriers between alkaline vent fluid and the mildly acidic Hadean ocean (pH ~5.5) create electrochemical potentials of ~180 mV — directly analogous to biological chemiosmotic coupling. Wimmer et al. (2021) calculated that **97% of the ~400 reactions needed to synthesize amino acids, nucleobases, and cofactors are exergonic** under serpentinizing vent conditions.

The mineral catalysis results are striking. **Preiner et al. (2020, *Nature Ecology & Evolution* 4:534–542)** demonstrated that greigite (Fe₃S₄), magnetite (Fe₃O₄), and awaruite (Ni₃Fe) catalyze CO₂ + H₂ → formate (up to **200 mM**), acetate (~100 µM), pyruvate (~10 µM), and methanol (~100 µM) at 100°C under alkaline conditions. Even more remarkably, **Beyazay et al. (2023, *Nature Communications* 14:570)** showed Ni₃Fe nanoparticles catalyze CO₂ fixation to pyruvate and citramalate at just **25°C under 25 bar** — room temperature catalysis on a mineral that forms naturally in serpentinizing systems.

The path from these C₁–C₃ intermediates to glycine proceeds via reductive amination. **Barge et al. (2019, *PNAS* 116:4828–4833)** demonstrated that pyruvate + NH₃ + mixed-valence iron oxyhydroxides produces alanine, with maximum yield at **1:1 Fe(II):Fe(III) ratio** and alkaline pH. A landmark 2024 study in *PNAS* (doi:10.1073/pnas.2408248121) showed that **ferroan brucite** [Fe₀.₃₃Mg₀.₆₇(OH)₂] mediates glycine synthesis from nitrate + glyoxylate, with up to **97% of produced glycine partitioned to the mineral fraction** — demonstrating both synthesis and concentration in a single mineral system. This finding is particularly significant because it expands plausible prebiotic environments to include oxidizing atmospheres where nitrate is stable.

**Black smoker vents** (300–400°C, pH 2–3) face a fundamental problem: glycine's thermal stability. Aqueous glycine decomposes via decarboxylation (→ methylamine + CO₂) and deamination (→ glycolic acid + NH₃), with activation energy **Ea ≈ 95.9–156 kJ/mol** (Sato et al., 2004). At 200°C, ~90% of amino acids decompose; at 250°C, the half-life drops to minutes. The thermal optimum for glycine dimerization is **pH 9.8, 140°C**, with Ea = 88 kJ/mol (Sakata et al., 2010, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta* 74:6841–6851). These data strongly favor alkaline vents over black smokers for sustained amino acid chemistry.

Wächtershäuser's iron-sulfur world hypothesis proposed that the exergonic formation of pyrite (FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂, **ΔG = −9.2 to −10.0 kcal/mol**) could drive prebiotic carbon fixation. Huber and Wächtershäuser (1997, *Science* 276:245–247) demonstrated thioester synthesis from CO + CH₃SH on FeS/NiS at 100°C, and subsequently showed peptide bond formation from amino acids activated with CO on (Ni,Fe)S surfaces (1998, *Science* 281:670–672). However, the proposed full autocatalytic reductive citric acid cycle on FeS surfaces has not been experimentally demonstrated, and Schoonen et al. (1999) could not replicate citric acid synthesis from CO₂ on FeS catalysts.

## HCN polymers, cyanosulfidic chemistry, and Fischer-Tropsch catalysis provide additional convergent pathways

**HCN polymerization** represents a distinct route to glycine that bypasses the Strecker synthesis entirely. HCN oligomerizes through a base-catalyzed pathway: dimer (iminoacetonitrile) → trimer (aminomalononitrile, AMN) → tetramer (diaminomaleonitrile, DAMN) → higher polymers. The rate-determining dimerization step has k = **0.096 L mol⁻¹ day⁻¹** at pH 9.2 and 40°C (Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel, 1966, *Science* 153:72). A critical threshold exists: polymerization is fourth-order in HCN while hydrolysis is first-order, so polymerization dominates only above **0.01–0.1 M HCN** (Sanchez et al., 1967, *Journal of Molecular Biology* 30:223). Since oceanic HCN at 100°C and pH 8 reaches only ~7 × 10⁻¹³ M (Miyakawa, Cleaves & Miller, 2002, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 32:209), eutectic concentration by freezing to **−23.4°C** (producing **74.5 mol% HCN** eutectic) is likely required.

Acid or base hydrolysis of HCN polymers yields **glycine as the most abundant product under all 23 experimental conditions tested** (Ruiz-Bermejo et al., 2013, *Life* 3:421), alongside up to 24 amino acids total, including **12 of the 20 protein amino acids**. Oró's pioneering work in the early 1960s first demonstrated this, heating 0.1–2.2 M ammoniacal HCN at 70°C for 25 days. Matthews and Moser (1966, *PNAS* 56:1087) proposed that HCN polymerizes directly to polyaminomalononitrile and thence to heteropolypeptides without free amino acid intermediates — a controversial hypothesis challenged by Ferris (1979, *Science* 203:1135) but never fully resolved.

**Cyanosulfidic chemistry** represents the most elegant systems chemistry approach. Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy, and Sutherland (2015, *Nature Chemistry* 7:301–307) demonstrated that HCN + H₂S + UV light + phosphate buffer at 35°C produces precursors to amino acids, ribonucleotides, and lipids simultaneously through a unified network. The mechanism involves Cu(I)-Cu(II) photoredox cycling driving reductive homologation of HCN, with H₂S as stoichiometric reductant. Aminoacetonitrile (glycine precursor), 2-aminopropanenitrile (alanine precursor), and sugar precursors emerge as inevitable products of the same network. As Patel et al. noted, "biology did not select all of its building blocks, but was simply presented with a specific set as a consequence of the (photo)chemistry of hydrogen cyanide and hydrogen sulfide."

**Fischer-Tropsch type (FTT) synthesis** operates through CO + H₂ + NH₃ on metallic catalysts at 360–600°C. Yoshino, Hayatsu, and Anders (1971, *Geochimica et Cosmochimica Acta* 35:927–938) first demonstrated amino acid synthesis including glycine on meteoritic iron catalysts. The mechanism involves FTT generation of formaldehyde and HCN on catalyst surfaces, followed by Strecker synthesis in cooler zones. In a CO-rich early atmosphere, native iron and FeNi particles could produce up to **7 × 10⁹ to 5 × 10¹² kg/yr** of HCN and other organics.

## Glycine is ubiquitous in the solar system — from meteorites to comets to laboratory ice analogs

**The Murchison meteorite** (CM2 carbonaceous chondrite, fell September 28, 1969, Australia) contains **>90 amino acids** at concentrations of ~3 ppm for glycine (~150–250 nmol/g). Isotopic analysis provides definitive evidence of extraterrestrial origin: δ¹³C values reach **+40‰** for amino acids (vs. terrestrial values typically <−20‰), with δD up to **+2,500‰** and near-racemic D/L ratios confirming abiotic synthesis (Cronin & Pizzarello, 1983; Pizzarello et al., 2004; Glavin & Dworkin, 2009, *PNAS* 106:5487). CR2 chondrites show even higher total amino acid concentrations, up to **~715 nmol/g** in some samples. The Strecker synthesis on aqueous parent bodies is supported by the dominance of α-amino acids and parallel production of α-hydroxy acids via the cyanohydrin pathway.

Cometary glycine was confirmed by two independent missions. The NASA **Stardust** spacecraft returned samples from Comet 81P/Wild 2 in 2006; Elsila, Glavin, and Dworkin (2009, *Meteoritics & Planetary Science* 44:1323–1330) detected glycine with extraterrestrial δ¹³C signatures on collector surfaces. The ESA **Rosetta** mission directly measured glycine in the coma gas of Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko using the ROSINA mass spectrometer (Altwegg et al., 2016, *Science Advances* 2:e1600285), along with methylamine and ethylamine (glycine precursors) and the first cometary detection of phosphorus.

**Interstellar glycine has NOT been confirmed** despite intensive searches. Kuan et al. (2003) claimed detection toward Sgr B2(N), but Snyder et al. (2005, *Astrophysical Journal* 619:914) rigorously refuted this. However, **aminoacetonitrile** — the direct Strecker precursor to glycine — was detected in Sgr B2(N) by Belloche et al. (2008, *Astronomy & Astrophysics*) at column density 2.8 × 10¹⁶ cm⁻², and interstellar glycolonitrile was detected toward the solar-type protostar IRAS 16293-2422 by Zeng et al. (2019, *MNRAS* 484:L43–L48).

Laboratory UV irradiation of interstellar ice analogs consistently produces glycine as the most abundant amino acid. Bernstein, Dworkin, Sandford, and Allamandola (2002, *Nature* 416:401–403) at NASA Ames showed that UV photolysis of H₂O:CH₃OH:NH₃:HCN ices at ~15 K produces racemic glycine, alanine, and serine. Simultaneously, Muñoz Caro et al. (2002, *Nature* 416:403–406) obtained **16 amino acids** from UV irradiation of ices without HCN, demonstrating that radical-radical pathways operate independently of Strecker chemistry. Elsila et al. (2007, *Astrophysical Journal* 660:911–918) used isotopic labeling to confirm that multiple formation mechanisms — not purely Strecker — are operative in ices.

## Mineral catalysts concentrate reactants, drive reactions, and protect products across every pathway

The mineral catalyst landscape for prebiotic glycine synthesis spans four major categories, each with distinct mechanistic roles.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** form the catalytic core of hydrothermal vent chemistry. Greigite (Fe₃S₄), structurally analogous to biological [4Fe-4S] clusters in ferredoxins, catalyzes CO₂ fixation and is thermodynamically stable as nanoparticles (Navrotsky et al., 2020, *PNAS*). Pyrite (FeS₂) provides thermodynamic driving force through its exergonic formation, while mackinawite (FeS) serves as the first-precipitated iron sulfide in hydrothermal systems and precursor to both greigite and pyrite. Yamaguchi et al. (2014, *Electrochimica Acta* 141:311–318) showed that Ni-substituted greigite (forming violarite, FeNi₂S₄) substantially improves electrochemical CO₂ reduction efficiency.

**Clay minerals**, first proposed by Bernal (1951) as prebiotic concentrators, play crucial roles in both concentration and catalysis. Montmorillonite's expanding interlayer (14–18 Å d-spacing) adsorbs amino acids from dilute solution and catalyzes their polymerization. Paecht-Horowitz (1976, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres* 7:369–381) demonstrated peptide formation up to **40-mers** on montmorillonite (vs. 4–6 mers without clay). Fraser et al. (2017, *Nature Communications*) showed layered double hydroxides concentrate, align, and template amino acids during wet-dry cycles.

**Photocatalytic minerals** drive UV-dependent pathways. Rutile TiO₂ (band gap 3.0 eV) ranks as the most effective mineral for glycine polymerization (Kitadai et al., 2017, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*). Senanayake and Idriss (2006, *PNAS* 103:1194–1198) demonstrated that TiO₂(001) converts formamide to nucleobases and amino acids under UV, with activation energy <10 kJ/mol. ZnS (band gap ~3.6 eV) catalyzes **reversible amination of α-keto acids** — glyoxylate ↔ glycine — under UV illumination (Wang et al., 2012, *Chemical Communications* 48:2146–2148), mimicking amino acid dehydrogenase enzymes.

**Transition metal surfaces** from serpentinization and meteoritic sources provide heterogeneous catalysis. Awaruite (Ni₃Fe), which forms in serpentinized ultramafic rocks, catalyzes CO₂ → formate/acetate/pyruvate at 100°C (Preiner et al., 2020) and even at 25°C/25 bar (Beyazay et al., 2023). A critical general role of mineral surfaces is **product sequestration**: the 2024 PNAS ferroan brucite study found 97% of synthesized glycine bound to the mineral, detectable only after acid digestion — meaning previous experiments may have dramatically underestimated prebiotic amino acid yields by analyzing only solution phases.

## Environmental conditions determine which pathways dominate at each synthesis step

The optimal environment varies by reaction step, creating a natural geochemical flow network:

| Synthesis step | Optimal environment | Key conditions |
|---|---|---|
| CO₂ → C₁ compounds (formate, CO, HCHO) | Alkaline hydrothermal vents | 100°C, pH 9–11, greigite/magnetite/awaruite catalysts |
| HCN synthesis | Atmosphere (spark/UV) | Reducing or CO-containing atmosphere + energy input |
| NH₃ production | Atmosphere or mineral surfaces | Lightning/UV in reducing atmosphere; TiO₂ photoreduction of N₂; ferroan brucite reduction of nitrate |
| Strecker synthesis (HCHO + HCN + NH₃ → aminoacetonitrile → glycine) | Ice eutectic or warm ponds | −23°C eutectic for HCN concentration; or mineral-catalyzed warm ponds |
| Reductive amination (glyoxylate + NH₃ → glycine) | Serpentinite systems | Ferroan brucite, pH 8–9, low-moderate T |
| Glycine oligomerization | Wet-dry cycling on clays | pH 9.8, 140°C optimum for dimerization; montmorillonite at 80–150°C |
| Product protection | Clay interlayers or ice matrices | Interlayer confinement; low temperature |

The **ice/eutectic concentration mechanism** deserves special emphasis. Freezing dilute HCN to −23.4°C produces a eutectic with 74.5 mol% HCN (Sanchez, Ferris & Orgel, 1966), enabling polymerization from solutions far too dilute for warm-ocean chemistry. Levy et al. (2000) detected adenine, guanine, and glycine from NH₄CN solutions frozen for 25 years at −20°C and −78°C. The 210–273 K range maintains liquid eutectic brines in ice matrices while protecting products from thermal decomposition (Menor-Salván & Marín-Yaseli, 2012, *Chemical Society Reviews* 41:5404–5415).

## Critical open questions, bottlenecks, and active debates shape the field's frontiers

**The concentration problem** remains the most fundamental challenge. Oceanic HCN concentrations of ~2 × 10⁻⁶ M at 0°C (Miyakawa et al., 2002) are orders of magnitude below the 0.01–0.1 M threshold for productive chemistry. Five concentration mechanisms have experimental support: eutectic freezing (concentrating HCN by >10⁶-fold), mineral surface adsorption (up to 97% sequestration), wet-dry cycling in evaporitic pools, hydrothermal vent micro-compartments, and — most recently — **thermophoretic accumulation** through crack-like geo-compartments, which Matreux et al. (2024, *Nature* 628:110–116) showed can boost concentration ratios by up to **1,000×** for >50 prebiotically relevant compounds.

**The atmosphere composition debate** remains partially unresolved. Trail, Watson, and Tailby (2011, *Nature* 480:79–82) used cerium oxidation states in Hadean zircons (≥4,350 Myr old) to demonstrate that volcanic outgassing was consistent with CO₂/N₂/H₂O rather than CH₄/NH₃/H₂. Yet Hashimoto et al. (2007) and Zahnle, Schaefer & Fegley (2010, *Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology* 2:a004895) argued that transient strongly reducing episodes after large impacts could sustain productive chemistry. The current synthesis position holds that **Earth's background atmosphere was weakly reducing (CO₂/N₂) but experienced transient reducing episodes** that provided windows for Miller-Urey-type chemistry.

**The water problem** — thermodynamic unfavorability of condensation reactions in aqueous solution — primarily constrains glycine polymerization rather than its synthesis. Peptide bond formation costs **ΔG ≈ +3.5 kcal/mol** in water. Solutions include wet-dry cycling (Lahav et al., 1978, *Science* 201:67–69), air-water interfaces reducing water activity (Griffith & Vaida, 2012, *PNAS* 109:15697), mineral surface drying (Lambert, 2013), aqueous microdroplet chemistry (Cooks et al., 2024), and high-pressure hydrothermal conditions — Imai et al. (1999, *Science* 283:831) synthesized oligoglycines up to **decaglycine** at 270°C, 10 MPa, 27-second residence time.

**Competing reactions** siphon precursors away from glycine: formaldehyde undergoes the formose reaction producing sugars and tar; HCN hydrolysis destroys cyanide before polymerization at low concentrations; Maillard reactions between amino acids and reducing sugars generate melanoidin polymers. The "messy chemistry" problem — prebiotic reactions producing complex mixtures rather than specific targets — is increasingly addressed through geological filtering, photochemical selection (UV degrading undesired products), and thermophoretic purification.

## Toward computational pathway design: what the network structure reveals

The prebiotic glycine synthesis network is not a collection of independent pathways but an interconnected web with identifiable hubs. **HCN** is the master node, feeding amino acids (Strecker), purines (Oró's adenine pentamer), pyrimidines (via oligomer hydrolysis), and lipid precursors (cyanosulfidic reductive homologation). **Formaldehyde** bridges sugar chemistry (formose reaction) and amino acid chemistry (Strecker) through glycolonitrile, which also accelerates HCN oligomerization ~5-fold (Schwartz & Goverde, 1982). **Aminoacetonitrile** serves as both the direct Strecker precursor to glycine and a reservoir molecule with UV half-life ~1,300 years in diffuse interstellar conditions, more stable than glycine itself.

For computational pathway generation systems like AstroAgents/GenesisAgents, the key quantitative parameters are: Strecker pathway rate-limiting barrier of **31 kcal/mol** (amide hydrolysis); HCN polymerization threshold of **0.01 M**; glycine thermal half-life of minutes at 250°C but years at 100°C; optimal dimerization at pH 9.8/140°C with Ea = 88 kJ/mol; and the recent ML-discovered "oxyglycolate pathway" (Huet et al., 2024, *Journal of Physical Chemistry Letters* 15:8697–8705) with a lowest rate-determining barrier of **51.2 kcal/mol** for direct glycine formation from CO/H₂/H₂O/NH₃. The emerging computational frontier — ML-based interatomic potentials enabling near-quantum-accuracy exploration at reduced cost (Anton et al., 2025, *ChemSystemsChem*; Benayad et al., 2024, *PNAS* 121:e2322040121) — promises to map unexplored regions of the prebiotic reaction network, but faces the methodological challenge that training these models requires some prior knowledge of the pathways they seek to discover.

## Conclusion

The evidence overwhelmingly establishes glycine as the most thermodynamically and kinetically accessible amino acid under prebiotic conditions — a conclusion reinforced by its dominance across every experimental system and extraterrestrial detection. The field has matured from isolated pathway studies to systems chemistry, recognizing that glycine synthesis cannot be divorced from the broader network producing nucleotides, lipids, and cofactors from shared HCN/HCHO/NH₃ feedstocks. The most productive frontier lies at the intersection of three developments: ferroan brucite/serpentinization chemistry expanding viable environments to include oxidizing atmospheres, cyanosulfidic network chemistry demonstrating unified origin of all three biopolymer classes, and thermophoretic geo-compartment concentration solving the dilution problem quantitatively. For computational pathway design, the network's hub-and-spoke architecture around HCN, formaldehyde, and aminoacetonitrile, combined with the well-characterized barrier heights and environmental constraints cataloged here, provides a structured foundation for algorithmic exploration of prebiotic synthesis space.
