# Prebiotic synthesis of acetyl-CoA: from minerals to metabolism

**Acetyl-CoA — or more precisely its simpler thioester analogs like methyl thioacetate — can be synthesized under plausible prebiotic conditions through at least five demonstrated routes**, all converging on transition-metal-sulfide-catalyzed carbonylation chemistry that mirrors the most ancient carbon fixation pathway in biology. The central reaction, CO + CH₃SH → CH₃COSCH₃ on nickel-iron sulfide surfaces, was first demonstrated by Huber and Wächtershäuser in 1997 at just 0.2–0.5% yield, but has since been pushed to **35–56% yield** at room temperature and neutral pH through geoelectrochemical approaches (Kitadai et al., 2021, *Communications Chemistry*). Meanwhile, the 2024 breakthrough prebiotic synthesis of pantetheine — CoA's functional subunit — by Fairchild, Islam, Singh, and Powner (*Science*, 2024) has dramatically narrowed the gap between simple thioesters and the full cofactor. These advances place acetyl thioester chemistry at the nexus of the origin-of-life problem, connecting geochemistry to the emergence of core metabolism.

## Five convergent routes to prebiotic acetyl thioesters

Multiple independent research programs have proposed or demonstrated pathways to acetyl thioesters from simple molecules (CO₂, CO, H₂, H₂S, CH₃SH). Each route emerges from a distinct geochemical scenario, yet all converge on remarkably similar chemistry — the metal-catalyzed union of a one-carbon carbonyl unit with a thiol.

**Route 1: Iron-sulfur world carbonylation (Wächtershäuser).** Günter Wächtershäuser proposed in 1988 (*Microbiological Reviews*) that life originated on iron sulfide mineral surfaces through autotrophic surface metabolism. The thermodynamic engine is the "pyrite-pulled" reaction: FeS + H₂S → FeS₂ + H₂ (ΔG° = **−38 to −42 kJ/mol**), which provides reducing equivalents for CO₂ fixation. In this framework, CO₂ is reduced to CO on mineral surfaces, then CO undergoes carbonylation with methanethiol to form methyl thioacetate — a prebiotic analog of acetyl-CoA. The mechanism involves nickel-mediated organometallic intermediates (Ni-CO, Ni-CH₃, Ni-acetyl) that parallel both the biological acetyl-CoA synthase reaction and the industrial Monsanto acetic acid process.

**Route 2: Alkaline hydrothermal vent synthesis (Russell and Martin).** Michael Russell proposed in 1994 (*Journal of Molecular Evolution*) that life emerged at submarine alkaline hydrothermal vents, where serpentinization-derived H₂-rich, alkaline fluids (pH 9–11) encountered the CO₂-rich, mildly acidic Hadean ocean (pH ~5–6). Iron-nickel sulfide precipitates lining vent micropores catalyze a geochemical analog of the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway: CO₂ → formate → CO on one branch, CO₂ → CH₃-group on another, with convergence at NiFe-sulfide surfaces to form acetyl thioesters. Natural **pH gradients of ~3–5 units** across inorganic membranes provide an electrochemical driving force of ~250–300 mV, sufficient to power CO₂ reduction. Martin and Russell (2007, *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B*) detailed how acetyl phosphate (ΔG'⁰ of hydrolysis = **−43 kJ/mol**, exceeding ATP's −31 kJ/mol) would serve as the primordial energy currency.

**Route 3: Native metal CO₂ reduction (Moran, Varma, Muchowska).** Varma, Muchowska, Chatelain, and Moran (2018, *Nature Ecology & Evolution*) showed that native iron (Fe⁰) directly reduces CO₂ to acetate (**up to 280 µM**), pyruvate, and formate at 30–100°C under 1–40 bar CO₂. This bypasses the kinetic barriers that stymied earlier attempts at H₂-driven CO₂ reduction. The midpoint potential of Fe⁰ oxidation is sufficient to reduce CO₂ without the flavin-based electron bifurcation that biological systems require. Preiner et al. (2020, *Nature Ecology & Evolution*) extended this using hydrothermal minerals — **greigite (Fe₃S₄), magnetite (Fe₃O₄), and awaruite (Ni₃Fe)** — to produce formate (up to **200 mM**), acetate (~100 µM), and pyruvate (~10 µM) from H₂ + CO₂ at 100°C. Beyazay et al. (2023, *Nature Communications*) achieved the same product spectrum at just **25°C** under 25 bar with Ni₃Fe nanoparticles, and demonstrated further conversion of pyruvate to citramalate.

**Route 4: Cyanosulfidic protometabolism (Sutherland).** John Sutherland's group at MRC Cambridge developed a systems chemistry approach where HCN and H₂S, driven by UV photoredox cycling with Cu(I)/Cu(II) catalysis, generate precursors to nucleotides, amino acids, and lipids from common feedstocks (Patel et al., 2015, *Nature Chemistry*). While this framework does not directly produce acetyl thioesters, it generates **pyruvate in ~14% yield** through UV-driven oxidation of α-hydroxy acids (Ritson, 2021, *Science Advances*) — a key branch-point intermediate for thioester formation via oxidative decarboxylation. Critically, Leqraa et al. (2020, *Scientific Reports*) demonstrated that pyruvate reacts with disulfides under UV irradiation to yield thioacetate esters without requiring an external oxidant, bridging cyanosulfidic chemistry to thioester metabolism.

**Route 5: High-pressure organometallic synthesis (Cody).** Cody et al. (2000, *Science*) demonstrated that at 250°C and 50–200 MPa — conditions simulating deep hydrothermal systems — CO reacts with iron sulfide and alkyl thiols to form organometallic iron-sulfur-carbonyl phases that produce pyruvic acid. This pressure-enhanced pathway operates through organometallic intermediates structurally analogous to CODH/ACS active sites.

## Key intermediates and branch points

The path from CO₂ to acetyl thioesters passes through a small number of critical intermediates, several of which serve as branch points connecting multiple biosynthetic pathways.

**Carbon monoxide** is the pivotal C₁ intermediate in virtually all routes. It is generated from CO₂ by electroreduction on iron sulfide minerals, by the reverse water-gas shift reaction (CO₂ + H₂ → CO + H₂O) on Fe/Ni surfaces, or by UV photolysis. CO binds to nickel centers on mineral surfaces — the same chemistry exploited by the CODH enzyme's C-cluster — and undergoes migratory insertion with methyl groups to form the C–C bond of the acetyl unit. **Formate (HCOO⁻)** is the first stable reduction product of CO₂, produced in extraordinary abundance — up to **200 mM** on awaruite (Preiner et al., 2020). Formate is the entry point of the methyl branch of the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway and can be further reduced to formaldehyde, methanol, and methyl groups.

**Methanethiol (CH₃SH)** is the essential thiol substrate. It is present in modern hydrothermal fluids at micromolar to low-millimolar concentrations and can be generated abiotically from CO₂ reduction in sulfide-rich environments. Methanethiol provides both the thiol leaving group and, in some formulations, the methyl group for the carbonylation reaction. **Pyruvate** occupies a unique position as the most connected branch-point metabolite — it is the precursor to all amino acids (via transamination), to acetyl-CoA (via oxidative decarboxylation), and to oxaloacetate (via carboxylation). Goldford et al. (2017, *Cell*) computationally identified pyruvate and glyoxylate as the most well-connected C-H-O compounds in a hypothetical phosphate-free proto-metabolism. Muchowska, Varma, and Moran (2019, *Nature*) demonstrated that Fe²⁺ promotes a reaction network starting from pyruvate and glyoxylate that builds **9 of 11 Krebs cycle intermediates**.

**S-Methyl thioacetate (CH₃COSCH₃)** is the simplest activated thioester analog of acetyl-CoA. Its hydrolysis releases **−31 kJ/mol** of free energy — comparable to ATP hydrolysis — making it a plausible primordial energy currency as proposed by de Duve's "thioester world" hypothesis (1991). **Acetyl phosphate** bridges thioester and phosphate metabolism: formed non-enzymatically from acetyl thioesters and inorganic phosphate, it has an even higher phosphorylating potential (ΔG'⁰ = −43 kJ/mol) than ATP.

## Mineral catalysts mirror enzyme active sites with striking fidelity

The most compelling evidence connecting prebiotic geochemistry to biochemistry is the structural and functional correspondence between hydrothermal mineral catalysts and the metalloenzyme active sites that catalyze acetyl-CoA synthesis in modern organisms.

**Greigite (Fe₃S₄)**, the sulfide analog of magnetite, contains cubic **[Fe₄S₄] structural units** that are geometrically identical to the [4Fe-4S] thiocubane clusters found in ferredoxins and in the CODH/ACS enzyme complex. Fe–S bond distances in greigite match those in ferredoxin active sites within **0.01–0.13 Å** (Roldan et al., 2015, *Chemical Communications*). Greigite was confirmed to be thermodynamically stable — not metastable as previously assumed — with low surface energy supporting nanoparticle catalytic activity (Navarro-González et al., 2020, *PNAS*). Electrochemically, greigite reduces CO₂ under ambient conditions to formic acid, methanol, acetic acid, and pyruvic acid (Roldan et al., 2015). **Nickel-bearing greigite** structurally resembles the C-cluster ([NiFe₃S₄]) of carbon monoxide dehydrogenase (CODH), which catalyzes the reversible reduction CO₂ ⇌ CO with virtually no overpotential. Doping FeS with Ni to form violarite (FeNi₂S₄) enhances CO₂ electroreduction selectivity **~85-fold** compared to pure FeS.

**Mackinawite (FeS)**, the initial precipitate from Fe²⁺ + S²⁻ in hydrothermal systems, has a layered tetragonal structure comprising [2Fe-2S] rhombic units — the same motif as [2Fe-2S] ferredoxin clusters. It forms spontaneous inorganic membranes at alkaline hydrothermal vents. When partially electroreduced to metallic Fe⁰ (designated FeS_PERM), mackinawite shows exceptional catalytic capability for multiple reactions in the reverse tricarboxylic acid cycle (Kitadai et al., 2019). Nan et al. (2024, *Nature Communications*) showed mackinawite catalyzes H₂-driven CO₂ reduction to methanol at 80–120°C, with **Mn-doped FeS increasing methanol production five-fold**.

**Awaruite (Ni₃Fe)**, a native nickel-iron alloy formed during serpentinization, proved the most efficient catalyst for multi-carbon product formation from H₂ + CO₂ in Preiner et al.'s 2020 experiments. Its catalytic role parallels the Ni⁰ site in the ACS A-cluster, where nickel cycles between oxidation states to mediate methyl migration and CO insertion. **Green rust/fougerite**, a mixed-valence Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺ layered double hydroxide, has been proposed by Russell (2023, *Frontiers in Microbiology*) as a membrane-spanning catalytic engine at alkaline vents. Its ~150 hydrous interlayers can concentrate anions up to **10⁶-fold**, potentially solving the concentration problem, while its redox versatility (stable across Fe²⁺/Fe_total ratios of 1/3 to 2/3) enables electron bifurcation analogous to biological systems.

The biological acetyl-CoA synthase (ACS) A-cluster contains a unique [Ni_p–Ni_d–Fe₄S₄] center where the proximal nickel (Ni_p) cycles between Ni⁰/Ni¹⁺/Ni²⁺ states during catalysis — binding CO, accepting a methyl group, forming an acetyl intermediate, and transferring it to CoA-SH via reductive thiolysis. Manesis et al. (2022, *PNAS*) created an artificial nickel metalloenzyme within the azurin protein scaffold that reproduces this chemistry at **up to 70% yield**, demonstrating that the catalytic principle requires only a single nickel center in a minimal protein environment. Wilson et al. (2024, *JACS*) synthesized heterometallic [NiFe₄S₄] clusters by inserting Ni⁰ into oxidized [Fe₄S₄]³⁺, establishing the first experimental precedent for the Ni¹⁺ oxidation state in iron-sulfur cluster chemistry — a critical mechanistic link between mineral and enzymatic catalysis. Jordan et al. (2021, *Nature Communications*) demonstrated that [4Fe-4S] and [2Fe-2S] clusters **self-assemble spontaneously** from Fe²⁺/Fe³⁺, S²⁻, and micromolar cysteine at alkaline pH, with bicarbonate stabilizing the clusters. These clusters exhibit midpoint redox potentials of approximately **−500 mV vs SHE**, appropriate for CO₂ reduction.

## Landmark experiments and quantitative benchmarks

The experimental evidence for prebiotic thioester synthesis has progressed dramatically from early proof-of-concept demonstrations to increasingly realistic and efficient systems.

**Huber and Wächtershäuser (1997, *Science* 276: 245–247)** provided the foundational demonstration: coprecipitated NiS/FeS in sealed anaerobic vials at 100°C under 1.05 bar CO, with 100 µmol CH₃SH in 10 mL aqueous solution at initial pH ~8, produced **41 µmol acetic acid** and detectable methyl thioacetate after 7 days. With NiS alone (no FeS), 7–9 µmol of methyl thioacetate formed in 20 hours. Adding catalytic selenium enabled one-pot synthesis from CO and H₂S alone, without externally added methanethiol. However, the CO concentration (~350 mM in solution) was **~500× higher** and CH₃SH (~3,700×) higher than concentrations measured in natural hydrothermal fluids (Camprubí et al., 2017, *IUBMB Life*), limiting geochemical plausibility.

**Kitadai et al. (2021, *Communications Chemistry* 4: 37)** achieved a transformative advance: under simulated geoelectrochemical conditions (−0.6 to −1.0 V vs SHE), NiS gradually reduced to Ni₃S₂ and then Ni⁰, accumulating surface-bound CO from CO₂ electroreduction. This partially reduced NiS (NiS_PERM) catalyzed methyl thioacetate formation at **room temperature, neutral pH, with up to 35% yield** based on CO. Coprecipitation with CoS pushed yields to **56%**. This represents a **~175-fold improvement** over Huber and Wächtershäuser's original yield, under far milder conditions. The geoelectrochemical driving force — redox gradients across hydrothermal chimney walls — is geologically realistic.

**Preiner et al. (2020, *Nature Ecology & Evolution* 4: 534–542)** demonstrated that three naturally occurring hydrothermal minerals catalyze a geochemical analog of the complete Wood-Ljungdahl pathway: greigite, magnetite, and awaruite convert H₂ + CO₂ at 100°C in alkaline solution to formate (**up to 200 mM**), acetate (~100 µM), pyruvate (~10 µM), methanol (~100 µM), and methane. The product spectrum precisely mirrors the biological pathway's intermediates and products.

**Cody et al. (2000, *Science* 289: 1337–1340)** demonstrated pyruvate synthesis at 250°C and 50–200 MPa from CO + FeS + alkyl thiols, showing that high-pressure hydrothermal conditions favor organometallic iron-sulfur-carbonyl intermediates. **Muchowska, Varma, and Moran (2019, *Nature* 569: 104–107)** showed Fe²⁺ promotes construction of 9 of 11 Krebs cycle intermediates from pyruvate and glyoxylate, with addition of hydroxylamine and Fe⁰ yielding four biological amino acids — demonstrating that prebiotic thioester-related chemistry connects seamlessly to amino acid biosynthesis.

Recent work has expanded both the reaction scope and environmental settings. **El Qami et al. (2025, *Scientific Reports*)** showed 1,4-diacids form cyclic anhydrides upon drying over silica, which react with thiols to produce succinyl and citryl thioester analogs — a self-activating mechanism requiring no ATP equivalent. **Frenkel-Pinter et al. (2022, *Nature Communications*)** demonstrated that simply drying mercaptoacids with amino acids produces thiodepsipeptides containing both peptide and thioester bonds across a wide range of pH and temperature. **Cho et al. (2025, *Nature Chemistry*)** showed that cysteine reacting spontaneously with short-chain thioesters produces protocell-like structures, while **Kent et al. (2025, *ACS Central Science*)** demonstrated that tRNAs acylated with thioesters support efficient protein biosynthesis by wild-type ribosomes — evidence that thioester intermediates may have preceded oxoester intermediates in early translation.

## Environments that favor each step of the pathway

Different geochemical settings optimize different steps of the pathway from CO₂ to acetyl thioesters, suggesting either a single environment with diverse microenvironments or a multi-setting scenario.

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** (Lost City-type: 40–120°C, pH 9–11, H₂ up to 14 mM, continuous for >100,000 years) are optimal for the initial CO₂ reduction steps. Serpentinization generates both the reductant (H₂) and the catalyst (awaruite, magnetite, greigite) simultaneously. The pH gradient across FeS/NiS membranes (~5 units on Hadean Earth) provides an electrochemical driving force of 250–300 mV — sufficient to power CO₂ reduction to CO and formate. Microporous inorganic compartments within chimney structures solve the concentration problem while providing proto-cellular confinement (Russell and Hall, 1997). However, thioester stability is severely limited under these conditions: **methyl thioacetate half-life at pH 10, 100°C is only ~43 seconds** (Mayer et al., 2024, *iScience*), compared to **155 days at pH 7, 23°C** (Brosnan et al., 2011, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*). This stability constraint strongly favors thioester accumulation in **cooler, more acidic microenvironments** — perhaps at the ocean-facing exterior of vent structures where mixing with acidic seawater moderates both temperature and pH.

**Surface evaporitic pools** and terrestrial hot springs favor condensation and polymerization steps. Wet-dry cycling drives anhydride and thioester bond formation during drying phases. UV irradiation provides photochemical energy for radical reactions, including pyruvate oxidative decarboxylation to acetyl thioesters (Leqraa et al., 2020). Nan et al. (2024, *Nature Communications*) demonstrated FeS-catalyzed CO₂ reduction to methanol at 80–120°C in conditions mimicking terrestrial hot springs, with UV-visible light enhancing activity. **Thermophoresis** in rock pore networks can selectively accumulate diverse prebiotic building blocks through temperature gradients (Matreux et al., 2024).

## Unresolved challenges and the path forward

Despite remarkable experimental progress, several fundamental problems remain.

**The CoA complexity gap** is perhaps the most significant. Acetyl-CoA is a large, information-rich molecule containing pantothenic acid, β-alanine, cysteamine, and 3'-phospho-ADP — each component presenting its own prebiotic synthesis challenge. However, three developments have substantially narrowed this gap. First, **Fairchild et al. (2024, *Science* 383: 911–918)** achieved the first prebiotically plausible synthesis of pantetheine — CoA's catalytically active subunit — through chemoselective nitrile chemistry in water at room temperature, dramatically outperforming Miller's earlier attempt (Keefe, Newton, and Miller, 1995, *Nature*). Second, Goldford et al. (2017, *Cell*) computationally demonstrated that substituting pantetheine for CoA maintains network viability in a phosphate-free proto-metabolism. Third, even simpler thiols — methanethiol, cysteamine, coenzyme M (HSCH₂CH₂SO₃⁻) — can serve the same chemical function, as demonstrated by near-quantitative aminoacyl-thiol yields using coenzyme M (Singh et al., 2025, *Nature*).

**The concentration problem** persists for dilute products in open aqueous environments. Proposed solutions include microporous vent confinement, thermophoretic accumulation in rock fissures, wet-dry cycling in surface pools, eutectic concentration in ice, and the remarkable **10⁶-fold concentration** achievable in green rust interlayers. Thiol-thioester exchange (k = 1.7 M⁻¹s⁻¹ at pH 7, 23°C) is several orders of magnitude faster than thioester hydrolysis, meaning that even dilute thioester pools can efficiently transfer their acetyl groups to new thiol acceptors before being lost to hydrolysis.

**Selectivity and the "chemical mess" problem** — the tendency of prebiotic reactions to produce complex mixtures of products rather than the specific molecules biology uses — remains a core challenge. Mineral surfaces provide some selectivity through preferential adsorption and oriented catalysis, but lack the precise substrate positioning of enzyme active sites. The emerging "systems chemistry" approach (Sutherland, Powner) addresses this by identifying reaction networks where selectivity emerges from the interplay of multiple concurrent reactions rather than from any single selective step.

**The mineral-to-enzyme transition** — how catalysis migrated from mineral surfaces to soluble iron-sulfur clusters to metalloproteins — is incompletely understood. Jordan et al.'s demonstration of spontaneous [4Fe-4S] cluster self-assembly from simple precursors and Wilson et al.'s synthesis of heterometallic [NiFe₄S₄] clusters provide critical missing pieces, but the full trajectory from greigite surfaces to CODH/ACS holoenzyme remains speculative. Notably, the redox bifunctionality of nickel (cycling between Ni⁰/Ni¹⁺/Ni²⁺/Ni³⁺) that is essential for ACS catalysis has not been observed in natural nickel-bearing minerals — only under imposed electrochemical conditions — raising questions about whether mineral catalysis alone could have supported the full carbonylation mechanism.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of acetyl thioesters has progressed from theoretical proposals to experimentally demonstrated chemistry achieving yields comparable to enzymatic reactions. The field has converged on a core insight: **the same nickel-iron-sulfide chemistry that catalyzes acetyl-CoA synthesis in the most ancient biological pathway also operates on mineral surfaces under geologically realistic conditions**. The structural identity between greigite [Fe₄S₄] units and ferredoxin clusters, and between Ni-doped iron sulfides and CODH/ACS active sites, is not merely analogical but reflects a direct evolutionary continuity from geochemistry to biochemistry. The most productive current direction combines the Strasbourg group's native-metal CO₂ reduction chemistry with the Kitadai group's geoelectrochemical thioester synthesis and the Powner group's prebiotic CoA-precursor synthesis — together these define a plausible, experimentally grounded pathway from volcanic gases to the central energy-coupling molecule of all life. The critical next milestone is demonstrating a complete, continuous sequence from CO₂ and H₂ to an acetyl thioester in a single geochemically realistic reactor — a goal that, given the pace of recent advances, appears increasingly within reach.
