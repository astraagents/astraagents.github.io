# Prebiotic synthesis of glutamine remains an unsolved origin-of-life puzzle

**Glutamine (C₅H₁₀N₂O₃) has never been detected in any prebiotic synthesis experiment or meteorite analysis**, making it one of the most challenging amino acids to account for in origin-of-life scenarios. While its parent molecule glutamic acid ranks among the top four prebiotically abundant amino acids — consistently produced in Miller-Urey experiments, found in carbonaceous chondrites, and accessible through multiple abiotic pathways — glutamine's side-chain amide bond introduces a thermodynamic and kinetic barrier that no demonstrated prebiotic chemistry has overcome. This asymmetry between glutamic acid availability and glutamine absence encapsulates a fundamental tension in prebiotic chemistry: the dicarboxylic amino acids form readily, but their amide derivatives require enzymatic activation that likely only emerged after the origin of life itself. The consensus view, supported by the amino acid chronology work of Higgs and Pudritz (*Astrobiology*, 2009), classifies glutamine as a "late" amino acid — one of the final additions to the genetic code, incorporated only after glutamine synthetase or a primitive precursor enzyme evolved.

## Multiple routes to glutamic acid converge, but none reach glutamine

The prebiotic synthesis landscape for glutamic acid is surprisingly rich, with at least five experimentally demonstrated pathways producing this C₅ dicarboxylic amino acid from simple starting materials. Each route has distinct strengths and limitations, yet all share a common endpoint: they produce glutamic acid, not glutamine.

**Reductive amination of α-ketoglutarate** represents the most efficient and biologically relevant pathway. Kaur, Moran, and colleagues demonstrated in 2024 (*Chem*, 10:1528–1540) that H₂ gas drives nonenzymatic reductive amination of α-ketoglutarate to glutamate using Ni/SiO₂-Al₂O₃ catalysts at just **22°C and pH 8**, achieving combined amino acid yields of **6–84%** depending on catalyst and conditions. Remarkably, ground Aletai iron meteorite also catalyzed this reaction, directly linking meteoritic minerals to amino acid synthesis. Nogal and colleagues (*Organic Chemistry Frontiers*, 2024) showed NADH-mediated reductive amination yielding **8% glutamate** at 72 hours. The foundational work by Hafenbradl, Keller, Wächtershäuser, and Stetter (*Tetrahedron Letters*, 1995) first demonstrated reductive amination of α-oxo acids using the FeS/H₂S system, while Huber and Wächtershäuser (*Tetrahedron Letters*, 2003) confirmed amino acid formation from keto-acid precursors using freshly precipitated FeS or Fe(OH)₂ with ammonia.

**Metal-catalyzed transamination** offers another high-yield route. Stubbs, Krishnamurthy, and Springsteen demonstrated a metal-free ancestral Krebs cycle analog in which α-ketoglutarate reacts with glycine to produce **glutamate in 44% yield** at 80°C, pH 5, with Al³⁺ catalysis (*Nature Chemistry*, 2020, 12:1016–1022). Degani and Halmann (*Nature*, 1967) had earlier shown non-enzymatic transamination at 85°C catalyzed by Al³⁺, Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺, and Fe³⁺ ions. Muchowska, Varma, and Moran (*Journal of the American Chemical Society*, 2021) systematically investigated metal-ion-catalyzed transamination under physiological conditions, confirming Cu²⁺, Ni²⁺, Co²⁺, and V⁵⁺ as effective catalysts. The critical prerequisite — prebiotic α-ketoglutarate — is now well-established: Stubbs and Springsteen showed it forms from glyoxylate and pyruvate via aldol and cross-Cannizzaro chemistry at pH 4.5 and 70°C without metal catalysts.

**Miller-Urey spark discharge experiments** consistently produce glutamic acid, though at low yields. Miller's 1955 analysis (*Journal of the American Chemical Society*, 77:2351–2361) reported **6 µmol glutamic acid from 710 mmol carbon input (0.051% yield)**, roughly 100-fold lower than glycine. The 2008 reanalysis of Miller's archived volcanic apparatus vials by Johnson, Cleaves, and Bada (*Science*, 322:404) detected glutamic acid among 22 amino acids using modern HPLC and LC-ToF-MS. Parker and colleagues (*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 2011, 108:5526–5531) found glutamic acid in Miller's 1958 H₂S-containing experiment alongside 23 amino acids total. The mechanism proceeds via Strecker synthesis in the aqueous phase: electric discharge generates HCN and C₄ aldehydes (including succinic semialdehyde) in the gas phase, which dissolve and react with ammonia to form aminonitriles that hydrolyze to amino acids.

**HCN oligomerization** provides yet another pathway. Matthews and Moser (*Science*, 1967) demonstrated that poly-α-cyanoglycine from HCN converts to heteropolypeptides hydrolyzable to glycine, alanine, valine, aspartic acid, and **glutamic acid**. Ruiz-Bermejo and colleagues (*Life*, 2013) confirmed in a comprehensive review that HCN polymers yield 12 of 20 protein amino acids upon hydrolysis. However, Cleaves and coworkers (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2010) cautioned that some amino acid identifications from HCN polymers require more rigorous confirmation with high-resolution LC-MS.

**Pyrite photocatalysis** represents an exciting recent development. A 2024 study in *Nature Communications* showed that natural pyrite catalyzes photocatalytic reductive amination of α-keto acids — including α-ketoglutarate to glutamic acid — under Xe lamp irradiation at **20°C**, with the product showing enantiomeric excess (D-form, 14.5–42.4% ee). The cyanosulfidic framework of Patel, Sutherland, and colleagues (*Nature Chemistry*, 2015, 7:301–307) primarily accesses C₂–C₃ amino acid precursors and has not directly demonstrated glutamic acid synthesis, though the emerging connection between Bucherer-Bergs chemistry and α-keto acid metabolism (Pulletikurti, Krishnamurthy *et al.*, *Nature Chemistry*, 2022, 14:1142–1150) may eventually bridge this gap.

## Critical intermediates reveal a bottleneck at C₅ carbon chain assembly

The key intermediates on the path to glutamine form a branching network whose central node is **α-ketoglutarate (2-oxoglutarate)**, the universal biological precursor to glutamic acid and the metabolic gateway for nitrogen assimilation.

α-Ketoglutarate occupies a unique position in prebiotic chemistry. Mayer and Moran (*Angewandte Chemie International Edition*, 2022, 61:e202212237) found it to be the **least intrinsically reactive** of the Krebs cycle keto acids toward reductive amination — a finding they argue explains why biology selected it as the thermodynamic anchor of the transamination landscape. Its prebiotic formation from glyoxylate and pyruvate through aldol chemistry, as demonstrated by Stubbs and Springsteen's group, mirrors the biological reductive TCA cycle and supports Morowitz and Smith's metabolism-first hypothesis (*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 2004, 101:13168–13173). Ter-Ovanessian, Krishnamurthy, and Springsteen (*Angewandte Chemie International Edition*, 2025, 64:e202507248) showed that serine and glycine in the presence of phosphate and Cu²⁺ generate glyoxylate and pyruvate, which then undergo aldol chemistry to form α-ketoglutarate, closing a plausible prebiotic cycle.

For the Strecker route, the required aldehyde precursor is **succinic semialdehyde** (4-oxobutanoic acid), a C₄ aldehyde with a terminal carboxyl group. This molecule is far less common in prebiotic inventories than formaldehyde or acetaldehyde, which limits the Strecker pathway's plausibility for glutamic acid compared to simpler amino acids. The reaction proceeds through a hemiaminal intermediate to an α-aminonitrile, whose hydrolysis yields glutamic acid. No direct experimental demonstration of glutamic acid Strecker synthesis from prebiotically sourced succinic semialdehyde has been reported.

**HCN-derived intermediates** — the dimer (iminoacetonitrile), trimer (aminomalononitrile), and tetramer (diaminomaleonitrile, DAMN) — serve as branch points for diverse prebiotic products including purines, pyrimidines, and amino acids. Higher HCN oligomers and the polymeric material often called "azulmic acid" hydrolyze under acidic or basic conditions to release amino acids including glutamic acid, though the precise molecular pathway from HCN polymer to C₅ amino acids remains poorly characterized. Ferris, Sanchez, and Orgel established the foundational HCN polymerization chemistry in a series of publications from 1966 to 1978 (*Journal of Molecular Biology*, *Journal of Molecular Evolution*).

## Environmental settings impose strict constraints on glutamine survival

The thermal fragility of glutamine creates a narrow window of viable prebiotic environments. **Glutamine is the least thermally stable of all 20 protein amino acids**, with solid-state decomposition beginning at just **185°C** (Weiss, Muth, Drumm, and Kirchner, *BMC Biophysics*, 2018). In aqueous solution at 37°C, free glutamine loses approximately **10% per day** to pyroglutamic acid formation and deamidation (Hamilton, *Biochemical Journal*, 1962). Even at 22–24°C, degradation proceeds at **0.23% per day** in water at pH 6.5 (Khan *et al.*, *Clinical Nutrition*, 2006). The activation energy for this degradation is **98.7 kJ/mol (23.6 kcal/mol)** at pH 6.41 (Arii *et al.*, *European Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences*, 1999).

**Alkaline hydrothermal vents** offer the most favorable setting for glutamic acid synthesis, though glutamine stability remains problematic even here. The Lost City hydrothermal field operates at **40–75°C and pH 9–9.8** (Kelley *et al.*, 2001), with fluids rich in H₂ (1–15 mmol/kg). The Russell-Martin model proposes that life arose in FeS/NiS-walled compartments where cool, CO₂-rich Hadean ocean water met hot, alkaline hydrothermal fluid. Kitadai (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2015) calculated that amino acid synthesis from CO₂, H₂, NH₃, and H₂S is energetically favorable at vent-ocean interfaces, with optimal synthesis temperatures around **50°C** in mixing zones. Serpentinization — the reaction of olivine with water producing serpentine, brucite, magnetite, and H₂ — generated an estimated **10¹¹ mol H₂/year** during the Precambrian (Sleep *et al.*, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 2004). Awaruite (Ni₃Fe) nanoparticles formed during serpentinization catalyze H₂-dependent CO₂ fixation to formate, acetate, and pyruvate at 100°C (Preiner *et al.*, 2020; Beyazay *et al.*, *Angewandte Chemie*, 2023).

**Black smoker vents (300–400°C, pH 2–5)** are net destroyers of amino acids. Bada, Miller, and Zhao (*Origins of Life and Evolution of the Biosphere*, 1995, 25:111–118) demonstrated that amino acids are irreversibly destroyed at 240°C, with glutamine expected to survive only seconds to minutes at such temperatures. At 200–250°C, aspartate and serine decompose within 20 minutes; glycine and alanine persist somewhat longer but still degrade within hours (Dalai *et al.*, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 2013). High pressures (1.0–5.5 GPa) can stabilize simpler amino acids (Ohara *et al.*, *Astrobiology*, 2007), but this protection is unlikely to extend to the labile amide group of glutamine.

**Wet-dry cycling in evaporitic pools** at 60–90°C drives condensation reactions and could concentrate reactants, but also accelerates glutamine decomposition. Campbell and colleagues (*Nature Communications*, 2019) showed that deliquescent minerals mediate glycine oligomerization during wet-dry cycles with yields 10-fold higher than controls. For glutamine, the drying phase could in principle drive amidation of glutamic acid with ammonia, but the same heating promotes competing pyroglutamic acid cyclization.

## Iron-sulfur minerals and nickel catalysts drive the most prebiotically relevant reactions

Mineral catalysts are essential for nearly every demonstrated prebiotic route to glutamic acid, and understanding their roles illuminates both opportunities and limitations for glutamine formation.

**Nickel-based catalysts** deliver the highest yields for reductive amination. The Kaur and Moran 2024 study used Ni/SiO₂-Al₂O₃ (10 mol%) to convert α-ketoglutarate to glutamate at room temperature with H₂ gas and ammonium ions. With Pd catalyst, selectivity exceeded **5:1 amino acid over hydroxy acid** with yields of **52–88%**. The native nickel alloy awaruite (Ni₃Fe), produced naturally during serpentinization, catalyzes the same H₂-dependent CO₂ fixation reactions that produce TCA cycle intermediates including pyruvate and potentially α-ketoglutarate. This connects the mineral inventory of serpentinizing systems directly to the amino acid synthesis network.

**Iron-sulfur minerals** underpin the Wächtershäuser iron-sulfur world hypothesis. Pyrite (FeS₂) serves as both an electron source (via oxidative formation from FeS + H₂S) and a catalytic surface. Freshly precipitated FeS drives reductive amination of α-keto acids with ammonia (Huber and Wächtershäuser, *Tetrahedron Letters*, 2003). Greigite (Fe₃S₄), whose crystal structure resembles biological [4Fe-4S] clusters, and mackinawite (FeS), whose layered structure mirrors [2Fe-2S] ferredoxin clusters, are proposed as ancestral catalysts for electron transfer reactions (Russell and Martin; Bonfio *et al.*, *Nature Chemistry*, 2017). Iron oxyhydroxides with mixed Fe(II)/Fe(III) valence states catalyze reductive amination of pyruvate to alanine at 70°C and alkaline pH (Barge *et al.*, *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 2019, 116:4828–4833), though extension to α-ketoglutarate has not yet been tested.

**Clay minerals** serve primarily as concentrators and polymerization catalysts rather than synthesis catalysts. Montmorillonite adsorbs amino acids from dilute solution, with glutamic acid binding through both backbone and side-chain carboxylate groups (Sverjensky group, NASA Astrobiology Institute). Fe²⁺-exchanged montmorillonite shows superior adsorption for biomolecules. Layered double hydroxides (LDH) concentrate, align, and template amino acids during wetting-drying cycles, with charge redistribution on the LDH surface activating carboxylate groups for peptide bond formation (Grégoire *et al.*, *Nature Communications*, 2017). Notably, montmorillonite also catalyzes **deamination** of glutamic acid (Naidja and Siffert, *Clay Minerals*, 1989; Mortland, *Journal of Molecular Catalysis*, 1984), meaning it could destroy glutamine as readily as it might help form it.

**No mineral catalyst has been shown to facilitate the specific amidation of glutamic acid's γ-carboxyl to produce glutamine.** This remains a critical gap in the experimental literature.

## Meteorite and experimental data consistently exclude glutamine

The experimental record paints a remarkably consistent picture. Across decades of prebiotic synthesis experiments spanning spark discharge, HCN polymerization, UV photolysis, proton irradiation, and hydrothermal simulation, **glutamine has never been identified among the products**. This absence is not merely an analytical artifact — though acid hydrolysis during sample workup would convert glutamine to glutamic acid, modern HPLC and LC-MS methods used in reanalyses (Johnson *et al.*, 2008; Parker *et al.*, 2011) would have detected glutamine had it been present in significant quantities before hydrolysis.

The **Murchison meteorite** (CM2 carbonaceous chondrite) contains glutamic acid at approximately **2–7 µg/g**, making it one of the more abundant protein amino acids in the meteorite alongside glycine (~5 µg/g). Cronin's group identified 52 amino acids in Murchison over the course of the 1970s–1990s. Engel and Nagy (*Nature*, 1982) measured a D/L glutamic acid ratio of 0.3, initially interpreted as an L-excess, though Glavin and colleagues (2020) later showed that L-glutamic acid excesses of 56–73% in the Aguas Zarcas CM2 meteorite are likely terrestrial contamination, with the D-glutamic acid component being indigenous. Glutamine has **not been detected** in any meteorite analysis.

In spark discharge experiments, glutamic acid yields follow an exponential decline with carbon number that is a universal feature of prebiotic amino acid synthesis: in Miller's 1955 data, glycine (630 µmol) exceeds glutamic acid (6 µmol) by roughly **100-fold**. Sagan and Khare (*Science*, 1971) produced glutamic acid via UV photolysis of CH₄/C₂H₆/NH₃/H₂O/H₂S, while Kobayashi's extensive proton irradiation work (*Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, 1990; *Life*, 2023) demonstrated amino acid formation from weakly reducing atmospheres, with glycine always dominant and C₅ acids in trace amounts.

Higgs and Pudritz's meta-analysis (*Astrobiology*, 2009, 9:483–490) established the consensus ranking of prebiotic amino acid abundance: **Gly > Ala > Asp > Glu > Val > Ser > Ile > Leu > Pro > Thr**. Glutamine falls among the "late" amino acids (grouped with Asn, Cys, Tyr, Met, Trp) that are universally absent from prebiotic contexts. This ranking correlates strongly with thermodynamic favorability of formation (ΔG_surf), confirming that the absence of glutamine reflects fundamental chemistry rather than experimental oversight.

## The amide bond problem and glutamine's quadruple instability

Converting glutamic acid to glutamine requires forming an amide bond between the γ-carboxyl group and ammonia — a reaction that is thermodynamically uphill by approximately **+14–16 kJ/mol** in aqueous solution, analogous to peptide bond formation. In biology, this barrier is overcome by ATP-dependent glutamine synthetase (GS; EC 6.3.1.2), which proceeds through a **γ-glutamyl phosphate intermediate**. GS is among the oldest functioning genes studied: de Carvalho Fernandes and colleagues (*Evolution*, 2022, 76:605–622) showed that gene duplications gave rise to three GS groups **before the Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA)**, underscoring the antiquity and essentiality of this reaction.

Several prebiotic activating agents could theoretically substitute for ATP, but none has been demonstrated to produce glutamine:

- **Carbonyl sulfide (COS)**: Leman, Orgel, and Ghadiri (*Science*, 2004, 306:283–286) achieved up to 80% peptide yields via N-carboxyanhydride intermediates. COS primarily activates α-amino groups for peptide bonds, not side-chain carboxyls, though γ-carboxyl activation is theoretically possible.
- **Cyanate (OCN⁻)**: Danger, Pascal, and colleagues (*Amino Acids*, 2012, 42:2331–2341) showed cyanate activates dicarboxylic acids through intramolecular anhydride formation. For glutamic acid, a cyclic glutaric anhydride intermediate could preferentially involve the γ-carboxyl, offering potential selectivity — the most promising pathway identified, but **undemonstrated experimentally**.
- **Cyanamide (H₂N-CN)** and **polyphosphates**: Classical condensing agents that can drive amide bond formation but with yields typically below 1–2% and poor selectivity between α- and γ-carboxyls.

Glutamine faces a **quadruple instability** under prebiotic conditions that makes accumulation nearly impossible without enzymatic protection:

1. **Deamidation** to glutamic acid + NH₃, proceeding through a glutarimide intermediate at neutral-to-alkaline pH (half-lives of **600–20,000 days** in peptide contexts at 37°C; Robinson and Robinson, *Journal of Peptide Research*, 2004)
2. **Cyclization** to pyroglutamic acid via intramolecular attack of the α-amino group on the γ-amide — the dominant degradation pathway for free glutamine
3. **Racemization** to D-glutamine (slower than deamidation, with activation energies of 28–30 kcal/mol)
4. **Decarboxylation** to 4-aminobutyramide + CO₂ (significant only above 200°C)

The selectivity problem compounds these difficulties: glutamic acid's γ-carboxyl (pKa 4.25) preferentially reacts with its own α-amino group to form pyroglutamic acid rather than with external ammonia to form glutamine. This intramolecular cyclization, well-documented by Yu and colleagues (*Journal of Pharmaceutical and Biomedical Analysis*, 2006), represents the single greatest chemical obstacle to prebiotic glutamine synthesis.

## Glutamine was likely a late evolutionary invention

The combined evidence from prebiotic chemistry, geochemistry, and comparative genomics converges on a clear conclusion: glutamine was not part of the primordial amino acid repertoire. Weber and Miller (*Journal of Molecular Evolution*, 1981) first argued that the instability of glutamine and asparagine and their absence from prebiotic experiments suggest these amino acids were late additions to biology. The co-evolution hypothesis proposes that glutamine was incorporated into the genetic code only after glutamine synthetase (or a primitive precursor) evolved, enabling enzymatic amidation of the readily available glutamic acid.

This evolutionary timeline is supported by several lines of evidence. First, the **GDH-GS-GOGAT system** (glutamate dehydrogenase, glutamine synthetase, glutamine-2-oxoglutarate aminotransferase) that manages nitrogen assimilation through the glutamate/glutamine pair is universal across all three domains of life and was present in LUCA in multiple forms. This suggests the glutamic acid → glutamine conversion was among the earliest enzymatic innovations. Second, asparagine faces identical challenges — its synthesis depends on glutamine as an amide nitrogen donor (asparagine synthetase: Asp + Gln + ATP → Asn + Glu + AMP + PPi), making it doubly dependent on glutamine availability and placing it even later in the evolutionary timeline. Third, Trifonov's temporal ordering of genetic code expansion (2004) and Higgs and Pudritz's thermodynamic analysis both independently place glutamine among the final amino acids encoded.

The most plausible prebiotic scenario envisions early proteins composed of the 10–12 "early" amino acids (including glutamic acid but not glutamine), with glutamine entering the code only after primitive glutamine synthetase activity emerged — possibly first as a simple ribozyme or mineral-catalyzed process, then as the sophisticated enzyme observed today. Whether any mineral surface or simple catalyst could have provided even rudimentary γ-carboxyl amidation activity remains an open and experimentally testable question.

## Conclusion

The prebiotic synthesis of glutamine stands as one of the clearest examples of the gap between simple amino acid chemistry and the full complexity of biology's molecular toolkit. Glutamic acid — glutamine's precursor — is accessible through at least five experimentally validated prebiotic routes, with reductive amination of α-ketoglutarate (yielding up to **84%** with Ni catalysis) and metal-catalyzed transamination (yielding **44%** with Al³⁺) being the most efficient. The α-ketoglutarate precursor itself forms from glyoxylate and pyruvate via non-enzymatic analogs of the Krebs cycle. Yet the final step — selective amidation of glutamic acid's γ-carboxyl with ammonia — remains undemonstrated under any prebiotic condition. Cyanate-mediated intramolecular anhydride chemistry (Danger and Pascal, 2012) offers the most chemically plausible route to selective γ-activation, but experimental validation is needed. The overarching lesson is that **glutamine's absence from prebiotic inventories is not a failure of experimental detection but a genuine reflection of chemical thermodynamics**: forming and maintaining an amide bond on a dicarboxylic amino acid's side chain in water, against competing cyclization and hydrolysis, represents a challenge that only evolved enzymes have solved.
