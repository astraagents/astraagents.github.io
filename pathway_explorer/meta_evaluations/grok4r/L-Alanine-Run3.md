## Comparative Analysis

The primary differentiator across these networks was **Network Completeness**—specifically, the ability to build the target molecule from simple, permitted starting materials without introducing "magic" or "orphan" intermediates. 

**Configs F, A, and B** represent top-tier network designs. They successfully mapped out pathways starting from simple gases (CO₂, H₂, NH₃). **Config F** stood out as the absolute best because it eschewed "black-box" shortcut reactions (like spark discharges) and instead painstakingly built the carbon skeleton step-by-step (C1 → C2 → C3) using abiotic analogs of the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway. This reflects the most cutting-edge theories in autotrophic origin-of-life research.

Conversely, **Configs C, D, and E** fell victim to a common pitfall in prebiotic modeling: assuming the availability of complex precursors. **Config E** was the worst offender, treating advanced metabolites like oxaloacetate, lactic acid, and serine as basic inputs. While the specific reactions in Config E were pulled from excellent literature (e.g., Muchowska, Ritson), a synthesis network is only as strong as its foundation. Because it skipped the difficult step of actually making its precursors, it scored the lowest. **Configs C and D** suffered similarly by introducing pyridoxal and DAP/glyoxylate without synthetic justification, preventing them from competing with the rigorous, bottom-up architectures of the top three.
