## Comparative Analysis

The highest-performing networks (**Config F** and **Config D**) succeeded by adhering strictly to the "bottom-up" constraint—building complex molecules exclusively from simple C1/N1 geochemical feedstocks. 

**Config F** distinguishes itself as the ultimate winner by bridging the historical divide in origin-of-life chemistry. Instead of treating the "iron-sulfur metabolism-first" camp and the "UV-cyanosulfidic" camp as mutually exclusive, it uses hydrothermal vents to build carbon backbones (acetate, pyruvate) and surface UV pools to generate nitrogenous hubs (aminoacetonitrile) and sugars. It then elegantly unites them via a biochemical transamination loop. 

**Config D** and **Config A** are both highly rigorous, traditional prebiotic networks. Config D is the definitive guide to Strecker-family chemistry, while Config A acts as an encyclopedic literature review, bringing in very recent discoveries like the ferroan-brucite pathway.

A systematic pattern that separated the lower-ranked configs (**B, C, and E**) was a failure in *directionality and context*. **Config E** treated glycine as a degradation product of complex amino acids—conceptually fascinating, but failing the requirement to build from simple gases. **Config B** similarly relied on complex pre-formed polymers (CGP). **Config C** struggled with environmental context, mapping dry ice-grain astrophysical mechanisms into wet terrestrial pools. 

Ultimately, **Config F** wins by proving that a prebiotic network can be strictly built from simple geochemical gases while simultaneously possessing the architectural elegance of modern biochemistry.
