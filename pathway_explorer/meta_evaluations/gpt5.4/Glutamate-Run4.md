## Comparative Analysis

**The hallmarks of elite prebiotic network design are clearly visible in Config C and Config E.** 
Most origin-of-life networks suffer from an "optimism bias"—assuming that if a reaction *can* happen, it *will* happen with 100% specificity. **Config C** separates itself entirely by doing the exact opposite: it embraces the messiness of prebiotic chemistry. By explicitly modeling the formation of cyanohydrins as a thermodynamic trap/storage reservoir (rather than an immediate productive step) and acknowledging the severe hydroxyacid side-product competition during Ni/H₂ reductive amination (Kaur et al., 2024), Config C achieves a level of realism unmatched by the others. Its utilization of diamidophosphate (DAP) to solve the classic pH incompatibilities of the Strecker and Bucherer-Bergs reactions demonstrates state-of-the-art literature knowledge.

**Config E** also stands out for its structural precision. Instead of treating "pyruvate + glyoxylate → α-KG" as a single magic step, it traces the exact mechanistic reality observed in the Muchowska/Moran labs, breaking it down through 4-hydroxy-2-oxoglutarate and 4-oxopent-2-enedioate. 

**Configs A and B** suffer slightly due to their reliance on NADH. While recent papers have used NADH to demonstrate *proof-of-principle* reductive aminations of α-KG, relying on such a complex dinucleotide cofactor in a foundational network is anachronistic for early Earth chemistry.

**Config F** deserves honorable mention for its deep chemical intuition. It correctly identifies a frequent error in origin-of-life papers: applying the Strecker reaction directly to pyruvate does not yield alanine, it yields 2-methylaspartate. Config F avoids this trap beautifully, proving that a deep understanding of organic mechanism is just as vital as broad literature curation. However, its strict adherence to a bottom-up rTCA analog forces it to rely on non-enzymatic steps that are notoriously poor-yielding.
