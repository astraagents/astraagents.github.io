## Comparative Analysis

**The Top Tier (Config A and F):** 
What separates **Config A** from the rest of the pack is its flawless integration of modern, cutting-edge prebiotic literature (2022-2024 PNAS/Nature papers) with unimpeachable physical organic chemistry. It recognizes known kinetic bottlenecks (like the final amide hydrolysis in the Strecker synthesis) and introduces experimentally validated bypasses, such as the CO₂-driven Bucherer-Bergs cyclization. **Config F** achieves second place by utilizing a similarly flawless chemical logic, particularly shining in its systems-level solution to prebiotic ammonia scarcity (using HCN photolysis to formamide as a local NH₃ pump).

**The Middle Tier (Config D, E, C):**
**Config D** is highly accurate but plays it very safe, reading like a standard textbook on prebiotic chemistry. **Config E** is the exact opposite: wildly creative and conceptually fascinating (synthesizing glycine by degrading heavier meteoritic amino acids and TCA intermediates), but it makes fundamental errors in reaction mechanisms (e.g., Threonine retro-aldol products). **Config C** connects its pathways well but strays too far into theoretical/computational chemistry, proposing reactions that look good in a simulation but face massive kinetic barriers in an actual early Earth environment.

**The Bottom Tier (Config B):**
**Config B** falls to the bottom because it violates fundamental laws of organic chemistry. The network hinges on an "oxyglycolate" pathway where ammonia directly displaces a hydroxyl group via Sₙ2 attack in aqueous basic conditions. Because hydroxide is an incredibly poor leaving group, this reaction will not proceed without a prior activation step (like phosphorylation), rendering the core of the network chemically impossible.
