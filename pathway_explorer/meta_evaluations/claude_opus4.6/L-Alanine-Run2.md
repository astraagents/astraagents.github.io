## Comparative Analysis
The defining feature separating the top-tier configs (C, E, A) from the rest is their integration of **modern systems chemistry** (2019-2024 literature). While older models treat prebiotic chemistry as a series of isolated, lucky reactions, the top configs model cooperative networks. 

**Config C** wins by identifying the Achilles' heel of the Strecker synthesis (the slow hydrolysis of aminonitriles) and solving it using a realistic environmental protection mechanism (formamide N-formylation during wet-dry cycles). Furthermore, it bridges the gap between mineral catalysis and biological enzymes using metal-pyridoxal cooperativity. **Config E** is a masterclass in "bottom-up" rigor, ensuring no intermediate is magically invoked—tracing even tricky reagents like hydroxylamine back to volcanic gases. **Config A** deserves high praise for lateral thinking, using the Bucherer-Bergs cycle to show how alanine and nucleobases could emerge from the exact same precursor pool.

Conversely, the lower-ranked configs suffered from topological or temporal errors. **Config B** is chemically excellent but introduces a severe temporal paradox by invoking RNA-directed translation to solve the homochirality of a prebiotic amino acid pool. **Configs D and F** rely too heavily on non-selective reactions (formose chemistry and generalized FTT), which typically yield intractable tars ("asphalt problem") rather than specific amino acid accumulation. Overall, the evaluation demonstrates that the most plausible origin-of-life networks rely on selective, protected intermediates and cross-environmental synergy rather than single-pot "spark" syntheses.
