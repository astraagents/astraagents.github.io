# Prebiotic Synthesis of L-Threonine: Comprehensive Literature Review

## 1. Known Prebiotic Synthesis Routes

Two experimentally demonstrated pathways produce L-threonine (C4H9NO3, SMILES: CC(C(C(=O)O)N)O, InChI: InChI=1S/C4H9NO3/c1-2(6)3(5)4(7)8/h2-3,6H,5H2,1H3,(H,7,8), MW: 119.12) from prebiotically available precursors. These span surface and hydrothermal environments.

### Pathway 1: Cyanosulfidic Nitrile Hydrolysis via Lactaldehyde Cyanohydrin (Surface Environment)

The cyanosulfidic protometabolism pathway demonstrated by Patel, Percivalle, Ritson, Duffy, and Sutherland (2015, *Nature Chemistry*, DOI: 10.1038/nchem.2202) constructs L-threonine through a 3-step sequence starting from **lactaldehyde** and **hydrogen cyanide**, proceeding through a cyanohydrin intermediate and an aminonitrile intermediate. This pathway follows the general cyanosulfidic strategy: an aldehyde undergoes cyanohydrin formation with HCN, amination with ammonia displaces the hydroxyl to form an aminonitrile, and final nitrile hydrolysis yields the amino acid.

**Step 1**: Lactaldehyde + HCN → Cyanohydrin intermediate (Cyanohydrin formation)
- SMILES: CC(O)C=O.C#N>>CC(O)CC(O)C#N
- Reactants: Lactaldehyde (C3H6O2, SMILES: CC(C=O)O, InChI: InChI=1S/C3H6O2/c1-3(5)2-4/h2-3,5H,1H3, MW: 74.08) + Hydrogen cyanide (CHN, SMILES: C#N, InChI: InChI=1S/CHN/c1-2/h1H, MW: 27.03)
- Product: Cyanohydrin intermediate (C5H9NO2, SMILES: CC(O)CC(O)C#N, InChI: InChI=1S/C5H9NO2/c1-4(7)2-5(8)3-6/h4-5,7-8H,2H2,1H3, MW: 115.06)
- Environment: Surface (evaporitic pools, cyanosulfidic conditions)
- Conditions: Aqueous, ambient temperature and pressure
- Mechanism: Nucleophilic addition of cyanide (CN⁻) to the aldehyde carbonyl carbon of lactaldehyde. The cyanide ion attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of the aldehyde, and subsequent protonation of the resulting alkoxide yields the cyanohydrin. This is a classic Kiliani-type cyanohydrin reaction that extends the carbon chain by one carbon unit while introducing a nitrile functional group. Lactaldehyde is a plausible prebiotic C3 aldol product available from cyanosulfidic chemistry.

**Step 2**: Cyanohydrin intermediate + NH3 → 2-Amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile + H2O (Aminonitrile formation)
- SMILES: CC(O)CC(O)C#N.N>>CC(O)C(N)C#N.O
- Reactants: Cyanohydrin intermediate (C5H9NO2, SMILES: CC(O)CC(O)C#N) + Ammonia (H3N, SMILES: N, InChI: InChI=1S/H3N/h1H3, MW: 17.03)
- Products: 2-Amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile (C4H8N2O, SMILES: CC(C(C#N)N)O, InChI: InChI=1S/C4H8N2O/c1-3(7)4(6)2-5/h3-4,7H,6H2,1H3, MW: 100.12) + H2O (SMILES: O)
- Environment: Surface (evaporitic pools, cyanosulfidic conditions)
- Conditions: Aqueous, ambient temperature and pressure
- Mechanism: Strecker-type amination — ammonia displaces the hydroxyl group at the alpha-carbon adjacent to the nitrile, converting the cyanohydrin to an aminonitrile with release of water. The reaction involves nucleophilic attack of NH3 on the carbon bearing the hydroxyl group, with rearrangement of the carbon skeleton to yield the C4 aminonitrile with the beta-hydroxy group characteristic of threonine.

**Step 3**: 2-Amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile + 2 H2O → L-Threonine + NH3 (Nitrile hydrolysis)
- SMILES: CC(O)C(N)C#N.O.O>>CC(O)C(N)C(=O)O.N
- Reactants: 2-Amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile (C4H8N2O, SMILES: CC(C(C#N)N)O) + 2 H2O (SMILES: O)
- Products: L-Threonine (C4H9NO3, SMILES: CC(C(C(=O)O)N)O, InChI: InChI=1S/C4H9NO3/c1-2(6)3(5)4(7)8/h2-3,6H,5H2,1H3,(H,7,8), MW: 119.12) + Ammonia (H3N, SMILES: N, InChI: InChI=1S/H3N/h1H3)
- Environment: Surface (evaporitic pools, cyanosulfidic conditions)
- Conditions: Aqueous, ambient temperature and pressure
- Mechanism: Nucleophilic addition of water to the nitrile group of 2-amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile, forming an amide intermediate (2-amino-3-hydroxybutanamide), followed by further hydrolysis to the carboxylic acid (threonine) with release of ammonia (NH3). This is the final step of the general cyanosulfidic aminonitrile-to-amino acid pathway, converting the C≡N triple bond to a carboxylic acid via stepwise hydration.

This three-step sequence is part of the broader Powell-Sutherland cyanosulfidic chemistry framework, in which HCN-derived nitriles serve as amino acid precursors via hydrolysis under wet-dry cycling or UV-irradiated surface pool conditions. The same paper demonstrates that this unified chemistry produces precursors to RNA, proteins, and lipids from simple feedstocks. The threonine pathway parallels the serine pathway (glycolaldehyde + HCN) but uses lactaldehyde as the starting aldehyde, introducing the methyl branch that distinguishes threonine from serine.

### Pathway 2: Aldol Condensation of L-Alanine with Formaldehyde (Hydrothermal Environment)

Aubrey, Cleaves, and Bada (2008, *Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres*, DOI: 10.1007/s11084-008-9153-2) demonstrated that **L-alanine** (C3H7NO2, SMILES: CC(C(=O)O)N, InChI: InChI=1S/C3H7NO2/c1-2(4)3(5)6/h2H,4H2,1H3,(H,5,6), MW: 89.09) reacts with **formaldehyde** (CH2O, SMILES: C=O, InChI: InChI=1S/CH2O/c1-2/h1H2, MW: 30.03) to produce L-threonine under submarine hydrothermal conditions.

**Reaction**: L-Alanine + Formaldehyde → L-Threonine
- SMILES: C[C@H](N)C(=O)O.C=O>>C[C@@H](O)[C@H](N)C(=O)O
- Environment: Hydrothermal (submarine hydrothermal systems)
- Conditions: Elevated temperature and pressure characteristic of submarine hydrothermal vents
- Mechanism: Aldol-type condensation — the alpha-carbon of L-alanine attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of formaldehyde, forming a new C–C bond and introducing a hydroxymethyl group (-CH(OH)-). This converts the C3 amino acid (alanine) into the C4 amino acid (threonine) by extending the carbon skeleton by one carbon unit. Formaldehyde is a key C1 electrophile that is abundantly available in hydrothermal vent fluids from reduction of CO2 and Fischer-Tropsch-type chemistry.

This pathway links threonine synthesis to L-alanine, which is itself produced by multiple prebiotic routes including: (a) reductive amination of pyruvate with hydroxylamine in the presence of iron (Muchowska et al., 2019), (b) hydrolysis of 2-aminopropanenitrile under cyanosulfidic conditions (Patel et al., 2015), and (c) reduction of L-serine under hydrothermal conditions (Aubrey et al., 2008). The reaction is directly analogous to the glycine + formaldehyde → serine aldol condensation demonstrated by the same research group, but applied one carbon unit higher in the amino acid homologous series.

- InChI: InChI=1S/C3H7NO2/c1-2(4)3(5)6/h2H,4H2,1H3,(H,5,6)
- MW: 89.09
- Role: **Key amino acid precursor for Pathway 2** — L-alanine is the substrate for the aldol condensation with formaldehyde that produces threonine (Aubrey et al., 2008). Alanine is itself produced by multiple prebiotic routes: reductive amination of pyruvate with hydroxylamine using iron catalysts at 70°C (Muchowska et al., 2019), hydrolysis of 2-aminopropanenitrile under cyanosulfidic conditions (Patel et al., 2015), and reduction of L-serine under hydrothermal conditions (Aubrey et al., 2008). Alanine is one of the most abundant amino acids produced in prebiotic chemistry experiments.

### Reactions Producing L-Threonine (Final Steps)

**Reaction F1: Nitrile hydrolysis of 2-amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile**
- 2-Amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile + 2 H2O → L-Threonine + NH3
- SMILES: CC(O)C(N)C#N.O.O>>CC(O)C(N)C(=O)O.N

- Conditions: Aqueous, ambient temperature and pressure

- Mechanism: Stepwise nitrile hydrolysis — water attacks the nitrile carbon, forming an amide intermediate (2-amino-3-hydroxybutanamide), which further hydrolyzes to the carboxylic acid (threonine) with release of ammonia. - SMILES: C[C@H](N)C(=O)O.C=O>>C[C@@H](O)[C@H](N)C(=O)O

- Mechanism: Aldol condensation — the alpha-carbon of L-alanine attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of formaldehyde, forming a new C–C bond and introducing a beta-hydroxyl group. This extends the C3 amino acid (alanine) to the C4 amino acid (threonine). The reaction is analogous to the glycine + formaldehyde → serine aldol condensation from the same study.

## 4. Mineral Catalysts and Surfaces

The threonine synthesis reactions described in the literature do not require specific mineral catalysts. However, the following environmental agents and conditions are relevant:

- **Cyanosulfidic conditions** (Pathway 1): The Patel et al. (2015) reactions operate under the cyanosulfidic chemistry framework, which involves hydrogen cyanide (HCN), hydrogen sulfide (H2S), and UV irradiation in surface pools. While no specific mineral catalysts are documented for the threonine-specific reactions, the broader cyanosulfidic network utilizes phosphate minerals for phosphorylation and UV light as an energy source.

- **Hydrothermal mineral surfaces** (Pathway 2): The Aubrey et al. (2008) aldol condensation operates under submarine hydrothermal conditions. While no specific mineral catalyst is documented for the alanine-formaldehyde condensation itself, the hydrothermal environment provides elevated temperatures and mineral surfaces (e.g., iron-sulfide minerals, clay minerals) that may facilitate the reaction. Additionally, the upstream production of L-alanine via reductive amination of pyruvate requires **Elemental Iron** (Fe⁰) and **ferrous iron** (Fe²⁺) as catalysts (Muchowska et al., 2019).

- **Ultraviolet radiation**: UV photons (200-300 nm) from the young Sun drive the cyanosulfidic photochemistry that generates the feedstocks (HCN, lactaldehyde) for Pathway 1.

## 5. Experimental Evidence

### Study 1: Patel et al. (2015)
- **Title**: "Common origins of RNA, protein and lipid precursors in a cyanosulfidic protometabolism"
- **Journal**: Nature Chemistry
- **DOI**: 10.1038/nchem.2202
- **Key finding**: Demonstrated that a unified cyanosulfidic chemistry starting from HCN and H2S can produce precursors to RNA, proteins, and lipids. For threonine specifically, the paper describes a three-step pathway: (1) HCN cyanohydrin addition to lactaldehyde produces a cyanohydrin intermediate (C5H9NO2), (2) amination with ammonia converts the cyanohydrin to 2-amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile (C4H8N2O), and (3) nitrile hydrolysis yields L-threonine with release of ammonia. This is the cyanosulfidic analog of the Strecker synthesis, where the starting aldehyde (lactaldehyde) determines the amino acid product (threonine). The same framework produces glycine (from formaldehyde), alanine (from acetaldehyde), serine (from glycolaldehyde), and valine (from dihydroxyacetone-derived acetone), demonstrating a unified chemical origin for the amino acid repertoire.

### Study 2: Aubrey, Cleaves, and Bada (2008)
- **Title**: "The Role of Submarine Hydrothermal Systems in the Synthesis of Amino Acids"
- **Journal**: Origins of Life and Evolution of Biospheres
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s11084-008-9153-2
- **Key finding**: Investigated amino acid synthesis under simulated submarine hydrothermal vent conditions. Demonstrated that L-alanine reacts with formaldehyde to produce L-threonine via aldol condensation. This reaction extends the carbon skeleton of a simpler amino acid (alanine, C3) to a more complex one (threonine, C4) by incorporating a C1 unit (formaldehyde). The same study demonstrated the analogous glycine + formaldehyde → serine aldol condensation, as well as serine reduction to alanine. These reactions establish a hydrothermal amino acid interconversion network where simpler amino acids serve as building blocks for more complex ones through aldol chemistry with formaldehyde.

## 6. Environmental Context

### Surface Environment (Primary for Pathway 1)
The cyanosulfidic threonine synthesis (Patel et al., 2015) operates under surface conditions:
- **Cyanosulfidic nitrile chemistry**: Lactaldehyde reacts with HCN to form a cyanohydrin, which is aminated by ammonia and then hydrolyzed to threonine. This operates in surface pools where HCN-derived chemistry (UV-driven, evaporitic) produces aminonitrile intermediates that hydrolyze in wet phases.
- **UV irradiation**: The young Sun provided intense UV flux (lacking an ozone shield), driving the photochemistry that generates HCN and other cyanosulfidic feedstocks.
- **Wet-dry cycling**: Evaporitic pools undergo wet-dry cycles that concentrate reactants and drive condensation reactions, facilitating the cyanohydrin formation and amination steps.
- **Temperature**: 300-370 K (ambient to warm surface conditions), compatible with the aqueous chemistry described.

### Hydrothermal Environment (Primary for Pathway 2)
The aldol condensation pathway (Aubrey et al., 2008) operates under submarine hydrothermal conditions:
- **L-Alanine availability**: L-alanine is produced by iron-catalyzed reductive amination of pyruvate at 70°C (Muchowska et al., 2019), by reduction of L-serine (Aubrey et al., 2008), and by cyanosulfidic aminonitrile hydrolysis (Patel et al., 2015). Multiple upstream pathways feed alanine into the hydrothermal environment.
- **Formaldehyde availability**: Formaldehyde is produced by hydrothermal reduction of CO2 and by Fischer-Tropsch-type chemistry on native iron and nickel-iron mineral surfaces. It is a key C1 building block abundantly available in hydrothermal vent fluids.
- **Temperature**: Elevated temperatures (350-600 K) characteristic of submarine hydrothermal vents drive the aldol condensation.

### Environmental Interconnection
The threonine synthesis network exhibits cross-environment flow:
1. **Surface → Hydrothermal**: L-Alanine produced by cyanosulfidic chemistry (Pathway 1-type aminonitrile hydrolysis in surface pools) could be transported to hydrothermal environments, where it undergoes aldol condensation with formaldehyde (Pathway 2).
2. **Hydrothermal → Surface**: Alanine and formaldehyde generated at hydrothermal vents could be transported to surface environments via ocean circulation.
3. **Parallel independent routes**: The two pathways operate independently in different environments (surface cyanosulfidic vs. hydrothermal aldol), providing robustness to the prebiotic threonine synthesis network.
4. **Both → Biochemical**: Threonine produced by either pathway enters the prebiotic amino acid pool available for peptide bond formation, and serves as a precursor for further biochemical assembly. Threonine itself can thermally decompose to glycine under hydrothermal conditions (Vallentyne, 1964; Bada et al., 1978), linking it to the broader amino acid recycling network.

## 7. Open Questions and Challenges

1. **Chirality**: Both demonstrated prebiotic routes produce racemic threonine (equal L and D forms). The origin of homochirality (biological preference for L-amino acids) remains unresolved. Mineral surface adsorption, photolysis with circularly polarized light, or amplification through autocatalytic crystallization have been proposed but not demonstrated specifically for threonine.

2. **Lactaldehyde availability**: Pathway 1 requires lactaldehyde (2-hydroxypropionaldehyde), whose prebiotic abundance and stability are debated. It could form from aldol chemistry of simpler aldehydes (glycolaldehyde + formaldehyde), from sugar photochemistry under cyanosulfidic conditions, or from reduction of pyruvaldehyde. Quantitative constraints on its steady-state concentration in surface environments are needed.

3. **Threonine thermal stability**: Threonine is thermally labile and decomposes to glycine under hydrothermal conditions (Vallentyne, 1964; Bada et al., 1978). The balance between threonine production (via aldol condensation, Pathway 2) and thermal decomposition at hydrothermal temperatures is a key kinetic question. The retro-aldol decomposition of threonine to glycine (the reverse of the serine-type aldol reaction) may limit threonine accumulation in hydrothermal systems.

4. **2-Amino-3-hydroxybutanenitrile stability**: Pathway 1 requires this aminonitrile intermediate to persist long enough for hydrolysis. Its stability under UV irradiation, varying pH, and temperature fluctuations in surface pools needs further study. Competing reactions (polymerization, reaction with other HCN-derived species) could reduce yields.

5. **Formaldehyde concentration**: Pathway 2 requires formaldehyde, which is volatile and reactive. Its prebiotic concentration in hydrothermal environments is debated. Mineral adsorption, continuous production from CO2 reduction via serpentinization-derived H2, and thermophoretic concentration may maintain sufficient steady-state levels.

6. **Alanine as precursor**: Pathway 2 requires L-alanine as a substrate, which must itself be produced by upstream prebiotic reactions. The availability of alanine in sufficient concentrations at hydrothermal vents, where it is simultaneously being consumed by aldol condensation and potentially decomposed at high temperatures, represents a kinetic bottleneck that needs quantitative modeling.

7. **Selectivity and side products**: The aldol condensation of alanine with formaldehyde (Pathway 2) could potentially produce other isomeric products besides threonine. The stereoselectivity and regioselectivity of this reaction under prebiotic conditions, and the relative yields of threonine versus allo-threonine or other isomers, need further characterization.
